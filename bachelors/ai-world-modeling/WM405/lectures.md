## Course Description

This practicum places students in real-world world modeling teams, where they apply the governance, architecture, and ethical frameworks learned throughout the program. Students work 15–20 hours per week at an approved internship site, contributing to active world modeling projects under the supervision of industry mentors. The practicum emphasizes the transition from academic understanding to professional practice — the moment when theory meets reality and students discover which principles hold, which bend, and which break under the pressure of real deadlines, real stakeholders, and real constraints.

**Prerequisites:** WM401, WM403 (concurrent enrollment permitted)
**Note:** This is a practicum course. Students spend the majority of their time at their internship site, with weekly seminars for reflection and integration.

## Internship Structure

### Placement and Supervision

Each student is matched with an approved internship site through the University of Yggdrasil's Industry Partnerships Office. Placement considers the student's areas of interest, technical skills, and career goals. The internship includes:

1. **Industry Mentor**: A professional world modeler at the internship site who provides day-to-day guidance, technical supervision, and career mentorship.
2. **Academic Advisor**: A University of Yggdrasil faculty member who ensures the internship meets academic standards and connects practical experience to theoretical frameworks.
3. **Weekly Seminar**: A 2-hour seminar where all interns share experiences, discuss challenges, and integrate their practical learning with the program's theoretical foundations.

## Practicum Phases

### Phase 1: Orientation and Integration (Weeks 1–2)

The first two weeks are dedicated to understanding the internship site's world modeling infrastructure, meeting the team, and identifying the project the student will contribute to. Students complete:

1. **Site Orientation**: Tour of the facility, introduction to the team, review of ongoing projects, and security/compliance training.
2. **Codebase Walkthrough**: Guided exploration of the site's world modeling codebase, with emphasis on governance features, constitutional constraints, and audit mechanisms.
3. **Project Selection**: In consultation with the industry mentor and academic advisor, students select or define a specific project contribution that aligns with both the site's needs and the student's learning goals.
4. **Learning Contract**: A written agreement specifying the project scope, expected deliverables, timeline, governance integration requirements, and assessment criteria.

**Deliverable**: Learning Contract (signed by student, industry mentor, and academic advisor)

### Phase 2: Active Contribution (Weeks 3–10)

The core of the practicum. Students work 15–20 hours per week on their chosen project, contributing meaningfully to the internship site's world modeling efforts. Weekly activities include:

1. **Daily Work**: Contributing code, documentation, governance specifications, audit tools, or model improvements to the project.
2. **Mentor Meetings**: Weekly 30-minute check-ins with the industry mentor to discuss progress, challenges, and professional development.
3. **Weekly Seminar**: 2-hour group session with all interns to share experiences and integrate practical learning with theory.
4. **Mid-Practicum Review**: At Week 6, a formal review with the industry mentor and academic advisor to assess progress and adjust the learning contract if needed.

During this phase, students are expected to apply at least three of the following frameworks from the program:
- The Norn Constitute (constitutional framework)
- The Þing Assembly Protocol (democratic governance)
- Emergent harm detection (statistical parity, power asymmetry, etc.)
- Priority architecture (P0/P1/P2 tier system)
- The Open Book Protocol (transparency and auditing)
- Preference aggregation methods (Condorcet, deliberative, etc.)

**Deliverable**: Mid-Practicum Progress Report (Week 6)

### Phase 3: Integration and Reflection (Weeks 11–14)

The final phase shifts from active contribution to integration and synthesis. Students:

1. **Complete Project Deliverable**: Finish the project work defined in the learning contract, including code, documentation, and governance specifications.
2. **Write Practicum Reflection Paper**: A 3000–4000 word paper reflecting on the internship experience, connecting practical observations to theoretical frameworks, and identifying areas where theory and practice diverged.
3. **Present at Practicum Symposium**: A 15-minute presentation at the end-of-semester Practicum Symposium, sharing findings with peers, faculty, and industry partners.
4. **Submit Mentor Evaluation**: The industry mentor submits a formal evaluation of the student's professional performance, technical contribution, and growth over the practicum.

**Deliverables**: Project Deliverable, Practicum Reflection Paper, Practicum Symposium Presentation

Just as Óðinn wandered Midgard in disguise to understand the human condition, students will enter professional settings to understand how world modeling theory translates into practice.

**Topics:**
- Professional ethics in world modeling
- Confidentiality and intellectual property at internship sites
- The transition from student to professional
- Setting realistic goals for the practicum
- Balancing academic requirements with workplace expectations

## Assessment Structure

| Component | Weight | Due |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| Learning Contract | 10% | Week 2 |
| Mid-Practicum Progress Report | 15% | Week 6 |
| Project Deliverable | 30% | Week 14 |
| Practicum Reflection Paper | 25% | Week 14 |
| Practicum Symposium Presentation | 10% | Weeks 11–12 |
| Mentor Evaluation | 10% | Week 14 |

### Learning Contract

A written agreement specifying:
- Project scope and expected deliverables
- Timeline with milestones
- Governance integration requirements (at least 3 frameworks from the program)
- Assessment criteria
- Emergency procedures (what to do if the project fails, the mentor leaves, etc.)

### Mid-Practicum Progress Report

A 1500–2000 word report covering:
1. **Project Progress**: What has been accomplished, what remains, and any adjustments to the learning contract.
2. **Governance Integration**: How at least two governance frameworks from the program have been applied in practice. Where did they work? Where did they fail? Why?
3. **Stakeholder Observations**: Who are the stakeholders at the internship site? How are their interests represented (or not) in the world model's governance?
4. **Reflection**: What has surprised you about the transition from theory to practice?

### Project Deliverable

The specific deliverable depends on the project chosen in the learning contract, but all projects must include:
1. **Technical Component**: Code, configuration, or specification that contributes to the internship site's world modeling work.
2. **Governance Component**: A governance specification (constitutional clause, audit regime, Þing Assembly structure, etc.) that integrates at least three frameworks from the program.
3. **Documentation**: Clear documentation of the project, its governance implications, and its integration with the site's existing governance structure.
4. **Emergent Harm Assessment**: An analysis of potential emergent harms from the project, with detection and mitigation strategies.

### Practicum Reflection Paper (3000–4000 words)

A reflective essay connecting practical experience to theoretical frameworks:

1. **The Journey**: Describe your practicum experience — what you did, what you learned, what challenged you.
2. **Theory Meets Practice**: Where did the program's frameworks (Norn Constitute, Þing Assembly Protocol, etc.) work in practice? Where did they fall short? Why?
3. **Governance in the Wild**: How does real-world governance differ from theoretical governance? What gaps did you identify?
4. **Professional Growth**: How has this practicum changed your understanding of world modeling as a profession? Of your own professional identity?
5. **Forward Path**: What would you do differently? What frameworks would you revise? What advice would you give to next year's practicum students?

### Mentor Evaluation

The industry mentor evaluates the student on:
- **Technical Competence**: Quality of work, problem-solving ability, technical skills.
- **Professional Conduct**: Reliability, communication, teamwork, ethical behavior.
- **Growth**: Improvement over the practicum, responsiveness to feedback, initiative.
- **Governance Integration**: Ability to apply governance frameworks in practice.

## Practicum Learning Outcomes

Upon completion of this practicum, students will be able to:

1. **Apply** governance frameworks (Norn Constitute, Þing Assembly Protocol, priority architecture, Open Book Protocol) to real-world world modeling projects.
2. **Evaluate** the effectiveness of governance structures in professional settings, identifying gaps between theory and practice.
3. **Facilitate** stakeholder engagement using deliberative methods inspired by the Þing Assembly Protocol.
4. **Detect** emergent harms in deployed world models using statistical parity, power asymmetry, and opinion dynamics methods.
5. **Communicate** governance requirements to both technical and non-technical stakeholders.
6. **Reflect** critically on the relationship between theoretical governance and professional practice.
7. **Navigate** organizational constraints on world model governance with integrity and professionalism.

## The World Modeler's Oath

At the conclusion of the Practicum Symposium, all interns recite the World Modeler's Oath — a professional commitment modeled on the Hippocratic Oath and the Norse oath of *tryggð* (troth, fidelity). The oath has been composed by the faculty of the World Modeling program in consultation with the Nordic AI Safety Authority, the Þingvellir Governance Institute, and the University of Yggdrasil's Ethics Board.

### The Oath

*I swear by Yggdrasil, the world-tree, and by the waters of Mímir's Well, and by the judgment of the Þing:*

*That I will practice world modeling according to my ability and judgment, for the benefit of all who are affected by the worlds I create, and not for the profit of the few at the expense of the many.*

*That I will apply the Norn Constitute in all my work — hard constraints that cannot be overridden, procedural constraints that require justification, and soft constraints that I will honor wherever possible.*

*That I will not permit discrimination, exploitation, or harm to emerge from my models without detecting it, reporting it, and working to mitigate it.*

*That I will maintain transparency in all my governance decisions, making the raw data, formal specifications, and natural language explanations of every decision available to all affected stakeholders.*

*That I will convene the Þing — giving every affected stakeholder a meaningful voice in the governance of the worlds I create, and honoring the assembly's decisions even when I disagree with them.*

*That I will remain vigilant — watching for emergent harm, constitutional violations, and governance failures — and that I will speak honestly about what I find, even when it is inconvenient.*

*That I will continue to learn, to question, and to improve the governance of world models, for the benefit of all who depend on the realities I simulate.*

*May I carry this oath in my heart, as the Norns weave it into the fabric of the worlds I create.*

**ᚠ Fehu — Wealth. The simulation produces value for all.**
**ᚦ Thurs — Judgment. The world modeler renders honest judgment.**
**ᚾ Nauðr — Necessity. Governance is not optional.**
**ᛗ Mannaz — Humanity. The people govern the worlds they inhabit.**
