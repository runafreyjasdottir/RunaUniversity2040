# IT205 — Network Administration

## Course Overview
**Credits:** 4 | **Prerequisites:** IT101, IT103 | **Term:** Year 2, Semester 1

Network Administration is the beating heart of the modern digital longhouse — the discipline that keeps data flowing across the Bifröst-like bridges between systems, users, and services. In this course, students move beyond the theoretical models of introductory networking into the hands-on craft of designing, deploying, securing, and maintaining enterprise-scale network infrastructure. Through a blend of vendor-neutral architectural principles and practical tool mastery — Wireshark packet analysis, Ansible-driven configuration management, firewall policy authoring, and SDN orchestration — you will develop the instincts of a seasoned network administrator capable of diagnosing outages before users notice and architecting resilient topologies that withstand both hardware failures and adversarial assault.

The year 2040 presents network administrators with challenges our predecessors could scarcely have imagined: terabit-scale backbone links carrying neural-simulation traffic, quantum-key-distributed encryption overlays, AI-driven traffic engineering that rewrites routing tables in microseconds, and the relentless migration from IPv4's exhausted address space into IPv6's vast frontier. This course grounds you in the enduring fundamentals — the protocols, the troubleshooting methodology, the security mindset — while equipping you with fluency in the automation and observability toolchains that define modern practice.

## Lecture 1: The Network Administrator's Domain — Architecture, Responsibility, and the OSI Model Revisited

Every network administrator inherits a domain: a bounded territory of switches, routers, firewalls, access points, and the copper and fibre arteries connecting them. Before we can configure a VLAN or tune a BGP session, we must understand the architectural principles that govern how these components compose into a coherent, manageable whole. This lecture establishes the conceptual foundations upon which all subsequent practice rests.

The Open Systems Interconnection (OSI) reference model, though conceived in 1977 at the height of mainframe computing, remains the indispensable mental map for network troubleshooting. We begin with a rigorous review — not the seven-layer memorization exercise of introductory courses, but a diagnostic framework. When a user reports "the application is slow," where do you begin? The physical layer (Layer 1) — is there light on the fibre, a link LED on the port? The data link layer (Layer 2) — are frames arriving intact, or is the switch port accumulating CRC errors? The network layer (Layer 3) — is the routing table converged, or is there a black hole swallowing packets? The transport layer (Layer 4) — is TCP window scaling negotiating correctly across the WAN link, or are we seeing retransmission storms? The session, presentation, and application layers (Layers 5-7) — is TLS negotiation completing in under 10ms, or is certificate validation stalling the handshake? A competent network administrator carries this layered mental model as instinctively as a blacksmith knows the temperature of steel by its colour. The key insight of 2040 practice is that the OSI model now extends beyond Layer 7: the "Layer 8" of organizational politics, budget cycles, and vendor relationships shapes network design as profoundly as any technical constraint.

Network architecture in 2040 is shaped by three dominant paradigms. First, the **spine-leaf topology**, which has supplanted the traditional three-tier (access-distribution-core) model in data centres and increasingly in campus networks. In a spine-leaf fabric, every leaf switch connects to every spine switch, creating a non-blocking, predictably-latency architecture where any host can reach any other host in exactly two hops. The Clos network mathematics underlying this design — named for Charles Clos's 1953 paper on non-blocking switching — guarantees that with sufficient spine capacity, no traffic pattern can saturate the fabric. Second, **Software-Defined Networking (SDN)** has matured from academic curiosity to production standard: the control plane (routing decisions) is decoupled from the data plane (packet forwarding) and centralized in a controller that maintains a global network graph. This enables traffic engineering at a sophistication impossible with distributed protocols alone — flows can be rerouted around congestion in sub-second timescales based on real-time telemetry. Third, the **Zero Trust security model** has rearchitected the perimeter: the network no longer trusts anything simply because it's "inside the firewall." Every connection, every packet flow, must authenticate and authorize independently, a principle we will explore in depth in Lectures 5 and 6.

The network administrator's responsibilities in 2040 span a broader scope than ever before. Beyond the traditional trio — configure, monitor, troubleshoot — the modern NOC (Network Operations Centre) engineer must be fluent in Infrastructure as Code (IaC), capable of version-controlling router configs in Git repositories and deploying changes through CI/CD pipelines with automated pre-flight validation. They must understand the security implications of every protocol enabled on their network, from the BGP peering sessions that determine global reachability to the mDNS advertisements bouncing around the office VLAN. They must read telemetry dashboards with the fluency of a physician reading vital signs, spotting anomalous patterns — a sudden spike in DNS queries to unfamiliar domains, a port flapping at 3 AM that correlates with a failing transceiver — before they cascade into outages. And they must communicate: translating between the technical reality of the network and the business requirements it serves, explaining to a VP why the "simple" change they requested requires a maintenance window and a rollback plan.

**Required Reading:** Kurose & Ross, *Computer Networking: A Top-Down Approach* (8th ed., 2025), Chapter 1; Claise & Clarke, *Network Management in the Age of Automation* (2038 Yggdrasil Press), Chapters 1-2; RFC 1122 "Requirements for Internet Hosts."

**Discussion Questions:**
1. How does the OSI model's separation of concerns both enable and complicate network troubleshooting? Provide a specific scenario where a Layer 2 symptom masked a Layer 4 root cause.
2. Compare the operational tradeoffs between a traditional three-tier campus architecture and a spine-leaf fabric. Under what conditions would you still recommend the three-tier model in 2040?
3. The Zero Trust model challenges decades of "trusted internal network" assumptions. What cultural and organizational obstacles does a network administrator face when advocating for Zero Trust adoption?

## Lecture 2: IP Addressing and Subnetting Mastery — CIDR, VLSM, and the IPv6 Frontier

If the OSI model is the network administrator's anatomy textbook, IP addressing is their language. Every device on the network — every server, sensor, drone controller, and neural processing unit — participates in the great conversation of the Internet through a uniquely assigned address. Misconfigured addressing is among the most common causes of network failure, and mastery of subnetting is the skill that separates the novice from the professional. This lecture drills into the addressing schemes that define modern network architecture, from the exhausted-but-still-dominant IPv4 space to the vast horizons of IPv6.

Classless Inter-Domain Routing (CIDR), formalized in RFC 1519, liberated network design from the rigid classful boundaries of early Internet architecture. Instead of being constrained to /8, /16, or /24 networks, administrators could carve address space to precisely match organizational needs. A `/22` network (1,022 usable hosts) could serve a mid-sized office; a `/28` (14 hosts) might suffice for a point-to-point link. The CIDR notation `192.168.1.0/24` concisely encodes both the network prefix and the subnet mask, and the ability to mentally decompose a `/19` into its constituent subnets — the network address, the broadcast address, the usable range — must become reflexive. We will practice VLSM (Variable Length Subnet Masking) exercises: given an organization assigned `10.20.0.0/16`, design an addressing scheme that allocates a `/18` to the headquarters campus (16,382 hosts), a `/20` to each of four regional offices (4,094 hosts each), `/24` subnets for each floor's access VLAN, and `/30` subnets for inter-router links, leaving room for growth.

IPv4 address exhaustion has been a reality since the IANA exhausted its free pool in 2011 and all five RIRs followed suit by 2019. In 2040, IPv4 addresses are a traded commodity — a single `/24` block commands thousands of dollars on the grey market. Network Address Translation (NAT), particularly Carrier-Grade NAT (CGNAT) operating at ISP scale, has extended IPv4's useful life but at significant cost: broken end-to-end connectivity, complications for peer-to-peer protocols, and a security model that confuses obscurity with protection. Yet IPv4 remains stubbornly present in 2040, particularly in embedded systems, legacy industrial control networks, and — paradoxically — many cloud providers' internal fabrics that have not yet completed their v6 migrations. The network administrator of 2040 must therefore be bilingual: fluent in both v4 and v6, capable of managing dual-stack deployments and diagnosing the subtle failures that arise when v6 falls back to v4 through misconfigured transition mechanisms.

IPv6, standardized in 1998 and production-ready for over two decades, is the inevitable destination. Its 128-bit address space — 340 undecillion addresses, roughly 4.8 × 10²⁸ addresses for every person on Earth — eliminates the scarcity that defined IPv4 networking. But IPv6 is not merely "IPv4 with longer addresses." It fundamentally rethinks several architectural assumptions: Stateless Address Autoconfiguration (SLAAC) eliminates the need for DHCP in many deployments; the elimination of broadcast in favour of multicast and anycast creates a quieter, more efficient network; and the mandatory support for IPsec at the network layer promises — though has not always delivered — a more secure baseline. The 2040 network administrator must understand IPv6 extension headers, Neighbor Discovery Protocol (NDP) and its security implications (RA Guard, SEND), the migration from NAT to transparent addressing, and the operational realities of dual-stack networks where v6 path MTU discovery can fail silently in ways that v4's fragmentation model never did.

**Required Reading:** RFC 4632 "CIDR Address Strategy"; RFC 8200 "IPv6 Specification"; Hinden & Deering, "IP Version 6 Addressing Architecture" (RFC 4291); Doyle & Carroll, *Routing TCP/IP Volume I* (2nd ed.), Chapters 1-3.

**Discussion Questions:**
1. A colleague argues that NAT provides a security benefit by hiding internal addresses. How would you respond, drawing on the principle that "security through obscurity" is not security?
2. Design a dual-stack addressing plan for a campus with 5,000 hosts, ensuring all internal services are reachable via both v4 and v6. What transition mechanisms (NAT64, DNS64, 464XLAT) would you deploy at the edge?
3. IPv6 adoption has been slower than its designers anticipated. What economic, technical, and organizational factors explain this, and what role does the network administrator play in accelerating migration?

## Lecture 3: Routing Protocols and the Architecture of Reachability

Routing is the intelligence of the network — the distributed computation by which every router builds a map of reachable destinations and determines the optimal path for each packet. Without routing, the Internet would be a collection of isolated broadcast domains; with it, a packet sent from a Yggdrasil University laboratory in Reykjavík can traverse seventeen autonomous systems and arrive at a server in Singapore in under 150 milliseconds. This lecture examines the protocols that make this possible and the architectural decisions that separate a well-routed network from a fragile one.

We begin with the interior gateway protocols (IGPs) that operate within a single administrative domain. **OSPF (Open Shortest Path First)**, an IETF-standardized link-state protocol, remains the workhorse of enterprise routing in 2040. Each OSPF router builds a complete topology graph of its area using Dijkstra's shortest-path-first algorithm (a computation of O(E log V) complexity for a network with E edges and V vertices), then independently computes the optimal path tree with itself as root. The key design decisions in OSPF deployment — area partitioning, LSA (Link State Advertisement) type management, stub/totally-stubby/NSSA area configurations — determine whether the network converges in milliseconds or minutes after a topology change. We will examine the OSPF database exchange process in detail: the Hello protocol, the Designated Router election on broadcast segments, the Database Description exchange, and the reliable flooding of LSAs through Link State Update packets. **IS-IS**, OSPF's less-visible sibling originally designed for the OSI protocol suite and later extended for IP, has gained renewed relevance in 2040's large-scale data centre fabrics due to its TLV-based extensibility and simpler architecture.

**BGP (Border Gateway Protocol)**, now at version 4 and specified in RFC 4271, is the protocol that binds the Internet together. Unlike the IGPs, which optimize for shortest paths within a domain, BGP is a path-vector protocol optimized for policy: an autonomous system announces which prefixes it can reach, and each AS along the path can filter, prepend, or otherwise modify the announcement based on business relationships, peering agreements, and security considerations. A BGP table in 2040 contains over 1.5 million IPv4 prefixes and approximately 200,000 IPv6 prefixes, and the decision process by which a router selects the best path — weighing Local Preference, AS Path length, origin type, MED (Multi-Exit Discriminator), and a dozen other attributes — is among the most complex algorithms in operational networking. We will practice configuring eBGP peering sessions, crafting route maps and prefix lists to implement inbound and outbound traffic engineering, and diagnosing the pathologies of BGP: route leaks (the 2019 Verizon-DQE incident, the 2021 AS55410 leak), prefix hijacking, and the slow convergence that can black-hole traffic for minutes while the path-hunting algorithm explores alternatives.

In 2040, routing has been augmented — though not replaced — by **Software-Defined Networking (SDN)** controllers that maintain a global network view and can program forwarding behaviour through protocols like OpenFlow or P4. Segment Routing (SR), standardized in RFC 8402, encodes the forwarding path directly in the packet header as a stack of segment identifiers (SIDs), eliminating the need for per-flow state in intermediate routers. SR-MPLS and SRv6 (Segment Routing over IPv6) represent the convergence of distributed and centralized routing paradigms: the controller computes the optimal path, but the network executes it using the same label-switching mechanisms that have carried traffic for decades. The network administrator of 2040 must be conversant with both worlds — capable of debugging a BGP session on a CLI one moment and inspecting an SDN controller's path computation the next.

**Required Reading:** Moy, *OSPF: Anatomy of an Internet Routing Protocol* (1998); RFC 4271 "BGP-4"; Doyle & Carroll, *Routing TCP/IP Volume II* (2nd ed.), Chapters 1-6; Filsfils et al., "Segment Routing Architecture" (RFC 8402).

**Discussion Questions:**
1. OSPF areas were designed to limit link-state database size and flooding scope. In an era of routers with terabytes of memory and 100 Gbps control-plane bandwidth, are areas still necessary, or are they an architectural fossil?
2. BGP's path selection process prioritizes policy over performance — a path with worse latency might be preferred if it satisfies a commercial peering agreement. Is this a fundamental conflict of interest that harms the Internet, or a necessary feature of a federated network?
3. How does Segment Routing change the division of labour between distributed routing protocols and centralized controllers? What operational risks does this shift introduce?

## Lecture 4: Switching Architecture and VLAN Design — The Campus Network

If routing is the intelligence of the network, switching is its muscle — the high-speed, hardware-accelerated forwarding that moves frames between hosts on the same Layer 2 domain. Campus network design, the art of connecting thousands of users across buildings and floors through a hierarchy of switches, requires careful attention to loop prevention, broadcast containment, and the logical segmentation that maps organizational boundaries onto network topology. This lecture moves from the switching fundamentals to the design patterns that define modern campus architectures.

The spanning tree protocols — STP (802.1D), Rapid Spanning Tree (RSTP, 802.1w), and Multiple Spanning Tree (MSTP, 802.1s) — solve a fundamental topological problem: Ethernet requires a loop-free topology, but redundant links are essential for resilience. Spanning tree operates by electing a root bridge, then selectively blocking ports that would create loops, building a tree that reaches every switch exactly once. The configuration decisions that determine spanning tree behaviour — root bridge placement (should the core switch be the root, or an edge switch accidentally elected because someone plugged in a lab device with bridge priority 0?), PortFast and BPDU Guard on access ports, the careful assignment of VLANs to MST instances — are where network administration intersects with operational discipline. A misconfigured spanning tree can silently redirect traffic through suboptimal paths, create broadcast storms when a blocked link transitions to forwarding without proper synchronization, or — in the worst case — melt down an entire data centre when a bridging loop forms. The 2040 administrator leans heavily on automation to enforce spanning tree policy: the network's intended topology is declared in a Git repository, and Ansible playbooks push the consistent configuration that guarantees the core switch is root and no rogue bridge can disrupt the tree.

VLANs (Virtual Local Area Networks), standardized in IEEE 802.1Q, carve a single physical switch fabric into multiple logically isolated broadcast domains. The design of a VLAN plan — which departments, device types, and security zones map to which VLANs — is an exercise in organizational sociology as much as network engineering. Should the Finance department share a VLAN with the HR department because they're on the same floor, or be separated because their security postures differ? Should IP phones occupy a dedicated voice VLAN (as is best practice, ensuring QoS and isolating the notoriously chatty phone firmware from the data network), or can modern VoIP stack handle coexistence? The 802.1Q tagging mechanism, which inserts a 4-byte tag into the Ethernet frame header containing a 12-bit VLAN ID (allowing 4,094 usable VLANs), is simple in concept but subtle in implementation: the native VLAN on a trunk port, the difference between an access port (one VLAN, untagged) and a trunk port (multiple VLANs, tagged), the security implications of VLAN hopping attacks that exploit switch spoofing or double-tagging.

The 2040 campus network increasingly adopts **fabric architectures** that abstract the underlying VLAN complexity. Technologies like VXLAN (Virtual Extensible LAN), originally designed for data centre overlays, are extending into the campus: a 24-bit VNI (VXLAN Network Identifier) supports 16 million logical segments, and the underlay — typically a routed IPv6 fabric using EBGP for route distribution — carries encapsulated traffic between VXLAN Tunnel Endpoints (VTEPs). The network administrator works at the overlay, defining logical networks and policies, while the underlay self-configures. Yet the fundamentals remain: when a user in VLAN 100 cannot reach a server in VLAN 200, the troubleshooting path still begins with verifying the VLAN assignment on the access port, confirming the trunk configuration, checking that the SVIs (Switched Virtual Interfaces) have correct IP addresses, and tracing the routing table.

**Required Reading:** IEEE 802.1Q-2022 "Bridges and Bridged Networks"; Perlman, *Interconnections: Bridges, Routers, Switches, and Internetworking Protocols* (2nd ed.); RFC 7348 "VXLAN: A Framework for Overlaying Virtualized Layer 2 Networks over Layer 3 Networks."

**Discussion Questions:**
1. Spanning Tree Protocol has a reputation for fragility. Compare the operational tradeoffs between a spanning-tree-based campus and one built on a routed access layer with Layer 3 to the edge.
2. VLAN design often reflects organizational hierarchy. What happens when the organization restructures — departments merge or split — and the VLAN plan must change? Propose a naming and numbering scheme resilient to organizational change.
3. VXLAN fabrics extend Layer 2 connectivity across Layer 3 boundaries. What new failure modes does this introduce, and how should the network administrator prepare for them?

## Lecture 5: Virtual Private Networks and Secure Remote Access — From IPsec to Zero Trust

The perimeter dissolved sometime in the 2020s. When every employee carries a device that connects from coffee shops, co-working spaces, and home offices, the network's boundary is no longer defined by the walls of a data centre or the ports of a core switch. The Virtual Private Network (VPN) — the encrypted tunnel that extends the trusted network across the untrusted Internet — became simultaneously more essential and more problematic. This lecture examines the protocols, architectures, and security models that govern remote access in 2040.

**IPsec (Internet Protocol Security)** is the veteran of the VPN world, standardized in 1995 and battle-tested across three decades of deployment. It operates at Layer 3, encrypting and authenticating IP packets between two endpoints — either two security gateways (site-to-site VPN, connecting the Oslo office to the Reykjavík office) or a remote host and a gateway (remote access VPN). The IPsec suite comprises two security protocols: AH (Authentication Header), which provides integrity but not confidentiality (and is rarely used in modern deployments), and ESP (Encapsulating Security Payload), which provides both. The IKEv2 (Internet Key Exchange version 2) protocol handles the complex dance of peer authentication, algorithm negotiation, and key material generation. The network administrator configuring an IPsec VPN must navigate a labyrinth of choices: pre-shared keys or certificate-based authentication? AES-256-GCM or ChaCha20-Poly1305 for encryption? Perfect Forward Secrecy (PFS) through ephemeral Diffie-Hellman exchange, ensuring that compromise of the long-term key does not retroactively decrypt captured traffic? IKEv2's MOBIKE extension, which allows a mobile client to roam between networks without re-establishing the SA, is essential for the 2040 workforce that transitions between cellular, Wi-Fi, and satellite connections mid-session.

**WireGuard**, designed by Jason A. Donenfeld and merged into the Linux kernel in 2020, represents a radical simplification of the VPN paradigm. Where IPsec's specifications span dozens of RFCs totalling thousands of pages, WireGuard's core protocol can be described in a single research paper. It eschews cryptographic agility — the ability to negotiate algorithms — in favour of a fixed, modern suite: Curve25519 for key exchange, ChaCha20 for symmetric encryption, Poly1305 for authentication, BLAKE2s for hashing, and SipHash24 for hash table keys. The result is a codebase of approximately 4,000 lines (versus IPsec's hundreds of thousands) that is auditable, fast (kernel-space implementation), and resistant to the configuration errors that plague IPsec deployments. WireGuard's "cryptokey routing" model — where each peer's public key is associated with a list of allowed IP addresses, and incoming packets are silently dropped if they don't cryptographically verify — elegantly collapses authentication, authorization, and routing into a single table lookup. In 2040, WireGuard has largely displaced legacy VPN protocols for point-to-point tunnels, though IPsec retains its position in site-to-site deployments where interoperability with legacy equipment is required.

The most significant architectural shift of the 2030s, however, has been the transition from VPN-centric remote access to **Zero Trust Network Access (ZTNA)**. The traditional VPN model — connect to the network, then access resources — grants the remote device broad network access, creating a lateral movement risk if that device is compromised. ZTNA inverts this: the user authenticates to an identity-aware proxy, and each application access is individually authorized based on user identity, device posture (is the endpoint patched? is its antivirus current?), and context (is the request coming from a geolocation consistent with the user's pattern?). The device never gains general network access; it only receives connections to specific, authorized applications. Architectures like Google's BeyondCorp, the NIST SP 800-207 Zero Trust Architecture framework, and commercial implementations from Zscaler, Cloudflare, and Netskope represent this paradigm. The 2040 network administrator must understand both the legacy VPN infrastructure they may inherit and the ZTNA architecture they will deploy, recognizing that the migration from one to the other is as much an organizational change management exercise as a technical one.

**Required Reading:** RFC 7296 "IKEv2"; Donenfeld, "WireGuard: Next Generation Kernel Network Tunnel" (NDSS 2017); NIST SP 800-207 "Zero Trust Architecture"; Rose, Borchert, Mitchell, & Connelly, "Google's BeyondCorp Approach to Enterprise Security" (USENIX ;login:, 2016).

**Discussion Questions:**
1. WireGuard's refusal to support cryptographic agility — "the protocol is the cipher suite" — is either its greatest strength or its greatest weakness. Argue both sides.
2. A VPN grants network access; ZTNA grants application access. What attack surfaces does each model leave open that the other closes?
3. How would you design a phased migration from a legacy IPsec remote-access VPN to a ZTNA architecture for a mid-sized enterprise, ensuring no loss of availability during the transition?

## Lecture 6: Firewall Architecture and Security Policy Design

The firewall is the gatekeeper of the network — the enforcement point where policy decisions about permitted and denied traffic are rendered into packet-level action. From the simple packet-filtering routers of the 1990s to the deep-packet-inspecting, threat-intelligence-fed next-generation firewalls (NGFWs) of 2040, the fundamental principle endures: inspect every packet against a rule set, and either permit, deny, or log. This lecture equips you with the theory and practice of firewall policy design, from the individual rule to the enterprise-wide security posture.

Firewall architectures have evolved through several generations. **Stateless packet filters** (Generation 1) examine each packet in isolation: source IP, destination IP, protocol, source port, destination port. They are fast but blind — they cannot distinguish between a legitimate HTTP response and an attack tunneled over port 80. **Stateful inspection firewalls** (Generation 2) maintain a connection table, tracking the state of each TCP session (SYN, SYN-ACK, ACK, FIN) and UDP "pseudo-connections," allowing them to permit return traffic that belongs to an established session without opening the corresponding inbound port. iptables/netfilter in Linux — with its tables (filter, nat, mangle, raw) and chains (INPUT, OUTPUT, FORWARD, PREROUTING, POSTROUTING) — remains the most widely deployed stateful firewall in 2040, powering everything from cloud-hosted virtual firewalls to the embedded packet filters in IoT gateways. We will practice crafting iptables and nftables rulesets that implement layered security policies: default-deny on inbound traffic, stateful allow for established connections, rate limiting on SSH to thwart brute-force attacks, logging of denied packets for forensic analysis.

**Next-Generation Firewalls (NGFWs)** represent the third generation, integrating deep packet inspection (DPI), application identification, and threat intelligence feeds into the packet processing pipeline. A NGFW doesn't just see "TCP port 443" — it identifies the application as "TLS-encrypted traffic from the Salesforce web application," distinguishing it from "TLS-encrypted traffic to an unknown server that happens to use port 443 for C2 (Command and Control)." NGFWs incorporate intrusion prevention systems (IPS) that can block exploit attempts in real time, URL filtering that enforces acceptable-use policies, and SSL/TLS decryption that — controversially — terminates and re-encrypts traffic to inspect the plaintext. The 2040 administrator deploying NGFW policies must navigate the tension between visibility (decrypting traffic to detect threats) and privacy (respecting the confidentiality guarantees that TLS was designed to provide), a tension that has only intensified as end-to-end encryption becomes the default for all communications.

The craft of firewall rule design is as much about process as syntax. A poorly organized rule set — a thousand rules accumulated over years, with overlapping scopes, contradictory actions, and no documentation — is a security liability. Best practices in 2040 include: **rule ordering by frequency** (the most-matched rules first, to minimize processing overhead); **explicit deny-all at the bottom** of every policy list with logging enabled; **change management that requires justification, peer review, and scheduled expiration** for temporary rules (the "firewall rule that was needed for a weekend test in 2038 and was never removed" is a real security problem); and **infrastructure-as-code treatment** of firewall policies, where rules are defined in structured configuration files, version-controlled in Git, peer-reviewed through pull requests, and deployed through CI/CD pipelines that perform pre-flight syntax validation and policy simulation. Tools like Capirca, Aerleon, and commercial firewall orchestrators have made this approach practical. A firewall rule committed to a repository with a reviewer's approval and an automated deployment pipeline is orders of magnitude safer than a rule typed into a vendor GUI at 2 AM during an outage.

**Required Reading:** Chapman & Zwicky, *Building Internet Firewalls* (2nd ed.); Cheswick, Bellovin, & Rubin, *Firewalls and Internet Security: Repelling the Wily Hacker* (2nd ed.); NIST SP 800-41 Rev. 1 "Guidelines on Firewalls and Firewall Policy."

**Discussion Questions:**
1. SSL/TLS decryption at the firewall enables threat detection but undermines end-to-end encryption. Under what circumstances is this tradeoff ethically and operationally justified?
2. A firewall rule with source "any" and destination "any" permitting port 22 is a security anti-pattern. Design a secure alternative for remote SSH access to a management network.
3. How does treating firewall policy as code — version-controlled, peer-reviewed, CI/CD-deployed — change the operational reliability and security of the network? What new risks does it introduce?

## Lecture 7: Intrusion Detection, Network Forensics, and Threat Response

Firewalls prevent; intrusion detection systems (IDS) detect. The security posture of a 2040 network rests on this layered defence: the firewall blocks what it can identify as malicious based on known patterns, while the IDS — and its active counterpart, the intrusion prevention system (IPS) — monitors traffic for signs of compromise that evaded the perimeter. This lecture develops the network administrator's capabilities in threat detection, forensic analysis, and incident response.

Intrusion detection operates on two principles: **signature-based detection**, which matches traffic against a database of known attack patterns (the Snort or Suricata rule that triggers on a specific buffer overflow exploit), and **anomaly-based detection**, which builds a statistical baseline of normal network behaviour and alerts on deviations (a server that normally sends 50 DNS queries per hour suddenly sending 5,000 — possibly a data exfiltration attempt tunnelled over DNS). The 2040 IDS ecosystem has been transformed by machine learning: neural network models trained on years of labelled traffic data can identify novel attack patterns that no signature writer has yet codified. However, these ML-based systems introduce their own challenges — false positives that desensitize the SOC (Security Operations Centre) team, adversarial examples that can fool the classifier, and the "black box" problem where the analyst cannot explain *why* an alert fired.

Network forensics — the discipline of capturing, preserving, and analyzing network traffic for investigative purposes — requires both technical fluency and procedural rigour. The network administrator responding to an incident must know how to deploy packet capture (tcpdump, tshark, or a dedicated network TAP that mirrors traffic to a forensic storage appliance), apply display filters to isolate relevant flows (`tcpdump -r capture.pcap 'host 192.168.1.100 and port 443'`), reconstruct TCP streams to recover transferred files or command-and-control communications, and — critically — maintain the chain of custody that makes forensic evidence admissible in legal proceedings. PCAP (packet capture) files are the raw footage of network crime scenes, and the ability to read them fluently — following the TCP three-way handshake, spotting the TLS ClientHello that reveals the SNI (Server Name Indication) of the destination, tracing HTTP headers that may contain exfiltration in custom fields — is essential.

Incident response follows a structured lifecycle, most commonly the NIST framework: **Preparation** (building the incident response team, deploying detection tools, establishing communication channels), **Detection and Analysis** (identifying the incident, determining its scope, preserving evidence), **Containment, Eradication, and Recovery** (isolating affected systems, removing the attacker's foothold, restoring from clean backups), and **Post-Incident Activity** (the blameless post-mortem that extracts lessons learned and updates procedures). The network administrator's role in each phase is specific: during detection, they provide packet captures and NetFlow data; during containment, they implement ACLs that block the attacker's IP ranges and sinkhole the C2 domains; during recovery, they verify that restored systems are not re-compromised on reconnection. We will work through a detailed case study: a ransomware attack that entered through a phishing email, moved laterally through RDP (Remote Desktop Protocol) connections, and began encrypting file shares — and the network-level responses that could have detected it at each stage.

**Required Reading:** Bejtlich, *The Tao of Network Security Monitoring* (2005); Sanders, *Practical Packet Analysis* (3rd ed., 2017); NIST SP 800-61 Rev. 2 "Computer Security Incident Handling Guide"; MITRE ATT&CK Framework (https://attack.mitre.org).

**Discussion Questions:**
1. ML-based anomaly detection promises to catch unknown threats but generates high false-positive rates. How should a NOC triage these alerts without succumbing to alert fatigue?
2. Full packet capture for forensic purposes is storage-intensive. A 10 Gbps link generates approximately 1.25 GB of traffic per second. What sampling, filtering, or retention strategies would you employ for a mid-sized enterprise?
3. Describe three network-level indicators of a ransomware attack in progress — what would you see in NetFlow data, DNS queries, and SMB traffic patterns?

## Lecture 8: Network Monitoring and Observability — The Eyes of the Norn

A network unmonitored is a network unmanaged. The ability to see — to measure latency at every hop, to graph interface utilization over time, to correlate a spike in TCP retransmissions with a failing optical transceiver — is what separates proactive administration from reactive firefighting. This lecture covers the tools, protocols, and design patterns that give the network administrator the Eyes of the Norn: comprehensive visibility into the network's health and behaviour.

The monitoring stack in 2040 rests on three pillars. First, **telemetry collection**: SNMP (Simple Network Management Protocol), now in its third major version with robust authentication and encryption, remains the universal language of device monitoring. Every managed switch and router exposes MIBs (Management Information Bases) that report interface counters (ifInOctets, ifOutErrors, ifInDiscards), CPU utilization, temperature sensors, and thousands of other metrics. The 2040 administrator configures SNMPv3 with authPriv security, defines the polling intervals that balance granularity against network overhead, and crafts the SNMP walks that populate time-series databases. Second, **flow data**: NetFlow (Cisco), IPFIX (IETF-standardized), and sFlow provide a higher-level view — not per-interface counters but per-conversation records: source IP, destination IP, protocol, source port, destination port, byte count, packet count. Flow data answers the questions that SNMP cannot: "Who is consuming the most bandwidth on the WAN link?" "Is this traffic spike legitimate video conferencing or data exfiltration?" Third, **synthetic monitoring**: active probes — pings, DNS queries, HTTP requests, TCP connection attempts — that simulate user traffic and measure the network's performance from the user's perspective. A link might show "up" in SNMP but be dropping 30% of TCP connections due to a duplex mismatch; only synthetic monitoring reveals this.

The 2040 observability pipeline typically follows the architecture: **Prometheus** (the CNCF-graduated time-series database) scrapes metrics from network devices via SNMP exporters (the `snmp_exporter` translating MIBs into Prometheus metrics), the **Grafana** visualization platform renders dashboards that present network health at a glance, **Loki** ingests syslog messages for log aggregation and correlation, and **Alertmanager** routes notifications — a port going down, a BGP session flapping, a link exceeding 90% utilization — to the appropriate channels: PagerDuty for critical incidents, Slack for warnings, a ticket in the IT service management system for routine issues. The network administrator designs these dashboards not as decorative wall art but as operational tools: the "single pane of glass" that shows, in one view, the status of every WAN link, every core switch, every critical service endpoint, with drill-down capability that moves from "the Stockholm office is unreachable" to "the BGP session on the Stockholm edge router has been down for 4 minutes" in two clicks.

Observability in 2040 increasingly incorporates **AIOps** — machine learning models that ingest telemetry streams and detect patterns invisible to human operators. An AIOps system might learn that every Tuesday at 2 AM, the backup replication traffic saturates the WAN link, and pre-emptively adjust QoS (Quality of Service) policies before the saturation occurs. It might correlate a subtle increase in DNS latency with a specific upstream resolver and flag it for investigation before users notice. The network administrator's role shifts accordingly: from staring at dashboards waiting for red to appear, to investigating the anomalies surfaced by AI, tuning the models that generate alerts, and ensuring the observability infrastructure itself remains resilient.

**Required Reading:** Claise & Clarke, *Network Management in the Age of Automation* (2038 Yggdrasil Press), Chapters 3-5; Turnbull, *The Art of Monitoring* (2016); Julien, *Prometheus: Up & Running* (2nd ed., 2024); Fauchelle, *Grafana: A Practical Guide* (2035).

## Lecture 9: Wireless Network Administration — Spectrum, Standards, and Security

The wireless network is no longer a convenience — it is the primary access medium for the majority of network clients in 2040. Laptops, phones, augmented reality headsets, IoT sensors, autonomous delivery drones, and environmental monitoring stations all connect through radio waves that must be carefully managed to avoid interference, ensure coverage, and maintain security. This lecture covers the physics, protocols, and operational practices of enterprise wireless administration.

The physical layer of Wi-Fi operates in two frequency bands: the 2.4 GHz ISM band (crowded, with only three non-overlapping 20 MHz channels, but with better wall penetration) and the 5 GHz band (more channels, less interference, shorter range). In 2040, **Wi-Fi 7 (802.11be)** and the emerging **Wi-Fi 8 (802.11bn)** standards push the envelope: 320 MHz channel widths, 4096-QAM modulation, Multi-Link Operation (MLO) that aggregates multiple frequency bands into a single logical connection, and theoretical throughput exceeding 40 Gbps. The network administrator conducting a wireless site survey must understand how these parameters interact with the physical environment: a concrete wall attenuates 5 GHz signals by 15-20 dB; a metal filing cabinet creates a multipath reflection zone; a microwave oven (still!) emits 2.4 GHz interference. Professional site survey tools — Ekahau, AirMagnet, or the open-source WiFi Explorer — generate heat maps that predict signal strength, SNR (Signal-to-Noise Ratio), and channel overlap across the deployment area, guiding access point placement and channel assignment.

Enterprise wireless architecture in 2040 is dominated by **controller-based management**, whether on-premises (Cisco Catalyst 9800 series, Aruba Mobility Controllers) or cloud-managed (Meraki Dashboard, Juniper Mist). The controller centralizes configuration, handles client roaming (ensuring a voice call doesn't drop as the user walks from the lobby AP to the conference room AP), and enforces security policies. The **Lightweight Access Point Protocol (LWAPP/CAPWAP)** tunnels client traffic from the AP back to the controller, enabling consistent policy enforcement regardless of which AP the client associates with. However, the trend toward **controller-less architectures** — where APs coordinate among themselves using distributed protocols, and traffic is locally switched rather than tunnelled — has gained momentum for deployments where controller latency or bandwidth is a concern. The 2040 administrator designs the wireless architecture to match the use case: tunnelled for high-security environments where all traffic must be inspected, locally-switched for high-bandwidth scenarios like VR streaming labs.

Wireless security demands particular attention because radio waves respect no walls. **WPA3**, mandatory for all Wi-Fi 7 certified devices, addresses the cryptographic weaknesses of its predecessors: Simultaneous Authentication of Equals (SAE), a password-authenticated key exchange resistant to offline dictionary attacks, replaces WPA2's Pre-Shared Key (PSK) handshake; Protected Management Frames (PMF) encrypt management traffic, preventing deauthentication attacks; and 192-bit security mode provides CNSA (Commercial National Security Algorithm) compliance for government and defence deployments. Beyond encryption, the wireless administrator must configure **802.1X authentication** with RADIUS backends, ensuring that each user authenticates individually (not sharing a common pre-shared key); implement **Wireless Intrusion Prevention Systems (WIPS)** that detect rogue APs, evil twin attacks, and MAC spoofing; and design the **guest network architecture** — an isolated VLAN with captive portal authentication, bandwidth throttling, and client isolation — that provides Internet access without exposing the internal network.

**Required Reading:** IEEE 802.11-2024 "Wireless LAN Medium Access Control and Physical Layer Specifications"; Gast, *802.11 Wireless Networks: The Definitive Guide* (2nd ed.); Vanhoef & Piessens, "Key Reinstallation Attacks: Forcing Nonce Reuse in WPA2" (CCS 2017); Wi-Fi Alliance, "WPA3 Specification" (2018).

3. Guest Wi-Fi is often implemented as a separate SSID with a pre-shared key on a sign in the lobby. Critique this approach from a security perspective and propose an alternative that balances usability with security.

## Lecture 10: Software-Defined Networking — The Programmable Network

The network industry experienced its Copernican revolution in the 2010s, and by 2040 the paradigm shift is complete: the network is programmable. Software-Defined Networking (SDN) decouples the control plane — the algorithms that decide where packets should go — from the data plane — the hardware that actually forwards them. This architectural separation, akin to the separation of the operating system kernel from user-space applications, transforms the network from a collection of independently configured appliances into a programmable platform. This lecture equips you with the theory and practice of SDN, from the OpenFlow protocol to the intent-based networking systems that define the cutting edge.

The canonical SDN architecture comprises three layers. The **infrastructure layer** consists of the switches and routers that forward packets at line rate — the data plane. In 2040, these are increasingly **bare-metal switches** (also called white-box switches) running open network operating systems like SONiC (Software for Open Networking in the Cloud), which decouples the switch hardware from the network OS. The **control layer** consists of the SDN controller — ONOS (Open Network Operating System), OpenDaylight, or commercial equivalents — which maintains a global view of the network topology and programs the forwarding behaviour of every switch. The **application layer** consists of the programs that consume the controller's northbound API to implement network services: a load balancer that redirects traffic when a server fails, a security application that quarantines compromised hosts, a traffic engineering application that reroutes flows around congestion.

**OpenFlow**, the protocol that ignited the SDN movement, operates on a simple abstraction: the flow table. Each flow entry contains match fields (ingress port, source/destination MAC, source/destination IP, source/destination port, and dozens of other header fields), a priority, and an action (forward to port X, drop, send to controller, modify header field Y). When a packet arrives, the switch performs a longest-prefix match against its flow table and executes the highest-priority matching entry's action. The controller populates these flow tables reactively (when a packet misses the table, the switch sends it to the controller, which computes a path and installs the necessary flow entries) or proactively (the controller pre-populates flow tables based on its knowledge of the intended topology). The 2040 administrator working with SDN must understand the performance characteristics of this architecture: a reactive flow setup adds latency to the first packet of every new flow, while proactive flow installation can consume significant TCAM (Ternary Content-Addressable Memory) space, a scarce and expensive resource on switch ASICs.

The evolution beyond OpenFlow has led to **P4 (Programming Protocol-independent Packet Processors)** — a domain-specific language for describing how packets should be processed, compiled to run directly on programmable switch ASICs like the Intel Tofino series or the Broadcom Trident series. With P4, the network administrator is no longer limited to the fixed feature set of a vendor's proprietary firmware; they can define new protocols, new header formats, new forwarding behaviours, and deploy them to production switches without waiting for a vendor release cycle. A P4 program that implements in-band network telemetry (INT) — embedding per-hop latency and queue depth information into every packet as it traverses the network — gives the network operator visibility that traditional SNMP polling could never provide. The 2040 administrator who can read and write P4 programs, and who understands the In-band Network Telemetry (INT) and INT sink architectures, possesses a skill that distinguishes the network engineer from the network operator.

At the highest level of abstraction, **Intent-Based Networking (IBN)** systems — Cisco DNA Center, Juniper Apstra, Apstra's original intent-based analytics — allow the administrator to declare "what" the network should achieve rather than "how" to configure it. An intent might be: "All traffic between the Finance VLAN and the Payment Processing VLAN must be encrypted and must not exceed 50ms latency." The IBN system translates this intent into device-level configurations, continuously validates that the network is meeting the intent, and alerts or self-corrects when it is not. This represents the ultimate realization of the SDN vision: the network as a self-managing system that the administrator steers rather than operates.

**Required Reading:** Kreutz et al., "Software-Defined Networking: A Comprehensive Survey" (Proceedings of the IEEE, 2015); Bosshart et al., "P4: Programming Protocol-Independent Packet Processors" (SIGCOMM, 2014); RFC 7426 "SDN: Layers and Architecture Terminology"; Vanbever & Rexford, *Software-Defined Networks: A Systems Approach* (2035).

**Discussion Questions:**

## Lecture 11: Network Automation — Ansible, Python, and the Infrastructure as Code Revolution

The network of 2040 is too large, too dynamic, and too critical to be configured manually. The paradigm of Infrastructure as Code (IaC) — applying software development practices to infrastructure management — has transformed network administration from a craft of CLI typing into an engineering discipline of version control, testing, and automated deployment. This lecture provides hands-on fluency with the tools and patterns of network automation.

**Ansible**, the Red Hat-sponsored open-source automation platform, has become the de facto standard for network configuration management. Its agentless architecture — connecting to network devices over SSH or NETCONF, executing modules that translate declarative YAML playbooks into device-specific CLI commands — makes it applicable to virtually any network platform. A typical Ansible playbook for network configuration might: gather facts from all switches in the inventory (`ios_facts`, `nxos_facts`), render VLAN configurations from a Jinja2 template that substitutes site-specific variables, push the configurations to devices with rollback on failure, and verify the deployment by checking that the new VLANs appear in `show vlan` output. The 2040 administrator maintains a Git repository — the "source of truth" — containing the intended state of every network device, and Ansible (or its successors) works continuously to converge the actual state toward the intended state. We will practice writing playbooks that configure OSPF, BGP, ACLs, and VLANs across a simulated multi-vendor network, using Ansible's network resource modules (`cisco.ios.ios_vlans`, `arista.eos.eos_bgp`) that provide idempotent, declarative interfaces.

**Python** is the lingua franca of network automation. Libraries like **Netmiko** (a multi-vendor SSH library that abstracts away the differences between Cisco IOS, Juniper JunOS, Arista EOS, and dozens of other CLI dialects), **NAPALM** (Network Automation and Programmability Abstraction Layer with Multivendor support — a unified API for retrieving device state and pushing configuration), and **Nornir** (a Python automation framework that combines inventory management, task execution, and concurrent operations) give the network administrator programmatic control without requiring the device to support a model-driven API. A Python script using Netmiko might connect to 500 switches concurrently (using `concurrent.futures.ThreadPoolExecutor` with 20 worker threads), execute `show interfaces status` on each, parse the output with TextFSM templates (which convert semi-structured CLI output into structured Python dictionaries), and flag any interface showing errors to a monitoring system. The ability to rapidly prototype network automation in a Jupyter notebook — connecting to a device, retrieving state, analyzing it with pandas, visualizing it with matplotlib — has become as essential to the network administrator as the serial console cable was to previous generations.

Beyond configuration management, network automation in 2040 encompasses **CI/CD for network changes**: a Git push to the network repository triggers a pipeline (Jenkins, GitLab CI, or GitHub Actions) that runs pre-flight validation (syntax checking, policy compliance scanning with tools like Batfish, a dry-run against a network digital twin), opens a change ticket in the IT service management system, and — upon approval — executes the deployment playbook with canary testing (deploy to one switch first, verify, then roll out to the rest). **Network testing** has matured from "ping it and hope" to comprehensive validation frameworks: `pyATS` (Cisco's Python Automated Test System) defines test cases that verify routing tables, BGP session states, and reachability; `Suzieq` collects and analyzes network state over time, enabling assertions like "every BGP peer should have received at least 100,000 prefixes." The network administrator who treats their network like a software system — with version control, automated testing, and deployment pipelines — produces configurations that are reproducible, auditable, and resilient.

**Required Reading:** Edelman, Lowe, & Oswalt, *Network Programmability and Automation* (2nd ed., 2023); Geier, *Ansible for Network Automation* (2037 Yggdrasil Press); Chou, *Mastering Python Networking* (4th ed., 2039); `napalm.readthedocs.io`; `docs.ansible.com/ansible/latest/network`.

**Discussion Questions:**
1. Ansible's agentless architecture is elegant but means that every playbook run must establish SSH connections, authenticate, and execute commands serially or with limited parallelism. Compare this to agent-based approaches (Salt, Puppet) for network device management.
2. A Git repository as the "source of truth" for network configuration assumes that no one makes changes directly on the device. What technical and cultural mechanisms enforce this discipline in practice?
3. Network CI/CD pipelines can deploy a change to hundreds of devices in minutes, but a bad configuration can cause an outage at the same speed. Design the safety mechanisms — canary deployment, automated rollback, blast-radius limiting — that a responsible pipeline must implement.

## Lecture 12: The Future of Network Administration — AI-Driven NOCs, Quantum Networks, and the 2040 Horizon

As we conclude IT205, we lift our gaze from the operational details of today's networks to the technologies and paradigms that will define the network administrator's craft over the coming decade. The network of 2040 is already in transition, and the administrator who understands these trajectories — who prepares for them rather than reacting to them — will lead rather than follow.

**AI-Driven Network Operations** are no longer a marketing slide; they are a production reality. Machine learning models, trained on years of telemetry from production networks, now perform tasks that once required senior engineers: **anomaly detection** that identifies a failing optical transceiver 48 hours before it drops below the bit error rate threshold; **root cause analysis** that correlates a spike in application latency with a BGP route change that shifted traffic to a suboptimal path; **predictive capacity planning** that forecasts link saturation based on growth trends and recommends circuit upgrades before congestion occurs; and **automated remediation** that, upon detecting a switch port flapping, applies the standard mitigation (shut/no shut the port, check the cable, open a ticket with the data centre technician) without human intervention. The AI-augmented NOC of 2040 operates with a fraction of the staff that a traditional NOC required, but demands a different skill set: the network administrator must understand the AI's decision-making process well enough to verify its recommendations, must curate the training data that shapes its behaviour, and must intervene when the AI encounters a situation outside its training distribution.

**Quantum networking** presents both a threat and an opportunity. Quantum computers, when they achieve sufficient scale, will break the asymmetric cryptography (RSA, ECC, Diffie-Hellman) that secures virtually all network communications — a threat that has driven the NIST Post-Quantum Cryptography standardization process, which selected its first algorithms (CRYSTALS-Kyber for key encapsulation, CRYSTALS-Dilithium for signatures) in 2024. The network administrator of 2040 must plan the migration to post-quantum cryptography: upgrading TLS certificates on web servers, replacing SSH host keys, ensuring that VPN gateways support post-quantum key exchange. Simultaneously, **Quantum Key Distribution (QKD)** — the use of quantum mechanical principles (specifically, the no-cloning theorem and the observer effect) to establish shared secret keys with information-theoretic security guarantees — is transitioning from physics laboratories to metropolitan-area networks. The 2040 network administrator may manage a QKD link between two data centres, understanding that while QKD provides unbreakable key distribution, it requires dedicated fibre paths and trusted relay nodes, and must be integrated with conventional cryptography for practical deployment.

The broader technology landscape of 2040 — **terabit Ethernet** (802.3df standardizing 800 GbE and 1.6 TbE), **satellite mesh networks** (low-earth-orbit constellations providing sub-20ms latency globally), **in-network computation** (switch ASICs that perform aggregation, filtering, and even neural network inference on packets in flight), and the **network-digital twin** (a real-time simulation of the entire network, kept in sync through streaming telemetry, on which changes are tested before deployment) — demands a network administrator who is comfortable with constant learning. The tools change; the protocols evolve; but the fundamentals — the troubleshooting mindset, the security-first posture, the discipline of configuration management, the empathy for the users who depend on the network working — endure. The network administrator of 2040 is a hybrid professional: part software engineer, part security analyst, part infrastructure architect, and entirely indispensable.

**Required Reading:** NIST, "Post-Quantum Cryptography Standardization" (2024); Bennett & Brassard, "Quantum Cryptography: Public Key Distribution and Coin Tossing" (IEEE, 1984); Kim et al., "In-band Network Telemetry via Programmable Dataplanes" (SIGCOMM, 2015); Yggdrasil University White Paper, "The AI-Augmented NOC: Three Years of Operational Data" (2039).

**Discussion Questions:**
1. An AI system recommends shutting down a core router interface based on anomaly detection. As the on-call network administrator, what verification steps do you take before approving this automated action?
2. Post-quantum cryptography algorithms have larger key sizes and higher computational overhead than their classical predecessors. How does this affect network protocols that assume small, fast cryptographic operations?
3. The network-digital twin promises to eliminate change-induced outages by testing every change in simulation first. What limitations of simulation — incomplete models, unforeseen interactions, human factors — could still lead to production failures, and how should the administrator mitigate them?
