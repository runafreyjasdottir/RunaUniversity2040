# IT407: Elective: Specialization Track
## Bachelor of Science in Information Technology — University of Yggdrasil, 2040

**Credits:** 4
**Description:** A flexible elective allowing students to pursue deep specialization in one of six tracks: Cloud Architecture, Security Operations, Data Engineering, AI/ML Infrastructure, Edge & IoT Systems, or IT Leadership. Students design a learning contract with a faculty advisor and produce a substantial portfolio artifact.

**Prerequisites:** Senior standing, completion of all Year 1-3 IT core courses

**Instructor:** Individual faculty advisors, coordinated by Dr. Sigrún Vérendóttir

**Course Philosophy:** The IT field is broad — too broad for any single person to master entirely. The elective specialization track honors that diversity by letting you go deep in the area that most excites you. This is your opportunity to become an expert, not just a generalist. The learning is self-directed — you design the curriculum, with faculty guidance — because the most important skill in IT is knowing how to learn.

### Lecture 1: Choosing Your Path — The Six Specialization Tracks

**Course:** IT407 — Elective: Specialization Track
**Degree:** Bachelor of Science in Information Technology, 2040

---

#### Overview

This lecture introduces the six specialization tracks and helps students select the one aligned with their interests and career goals. Each track is described with its core competencies, typical roles, and sample portfolio projects.

#### The Six Tracks

**1. Cloud Architecture:** Design and deploy cloud-native infrastructure at scale. Competencies: multi-cloud architecture, Kubernetes, service mesh, serverless, FinOps, infrastructure as code. Roles: Cloud Architect, Platform Engineer. Sample project: design and deploy a multi-region, auto-scaling application platform.

**2. Security Operations:** Defend organizations against cyber threats. Competencies: threat detection and response, SIEM/SOAR, digital forensics, penetration testing, security architecture, zero trust. Roles: Security Engineer, SOC Analyst. Sample project: build a detection and response pipeline processing 10,000 events/second.

**3. Data Engineering:** Design and operate data platforms for analytics and AI. Competencies: data pipelines (batch and streaming), data warehousing, ETL/ELT, data governance, database administration. Roles: Data Engineer, Database Reliability Engineer. Sample project: build a real-time data pipeline ingesting and transforming multi-terabyte datasets.

**4. AI/ML Infrastructure:** Build the platforms that power AI. Competencies: MLOps, GPU/NPU cluster management, model serving, training pipelines, vector databases, AI model governance. Roles: MLOps Engineer, AI Infrastructure Engineer. Sample project: deploy and manage a GPU cluster for training and serving foundation models.

**5. Edge & IoT Systems:** Design distributed systems at the periphery. Competencies: edge computing, embedded AI, IoT protocols, fleet management, low-power design, sensor networks. Roles: Edge Engineer, IoT Architect. Sample project: deploy a fleet of 100 edge nodes processing sensor data in real-time.

**6. IT Leadership:** Lead technology organizations. Competencies: IT strategy, service management, vendor management, budgeting, team building, communication. Roles: IT Manager, Service Delivery Manager. Sample project: develop a comprehensive IT strategy and transformation roadmap for a case-study organization.

### Lecture 2: The Learning Contract — Designing Your Curriculum

**Course:** IT407 — Elective: Specialization Track
**Degree:** Bachelor of Science in Information Technology, 2040

---

#### Overview

Self-directed learning requires structure. The learning contract is your personalized syllabus — what you'll learn, how you'll learn it, how you'll demonstrate mastery, and the timeline. This lecture guides students through designing an effective learning contract.

#### Key Topics

- **Learning Contract Components:** (1) **Specialization track** and rationale; (2) **Learning objectives** — 5-8 specific, measurable outcomes; (3) **Learning resources** — books, courses, documentation, mentors; (4) **Learning activities** — reading, tutorials, projects, shadowing; (5) **Portfolio artifact** — the tangible output demonstrating mastery; (6) **Timeline** — weekly plan for the semester; (7) **Success criteria** — how will you and your advisor know you've succeeded?
- **Writing Good Learning Objectives:** Use Bloom's taxonomy: Remember → Understand → Apply → Analyze → Evaluate → Create. For a 400-level course, objectives should be at the Analyze/Evaluate/Create level. Example: "Design and implement a threat detection pipeline that processes logs from 10+ sources and achieves <1% false positive rate."
- **Portfolio Artifact Standards:** The artifact must be: (1) substantial — representing 100+ hours of work; (2) original — your own work, with clear contribution boundaries if collaborative; (3) documented — with architecture, design rationale, and operations guide; (4) demonstrable — something you can present and discuss; (5) professional quality — suitable for a job portfolio.

#### Required Reading

- Knowles, M., Holton, E., & Swanson, R. (2038). *The Adult Learner* (9th ed.). Chapter: Self-Directed Learning.

### Lecture 7: Research Methods — Finding and Evaluating Knowledge

**Course:** IT407 — Elective: Specialization Track
**Degree:** Bachelor of Science in Information Technology, 2040

---

#### Overview

Specialization requires going beyond textbooks into the research literature — conference papers, technical reports, RFCs, and industry whitepapers. This lecture covers research methods for IT professionals: finding relevant sources, evaluating credibility, synthesizing knowledge, and applying research to practice.

#### Key Topics

- **Information Sources:** (1) Academic — ACM Digital Library, IEEE Xplore, arXiv; (2) Industry — vendor documentation, engineering blogs, whitepapers; (3) Standards — IETF RFCs, NIST publications, ISO standards; (4) Community — conference talks, podcasts, open-source documentation. Different sources have different credibility profiles — learn to evaluate.
- **Evaluating Credibility:** (1) Who wrote it? What are their credentials and potential biases? (2) Is it peer-reviewed? (3) Is it current? (In IT, a 5-year-old source may be obsolete.) (4) Is it corroborated by other sources? (5) Is it self-published marketing material disguised as research?
- **Literature Synthesis:** Your portfolio artifact should demonstrate engagement with the literature: (1) a literature review section summarizing relevant prior work; (2) citations for key concepts and techniques; (3) comparison of your approach to others in the literature; (4) identification of gaps your work addresses.

#### Required Reading

- UoY Library. (2040). *Research Methods for IT Professionals*.

### Lecture 8: Portfolio Development — Building Evidence of Expertise

**Course:** IT407 — Elective: Specialization Track
**Degree:** Bachelor of Science in Information Technology, 2040

---

#### Overview

Your portfolio artifact is more than a project — it's evidence of expertise that you'll use in job interviews, performance reviews, and professional advancement. This lecture covers portfolio design: what to include, how to present it, and how to make it discoverable.

#### Key Topics

- **Portfolio Components:** (1) **Artifact repository** — clean, well-documented code on GitHub; (2) **README** — explains what, why, how, and results; (3) **Architecture document** — diagrams, decisions, rationale; (4) **Demo** — live or recorded demonstration; (5) **Results** — metrics, benchmarks, or qualitative outcomes; (6) **Reflection** — what you learned, what you'd do differently.
- **Making Your Portfolio Discoverable:** (1) use relevant keywords and technologies; (2) write a blog post or article about your project; (3) present at a meetup or conference; (4) include it in your resume, LinkedIn, and personal website. The best portfolio is one that people find.
- **The Portfolio as Career Asset:** A strong portfolio demonstrates capability better than a resume. For early-career IT professionals, portfolio projects often carry more weight than grades. Invest the time to make it excellent.

### Lecture 9: Peer Review and Collaborative Feedback

**Course:** IT407 — Elective: Specialization Track
**Degree:** Bachelor of Science in Information Technology, 2040

---

#### Overview

Specialization doesn't mean isolation. Peer review — giving and receiving constructive feedback on work-in-progress — improves quality and builds the collaboration skills essential for professional practice.

#### Key Topics

- **Giving Effective Feedback:** (1) be specific — "The architecture diagram would benefit from showing data flow between components" not "It's confusing"; (2) be constructive — suggest improvements, not just criticism; (3) be kind — the person receiving feedback invested significant effort; (4) be timely — feedback on a draft is useful; feedback after submission is not.
- **Receiving Feedback:** (1) listen without defensiveness — the feedback is about the work, not you; (2) ask clarifying questions — make sure you understand; (3) decide what to incorporate — you don't have to accept all feedback, but you should consider it; (4) thank the reviewer — giving thoughtful feedback takes effort.
- **Peer Review Sessions:** Structured sessions where students present work-in-progress and receive feedback from 2-3 peers and a faculty advisor. Three sessions per semester — early (concept), middle (prototype), late (near-final).

#### Required Reading

- UoY Writing Center. (2039). *Peer Review Best Practices*.
