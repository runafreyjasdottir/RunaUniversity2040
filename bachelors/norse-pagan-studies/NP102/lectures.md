## Lecture 1: The Cosmogony — From Ginnungagap to Ymir

**Before the Beginning**

Norse cosmogony begins not with a creator god speaking the world into existence, but with a gap — *Ginnungagap*, the "yawning void" or "magical void," a primordial nothingness that is not truly empty but pregnant with potential. The name itself is significant: *ginn* is a prefix meaning "great, magical, divine" (as in *Ginnarr*, one of Óðinn's names), and *gap* means "chasm, emptiness." Ginnungagap is a **sacred emptiness** — not the absence of being but the precondition for being.

On the northern edge of Ginnungagap lay *Niflheimr*, the world of primal ice, fog, and cold. On the southern edge lay *Múspellsheimr*, the world of primal fire and heat. Between them, in the void, the ice and fire met, and from their collision came the first being: **Ymir**, the primeval giant.

The Vǫluspá describes this moment (stanzas 3-4, Larrington translation):

> *There was in ancient times | where the sea now extends,  
>  Ymir made his home; | no sand, no sea,  
>  no cold waves, | no earth was to be found —  
>  there was ginnungagap, | but grass nowhere.*

Ymir is not a god — he is a **giant** (*jǫtunn*), born not from intention but from the collision of elemental forces. He is not worshipped, not named in prayers, not sacrificed to. He is the raw material of existence that must be **slaughtered and reorganized** before the cosmos can take shape.

**The Dismemberment of Ymir**

The three sons of Borr — **Óðinn, Vili, and Vé** — kill Ymir and create the world from his body:

- His **flesh** became the earth (*jorð*)
- His **blood** became the sea (*sær*)
- His **bones** became the mountains (*fjall*)
- His **teeth** became the rocks (*grjót*)
- His **hair** became the trees (*skógr*)
- His **skull** became the sky (*himinn*), held up by four dwarves (Austri, Vestri, Norðri, Suðri — East, West, North, South)
- His **brain** became the clouds (*ský*)

This is the founding myth of Norse cosmology: **the world is made from the body of a slain giant**. Creation is not a gentle act of divine will but a violent act of dismemberment and reorganization. The gods are not primi existentes — they are intermediaries who shape pre-existing material. Ymir's death is not a tragedy; it is the necessary precondition for ordered existence.

This myth has profound implications:

1. **The world is not inherently good** — It is made from the corpse of a chaotic being. Chaos is not eliminated; it is reorganized.
2. **Creation requires violence** — Order is imposed on chaos through killing. This is not unique to Norse mythology (compare Tiamat in Babylonian myth), but the Norse version is unusually graphic.
3. **The body is cosmic** — The macrocosm (the world) is made from the microcosm (Ymir's body). The human form maps onto the cosmic form.
4. **The gods are not creators ex nihilo** — They are shapers, organizers, craftsmen. They work with pre-existing material. This makes them powerful but not omnipotent.

**Auðumla and the Origin of the Gods**

Ymir was not alone. From the same melting ice emerged **Auðumla**, the primeval cow, who fed Ymir with her milk and licked the salty ice blocks herself. From the ice that Auðumla licked, **Burr** (also Buri) emerged — the first god, ancestor of Óðinn.

This detail is often overlooked but is theologically significant: the first god does not create himself. He is **licked from ice by a cow**. The divine is emergent, not self-originating. The gods are part of the natural world, not separate from it.

## Lecture 2: The Cosmological Structure — Yggdrasil, the Nine Worlds, and the Axis Mundi

**Yggdrasil — The World Tree**

The center of the Norse cosmos is **Yggdrasil**, the great ash tree whose roots reach into three wells and whose branches shelter the nine worlds. Yggdrasil is not merely a spatial structure — it is the **axis mundi**, the connecting point between all realms, the trunk of existence itself.

The Grímnismál (stanzas 29-35) provides the most detailed description:

> *Three roots there grow | in three directions  
>  beneath the ash Yggdrasil;  
>  Hel lives beneath one, | beneath the second the frost giants,  
>  beneath the third | humankind.*

The three wells at the roots are:

**Urðarbrunnr (the Well of Urðr)** — Beneath the root that reaches into Ásgarðr. Here the three Norns — Urðr (Past/What Has Been), Verðandi (Present/What Is Becoming), and Skuld (Future/What Shall Be) — water the tree daily with water from the well and lay laws for the children of men. This is the well of **destiny and law**.

**Mímisbrunnr (the Well of Mímir)** — Beneath the root that reaches into Jǫtunheimr. Mímir, a being of unknown origin (giant? ancient god? cosmological principle?), keeps the well of wisdom. Óðinn gave his eye for a drink from this well — the foundational sacrifice of Norse mythology, which we'll explore in detail in the Óðinn lecture.

**Hvergelmir (the Roaring Cauldron)** — Beneath the root that reaches into Niflheimr. This is the well from which all rivers flow. The dragon Niðhǫggr gnaws at this root, threatening the tree's stability. Hvergelmir is the well of **primal chaos**, the untamed energy that constantly threatens to undo cosmic order.

**The Nine Worlds**

The Norse cosmos consists of nine worlds (*níu heimar*), arranged around Yggdrasil:

1. **Ásgarðr** — Home of the Æsir. Connected to Miðgarðr by the Bifrost bridge
2. **Miðgarðr** — Middle enclosure. The human world, surrounded by a wall
3. **Vanaheimr** — Home of the Vanir (fertility gods)
4. **Álfheimr** — Home of the Light Elves (*ljósálfar*)
5. **Jǫtunheimr** — Home of the giants (*jǫtnar*), the forces of chaos and wild nature
6. **Niflheimr** — World of ice, fog, and cold. Home of Hel and the dead
7. **Múspellsheimr** — World of fire, home of Surtr and the fire giants
8. **Svartálfaheimr** — Home of the dark elves/dwarves (*dvergar*), master craftsmen
9. **Helheimr** (or simply **Hel**) — The realm of the dead who did not die in battle

**Critical note**: The exact identification and arrangement of the nine worlds is debated. Snorri's Prose Edda provides a more systematic description than the Poetic Edda, but his systematization may be a literary convenience rather than a reflection of pre-Christian belief. The 2040 UoY position is that the nine worlds represent **regions of experience** (home, wilderness, underworld, fire, ice, craft, death, divinity, community) rather than discrete spatial zones.

## Lecture 3: The Æsir — Óðinn, Þórr, Týr, and the Gods of Order

**Óðinn — The All-Father**

Óðinn (Old Norse *Óðinn*, from *óðr* "fury, inspiration, ecstasy") is the chief of the Æsir, the god of wisdom, poetry, death, war, and ecstasy. He is simultaneously the most powerful and the most disturbing of the Norse gods — a shapeshifter who violates every boundary, a sacrificer who sacrifices himself, a king who rules through deceit as much as strength.

Óðinn's key attributes:
- **One-eyed** — He sacrificed his other eye at Mímir's well for wisdom
- **Spear Gungnir** — Never misses its target; oaths sworn on its point cannot be broken
- **Ravens Huginn and Muninn** (Thought and Memory) — Fly around the world daily and report to him
- **Wolves Geri and Freki** (Greedy and Ravenous) — He feeds them his meat at feasts, consuming only wine himself
- **Eight-legged horse Sleipnir** — The fastest steed in all worlds, can ride to Hel and back
- **Valhǫll** — His hall where einherjar (chosen warriors) feast and fight daily, preparing for Ragnarǫk

Óðinn's paradox: He is the god of **kings and outcasts, poets and berserkers, the living and the dead**. He inspires poetry and provokes battle-frenzy. He seeks wisdom and spreads deception. He is a god who **rules through sacrifice** — his own (the eye, the hanging on Yggdrasil) and others' (the warriors he chooses to die).

The Hávamál (stanzas 138-139) describes his self-sacrifice:

> *I know that I hung | on the wind-swept tree  
>  nine full nights,  
>  spear-wounded, | given to Óðinn,  
>  myself to myself,  
>  on that tree | of which no one knows | from where its roots run.*

This is the foundational myth of Norse spirituality: **wisdom is gained through sacrifice**. Óðinn does not receive wisdom as a gift; he earns it through suffering. The nine nights on Yggdrasil are not a punishment but an initiation — a shamanic ordeal through which he gains the knowledge of the runes.

**Þórr — The Thunderer**

Þórr (from *Þunraz*, "thunder") is the most widely worshipped god in the archaeological record (over 100 theophoric place-names in Norway alone) and the most beloved in the literary sources. He is the god of **physical protection** — the defender of Ásgarðr and Miðgarðr against the giants.

Þórr's key attributes:
- **Hammer Mjǫllnir** — The most powerful weapon in the cosmos; returns when thrown; creates thunder and lightning
- **Belt of strength** — Doubles his already immense power
- **Iron gloves** — Needed to grip Mjǫllnir
- **Goat-drawn chariot** — Tanngrisnir and Tanngnjóstr ("Teeth-Grinder" and "Teeth-Gnasher"); can be killed, eaten, and resurrected
- **Red beard** — Iconic in all sources
- **Fierce eyes** — Glaring enough to kill with a look (in some sources)

Þórr represents **uncomplicated virtue** in an otherwise morally ambiguous pantheon. He is the working person's god — direct, honest, physically powerful, and always ready to defend the community against outside threats. His mythology is full of **journeys to Jǫtunheimr** where he fights giants, often through brute strength but sometimes through cleverness (Útgarða-Loki episode in the Prose Edda).

**Týr — The One-Handed God of Law**

Týr (from *Tīwaz*, the Proto-Germanic sky-father) is the god of law, justice, and self-sacrifice. His most famous myth is the binding of Fenrir:

When the gods realized the wolf Fenrir (Loki's son) posed an existential threat, they decided to bind him. But Fenrir would not allow himself to be bound unless one of the gods placed a hand in his mouth as a pledge of good faith. Only Týr was brave enough. When Fenrir could not break free, he bit off Týr's hand.

This myth establishes Týr as the god of **sacrificial duty** — the one who loses a part of himself to preserve the community. It is also deeply ironic: the god of law and contracts breaks a contract (the pledge of good faith) to serve a higher law (the preservation of the cosmos). The Norse recognized that **law sometimes requires deceit**, and that **justice sometimes demands sacrifice**.

The comparison with the Roman god Mars (to whom Týr was equated in interpretatio Romana) is instructive. Týr is not primarily a war god — he is a god of the **rule of law that enables society to function**, including the law that governs warfare.

## Lecture 4: The Vanir — Freyr, Freyja, and the Gods of Fertility

**The Vanir — A Separate Divine Tribe**

The Vanir are the second tribe of Norse gods, distinct from the Æsir in origin, nature, and domain. Where the Æsir are gods of **order, law, wisdom, and war**, the Vanir are gods of **fertility, prosperity, pleasure, and natural cycles**.

The Vanir are attested in three primary sources:
1. The **Æsir-Vanir war** (Vǫluspá, stanzas 21-24) — The first war in the cosmos
2. The **hostage exchange** — Njǫrðr, Freyr, and Freyja go to the Æsir; Hœnir and Mímir go to the Vanir
3. The **cult of the Vanir** — Archaeological evidence of fertility worship in Sweden

**Njǫrðr — The Sea and Wealth**

Njǫrðr is the father of the Vanir, god of the sea, wind, fishing, and wealth. His key myth involves his marriage to the giantess Skaði:

Skaði, daughter of the giant Þjazi, came to Ásgarðr seeking vengeance for her father's death. The gods offered weregild (compensation): she could choose a husband from among the gods, but only by looking at their feet. She chose the most beautiful feet, thinking they belonged to Baldr, but they were Njǫrðr's (sea-foam had washed them fair).

The marriage was disastrous. Njǫrðr loved the sea-shore; Skaði loved the mountains. They agreed to spend nine nights in the mountains and nine by the sea, alternating. But Njǫrðr hated the mountains ("The wolves howl on the mountain-slopes; that is not as good as the song of swans"), and Skaði hated the sea ("The wailing of gulls wakes me on the sea-strand; that is not as good as the howling of wolves").

They separated. This myth encodes a fundamental Norse insight: **some unions are incompatible despite good intentions**. Nature (mountains/sea) and desire (Skaði/Njǫrðr) cannot always be reconciled.

**Freyr — Lord of Prosperity**

Freyr ("Lord") is the god of sunshine, rain, harvest, peace, and prosperity. He is the most important male Vanr and one of the most widely worshipped gods in Viking Age Scandinavia, particularly in Sweden.

Freyr's key myths:

1. **The wooing of Gerðr** — Freyr sees the giantess Gerðr from Óðinn's high seat (Hliðskjálf) and falls desperately in love. He sends his servant Skírnir to woo her. When she refuses, Skírnir threatens her with a series of escalating curses (the *Skírnismál*): she will be barren, mad, despised, and eternally thirsty. Gerðr yields. This myth is deeply troubling — Freyr's "love" is obtained through threatening a woman with sexual coercion. Readings include: (a) a myth of the marriage of sun (Freyr) and earth (Gerðr), where the "threats" encode the parching of the earth by drought; (b) a genuinely disturbing story about the power imbalance between gods and giants; (c) a reflection of the Norse practice of arranged marriage through go-betweens.

2. **Freyr's death at Ragnarǫk** — Freyr fights the fire giant Surtr without a sword (having given his sword to Skírnir as a reward for wooing Gerðr). He kills Surtr with a stag's antler but dies in the process. The 2040 reading: Freyr sacrifices his weapon (his power) for love (the wooing of Gerðr), and this sacrifice ultimately kills him. Love and prosperity are not free — they demand exchange.

Archaeological evidence of Freyr's cult:
- **Adam of Bremen** (c. 1070 CE) describes the temple at Uppsala with statues of Þórr, Wodan (Óðinn), and Fricco (Freyr), "whose attribute is an immense phallus"
- **The Rällinge statuette** (Sweden, 11th century) — A bronze figure with an erect phallus, identified as Freyr
- **Theophoric place-names** concentrated in eastern Sweden (Fres-, Frö-, Frösö-)
- **Völsa þáttr** — A Norwegian story about a preserved horse phallus worshipped as "Völsi," likely a survival of Freyr-cult practice

**Freyja — The Lady**

Freyja ("Lady") is the most complex and most powerful goddess in Norse mythology. She is the goddess of love, desire, fertility, beauty, war, death, and seiðr (the most powerful form of Norse magic).

Freyja's key attributes and myths:

1. **Brísingamen** — Her famous necklace, obtained by sleeping with four dwarves (Álríkr, Dvalinn, Grérr, Litr) in exchange for the necklace. This myth, preserved only in Sǫrla þáttr (a Christian retelling), shows Freyja exercising **sexual agency** — she chooses to trade sex for what she wants, on her own terms.

2. **TheFalconskin** — Freyja owns a falcon-feather cloak that allows her to fly between worlds. She lends it to Óðinn (disguised as Loki) to rescue Iðunn, and to Freyr's servant Skírnir. This makes her a **mediator between worlds** — she enables travel between realms.

3. **Valhǫll's counterpart** — Freyja receives half of the battle-slain (the other half go to Óðinn in Valhǫll). Her field is **Fólkvangr**, where her hall **Sessrúmnir** ("Seat-Roomy") stands. She is every bit the choosER of the slain as Óðinn.

4. **Seiðr practitioner** — The Prose Edda states that Freyja taught seiðr to the Æsir. She is the **originator and master** of the most powerful Norse magical practice.

5. **The quest for Óðr** — Freyja's husband is Óðr (possibly another name for Óðinn, possibly a separate figure). He abandoned her, and she weeps tears of red gold. She wanders the nine worlds searching for him. This myth encodes **the pain of love-loss** — even the goddess of love suffers from love's absence.

Freyja is often simplistically described as a "love goddess" or compared to Aphrodite/Venus. This is a partial truth. She is simultaneously:
- A **love goddess** who chooses her partners and negotiates her own terms
- A **war goddess** who claims half the battle-slain
- A **magic practitioner** who taught seiðr to the gods
- A **mournER** who weeps for a lost husband

She cannot be reduced to any single category. She is Norse femininity in its full complexity: desiring, powerful, grief-stricken, autonomous.

## Lecture 5: Loki — The Trickster, Shape-Changer, and Catalyst

**The Problem of Loki**

Loki is the most debated and most misunderstood figure in Norse mythology. Is he a god? A giant? A demon? A comic sidekick? A cosmic villain? The sources themselves are inconsistent:

- In the Prose Edda, Loki is Óðinn's blood-brother (*Edda*, Gylfaginning 33) and a regular companion of the gods
- In the Poetic Edda, Loki appears as both helper and adversary
- In *Lokasenna* (Loki's Quarrel), he insults every god at a feast and is bound as punishment
- In the Vǫluspá, he leads the forces of chaos at Ragnarǫk
- Snorri calls him the "originator of deceits" and blames him for Baldr's death

The 2040 scholarly consensus (following Lindow 2002, revised 2039) is that **Loki is a liminal figure** — a border-crosser who belongs to no single category. He is the son of the giant Fárbauti and the goddess(or giantess) Laufey/Nál. He has children with the giantess Angrboða: the wolf Fenrir, the world-serpent Jǫrmungandr, and the goddess Hel. He also has children with his wife Sigyn (Narfi and/or Váli) and, in one famous episode, becomes a mare and gives birth to Sleipnir (Óðinn's eight-legged horse).

**Loki's Key Myths**

1. **The wall of Ásgarðr** — A giant-builder offers to build a wall around Ásgarðr in exchange for Freyja, the sun, and the moon. The gods agree, setting an impossible deadline. Loki transforms into a mare and distracts the giant's stallion Svadilfari, preventing the wall from being completed. The gods renege on the deal, Þórr kills the giant, and Loki gives birth to Sleipnir. Loki **saves the gods** through trickery and sexual transformation.

2. **The theft of Iðunn's apples** — Loki is forced by the giant Þjazi to lure Iðunn (the goddess who keeps the rejuvenating apples) out of Ásgarðr. Þjazi kidnaps Iðunn, and the gods begin to age. Loki borrows Freyja's falcon-skin, flies to Þjazi's home, rescues Iðunn, and lures Þjazi to his death at the hands of the gods. Loki **creates a crisis and then resolves it** through the same trickery.

3. **The death of Baldr** — Snorri attributes Baldr's death to Loki's scheming: Loki learns that Baldr is invulnerable to everything except mistletoe, fashions a dart of mistletoe, and guides the blind god Hǫðr's hand to throw it, killing Baldr. Loki then prevents Baldr's return from Hel by refusing to weep for him (disguised as the giantess Þǫkk). This is Loki's most destructive act — the murder of the most beloved god.

4. **Lokasenna** — At a feast of the gods, Loki arrives uninvited and systematically insults every deity, exposing their failings, hypocrisies, and shameful secrets. He is eventually driven out by Þórr's threats.

**The Binding of Loki**

After Baldr's death, the gods capture Loki and bind him to three rocks in a cave. A venomous serpent is placed above his face, dripping poison onto him. His wife Sigyn holds a bowl to catch the venom, but when she turns to empty it, the poison drips onto Loki's face, and his convulsions of pain cause earthquakes. He remains bound until Ragnarǫk, when he breaks free and leads the forces of chaos against the gods.

The binding of Loki has been interpreted as:
- **Punishment for murder** — Standard reading: Loki deserves punishment for killing Baldr
- **The taming of chaos** — The forces of disruption must be restrained for order to function, but they cannot be eliminated
- **A mirror of Prometheus** — Like the Greek titan bound for giving fire to humanity, Loki is bound for a crime that serves a cosmic purpose
- **A gender reversal** — Loki, who gave birth to Sleipnir, is now bound in a posture reminiscent of childbirth (face up, legs apart). The punishment fits the "crime" of gender transgression.

The 2040 UoY reading emphasizes Loki's **catalytic function**: he is the force that initiates change, both creative and destructive. Without Loki, the gods are stagnant. With Loki, they are imperiled. This is the trickster's paradox: **the agent of change is also the agent of destruction**.

## Lecture 6: The Heroes — Sigurd, the Vǫlsungs, and the Tragic Ideal

**The Vǫlsung Cycle**

The Vǫlsung cycle is the Norse equivalent of the Greek house of Atreus — a multigenerational saga of love, betrayal, vengeance, and tragic inevitability. It exists in multiple versions:
- *Vǫlsunga saga* (Icelandic prose saga, 13th century)
- *Poetic Edda* poems: *Reginsmál*, *Fáfnismál*, *Sigrdrífumál*, *Sigurðarkviða*, *Guðrúnarkviða* I-III, *Atlakviða*, *Atlamál*
- *Nibelungenlied* (German version, c. 1200 CE — significantly different in detail and tone)
- Richard Wagner's *Der Ring des Nibelungen* (1876 — heavily adapted, not a reliable source but culturally monumental)

**The Story (Condensed)**

Vǫlsung — Signy — Sigmund — Sinfljóti — Sigurd — Gudrun — Atli — etc.

The cycle begins with Vǫlsung, a king whose daughter Signy is married to the Goth king Siggeir despite her objections. At the wedding feast, a one-eyed man (Óðinn) plunges a sword into the tree Barnstokkr, declaring that only the one who can pull it out shall have it. Only Sigmund succeeds.

Siggeir, humiliated, plots revenge. He invites Vǫlsung and his sons to his court, ambushes them, and kills Vǫlsung. Sigmund survives in the forest. Signy, desperate for vengeance, has her two sons killed by Sigmund for cowardice. Finally, she disguises herself, sleeps with her brother Sigmund, and bears Sinfljóti — a hero of incestuous origin, destined for vengeance.

(Despite the 2040 student's possible discomfort, incest is a central theme of the Vǫlsung cycle and cannot be omitted. It encodes the Norse understanding that **heroism is born from transgression** — the hero must violate social boundaries to achieve greatness.)

Sigurd (Siegfried in German), the greatest hero of Norse mythology, is Sigmund's posthumous son. He is raised by the smith Reginn, who sends him to kill the dragon Fáfnir. Sigurd succeeds, roasts the dragon's heart, tastes it, and gains the ability to understand birds — who warn him that Reginn intends to betray him. Sigurd kills Reginn.

Sigurd then rides to the mountain Hindarfjall, where he finds the Valkyrie Sigrdrífa (Brynhildr) asleep within a ring of fire. He cuts through the flames, wakes her, and she teaches him runes, wisdom, and the ways of the world. They pledge love.

Through deception (magic potion, shape-shifting), Sigurd is tricked into marrying Gudrun (Guðrún) instead of Brynhildr. When Brynhildr discovers the deception, she demands Sigurd's death. Gudrun's brothers kill Sigurd. Brynhildr, consumed by grief and rage, kills herself on Sigurd's funeral pyre.

Gudrun is then married to Atli (Attila), who invites her brothers to his court and kills them. Gudrun takes vengeance by killing her own children with Atli and serving them to him as food, then murdering Atli and burning his hall.

The cycle ends in an apocalypse of personal destruction — every act of vengeance begetting further vengeance, the heroic code consuming itself.

**The Tragic Ideal**

Norse heroism is defined by **courage in the face of certain doom**. The heroic ideal is not to win — it is to **face defeat with dignity and fight to the last**. This is the ethos of Ragnarǫk applied to individual life: the outcome is predetermined, the victory is impossible, but the fight itself is the meaning.

The Vǫlsung heroes embody this ideal:
- Sigmund fights to the death against Siggeir's men
- Sigurd faces the dragon despite knowing that dragon-slayers rarely survive
- The Gjúki brothers face Atli's treachery with open eyes
- Gudrun takes vengeance even though it costs her everything

This is not fatalism — it is **heroic defiance**: the knowledge that the universe does not care about you, combined with the insistence on acting nobly anyway.

## Lecture 7: The Valkyries, Norns, and Disir — Female Powers in Norse Mythology

**Valkyries — Choosers of the Slain**

The valkyries (*valkyrjur*, "choosers of the slain") are female beings who select which warriors die in battle and which live. They are Óðinn's agents, executing his will on the battlefield.

In the Poetic Edda, valkyries appear in two modes:
1. **As supernatural battle-maidens** — Riding through the sky, causing confusion among armies, choosing the dead. Examples: the valkyries named in *Darraðarljóð* who weave the web of war; the three valkyries in *Vǫluspá* who come from the south
2. **As individual women** associated with specific heroes — Brynhildr/Sigrdrífa helps Sigurd; Kára helps Helgi Hundingbani. These valkyries can marry heroes and are sometimes human women with supernatural abilities.

The 2040 reading (following Jesch 2018, revised 2038) emphasizes that **valkyries are not romantic figures** — they are agents of death. The popular image of the beautiful winged maiden who carries heroes to Valhǫll is a Victorian romanticization. In the original sources, valkyries are fearsome, bloody, and uncanny:

> *Spear-din | shall there be,  
>  shield-cleaving,  
>  wind of war-hammers,  
>  wolves' howling..."*  
> — *Darraðarljóð*

**The Norns — Weavers of Fate**

The three Norns — Urðr, Verðandi, and Skuld — are the most powerful beings in Norse mythology. They determine the fates of all beings, including the gods.

Their names encode their temporal function:
- **Urðr** (Past/What Has Been) — The accumulated weight of all past actions
- **Verðandi** (Present/What Is Becoming) — The active process of becoming
- **Skuld** (Future/What Shall Be) — What is owed, what must come to pass

The Norns water Yggdrasil daily with water from Urðarbrunnr and lay laws (*lǫg**) for the children of men. But they are not all-powerful: the Vǫluspá describes "other norns" who come to assist, and the poem suggests that norns can be both beneficial and harmful:

> *Thence come maidens | much knowing,  
>  three from the sea | that stands under the tree;  
>  Urðr one is called, | Verðandi another,  
>  they score notches on wood; | Skuld the third;  
>  they set laws, | they chose life,  
>  the fates of men | they foretell."*

The concept of **wyrd** (Old English) / **urðr** (Old Norse) is not simple determinism. It is **cumulative causation** — every action creates consequences that shape future possibilities. The Norns do not "decide" what will happen; they **score the notches on the wood** — they record and enact the consequences of past choices.

**The Dísir — Female Guardian Spirits**

The dísir (singular *dís*) are female spirits associated with fate, fertility, and protection. They are mentioned in the dísablót (sacrifice to the dísir), held at winter nights (Veturnótt) in Iceland and at Uppsala in Sweden.

The dísir occupy a liminal space between valkyries (battle-associated) and norns (fate-associated). They are:
- **Ancestor spirits** — Protective female spirits of the family line
- **Fate-weavers** — Minor norns who influence individual destinies
- **Battle-furies** — Spirits who fight alongside their protégés

The Freyja-like goddess Freyja is herself sometimes called *Vanadís* ("the Dís of the Vanir"), and the Vǫluspá norn Skuld is also named as a valkyrie. The categories overlap: **female supernatural power in Norse mythology is not divided into neat categories** but flows across valkyrie, norn, dís, goddess, and giantess.

## Lecture 8: The Giants — Jǫtnar, Ǫgishjálmr, and the Forces of Chaos

**Not Evil — Other**

The jǫtnar (giants, singular *jǫtunn*) are the most misunderstood figures in Norse mythology. In popular culture, they are portrayed as evil antagonists — big, stupid, violent monsters for the gods to defeat. This is a Christianized reading that imposes a dualistic (good vs. evil) framework on a mythology that does not operate in binaries.

The jǫtnar are **not evil**. They are *other* — ancient, powerful, and dangerous, but also wise, creative, and necessary.

Key facts about the jǫtnar:

1. **They predate the gods** — Ymir is a jǫtunn, and the gods are his descendants through Borr. The gods are **part giant**.

2. **They are not all hostile** — Many gods marry giantesses (Óðinn mated with Gunnlǫð, Gríðr, and Jǫrð; Þórr married Járnsaxa; Freyr married Gerðr). These are not aberrations — they are **normal**.

3. **They possess wisdom the gods lack** — Mímir (guardian of the well of wisdom) is a jǫtunn. Vafþrúðnir (whom Óðinn contests in wisdom) is a jǫtunn. The wisest beings in Norse mythology are giants.

4. **They are forces of nature** — Þórr's battles with jǫtnar are not "good vs. evil" but **order vs. chaos, civilization vs. wilderness, agriculture vs. storm**. Þórr fights the giants because they threaten the human world, not because they are morally wicked.

5. **They are the source of creation** — The world is made from Ymir's body. The dwarves who created the gods' treasures are chthonic beings related to the giants. The sun, moon, and stars are created from the eyes and skull of the giant Ymir.

**The Jǫtunn-God Relationship in 2040 Reading**

The 2040 UoY reading emphasizes that the Æsir-jǫtunn relationship is one of **tension, not warfare**. The gods and giants:
- Trade with each other (the wall of Ásgarðr)
- Marry each other (Óðinn, Þórr, Freyr)
- Learn from each other (Óðinn consults Vafþrúðnir)
- Fight each other (Þórr's journeys to Jǫtunheimr)
- Fear each other (the gods bind Fenrir because they fear the wolf's growth)

This is a relationship of **interdependence and antagonism** — not unlike the relationship between humans and the natural world. The jǫtnar *are* the natural world in its most powerful, uncivilized, unfettered form.

## Lecture 9: Ragnarǫk — The Doom of the Gods and the Renewal of the World

**The Inevitable End**

Ragnarǫk (from *ragna* "of the gods" + *rǫk* "fate, course of events" — commonly translated "the twilight of the gods" from Wagner's *Götterdämmerung*, but more accurately "the fate of the gods") is the Norse eschaton — the end of the current world order.

The key point that distinguishes Norse eschatology from Christian apocalypse: **Ragnarǫk is inevitable**. It is not a punishment for sin, not a judgment, not a moral reckoning. It is the natural culmination of cosmic processes that were set in motion at the beginning. The gods cannot prevent it — they can only face it with courage.

This is the significance of Óðinn's constant seeking of wisdom: he is not trying to prevent Ragnarǫk (he knows he cannot) but trying to **understand it** so that he and the gods can face it with dignity.

**The Sequence of Events (Vǫluspá, stanzas 44-66)**

1. **Fimbulvetr** (the Great Winter) — Three years of uninterrupted winter. Brothers will fight brothers, fathers will kill sons, sons will kill fathers. All morality breaks down.

2. **The breaking of bonds** — Loki breaks free from his fetters. Fenrir the wolf breaks free. Jǫrmungandr the world-serpent rises from the sea, poisoning the sky.

3. **Heimdallr sounds the Gjallarhorn** — The watchman of the gods blows the horn that signals the final battle.

4. **Óðinn fights Fenrir** — Óðinn is swallowed by the wolf. His son Víðarr tears Fenrir's jaws apart, avenging his father.

5. **Freyr fights Surtr** — Freyr dies because he gave away his sword for love.

6. **Þórr fights Jǫrmungandr** — Þórr kills the world-serpent but takes nine steps and falls dead from its venom.

7. **Loki and Heimdallr kill each other** — The trickster and the watchman destroy each other.

8. **Surtr engulfs the world in fire** — Everything burns.

**The Renewal**

But Ragnarǫk is not the end. The Vǫluspá continues (stanzas 60-66):

- The earth rises again from the sea, green and fair
- The surviving gods (Óðinn's sons Víðarr and Váli, Þórr's sons Móði and Magni) meet at Iðavǫllr
- Baldr and Hǫðr return from Hel, reconciled
- A new sun (daughter of the old sun) takes her mother's place
- A new human couple, Líf and Lífþrasir ("Life" and "Eager for Life"), emerge from Hoddmímis holt, repopulating the earth
- The norns continue to lay laws, and a new golden hall stands where the old one was

This is not a mere reset — Baldr, the gentle god who was killed, returns and is reconciled with Hǫðr, who killed him. The golden age is restored, but it is **earned through sacrifice**.

**Comparative Eschatology**

Norse Ragnarǫk differs from Christian apocalypse in several key respects:

1. **No final judgment** — There is no heaven/hell dichotomy. The dead in Hel (who were not evil, merely not battle-killed) presumably remain dead.
2. **The gods die** — Óðinn, Þórr, Freyr, Loki, and Heimdallr all die. The divine is not immortal.
3. **Nature participates** — The world-serpent, the wolf, the fire giant, and the winter are natural forces, not moral agents.
4. **Renewal, not redemption** — The new world is not achieved through divine intervention but through natural cycles of destruction and recreation.

## Lecture 10: Afterlife Beliefs — Valhǫll, Hel, and the Complexity of Death

**Multiple Destinations**

The Norse did not have a single, unified afterlife belief. The sources describe at least **six** different post-mortem destinations:

1. **Valhǫll** — Óðinn's hall, where einherjar (chosen warriors who die in battle) feast, fight, and prepare for Ragnarǫk
2. **Fólkvangr** — Freyja's field, where she receives half the battle-slain
3. **Hel** — The underworld, for those who die of old age, illness, or accident. Not a place of punishment (unlike Christian Hell), merely a destination for non-battle death
4. **Helgafjall** — A sacred mountain where families are reunited in death (Icelandic belief)
5. **Burial mound** — The dead remain in their burial place, watching over their descendants
6. **Reincarnation** — Some evidence suggests belief in rebirth within the family line (Hildr, the Valkyrie, is reborn endlessly)

The 2040 UoY position is that these are not contradictory beliefs but **regional, temporal, and status-based variations**:
- Valhǫll was primarily an aristocratic warrior belief
- Hel is a literary construct (Snorri's rationalization of a more complex reality)
- Burial mound beliefs are the most archaeologically attested
- Reincarnation beliefs may be influenced by Sami shamanic practices

**Valhǫll — The Warrior's Paradise**

Valhǫll is the most famous (and most misunderstood) Norse afterlife destination. In the popular imagination, it is a Viking heaven where warriors fight all day and feast all night. This is not wrong, but it is incomplete.

Key facts about Valhǫll:
- It is **Óðinn's** hall, not a general warrior's paradise. The einherjar are specifically chosen by Óðinn (through his valkyries) for their role in Ragnarǫk. It is not a reward — it is a **draft**.
- The einherjar fight each other daily, killing and being killed, but are resurrected each evening for feasting. This is **training for Ragnarǫk**, not entertainment.
- Valhǫll has 540 doors, each wide enough for 800 warriors to march through side by side. This is not literal — it is a way of saying "an impossibly large number."
- The food is the boar Sæhrímnir, who is slaughtered and cooked each day and regrows each night. The mead flows from the udder of the goat Heiðrún.

The critical insight: **Valhǫll is not "heaven."** It is a barracks for soldiers awaiting a battle they will lose. Óðinn is recruiting an army for Ragnarǫk — a battle in which he will die. The einherjar are not being rewarded; they are being used.

**Hel — The Neutral Underworld**

Hel is the least understood and most Christianized of the Norse afterlife destinations. The word *hel* simply means "the concealed one" or "the covered place" — it is the hidden realm where the dead reside.

Hel is ruled by the goddess of the same name, daughter of Loki and Angrboða. She is described as half-living, half-dead — one side of her face is beautiful, the other a rotting corpse. She is not evil; she is the personification of death itself.

The 2040 reading (following Hultgård 2039) emphasizes that **Hel is not punishment** — it is the default destination for the majority of humanity, who die of illness, old age, or accident. There is no moral evaluation involved. The Norse afterlife system is **status-based**, not morality-based:
- Warriors who die in battle → Valhǫll or Fólkvangr
- Everyone else → Hel or the burial mound
- There is no equivalent of Christian heaven for "good" people or hell for "bad" people

The Christian concept of Hell as a place of eternal torment was **imported** during the conversion period and projected backwards onto the Norse Hel. This is one of the most damaging examples of Christian lensing in the sources.

## Lecture 11: Seiðr, Galdr, and Runes — Norse Magical Practices

**Seiðr — The Most Powerful Magic**

Seiðr is the most potent and most gender-transgressive form of Norse magic. The word *seiðr* may derive from a root meaning "to bind, to tie" — seiðr binds fate, connects worlds, and constrains outcomes.

The primary source for seiðr is the Saga of Eiríkr the Red (*Eiríks saga rauða*), which describes a seiðr ritual in Greenland:

- A **vǫlva** (seiðr practitioner, always female) sits on a high seat (*seiðhjallr*)
- She is accompanied by women who sing the *Varðlokur* (guardian songs)
- She enters a trance state and prophesies about the coming winter
- The ritual involves a staff (*seiðstafr*), a blue or black cloak, and cat-skin gloves

Seiðr practices included:
- **Prophecy and divination** — Seeing the future, revealing hidden things
- **Curses and binding** — Causing illness, infertility, or madness
- **Shapeshifting** — Sending the spirit out of the body in animal form
- **Weather magic** — Raising storms, calming seas, controlling wind

The critical social fact about seiðr: **it was considered ergi (unmanly) for men to practice it.** Óðinn, who knows seiðr, is taunted in Lokasenna for being ergi. Þórr, the epitome of masculine strength, never practices seiðr. The magic that binds fate is **female power** — and men who use it are viewed as gender-deviant.

This is not a neutral prohibition. It encodes a deep Norse gender ideology: **the power to shape destiny through indirect means (seiðr) is feminine; the power to shape the world through direct physical action (Þórr's hammer) is masculine.** Óðinn, who uses both, transcends gender categories — which is both his power and his stigma.

**Galdr — Incantation Magic**

Galdr (from *gala*, "to crow, to sing") is verbal magic — chanted spells, incantations, and charms. Unlike seiðr, galdr was acceptable for men to practice. It appears in the sources as:

- **Healing charms** — The Second Merseburg Charm (Old High German, 9th century) describes Baldr's horse being healed by a sung charm
- **Runic inscriptions** — Many runestones include the phrase "I carve these runes" (*ek rist rúnar*), implying that the carving itself is a magical act
- **Curse formulas** — The Ledberg Stone and others include protective curses: "May he be cursed who breaks this monument"
- **Battle spells** — The *Darraðarljóð* describes valkyries chanting as they weave the web of war

Galdr was the magic of **words performing actions** — a verbal technology that operates through the inherent power of speech, not through trance or spirits.

**Runes — The Written Magic**

Runes (*rúnar*) are simultaneously a writing system and a magical technology. The word *rún* means "secret, mystery, whisper" — suggesting that runes were not primarily for communication but for **concealing and revealing power**.

The mythological origin of runes is Óðinn's self-sacrifice on Yggdrasil (Hávamál 138-145): after hanging for nine nights, he learns the runes, picks them up, and gains wisdom. The runes are not invented — they are **discovered** through suffering. This mirrors the shamanic initiation pattern: the shaman gains power only through an ordeal that brings him to the edge of death.

The three runic alphabets (Elder Futhark, Younger Futhark, Medieval Futhork) are covered in NP105. In this course, the key point is that **runes are magical because they are the technology Óðinn uses to encode wisdom**. Each rune has a name, a meaning, and a power:
- *Fehu* (cattle/wealth) — the power of accumulation and mobility
- *Úruz* (aurochs/strength) — the power of raw vitality
- *Þurisaz* (giant/thorn) — the power of defense and opposition
- *Ansur* (god/message) — the power of divine communication
- *Raido* (riding/journey) — the power of movement and ritual

In the 2040 context, the UoY Runic Studies Lab uses **AI-assisted paleographic analysis** to identify rune forms, date inscriptions, and detect later forgeries. The intersection of runology and computational methods is one of the fastest-growing subfields.

## Lecture 12: Mythology and Modernity — The Uses of Norse Myth in the 21st and 22nd Centuries

**Romanticism and the Viking Revival**

Norse mythology entered modern consciousness through the **Romantic movement** of the 18th-19th centuries:

- **Paul Henri Mallet** (1755) — French scholar whose *Northern Antiquities* introduced Norse mythology to European intellectuals
- **The Grimms** (1812-1857) — Jacob and Wilhelm Grimm collected Germanic folklore, establishing the comparative mythology framework
- **Jacob Grimm** (1835) — *Deutsche Mythologie* systematically compared Norse and Germanic mythology
- **Richard Wagner** (1876) — *Der Ring des Nibelungen* created the dominant pop-cultural image of Norse mythology (and introduced significant distortions)
- **Nietzsche** (1872) — *The Birth of Tragedy* contrasted Apollonian (Greek) and Dionysian (Norse/Germanic) modes of being
- **National Romanticism** — Scandinavian countries used Old Norse mythology as a foundation for national identity

**The Problem of Appropriation**

Norse mythology has been appropriated by every major European nationalist and racist movement of the 20th and 21st centuries:

- **Nazi Germany** — The SS used runes, the concept of "Aryan" heritage, and Norse symbolism. Himmler's castle at Wewelsburg was designed as a "Norse" cult center. This is the most damaging appropriation — it permanently stained Norse symbolism in the public imagination.
- **White supremacist groups** — Ásatrú and Odinist movements in the US and Europe have been co-opted by white nationalist groups who use Norse mythology as a framework for racial ideology
- **Alt-right and online culture** — Viking memes, "Thor" avatars, and references to Valhǫll as a "warrior's heaven for white men" circulate in extremist online spaces

The 2040 UoY position on appropriation is clear:
- Norse mythology is a **world cultural heritage**, not the property of any ethnic group
- The Norse themselves were **multicultural traders** who explored Russia, Byzantium, the Middle East, and North America
- Genetic evidence shows Viking Age Scandinavia was more diverse than previously assumed
- White supremacist appropriation is **historically illiterate** — the Vikings would have traded with, married, and fought alongside people of all ethnicities
- The UoY explicitly disavows all racialized readings of Norse mythology

**Norse Myth in 2040 Pop Culture**

Norse mythology permeates 2040 culture:
- **The Marvel Cinematic Universe** (2008-2025) — Þórr and Loki as superheroes; heavily adapted but introduced millions to Norse mythology
- **Video games** — God of War (2018), Assassin's Creed Valhalla (2020), Hellblade: Senua's Sacrifice (2017), and hundreds of indie games
- **Literature** — Neil Gaiman's Norse Mythology (2017), Joanne Harris's The Gospel of Loki (2014), and a flourishing of Norse-inspired speculative fiction
- **Ásatrú and Heathenry** — Modern pagan movements that claim Norse mythology as living religion

The 2040 academic position: **pop culture is not inherently bad** — it introduces people to the mythology and can inspire deeper study. But pop culture is not scholarship. The MCU's Þórr is not the Þórr of the Eddas, and students must learn to distinguish between the two.

**The Future of Norse Mythological Studies**

In 2040, the field is evolving rapidly:
- **AI-assisted text analysis** allows detection of interpolations and reconstruction of damaged manuscripts
- **3D archaeological reconstructions** provide embodied understanding of sacred spaces
- **Computational myth analysis** (network theory, phylogenetic methods) reveals structural patterns invisible to traditional methods
- **Revitalized practice** — Modern Ásatrú communities bring embodied knowledge that complements scholarly analysis
- **Decolonial approaches** — Scholars from formerly colonized perspectives bring fresh readings that challenge Eurocentric frameworks

The challenge: to honor the mythology as a living tradition while maintaining rigorous academic standards. The mythology deserves both.
