# NP107: Pre-Christian Scandinavian Society
## Bachelor of Arts in Norse Pagan Studies — University of Yggdrasil, 2040

**Credits:** 4
**Description:** The social world of pre-Christian Scandinavia — kinship, hierarchy, law, economy, gender, and the structures of everyday life that formed the matrix within which Norse paganism was practiced. This course reconstructs the social organization of the North before the conversion, drawing on archaeology, saga literature, runic inscriptions, law codes, and comparative anthropology. Students examine how social structure shaped — and was shaped by — religious belief and practice.

## Lecture 1: The Social World Before Christendom — Sources, Methods, and the Problem of Evidence

Reconstructing pre-Christian Scandinavian society is a task of extraordinary difficulty. The society we wish to understand was non-literate in any modern sense — runic literacy existed, but the runestones that survive are overwhelmingly memorial inscriptions, not administrative records, personal correspondence, or legal codes. The rich textual record that describes Norse society — the sagas, the law codes, the historical writings — was produced after the Christian conversion, by Christian authors, in a society that had been transformed by the very processes (Christianization, state formation, urbanization) that ended the pre-Christian era.

**The Source Problem in Detail:** Every category of evidence for pre-Christian Scandinavian society is compromised in some way:

- **Archaeology** is contemporary with the pre-Christian period — the material remains of Viking Age farms, towns, cemeteries, and assembly sites were produced by the people we want to understand. But archaeology is mute on the questions that matter most for social organization: How was power exercised? What were the rules of inheritance? How were disputes resolved? What did people believe about their obligations to kin, to chieftains, to the gods? The archaeologist can excavate a chieftain's hall and measure its dimensions; the archaeologist cannot excavate the political authority that the hall embodied.

- **Saga literature** is voluble on all these questions — the Íslendingasǫgur are, among other things, a detailed ethnography of social life: feuds, lawsuits, marriages, inheritance disputes, gift exchanges, oath-taking, assembly politics. But the sagas were written in the 13th century, 200-400 years after the events they describe, by Christian authors with literary and political agendas. The social world they depict may be more a 13th-century imagination of the past than a reliable record of it.

- **Law codes** — the provincial laws of Norway (Gulaþingslǫg, Frostaþingslǫg), Sweden (the landskapslagar), and Denmark, and the Icelandic Grágás — purport to record pre-Christian legal traditions, but they were written down after the conversion and extensively revised under Christian influence. The Grágás, the most detailed surviving law code from the Norse world, was written in the 12th-13th centuries and explicitly includes Christian provisions (tithing, church attendance) alongside material that may descend from pre-Christian practice.

- **Runic inscriptions** are contemporary, vernacular, and produced by Scandinavians themselves — the only voices from the pre-Christian period that speak to us directly. But they are terse, formulaic, and overwhelmingly funerary: "X raised this stone in memory of Y, his father. God help his soul." The social information they encode — kinship terminology, inheritance patterns, the identities of commemorators and commemorated — is invaluable but limited.

- **Comparative evidence** — the well-documented societies of pre-Christian Germanic Europe (Tacitus's *Germania*, the Anglo-Saxon laws, the continental Germanic law codes) — provides analogs, but the Scandinavian evidence is sufficiently distinctive (the þing system, the role of the goði, the absence of kingship in Iceland) that comparison must be cautious.

**The 2040 Reconstruction Method:** The current scholarly approach — taught throughout the NP curriculum — is to triangulate: no single category of evidence is sufficient, but where multiple categories converge, the reconstruction gains credibility. For example: the sagas describe a social hierarchy of jarlar (earls), karlar (freemen), and þrælar (thralls/slaves); the archaeological record shows dramatic variation in house size, burial wealth, and diet that is consistent with such a hierarchy; and the runic inscriptions use terminology (drengr, þegn) that maps onto specific social ranks. The convergence does not prove the sagas are accurate in detail, but it does indicate that the broad structure of social hierarchy they describe was real.

The 2040 methodological toolkit adds new resources: the Viking Evidence Platform allows systematic comparison of spatial and chronological patterns across evidence types; Bayesian chronological modeling refines the dating of social change; and aDNA and isotopic studies provide new evidence for kinship, mobility, and social stratification. But the fundamental challenge remains: we are reconstructing a society from fragments, and the reconstruction will always be provisional.

## Lecture 2: The Social Hierarchy — Jarlar, Karlar, and Þrælar

The most famous description of Norse social structure is the Eddic poem *Rígsþula* ("The Lay of Rígr"), preserved in a 14th-century manuscript but probably composed in the 10th or 11th century. In the poem, the god Heimdallr — calling himself Rígr — visits three households: those of Ái and Edda (Great-Grandfather and Great-Grandmother), Afi and Amma (Grandfather and Grandmother), and Faðir and Móðir (Father and Mother). From these visits are born the three social orders: þræll (thrall/slave), karl (freeman), and jarl (earl/noble). The poem is a charter myth for social hierarchy, and it is also our most explicit textual description of it.

**The Jarlar — The Aristocracy:** The jarl was the highest social rank below kingship. Jarlar controlled substantial territories, commanded armed followings (the *hirð*), presided over local assemblies, and traced their ancestry to gods or legendary heroes — the *Rígsþula* explicitly makes the god Rígr the progenitor of the jarlar. The archaeological correlate of jarl-status includes: the largest longhouses (50-80 meters in length at sites like Borg in Lofoten and Lejre in Zealand), the richest burials (ship burials, chamber graves with weapons, riding gear, and imported luxury goods), and the hall buildings (skáli) that were the centers of political and ritual life.

The jarl's authority was personal, not institutional. It depended on the jarl's ability to attract and retain followers through gift-giving (the "ring-giver" — hringdrífr — of skaldic poetry), to protect his dependents from enemies, and to secure favorable outcomes in legal disputes. A jarl who failed to deliver — who was stingy with gifts, who lost battles, who could not protect his people — lost followers, and a jarl without followers was a jarl in name only. The system was fluid and competitive; the sagas are populated with "new men" who rise through ability and "old families" who decline through incompetence.

**The Karlar — The Freemen:** The karl was the free, landowning farmer — the backbone of Norse society. Karlar owned their farms, participated in the assembly (þing), bore arms, and were entitled to compensation for injuries under the law (wergild — mannhelgi, personal inviolability). They were not a homogeneous class: the bóndi (householder) with a substantial farm and dependents was socially and economically distinct from the leigumaðr (tenant farmer) working someone else's land, but both were legally "free" in a way that þrælar were not.

The karl's world was the farmstead — the cluster of buildings (longhouse, byre, barn, smithy, outbuildings) that housed a nuclear or extended family, its dependents, and its livestock. The farmstead was economically self-sufficient in staples (grain, dairy, meat, wool, leather, timber) but dependent on exchange for prestige goods (metalwork, imported textiles, whetstones, soapstone vessels). The rhythm of the karl's life was seasonal — sowing, haymaking, harvest, slaughter — punctuated by assembly meetings, religious festivals (blót), and (for some) Viking expeditions during the summer months.

**The Þrælar — The Unfree:** Slavery was a fundamental institution of pre-Christian Scandinavian society, and it is one that the Christian authors of the sagas — and many modern scholars — have been reluctant to confront directly. The þræll (male slave) or ambátt (female slave) was property, without legal rights, subject to the owner's will up to and including killing (though killing a slave was wasteful and socially disapproved in most circumstances). Slaves performed the most physically demanding and socially degraded labor: cutting turf, herding livestock in remote pastures, grinding grain, cleaning, and serving.

The sources of slaves were diverse: captives taken in Viking raids (the Irish annals record the taking of large numbers of slaves from Ireland and Britain, and the Arab geographer Ibn Rustah describes the Rus' as a people who "make raids against the Saqaliba [Slavs], taking them as slaves and selling them"); criminals condemned to slavery as punishment; debtors who sold themselves or their family members into slavery; and children born to enslaved mothers (slavery was hereditary through the female line). The slave trade was a major economic activity — the great trading centers of the Viking Age (Hedeby, Birka, Kaupang, Dublin) were hubs of the slave trade, and slaves were one of the primary exports from Scandinavia to the Islamic world via the Rus' river routes.

The evidence for the scale of slavery in pre-Christian Scandinavia is contested. The sagas depict slaveholding as normal — every substantial farm had a few slaves — but the sagas may exaggerate. The archaeological evidence is ambiguous: slave quarters are difficult to identify archaeologically, and slave burials (if they were buried at all) are unlikely to be recognizable. The 2040 consensus is that slavery was widespread and economically significant, particularly in the 9th and 10th centuries when Viking raiding produced a large supply of captives, but that its scale diminished in the 11th century as Christianization made slaveholding ideologically uncomfortable (though the Church did not categorically oppose slavery — it took centuries for slavery to disappear from the Scandinavian legal codes, and it persisted in Sweden into the 14th century).

**Social Mobility:** The three-tiered hierarchy was not a caste system. Individuals could move between ranks: a thrall could be freed (the freedman — leysingi — occupied an intermediate status, legally free but bound to the former master's household); a successful karl could accumulate wealth and followers and rise to chieftain status; a jarl could be reduced to a farmer by political misfortune. The sagas are full of stories of social mobility in both directions, and the absence of formal, institutionalized barriers to mobility is one of the features that distinguishes Norse society from the feudal systems of contemporary Christian Europe.

## Lecture 3: Kinship and the Family — The Bonds That Made the Social World

Kinship — the network of relationships created by blood, marriage, and fosterage — was the primary organizing principle of pre-Christian Scandinavian society. Before the state, before the Church, before any institution larger than the household, there was the kindred: the ætt. An individual's identity, obligations, rights, and security were defined by kinship. To be without kin — to be einn, alone — was to be vulnerable, suspect, and effectively outside society.

**The Kindred (Ætt):** The ætt was a bilateral descent group — an individual belonged to both the father's and the mother's kindred, though the paternal line was primary for inheritance and status. The ætt was not a formal corporate group with a leader and a treasury; it was a network of recognized relationships that created mutual obligations: the obligation to support kin in legal disputes (providing witnesses, oaths, and armed backing), the obligation to avenge kin who were killed, and the obligation to share in the compensation (wergild) paid for a kinsman's killing.

The reach of the ætt was defined by the concept of *bót* — compensation. The law codes specify the compensation due for injury or killing, and the amount varied with the victim's social rank and the closeness of kinship. A killing was compensated to the victim's immediate family at a higher rate, to more distant kin at a lower rate, and beyond a certain degree of kinship (typically fourth or fifth cousins) there was no claim. The compensation system defined the boundaries of the effective kindred: these are the people who owe you compensation, or to whom you owe it.

**Marriage, Inheritance, and Alliance:** Marriage in pre-Christian Scandinavia was an alliance between kindreds, not a private arrangement between individuals. The central institution was the *mundr* — the bride-price paid by the groom's family to the bride's family, which transferred guardianship of the woman from her father to her husband. The bride also brought a dowry (*heimanfylgja*), which remained her property and was returned to her kindred if the marriage ended without children. Marriage was secular, contractual, and dissoluble — divorce was available to both parties for specified reasons (violence, abandonment, infertility, failure to consummate), and the sagas record many divorces.

Inheritance was patrilineal by default: sons inherited from fathers. But the system was more flexible than strict primogeniture. All sons inherited a share (the odal system — óðal — the right to inherit family land), and daughters inherited in the absence of sons. The concept of óðal is crucial: land that had been held by a family for multiple generations acquired a special status — it could not be sold outside the kindred without giving kinsmen the right of first refusal. Óðal land was the material anchor of the kindred's identity; to lose the óðal was to lose the family's connection to its ancestors and its place in the landscape.

**Fosterage and Fictive Kinship:** Kinship was not only biological. Fosterage — the practice of sending a child to be raised in another household — was common among the elite and created bonds of obligation between foster-families that were nearly as strong as blood ties. The sagas are full of foster-brothers (fóstbrœðr) who fight and die for each other; the fosterage relationship was a political alliance as much as a personal one. Blood-brotherhood (a ritual in which two men mingled their blood and swore mutual obligation) created a form of fictive kinship that could transcend kindred boundaries. And the guest-friendship (gestrisni — hospitality) created obligations between host and guest that were, in some respects, kin-like.

**The 2040 Genetic Evidence:** The aDNA revolution has provided new evidence for kinship in the Viking Age that neither archaeology nor texts could supply. The 2038 study of the Salme ship burials in Estonia (c. 750 CE, pre-Viking) — two ships containing the remains of 41 men who died violently — used aDNA to demonstrate that four of the men were brothers, and that a fifth was a maternal uncle. These were kin groups — a family raiding party, or a war band organized on kinship lines. The genetic evidence does not settle the interpretation, but it adds a dimension — the biological reality of kinship — that the texts could only suggest. Similar studies of Viking Age cemeteries in Denmark and Sweden (2035-2039) have revealed family burial plots, with genetic relationships clustering spatially within the cemetery — evidence that burial location was organized by kinship, a pattern that the texts describe but that the archaeology alone could not confirm.

## Lecture 4: The Þing — Assembly, Law, and Governance Without a State

The most distinctive institution of pre-Christian Scandinavian society was the þing (assembly) — a regular gathering of free men to make law, settle disputes, and conduct public business. The þing was the mechanism by which a stateless society governed itself: no king, no bureaucracy, no standing army, no taxation, and yet a functioning legal order that persisted for centuries.

**The Þing System:** Every region had its own assembly. In Norway, the regional assemblies (fylkisþing, later lagþing) met at established sites — often marked by natural features (a hill, a mound, a prominent stone) and associated with pre-Christian ritual (blót). In Iceland, the system was particularly well developed: the local assemblies (héraðsþing, várþing) met in spring and autumn, and the national assembly — the Alþingi — met for two weeks each June at Þingvellir (the "Assembly Plains"), a dramatic landscape of rift valleys and lava fields east of Reykjavík. The Alþingi was the supreme legal and political institution of the Icelandic Commonwealth (c. 930-1262).

The þing was an assembly of free men — karlar, with their weapons (the "weapon-take," váþnatak, was the formal conclusion of the assembly). Women, children, and slaves did not participate. The assembly was presided over by the lǫgsǫgumaðr ("law-speaker") in Iceland or by the regional chieftains (goðar) elsewhere. The lǫgsǫgumaðr was not a judge or a legislator — he was the living repository of the law, and his role was to recite the law from memory (one-third of the law code each year over a three-year cycle) so that the assembled freemen could make decisions according to it.

**Law and Dispute Resolution:** The law was customary — it was not enacted by a sovereign but declared by the assembly, drawing on precedent and tradition. The law was also procedural, not substantive in a modern sense — it specified how disputes were to be resolved (who could bring a case, what evidence was admissible, how judgments were enforced) rather than a comprehensive code of rights and duties.

The typical legal dispute — and the sagas are essentially legal dramas — followed a sequence: (1) a wrong was committed (a killing, a theft, a boundary dispute, an insult); (2) the injured party brought a case at the þing, summoning the accused with witnesses; (3) the case was heard by a panel of judges (dómr) drawn from the assembly; (4) if the accused was found guilty, the penalty was typically compensation (bót) or outlawry (skóggangr — "forest-going," full outlawry, in which the outlaw could be killed without penalty and could receive no aid; or fjǫrbaugsgarðr — "life-money fence," lesser outlawry, in which the outlaw had to leave the country for three years but retained certain rights).

Enforcement was the weak point of the system. The þing had no police force, no standing army, no capacity to enforce its judgments. Enforcement depended on the successful litigant's ability to mobilize kin and allies to compel compliance — or to take revenge if compliance was not forthcoming. The system worked when the balance of power was relatively equal and the incentives for compromise were strong; it failed when one party was overwhelmingly stronger than the other, or when the stakes were so high that no compromise was acceptable. The great feuds of the sagas — Njáll's burning, the deaths of Gunnarr and Gísli — are stories of the legal system's failure under extreme pressure.

**The Assembly Site as Sacred Landscape:** Þing sites were frequently associated with pre-Christian ritual. The name of the Swedish assembly of Gamla Uppsala (Old Uppsala) preserves the site's dual function as a cult center (the temple at Uppsala described by Adam of Bremen) and a political center. The Alþingi at Þingvellir was situated on the boundary of several goðorð (chieftaincies), in a landscape dominated by the rift valley of the Mid-Atlantic Ridge — a place of evident natural power where the earth itself was visibly splitting apart. The association of law and religion was not incidental: the law was, in some sense, sacred — the order it maintained was cosmic as well as social. The breaking of the law was not merely a civil wrong but a disruption of the order that the gods upheld.

**The 2040 Archaeology of Assembly Sites:** Recent archaeological work at assembly sites has transformed our understanding of these institutions. Geophysical survey at Þingvellir (2035-2038) has identified the foundations of temporary booths (búðir) where assembly participants camped, the outline of the lǫgberg ("law-rock") area from which the law-speaker addressed the assembly, and evidence of large-scale feasting — animal bone middens, cooking pits, and fragments of imported pottery — that confirms the sagas' depiction of the assembly as a social and economic as well as a political event. Similar work at the Gulating assembly site in western Norway has revealed a complex ritual landscape including burial mounds, a processional route, and a possible blót site. The archaeological evidence confirms that the þing was not merely a functional gathering but a performative event — a theater of law and power enacted in a charged landscape.

## Lecture 5: Feud, Honor, and the Management of Violence

Pre-Christian Scandinavian society was violent. The sagas are full of killings — impulsive, calculated, retaliatory. But the violence was not random or anarchic; it was structured by the logic of feud — a system of reciprocal violence tempered by the possibility of settlement, in which honor was the currency and the kindred was the unit of account.

**The Logic of Feud:** Feud is not war. War is organized violence between political units; feud is violence within a community, between groups that recognize each other as members of the same moral community, governed by shared norms about who may be killed, under what circumstances, and with what consequences. The feud system worked — when it worked — by deterrence: the knowledge that a killing would provoke retaliation from the victim's kindred discouraged potential killers. It worked — when it worked — by channeling violence into forms that were survivable for the community: the cycle of killing and counter-killing was embedded in a cycle of negotiation and settlement that, ideally, ended in compensation and reconciliation.

The engine of feud was honor — heiðr (honor, esteem, worth). Honor was a public quality: it existed in the estimation of others, and it could be damaged by insult, by injury, or by failure to respond to insult or injury. The man who did not avenge a killing lost honor; the man who avenged a killing beyond what was proportionate risked escalating the feud beyond the possibility of settlement. The sagas are a form of honor-literature: they trace the consequences of insults, the calculations of vengeance, the negotiations for settlement, and the moments when the system breaks down and honor consumes everything.

**The Limits of Feud:** The feud system was not a stable equilibrium. It was vulnerable to escalation: a killing provoked a retaliation that was slightly more severe than the original offense, which provoked a further retaliation, and so on until the original grievance was irrelevant and the feud had become its own cause. The system was vulnerable to asymmetry: a powerful chieftain could kill with impunity because no one could retaliate against him, while a poor farmer was vulnerable to any aggressor. And the system was vulnerable to the insoluble grievance — the killing that could not be compensated because the victim was too valuable, the insult that could not be forgotten because it struck at the core of identity.

The most famous feud in saga literature — the events leading to the burning of Njáll in *Njáls saga* — illustrates all of these dynamics. The initial conflict is trivial (a dispute over a seat at a wedding feast), but it escalates through a series of killings, each one slightly more serious than the last, until the two kindreds are locked in a cycle of violence that can only end with the destruction of one of them. The tragedy of Njáll is that he is the man most committed to settlement and law — he tries repeatedly to negotiate, to compensate, to de-escalate — and he is destroyed anyway, because the system he believes in cannot contain the forces it has unleashed.

**Feud and Gender:** Women played a crucial role in the feud system — not as direct participants in violence (though the sagas include occasional shield-maidens and women who take revenge themselves) but as the keepers of memory and the goaders of men. It is the women in the sagas who remind their male kin of unavenged killings, who display the bloody clothes of the dead to shame the living into action, and who compose verses commemorating wrongs and demanding vengeance. The figure of the hvǫt — the woman who incites her male kin to vengeance — is a recurring trope in the sagas, and while it may be a literary convention, it also reflects a real social dynamic: women, excluded from direct participation in violence and legal process, exercised influence through the manipulation of honor.

The most famous hvǫt passage: Hildigunnr, in *Njáls saga*, throws the bloody cloak of her murdered husband Hǫskuldr over her uncle Flosi's shoulders, the clotting blood falling on him, and demands that he avenge the killing. Flosi does so — and the result is the burning of Njáll and his family, the event that precipitates the saga's tragic conclusion. The scene is almost unbearable in its intensity, and it encapsulates the terrible logic of the feud: grief, honor, kinship, and violence, woven together into a fabric that cannot be unraveled.

## Lecture 6: Economy and Subsistence — Land, Livestock, and Exchange

The material basis of pre-Christian Scandinavian society was the farm — an integrated economic unit that produced food, fiber, fuel, and building materials. But the farm was not self-sufficient in everything, and the exchange networks that moved goods within Scandinavia and beyond were essential to the functioning of society at every level.

**The Farming System:** The typical Scandinavian farm combined cereal agriculture (barley — *bygg* — as the staple, supplemented by oats, rye, and in some areas wheat) with animal husbandry (cattle for dairy and meat, sheep for wool and meat, pigs for meat, goats for milk, horses for transport and prestige). The balance between agriculture and pastoralism varied with latitude and elevation: in the fertile lowlands of southern Scandinavia, cereal agriculture dominated; in the uplands and the North Atlantic colonies, pastoralism was primary.

The farm was a complex of buildings: the longhouse (skáli or eldhús — the main dwelling, housing the family, the central hearth, and often the byre in a byre-dwelling arrangement), outbuildings for storage and specialized crafts (smithy, weaving shed), and often a separate bathhouse (baðstofa). The farmstead was surrounded by the cultivated infield (tún), manured intensively and producing the grain and hay that sustained the household, and the outfield (úthagi), used for grazing and collecting wild resources (timber, peat, berries, game).

**The Gift Economy and Prestige Goods:** Beyond subsistence, the Norse economy was organized around the circulation of prestige goods — weapons, jewelry, imported textiles, glass, wine — that were not necessities but were essential to the maintenance of social relationships. The chieftain who received a fine sword from his lord redistributed rings and weapons to his followers, who reciprocated with loyalty and service. The gift was never free: it created an obligation to reciprocate, and the delay between gift and counter-gift was the period in which the social relationship was actively maintained. The "ring-giver" — the hringdrífr of skaldic poetry — was not dispensing charity but purchasing loyalty, and the followers who received rings were not passive recipients but active participants in a relationship of mutual obligation.

The gift economy operated at every level of society. A farmer gave hospitality to a traveler, who reciprocated with news and gifts on a return journey. A chieftain gave a feast for his neighbors, who reciprocated with support at the assembly. The gods gave the seasons and the harvest, and humans reciprocated with blót — the sacrificial feast in which the animal was killed, the blood was sprinkled, and the meat was shared. The economy was embedded in social relationships; there was no separate sphere of "the economic" in the modern sense.

**Trade and the Long-Distance Networks:** Beyond the local and regional gift economy, pre-Christian Scandinavia was integrated into long-distance trade networks that stretched from the North Atlantic to the Caliphate. The great trading centers — Hedeby at the base of the Jutland peninsula, Birka on Lake Mälaren, Kaupang in Vestfold, Ribe on the Danish west coast, and Dublin in the Irish Sea — were hubs where goods from across the known world were exchanged. The archaeological record at these sites includes: silver dirhams from the Islamic Caliphate (over 100,000 dirhams have been found in Scandinavian hoards, most minted in the 9th and 10th centuries), glass beads from the Mediterranean and the Near East, silk from Byzantium, walrus ivory from Greenland, soapstone from Norway, whetstones from Eidsborg, amber from the Baltic, and slaves from everywhere the Vikings raided.

The medium of exchange was silver, typically in the form of hacksilver — cut fragments of coins, ingots, and jewelry weighed on scales. The Scandinavian economy was a "bullion economy": silver was valued by weight, not by coin denomination, and any piece of silver could serve as money. The transition to a coin-based economy occurred only in the 11th century, when Scandinavian kings began minting their own coins (Sveinn tjúguskegg in Denmark, c. 995; Óláfr Tryggvason in Norway, c. 995; Óláfr skǫtkonungur in Sweden, c. 995) as part of the broader processes of state formation and Christianization that transformed Scandinavian society.

**The 2040 Economic Reconstruction:** Isotope analysis has added a new dimension to the study of the Viking Age economy. Strontium isotope analysis of livestock bones from the trading centers reveals that cattle, sheep, and pigs were being brought to these sites from substantial distances — evidence for a regional provisioning system that supplied the urban populations. Lipid residue analysis of pottery from the trading centers reveals a mixed diet including dairy, terrestrial meat, and marine fish, with marine signatures that vary by site and over time. And the systematic metal-detector surveys that are now standard at Viking Age sites (the "Danefæ" system in Denmark, "Fornsök" in Sweden) have transformed the distribution maps of coins, weights, and metalwork, revealing the density and extent of the exchange networks.

## Lecture 7: Gender and the Social Order — Men's World, Women's World, and the Spaces Between

Gender was one of the fundamental organizing principles of pre-Christian Scandinavian society — perhaps the most fundamental, after kinship. The division of labor, the allocation of space, the attribution of authority, and the conceptualization of the cosmos were all structured by gender. But the Viking Age gender order was not as rigid as the stereotypes suggest, and the boundary between male and female was more permeable — and more anxiously policed — than a simple binary model would predict.

**The Division of Labor:** The classic division — men farmed, fought, and traveled; women managed the household, produced textiles, and raised children — is broadly accurate but incomplete. Men's work included plowing, herding, hunting, fishing, building, smithing, trading, and raiding. Women's work included milking, cheese-making, cooking, brewing, spinning, weaving, sewing, and childcare. But the boundaries were flexible: in the absence of men (who might be away raiding or trading for months or years), women managed farms, supervised laborers, and made economic decisions. The runic inscriptions record women who raised runestones, who managed property, and who traveled — the Ingvar runestones include women who "inherited the estate" and "directed the expedition."

Textile production was the central female activity — and it was economically crucial. The wool sails that powered the Viking ships were products of women's labor: the sheep had to be bred for wool quality, shorn, the wool washed, carded, spun into thread, woven into cloth on the warp-weighted loom, and the cloth cut and sewn into sails. A single longship sail required the wool of approximately 200 sheep and thousands of hours of women's work. The textile economy was the hidden engine of Viking maritime expansion, and it was entirely in women's hands.

**Gender and Space:** The longhouse was divided into gendered zones. The central hearth was shared, but the western end of the house was typically associated with women's activities (the dyngja — the women's chamber, with the loom and the dairy equipment), and the eastern end with men's activities (the skáli proper, with the high seat and the weapons). The boundary was not absolute — women entered the men's end to serve food and drink, men entered the women's end for various purposes — but it was socially meaningful. The sagas record insults directed at men who spent too much time in the women's quarters, suggesting that the spatial boundary was also a gender boundary.

**The Permeable Gender Boundary:** The most striking evidence for gender permeability in pre-Christian Scandinavia is the complex of ideas surrounding *ergi* — a term that connotes unmanliness, passivity, and (in its most charged sense) the receptive role in male homosexual intercourse. A man accused of ergi was subject to the strongest social sanction — the accusation was legally actionable as full insult (fullréttisorð) and could be answered with killing. Yet the same sources record that seiðr — the shamanic ritual practice associated with Óðinn and Freyja — was considered ergi for men to perform, and yet Óðinn himself was said (in *Lokasenna* and *Ynglinga saga*) to practice seiðr. The god who was the patron of warriors, kings, and poets was also the god who crossed the gender boundary, and his followers — the berserkir, the einherjar — occupied a liminal space between the human and the animal, the male and the female, the living and the dead.

The 21st- and 22nd-century scholarship on gender in the Viking Age has emphasized this complexity. The 2017 genomic confirmation that the Birka warrior grave Bj 581 contained a biological female forced a reconsideration of the assumption that "warrior grave" equals "male." The ongoing debate — was this individual a woman who lived as a warrior, a woman buried with warrior insignia for symbolic reasons, or a biological female who was socially male? — is unresolvable on current evidence, but the debate itself has been productive, exposing the depth of the field's assumptions about gender and demonstrating how much we do not know about how the Norse themselves understood the relationship between biological sex and social gender.

**The 2040 Gender Studies Framework:** Current scholarship (heavily influenced by the Gender Studies module of VS407) approaches Viking Age gender not as a fixed binary but as a performative system — gender was something people did, not merely something they were. The warrior who wielded a sword was performing masculinity; the woman who wove was performing femininity; the seiðr-practitioner who crossed the gender boundary was performing a third category that the sources struggle to name. The approach does not deny biological sex or the statistical association between sex and gender — it complicates the relationship, and it attends to the rare cases — the women warriors, the seiðr-men, the burials with mixed grave goods — not as anomalies to be explained away but as evidence for a gender system that was more complex than the stereotypes allow.

## Lecture 8: Religion and Society — The Sacred Embedded in the Social

Pre-Christian Scandinavian religion was not a separate sphere of activity, distinct from the economic, the political, and the domestic. It was embedded in every aspect of social life: the rhythm of the seasons was marked by blót; the assembly was held at sacred sites and opened with religious ritual; the chieftain's authority was both political and priestly; and the household was the primary locus of religious practice. Religion and society were not two things; they were the same thing, seen from different angles.

**The Goði as Priest-Chieftain:** In Iceland, the term *goði* (plural *goðar*) designated a chieftain whose authority combined political, legal, and religious functions. The goði presided over the local assembly, represented his followers in legal disputes, and led the religious rituals — particularly the blót, the sacrificial feast at which animals were killed, their blood sprinkled on participants and cult objects (hlaut-teinar — sacrificial twigs), and their meat consumed in a communal meal. The goði was not a priest in the Christian sense — there was no ordination, no separate clerical class, no theological training. His religious authority was an aspect of his political authority; to be a chieftain was, among other things, to maintain a proper relationship with the gods on behalf of the community.

The relationship between political and religious authority had practical consequences. When the Christian conversion came to Iceland (1000 CE), the decision was made at the Alþingi — the political assembly — and the goðar who accepted Christianity continued to exercise political authority. The conversion was, in effect, the goðar changing their religious affiliation while retaining their social position — a pattern that reflects the integration of religious and political authority in the pre-Christian system.

**The Household Cult:** Beyond the public rituals led by the goði, religious practice was centered on the household. The farmstead was a cult site: the central hearth (the eldhús) was the focus of domestic ritual (offerings of food and drink into the fire), the high-seat pillars (ǫndvegissúlur) were carved with images of the gods and carried from the old homestead to the new when a family relocated, and the farm's boundaries were marked by sacred stones or trees. The householder — the húsbóndi — had priestly responsibilities for his own household: leading the domestic blót, maintaining the sacred objects, and ensuring the proper relationship with the landvættir (land-spirits) who inhabited the farm's landscape.

The archaeological evidence for the household cult is subtle but accumulating. Deposits of animal bone, pottery, and metal objects in postholes (the holes dug for the high-seat pillars and other structural timbers) have been interpreted as foundation offerings — sacrifices made when a building was constructed to secure the goodwill of the spirits. The regular deposition of quernstones (hand-mills for grinding grain) in pits on farmsteads may represent a fertility ritual — the quern as a symbol of the productive household. And the ubiquitous presence of amulets — small Thor's hammers, miniature sickles, fire-steels — in domestic contexts suggests that the household was a locus of protective ritual, not merely a dwelling.

**Sacred Landscape:** The pre-Christian landscape was alive with sacred presence. The bogs where offerings were deposited — weapons in the early Iron Age (the famous Hjortspring, Nydam, and Illerup finds), gold and silver in the Viking Age — were thresholds between the human world and the world of the gods and the dead. The groves (lundr) where blót was performed were not merely convenient gathering places — they were the dwelling places of the gods, and the trees themselves were numinous. Adam of Bremen's famous description of the temple at Uppsala (c. 1075) — "a great tree, extending its branches widely, which is always green, both in winter and summer" — captures the fusion of the cult building and the sacred tree that characterized Norse sacred landscape.

The 2040 archaeological approach to sacred landscape (heavily influenced by the Environmental Humanities module of VS407) does not treat "ritual" as a separate category of activity to be identified by the presence of unusual objects. Instead, it treats the entire landscape as potentially sacred — the farm as well as the temple, the field as well as the bog, the assembly site as well as the burial mound — and asks what patterns of deposition, construction, and orientation suggest about the ways in which the Norse experienced their environment as inhabited by non-human persons (gods, spirits, ancestors) with whom they had ongoing relationships, obligations, and negotiations.

## Lecture 9: Outlaws, Outsiders, and the Social Margins

Not everyone was securely embedded in the social order. The outlaw — the man (and occasionally woman) who had been expelled from the community, stripped of legal protection, and condemned to live in the wilderness — occupied the most extreme position of social exclusion. But there were other forms of marginality: the foreigner, the freed slave, the disabled, the elderly without kin, the practitioner of magic suspected of malevolence. The margins of society reveal the boundaries of the normal, and studying them reveals what the society valued, feared, and excluded.

**The Outlaw: Skóggangr and Fjǫrbaugsgarðr:** Full outlawry — skóggangr ("forest-going") — was the most severe penalty in Norse law. The outlaw was expelled from human society: he could be killed without penalty by anyone who encountered him; he could receive no aid, no shelter, no food from anyone; his property was forfeited; and his legal existence was extinguished. The outlaw was, in the evocative Old Norse phrase, *útlagi* — "outside the law." He was not merely a criminal; he had been transformed, legally, into a non-person.

The sagas that feature outlaws — particularly *Gísla saga Súrssonar* and *Grettis saga Ásmundarsonar* — are among the most psychologically complex works of medieval literature. Gísli and Grettir are not villains; they are sympathetic figures who have been outlawed for killings that, in the moral logic of the sagas, were justified or at least understandable. Their outlawry is a tragedy — the destruction of capable, admirable men by a social system that cannot accommodate the consequences of its own values. Gísli spends years hiding in remote parts of the fjords, supported by his loyal wife Auðr and a few trusted allies, before being hunted down and killed. Grettir survives for nineteen years as an outlaw, the longest in saga literature, before being killed by sorcery — the only opponent that can defeat him. The outlaw sagas are meditations on the limits of the social order, the value of the individual who stands outside it, and the cost — to the outlaw and to the society that expels him — of exclusion.

**The Foreigner and the Stranger:** Pre-Christian Scandinavian society was not homogeneous. The trading centers — Birka, Hedeby, Kaupang — were cosmopolitan places where merchants, craftspeople, and visitors from across Europe and the Islamic world lived and worked. The sagas record individuals from the British Isles, from the Frisian coast, from the Slavic lands, and from the Sámi north who integrated into Norse society — as slaves, as freedmen, as marriage partners, as allies. The genetic evidence from the 2030s aDNA studies confirms this picture: the Viking Age population of Scandinavia included individuals with ancestry from the British Isles, from the eastern Baltic, from the Mediterranean, and from Central Asia.

But the foreigner was also suspect. The laws discriminate against foreigners in various ways: reduced compensation for injury, restricted rights to inherit, and vulnerability to exploitation. The concept of *heilagr* — sacred, inviolable — applied to specific categories of person and place, and the foreigner who was not protected by a local host was outside this sphere of inviolability. The sagas record killings of foreigners who were "nobody's man" — no one to avenge them, no one to claim compensation. The boundary between the insider and the outsider was guarded, and the foreigner occupied a precarious intermediate space.

**The Disabled and the Vulnerable:** The saga evidence for disability is fragmentary but suggestive. Individuals with physical impairments appear in the sagas in various roles: some are marginalized (the beggar, the dependent), but others occupy positions of respect and authority. The god Óðinn is one-eyed — he sacrificed an eye for wisdom at the well of Mímir — and his partial blindness is associated with his supernatural knowledge, not with incapacity. The god Týr is one-handed — he lost his hand in the binding of the wolf Fenrir — and his injury is the mark of his courage, not a disqualification from the divine assembly.

The archaeological evidence for disability is growing as osteological analysis becomes more refined. Skeletons from Viking Age cemeteries show evidence of healed injuries (fractures, weapon trauma), congenital conditions, and degenerative diseases that would have caused impairment. The fact that these individuals survived — some to advanced ages — indicates that they received care from their communities. The "care model" of disability studies, applied to the Viking Age, suggests that pre-Christian Scandinavian communities made accommodations for disabled members — not from a modern ethic of inclusion, but from the practical and affective bonds of kinship and community.

**The Níðingr and Social Death:** The opposite of the honored ancestor was the *níðingr* — the person of no honor, the wretch, the despised one. The term níð (cognate with Old English *níþ*) designated contempt, disgrace, the absence of the social value that made a person a person. A níðstǫng (scorn-pole) — a pole with a horse's head mounted on it, directed toward an enemy's land — was a curse intended to bring níð upon its target. The concept of níð reveals what Norse society valued: honor, courage, loyalty, the respect of one's peers. To be a níðingr was to have lost all of these — social death, even if the body still lived.

## Lecture 10: The Conversion to Christianity — Social Transformation and the End of the Old Order

The Christian conversion of Scandinavia (c. 800-1150 CE) was the single most significant transformation in Scandinavian history. It changed not only the religion but the entire social order: the relationship between political authority and the sacred, the structure of the family, the calendar, the economy, the understanding of the person, and the meaning of life and death. The conversion was a social revolution, and it marked the end of the pre-Christian society that is the subject of this course.

**The Conversion as a Process, Not an Event:** The traditional narrative of the conversion — the missionary arrives, the king converts, the people follow — is a simplification produced by the Christian sources that narrate it. The reality was more complex: conversion was a process that took centuries, that varied dramatically by region, that involved negotiation and syncretism as well as confrontation, and that was driven as much by political and economic factors as by theological conviction.

The archaeological evidence reveals a long transitional period in which Christian and pre-Christian practices coexisted. Burials from the 10th and 11th centuries show a mixture of Christian and pre-Christian elements: a grave oriented east-west (Christian) but containing grave goods (pre-Christian); a burial with a cross pendant and a Thor's hammer in the same grave; a runestone with a Christian cross and a pre-Christian dragon ornament on the same face. The famous Jelling stone — Haraldr blátǫnn's monument declaring that he "made the Danes Christian" — is carved with a triumphant Christ on one face and a serpent-and-lion design in the pre-Christian Mammen style on another. The conversion was not a clean break but a messy, protracted transition.

**Kings, Bishops, and the New Political Order:** The conversion was intimately connected to state formation. The kings who adopted Christianity — Haraldr blátǫnn in Denmark, Óláfr Tryggvason and Óláfr helgi in Norway, Óláfr skǫtkonungur in Sweden — did so, in part, because Christianity provided ideological and institutional resources that paganism could not match. The Christian king was not merely a war-leader; he was God's anointed, with a divine mandate that transcended the personal authority of the pre-Christian chieftain. The Church provided administrative infrastructure — bishops, dioceses, parishes — that extended royal authority into the countryside. And the Church provided literacy — the written records, the land registers, the tax lists — that transformed governance.

The losers in this transformation were the goðar — the chieftain-priests whose authority was personal, local, and based on the old religious system. In Iceland, the goðar survived by converting to Christianity and transferring their sacral authority to the new Church while retaining their political influence; the Icelandic Church was, for its first two centuries, a chieftain's church — the goðar owned the church buildings, appointed the priests (often their own sons), and collected the tithes. In Norway, the old aristocracy was largely destroyed by the conversion and the civil wars that followed, replaced by a new elite of royal servants and ecclesiastical officers.

**Changes in Social Structure:** The conversion changed the texture of everyday life. The calendar was reorganized around the Christian feast days. The old dietary restrictions (horsemeat was a ritual food associated with pre-Christian blót; its consumption was banned by the Church) were enforced by law. Marriage was brought under ecclesiastical jurisdiction — the Church insisted on monogamy, prohibited divorce, and regulated consanguinity. The exposure of infants — a practice attested in the pre-Christian period, particularly for deformed or unwanted children — was prohibited. The ritually "unclean" status of certain categories of person (the disabled, the illegitimate) was redefined in Christian terms.

The most radical change was in the understanding of the person. Pre-Christian religion had no concept of the soul distinct from the body, no heaven or hell (Valhǫll and Hel were destinations, but they were not reward and punishment in the Christian sense, and the relationship between the living and the dead was continuous — the dead were present in the landscape, in the burial mound, in the family's memory). Christianity introduced the idea of the immortal soul, judgment after death, and the absolute distinction between the saved and the damned. This was not merely a new set of beliefs; it was a new way of being human.

**The Social Memory of Paganism:** The pre-Christian past was not forgotten — it was remembered, but the nature of the memory changed. The old stories — the myths of Óðinn, Þórr, and Freyja — were preserved in the Eddic poems and the sagas, but as literature, not as scripture. The old gods were demoted from deities to characters — interesting, powerful, but no longer worshiped. The old practices — the blót, the seiðr, the landvættir — were reclassified as superstition, witchcraft, or (in some cases) as the work of demons. The social memory of paganism was Christianized — remembered through a Christian lens, preserved but transformed.

Snorri Sturluson's *Edda* (c. 1220) is the most important document of this process. Snorri was a Christian — a chieftain, a politician, a historian — and he wrote his *Edda* as a handbook for poets who needed to understand the old kennings (the poetic circumlocutions that referred to the myths). But Snorri could not present the pagan gods as gods without risk of heresy. His solution — the "euhemerist" frame — was to present the Æsir as human kings from Troy who had migrated to Scandinavia and been remembered as gods after their deaths. The euhemerist frame allowed Snorri to preserve the myths while disavowing the theology: these are not gods, they are kings whom ignorant people mistook for gods. The frame is transparently artificial, but it served its purpose — it preserved the stories for posterity while protecting the author from charges of paganism.

## Lecture 11: Comparative Perspectives — What is Distinctive About Pre-Christian Scandinavian Society?

The final content lecture steps back from the specifics of Norse society to ask: what is distinctive about this social formation? How does it compare to other pre-modern societies — the Germanic societies of the Migration Period, the Celtic societies of pre-Roman Europe, the segmentary lineage societies of Africa, the "big man" societies of Melanesia?

**The Stateless Society:** The most distinctive feature of pre-Christian Scandinavian society — particularly in Iceland — is the absence of the state. No king, no bureaucracy, no standing army, no taxation, no formal institutions of coercion. And yet a functioning legal order, a rich literary culture, complex long-distance trade, and a remarkable degree of social stability (the Icelandic Commonwealth persisted for over 300 years without a state). This is not unique in world history — comparable stateless societies are documented from many periods and places — but it is unusual for a society of this complexity in medieval Europe.

How did it work? The standard answer — and it is partly right — is that the kinship system provided the functions that the state provides in other societies: security (the kindred avenged its members), dispute resolution (the kindred negotiated settlements), and welfare (the kindred supported its needy members). But this answer is incomplete. The þing system provided a forum for dispute resolution that transcended the kindred — a defendant without a strong kindred could, in principle, receive justice at the assembly. The goði system provided a form of leadership that was more institutionalized than the "big man" of Melanesian ethnography, less institutionalized than the king of medieval Europe. And the shared cultural framework — the law, the myths, the honor system — provided a normative order that constrained violence and enabled cooperation.

The Icelandic Commonwealth is a natural experiment in stateless governance, and it has attracted sustained attention from political scientists, anthropologists, and economists — as well as from libertarian thinkers who see it as a model of "anarcho-capitalism." The UoY approach is skeptical of this reading: the Commonwealth was not a free market in governance — it was embedded in kinship, custom, and religion in ways that modern libertarian theory does not capture. But the Commonwealth does demonstrate that the state is not the only possible form of complex social organization, and that human beings are capable of maintaining order without the formal institutions of coercion that modern political theory takes for granted.

**Comparison with Other Germanic Societies:** Pre-Christian Scandinavia shared a broad cultural inheritance with the other Germanic-speaking peoples of Europe — the Anglo-Saxons, the continental Saxons, the Frisians, the various tribal groups recorded by Tacitus in the 1st century CE. The common features include: the comitatus (the war-band organized around personal loyalty to a leader), the wergild system (compensation for injury and killing), the assembly (the Anglo-Saxon *moot*, the Germanic *Ding*), the importance of kinship, and the absence of a formal state apparatus. But there were differences: Scandinavian society was more maritime, more dependent on raiding and trade, and (in Iceland) more egalitarian and less hierarchical than the Germanic societies that evolved into the feudal kingdoms of medieval Europe.

**Comparison with the Sámi:** The Sámi — the Indigenous people of northern Scandinavia — were the Norse's neighbors, trading partners, and (in some periods) antagonists. The Sámi were reindeer pastoralists and hunter-gatherers, organized in siidas (communities) rather than in the kindred-and-chieftain system of the Norse. The two societies interacted for centuries, and the Norse both traded with the Sámi (for furs, walrus ivory, and falcons) and exploited them (extracting tribute — finnskattr) in a relationship that has been characterized as internal colonialism. The comparison between Norse and Sámi society illuminates both: the Norse system of stratified farming and chieftaincy was not the only possible social organization in the Scandinavian environment; the Sámi adapted differently to the same northern landscape, and their social structure — more egalitarian, more mobile, less hierarchical — represents an alternative pathway.

**The 2040 Comparative Toolkit:** The Comparative Indigenous Studies module of VS407 provides students with the theoretical framework for this comparison, and the Viking Evidence Platform now includes comparative datasets — Sámi archaeological sites, Celtic assembly sites, Germanic Migration Period burials — that enable the student to pursue comparative questions systematically. The course's comparative perspective does not aim to produce a single "type" into which Norse society is slotted, but rather to use comparison to sharpen our understanding of what is distinctive, what is shared, and what is genuinely puzzling about the Norse case.

## Lecture 12: The Pre-Christian Past in the 2040 Present — Reconstruction, Appropriation, and Meaning

The final lecture addresses the presence of pre-Christian Scandinavian society in the 2040 present. This is not a historical society we study at a comfortable distance. It is a society that is actively claimed — by modern Heathens who seek to reconstruct its religious practices, by neo-Nazis who appropriate its symbols, by heritage industries that commodify its image, and by the modern Scandinavian nations whose national identities were shaped by the memory (and the forgetting) of the pre-Christian past.

**The Heathen Reconstruction of Society:** For modern Heathens (see NP401-407), the reconstruction of pre-Christian society is not merely an academic exercise — it is a matter of religious practice. What social forms are implied by the blót? What is the proper relationship between the goði and the community? How should disputes be resolved in a community that seeks to live by Norse values? The Heathen community's answers to these questions vary widely — from strict reconstructionists who attempt to replicate the social forms of the Viking Age, to eclectic practitioners who integrate Norse values with modern liberal democratic norms. The course treats these efforts with respect but also with scholarly rigor: the reconstruction of a society is even more difficult than the reconstruction of a ritual or a myth, because social organization is embedded in material conditions (the farming economy, the kinship system, the legal institutions) that cannot simply be revived by an act of will.

**The Neo-Nazi Appropriation — A Continued Threat:** As discussed in VS401 Lecture 5 and NP401-407, the Nazi and neo-Nazi appropriation of Norse symbols is a persistent and damaging presence in the reception of the pre-Christian past. The social hierarchy of jarl-karl-þræll has been misread as a racial hierarchy; the concept of the ætt has been misread as an endorsement of racial purity; the exclusion of outsiders has been misread as a justification for xenophobia. The UoY position is that these misreadings are not merely intellectually wrong — they are morally evil. The pre-Christian Norse were not Nazis, and their society — complex, hierarchical, exclusionary in some ways, remarkably open in others — cannot be reduced to the racist fantasy that the neo-Nazis project onto it.

**The Living Past:** The pre-Christian Scandinavian past is not dead. It survives — transformed, fragmented, contested — in the 2040 present. It survives in the landscapes that were shaped by Viking Age farms and assemblies. It survives in the languages that still carry the words the Norse spoke. It survives in the stories — the myths, the sagas — that continue to be read, adapted, and argued over. And it survives in the questions that this course has posed: How do human beings organize themselves without a state? What is the relationship between kinship and society? What does it mean to live in a world where the sacred is embedded in the everyday, where the gods are present in the landscape, where honor is worth dying for?

These are not merely historical questions. They are human questions — and the pre-Christian Norse, in their distant, fragmentary, and endlessly fascinating way, addressed them in forms that continue to speak to us across the millennium that separates their world from ours.
