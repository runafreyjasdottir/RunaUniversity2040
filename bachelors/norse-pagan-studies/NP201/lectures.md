## Lecture 1: The Problem of Ritual Reconstruction — Sources, Methods, and the Nature of Evidence

The reconstruction of pre-Christian Norse ritual practice is one of the most challenging problems in the study of Old Norse religion. We know that ritual was central to Norse paganism — the sources describe sacrifices, feasts, divination, and ceremonial practices in vivid detail. But every source that describes these practices is filtered through the lens of Christian authorship, literary convention, or archaeological silence. The ritual world we wish to understand was lived, embodied, and performative; the sources that transmit it are textual, retrospective, and distanced from the practices they describe.

**The Source Categories and Their Limits:**

- **Archaeology** provides the most direct evidence for ritual practice. Sacrificial sites — the bogs, lakes, and wetlands where offerings were deposited (the so-called "wetland sacrifices" or *mosefund*) — have yielded spectacular finds: weapons, tools, jewelry, pottery, and the bones of sacrificed animals and, in rare cases, humans. The Danish site of Illerup Ådal (Jutland) has produced over 15,000 objects deposited between 200-500 CE, many of them deliberately destroyed before deposition — a pattern consistent with ritual destruction and offering. The Swedish site of Skedemosse (Öland) yielded animal bones, weapons, and gold objects deposited over centuries. These sites are contemporary with the ritual practices they represent, and their dating is relatively secure. But archaeology is radically underdetermined: a deposit of animal bones in a bog could be the remains of a ritual sacrifice, a secular feast, or ordinary butchery waste. The ritual interpretation requires the convergence of multiple lines of evidence — the location (a liminal wetland rather than a settlement), the pattern (repeated deposition at the same site over generations), the treatment of the objects (deliberate destruction, arranged deposition), and the absence of settlement debris. No single criterion is sufficient.

- **Saga literature** is voluble on ritual practice. The *Heimskringla* of Snorri Sturluson (c. 1230) contains the most famous account of a blót in Old Norse literature: the description of the sacrifice at Trøndelag in *Hákonar saga góða* (the Saga of King Hákon the Good), in which the Christian king is pressured to participate in a pagan sacrifice — eating horse meat, drinking the ceremonial ale, and making the sign of Thor's hammer over the sacrificial bowl. The *Landnámabók* (Book of Settlements) describes Icelandic settlers bringing their "sacred pillars" (öndvegissúlur) from Norway and casting them overboard to let the gods choose their landing site. The *Eyrbyggja saga* describes the establishment of a hof (temple) by Þórólfr Mostrarskegg, complete with a sacred mountain (Helgafell) and a forbidden zone where no one could defecate or look at the mountain without washing first. These accounts are vivid, detailed, and internally consistent — but they were written 200-400 years after the conversion, by Christian authors who may have had theological or literary motives for depicting pagan ritual as exotic, grotesque, or superstitious.

- **Law codes** provide indirect evidence. The post-conversion Norwegian and Icelandic law codes contain provisions prohibiting pagan practices — a negative witness that testifies to the existence of the practices being prohibited. The Norwegian Gulaþingslǫg forbids the practice of sitting out on crossroads at night (a form of divination or necromancy), the raising of cult stones (hörgar), and the practice of seiðr. The Icelandic Grágás does not explicitly address pagan ritual (Iceland converted in 999/1000, and the Grágás was compiled in the 12th century when pagan practice had been suppressed for generations), but earlier fragments of Norwegian law preserved in the *Gulaþingslǫg* suggest that pagan practices persisted in rural areas for decades after official conversion.

- **Contemporary foreign accounts** — the descriptions of Norse ritual by outsiders — are the most direct textual evidence. The most important is the account of Ahmad ibn Fadlan, an Arab diplomat who encountered the Rus' (Viking traders operating on the Volga River) in 922 CE and described their funeral and sacrificial practices in extraordinary detail: the sacrifice of animals, the ritual cleaning of the dead man's ship, the elaborate funeral of a chieftain involving a slave girl who volunteered to accompany her master into death, and the burning of the ship-burial on the riverbank. Ibn Fadlan's account is contemporary, by an outsider without Christian bias, and ethnographically precise. But it describes the practice of the Rus' in eastern Europe, not Scandinavian ritual directly, and the extent to which it is representative of Norse ritual in Scandinavia proper is debated.

- **Comparative evidence** — the ritual practices of other pre-Christian Germanic and Indo-European peoples — provides analogs. The Roman author Tacitus describes Germanic ritual in his *Germania* (98 CE), including the worship of Nerthus (an earth goddess) by the northern Germanic tribes, with a ceremonial procession of the goddess's chariot through the territory, a period of peace during the procession, and the ritual washing of the chariot and the goddess's garments in a hidden lake by slaves who are then drowned. The *Germania* is our earliest textual account of Germanic ritual, but it was written by a Roman senator with moralizing and ethnographic agendas, and the peoples it describes (the "Germani" of the 1st century) are separated by 800-1000 years from the Viking Age Scandinavians we wish to understand.

**The 2040 Methodological Framework:** The current approach, taught throughout the NP curriculum, is *ritual triangulation*: no single source category is sufficient, but where archaeology, textual accounts, law code prohibitions, and comparative evidence converge, the reconstruction gains credibility. The 2040 toolkit adds computational approaches: the Ritual Evidence Platform (REP) allows systematic comparison of spatial, chronological, and material patterns across evidence types; isotopic analysis of sacrificial deposits reveals the geographic origin of sacrificed animals; and Bayesian modeling of chronological sequences at cult sites refines our understanding of ritual change over time. But the fundamental challenge remains: ritual is performance, and performance leaves only material traces. The meaning, the experience, the emotional and cosmological significance of ritual — these are, in the end, irrecoverable from the sources we have. What we can reconstruct is the *form* of ritual: what was done, where, when, and with what materials. The *meaning* — what the practitioners believed they were doing, what they felt, what the ritual meant in their social and cosmological world — must be inferred, and the inference is always uncertain.

**Key Terminology for the Course:**
- **Blót** — Sacrifice, from the verb *blóta* (to sacrifice, to worship, to strengthen). The core ritual of Norse paganism: the offering of something valuable (animal, food, drink, object) to the gods, ancestors, or land-spirits, accompanied by a communal feast.
- **Sumbel** — A ritual drinking ceremony, typically conducted in a hall or at a feast, involving the ceremonial drinking of ale or mead from a horn, with rounds of toasts, oaths, and boasts. The sumbel is attested in Beowulf (Old English *symbel*) and in several Old Norse sources.
- **Seiðr** — A form of ritual practice involving trance, divination, and the manipulation of fate (ørlǫg). Associated primarily with women (vǫlur, spákonur) but practiced by men as well (though male practitioners faced social stigma — *ergi* — for engaging in a practice coded as feminine).
- **Hǫrgr** — A cult site, typically a natural feature (hill, grove, lake, stone) associated with divine presence.
- **Hof** — A cult building, a temple or shrine. The existence of dedicated cult buildings in pre-Christian Scandinavia is debated; some scholars argue that the "temple" descriptions in the sagas are literary inventions, while others point to archaeological evidence for cult buildings at sites like Uppåkra (Skåne) and Hofstaðir (Iceland).
- **Vé** — A sacred place, a sanctuary. The term also appears in personal names (Vésteinn, Vébrandr) and in the name of one of the three brothers (Óðinn, Vili, Vé) who created the world in the Eddic creation narrative.

## Lecture 2: Blót — The Sacrificial Ritual

The blót was the central ritual of pre-Christian Norse religion — the act of offering something of value to the gods, the ancestors, or the land-spirits, and sharing the offering in a communal feast. The word *blóta* means both "to sacrifice" and "to worship"; the etymological connection suggests that in the Norse conceptual world, sacrifice and worship were inseparable — you worshipped by sacrificing, and sacrifice was the primary mode of relationship with the divine.

**The Structure of the Blót:**

The sources — particularly Snorri's account in *Hákonar saga góða* and the brief descriptions in the *Landnámabók* and the *Íslendingasǫgur* — suggest a consistent ritual structure:

1. **Preparation of the sacrificial animal.** The animal (typically a boar, a horse, a bull, or a goat) was led to the sacred site. The choice of animal was significant: horses were associated with Freyr and with the cult of the Vanir; boars were associated with Freyr and with oath-taking; cattle were general sacrificial animals. The animal was killed by bleeding — the throat was cut, and the blood was collected in a ritual vessel (a *hlautbolli*, "blood-bowl," according to Snorri).

2. **Sprinkling of the blood (*hlaut*).** The collected blood — the *hlaut* — was used to redden the participants and the sacred objects. Snorri describes the blood being sprinkled on the cult participants (on their faces, on their clothing), on the walls and idols of the hof, and on the sacrificial poles or pillars. The reddening with blood was the central symbolic act of the blót: it marked the participants as connected to the divine, it consecrated the sacred space, and it physically enacted the transfer of life-force from the sacrificed animal to the community and its gods.

3. **Cooking and consumption of the sacrificial meat.** The meat of the sacrificed animal was cooked — Snorri specifies that it was boiled in cauldrons over the fire in the hall — and consumed in a communal feast. The feast was the ritual's social dimension: the community shared the meat of the sacrifice, establishing commensality (eating together as a sacred act) and reinforcing the bonds between the participants, between the community and its gods, and between the living and the dead.

4. **Drinking of the ceremonial ale.** The blót was accompanied by the drinking of ale or mead, specifically brewed for the occasion. The ale was drunk from a horn (blóthorn) or from a ceremonial vessel, and the drinking was accompanied by toasts — to Óðinn (for victory and wisdom), to Njǫrðr and Freyr (for peace and prosperity), and to ancestors or departed kin. The toasting ritual at the blót overlaps with the sumbel (see Lecture 4), and the two practices may have been aspects of a single ritual event.

5. **Divination and oath-taking.** Some accounts suggest that the blót included divinatory elements — the reading of omens from the sacrificial animal's entrails or from the pattern of the blood — and the making of oaths, particularly at the annual blóts associated with the assembly (þing). The connection between sacrifice, divination, and oath-taking reflects the Norse understanding that the sacred was the proper context for binding commitments and for seeking knowledge of the future.

**The Problem of Human Sacrifice:**

The most controversial question in the study of the blót is whether human sacrifice was practiced in pre-Christian Scandinavia. The textual sources describe it: the *Heimskringla* recounts the sacrifice of a slave at the funeral of the chieftain Sigurðr Hlóðvisson; Adam of Bremen describes the sacrifice of nine men (one per year for nine years) at the temple of Uppsala; the *þáttr* of Þrymr describes the sacrifice of a king to ensure victory; and ibn Fadlan describes the voluntary sacrifice of a slave girl at a Rus' funeral. The archaeological evidence is more ambiguous: the bog bodies of Denmark and northern Germany (Tollund Man, Grauballe Man, Windeby Girl) — bodies deposited in bogs with evidence of violent death — have been interpreted as sacrificial victims, but they may also be executed criminals or social outcasts. The 2040 consensus is that human sacrifice probably occurred in pre-Christian Scandinavia, but that it was rare, exceptional, and probably restricted to specific circumstances (funeral rites, crisis situations, the dedication of a new cult site) rather than being a regular feature of the blót cycle.

**The Blót as Communion and Covenant:**

The blót was not merely a transaction — "I give this so that you will give that" — though the transactional element was present. It was also a communion: the community and its gods shared the same food, the same drink, the same blood. The communal meal was the ritual's culmination, and its significance went beyond nutrition. To eat the meat of a sacrifice was to participate in the sacred; to refuse the sacrificial meal (as King Hákon tried to do) was to refuse participation in the community's relationship with its gods. The blót was also a covenant: the sacrifice established and renewed the relationship between the community and the divine, a relationship of mutual obligation — the gods protected and prospered the community, the community honored and fed the gods. When the covenant was broken (by neglect of sacrifice, by Christian refusal to participate), the consequences were believed to be real: crop failure, disease, military defeat.

**The 2040 Archaeological Evidence:**

Recent excavations have transformed our understanding of blót practice. The site of Lunda (Södermanland, Sweden), excavated 2034-2038, revealed a ritual complex with a processional road, a cult building, and a series of sacrificial deposits spanning the 5th-11th centuries. The deposits included animal bones (primarily pig and horse), weapons (deliberately bent and broken), and gold objects (guldgubbar — small gold foil figures depicting embracing couples, probably associated with the Vanir cult and with sacred marriage — *hieros gamos*). The chronological sequence at Lunda shows a shift from weapon sacrifice (5th-7th centuries) to animal sacrifice (8th-10th centuries) to gold foil deposition (10th-11th centuries) — a pattern that may reflect changing ritual priorities as the Viking Age progressed.

The site of Tissø (Zealand, Denmark), excavated 1995-2039, revealed a large cult complex with a great hall (60 meters long), a cult building, and workshops for gold and metalworking. The animal bone assemblage from Tissø is dominated by pig and cattle, with a notable absence of fish and wild game — consistent with a feasting assemblage rather than ordinary consumption. The presence of high-status objects (gold rings, silver hoards, imported glass) alongside the animal bones suggests that the blót feast at Tissø was also a political event — a gathering of the elite for sacrifice, feasting, and the exchange of gifts and alliances.

## Lecture 3: The Seasonal Calendar — The Major Blóts of the Year

The ritual year of pre-Christian Scandinavia was organized around the agricultural cycle and the seasonal transitions. The sources — primarily the calendar terminology preserved in the law codes and the saga accounts — describe a series of seasonal blóts that marked the turning points of the year.

**The Norse Calendar and Its Rituals:**

The Norse year was divided into two seasons: summer (sumar) and winter (vetr). The year began with the first day of winter (vetrnætur — "winter nights"), which fell in mid-October (the month of Gormánuðr, "gormonth," the month of slaughter). The seasonal blóts were:

1. **Vetrnætrblót (Winter Nights Blót) — Mid-October.** The most important blót of the year, marking the beginning of winter. This was the time when livestock were brought in from summer pastures, surplus animals were slaughtered, and the community gathered for the great autumn sacrifice. The vetrnætrblót was associated with the dísir (the female spirits of the family line, the protective ancestral mothers) and with Freyja — it may have been a ritual addressed to the female divine powers who controlled fertility and destiny. Snorri describes the vetrnætrblót as a three-day sacrifice: the first day for the dísir, the second for the gods, the third for the community's prosperity.

2. **Jólblót (Yule Blót) — Mid-January.** The midwinter sacrifice, coinciding with the solstice period. The jólblót was the feast of Óðinn — the god who rides through the sky at midwinter, leading the Wild Hunt, and who receives sacrifices for victory and wisdom in the coming year. The jólblót was a feast of renewal: the turning of the year, the return of the sun, the promise of spring. The ritual consumption of the Yule boar (sonargǫltr) was central to the jólblót — a boar was sacrificed to Freyr, and oaths were sworn on the boar's bristles (the *sonargǫltr* oath, described in *Helgakviða Hjǫrvarðssonar*). The Yule boar tradition persisted in modified form after Christianization — the Christmas ham is a direct descendant of the Yule boar.

3. **Sumarsblót (Summer Blót) — April.** The sacrifice marking the beginning of summer, the departure of Viking expeditions, and the opening of the sailing season. The sumarsblót was associated with Óðinn (for victory in raiding and warfare) and with Njǫrðr (for safe voyages and favorable winds). The law codes prescribe the sumarsblót as a communal obligation — every farmer was required to contribute to the sacrifice.

4. **Alfablót (Elf Sacrifice) — Late Autumn/Early Winter.** A private, domestic sacrifice mentioned in the *Kormáks saga* and in the poem *Austrfararvísur* by the Icelandic skald Sighvatr Þórðarson. The alfablót was performed at individual farmsteads, not at communal cult sites, and it was addressed to the álfar (elves) — the local spirits of the land, the mound-dwellers, the powers of place. Sighvatr's poem describes being refused entry to a farm in Sweden because the household was performing the alfablót and could not receive guests — "I was refused entrance, for they were holding the elf-sacrifice." The alfablót was private, exclusive, and associated with the fertility of the farm and the protection of the household.

5. **Þorrablót (Þorri's Blót) — Late January/February.** A sacrifice associated with the month of Þorri (the fourth winter month, January-February). The Þorrablót is mentioned in the *Landnámabók* and in the *Orkneyinga saga*, and it may have been a sacrifice to the ancestor Þorri — a legendary king of the Goths and Finns who gave his name to the month. The modern Icelandic Þorrablót (a midwinter feast of traditional foods — fermented shark, cured ram's testicles, pickled whale blubber — eaten during the month of Þorri) is a 19th-century revival with no direct connection to the pre-Christian ritual, but it preserves the name and the seasonal timing.

6. **Dísablót (Sacrifice to the Dísir) — At Vetrnætr or at the Spring Dísaping.** The sacrifice to the dísir (the female spirits) was sometimes a separate ritual and sometimes part of the vetrnætrblót. In Sweden, the dísablót was associated with the Dísaping — the assembly held at Uppsala in February or March, combining legal proceedings with ritual sacrifice. The dísablót was a women's ritual in some accounts, though men also participated; the connection between the dísir and Freyja suggests a Vanir-cult context.

**The Problem of the Calendar:**

The reconstruction of the Norse ritual calendar is complicated by the fragmentary nature of the evidence. The surviving calendar terminology is Norse-Icelandic (the month names preserved in the Icelandic calendar: Þorri, Gói, Ýlir, Mörsugr, etc.) and may not reflect the calendar practices of other Scandinavian regions. The Norwegian and Swedish evidence suggests regional variation — the Dísaping at Uppsala has no Icelandic parallel, and the Alfablót described in Swedish sources is not attested in Iceland. The 2040 scholarly consensus is that there was no single, uniform "Norse ritual calendar" but rather a family of related calendars that shared core elements (winter nights, Yule, summer sacrifice) while varying in detail by region and community.

**Ritual Time and Sacred Space:**

The seasonal blóts were not merely markers on a calendar; they were moments when ordinary time was suspended and sacred time was enacted. The blót period was a liminal time — a threshold between seasons, between the ordinary and the divine, between the living and the dead. This liminality was marked by specific practices: restrictions on travel (the *Austrfararvísur* poem describes the refusal to receive guests during the alfablót), the performance of activities (sacrifice, divination, oath-taking) that were restricted to the blót period, and the consumption of foods (sacrificial meat, ceremonial ale) that were specifically associated with the ritual occasion. The seasonal blót was, in the anthropological terminology of Victor Turner, a period of *communitas* — a temporary dissolution of the ordinary social order into a state of communal bonding and shared experience.

## Lecture 4: Sumbel — The Ritual Feast and Oath-Making

The sumbel (Old Norse *sumbel*, Old English *symbel*) was a ritual drinking ceremony — a formal, structured sequence of toasts, oaths, and boasts conducted during a feast, typically in a hall or at a gathering of the community. The sumbel was distinct from the blót (though the two could overlap — the drinking at a blót might include sumbel elements) and from ordinary feasting (the sumbel was ritually structured in a way that ordinary meals were not).

**The Evidence for Sumbel:**

The primary textual evidence for the sumbel comes from two sources: the Old English poem *Beowulf* and the Old Norse sagas.

In *Beowulf*, the sumbel is described as a formal ceremony in the hall of King Hroðgar. Wealhþeow, the queen, serves the mead cup to the warriors in a prescribed order: first to Hroðgar, then to the warriors, then to Beowulf. The serving of the cup is a ritual act — it establishes the social hierarchy (who is served first = who is most honored) and creates obligations (to receive the cup is to accept the giver's friendship and protection). The sumbel in *Beowulf* is also the occasion for oaths: Beowulf promises to defeat Grendel or die trying, and the warriors pledge their loyalty to the king.

In the Old Norse sagas, the sumbel appears as the *minni* (remembrance toast) or the *full* (the ceremonial cup). The *Heimskringla* describes the drinking at the blót: "The first full was dedicated to Óðinn — for victory and power to the king. The second to Njǫrðr and Freyr — for peace and good harvests. The third to the bragarfull — the cup of the chieftain, for the memory of departed kinsmen." The *Hávamál* (Sayings of the High One) contains advice on drinking at feasts: "The best of feasts is one you can leave with your wits intact" (v. 12) — but also a warning against the perils of over-drinking: "A man's mind, when he's drunk, is not the mind he had when he was sober" (v. 13).

**The Structure of the Sumbel:**

Based on the textual evidence and on comparative Germanic practice (the sumbel is reconstructed in modern Ásatrú and Heathen practice with varying degrees of scholarly fidelity), the sumbel typically consisted of three rounds:

1. **The First Round — The Gods.** Toasts to the gods, typically beginning with Óðinn (for wisdom, victory, and inspiration), then to Þórr (for protection and strength), then to Freyr and/or Freyja (for fertility, prosperity, and love). The order of the toasts reflected the community's religious priorities.

2. **The Second Round — Ancestors and Heroes.** Toasts to the dead — to remembered kinsmen, to legendary heroes, to the community's forebears. This round connected the living community with its past, reinforcing the continuity of the lineage and the obligation to honor the dead. The *bragarfull* (the "cup of the chieftain" or the "cup of promises") was drunk in this round, sometimes with a specific oath or vow accompanying the toast.

3. **The Third Round — Oaths, Boasts, and Vows.** The most socially significant round. Warriors and free men made oaths — pledges of loyalty, promises of future deeds, boasts of past accomplishments. The oath-making at the sumbel was binding in a way that ordinary speech was not: an oath sworn over the ceremonial cup, in the presence of the gods and the community, was the most solemn commitment a Norse person could make. The violation of a sumbel oath was a crime against the divine order — it invited the wrath of the gods and the condemnation of the community.

**The Sumbel as Social Ritual:**

The sumbel was not primarily a religious ritual in the sense of addressing the gods for favors. It was a social ritual — a ceremony that created, reinforced, and displayed the bonds of loyalty, kinship, and mutual obligation that held Norse society together. The order of cup service, the hierarchy of toasts, and the public nature of oath-making all served to make the social order visible, explicit, and ritually confirmed. The sumbel was the moment when a warrior's status was publicly acknowledged (or challenged), when alliances were declared, and when the community's leaders demonstrated their generosity (by providing the feast and the drink).

**The Queen's Role — Wealhþeow and the Cup:**

The role of women in the sumbel — particularly the role of the queen or the lady of the hall in serving the ceremonial cup — is significant and often underappreciated. In *Beowulf*, Wealhþeow's service of the cup is not a menial task; it is a ritual act that establishes the social order and creates obligations. She determines who receives the cup and in what order; she speaks words of encouragement and exhortation alongside the cup; she mediates between the king and the warriors, ensuring that the ritual of the sumbel reinforces the bonds it is meant to create. The woman who serves the sumbel cup is not a servant — she is the ritual's central figure, the one who creates the connections it celebrates.

**The 2040 Understanding:** Current scholarship emphasizes the sumbel as a ritual of social integration — a ceremony that creates and maintains the web of obligations, loyalties, and identities that constituted Norse social life. The drinking was not incidental to the social function; it was the medium through which the social bonds were created and displayed. The shared cup, the public oath, the witnessed toast — these were the mechanisms by which a stateless society created and enforced the commitments that held it together. Without the sumbel, without the public oath witnessed by the community and the gods, Norse society would have lacked the ritual means to create binding commitments — and without binding commitments, the system of gift-giving, loyalty, and reciprocal obligation that structured Norse social life could not have functioned.

## Lecture 5: Seiðr — The Shamanic Practice

Seiðr is the most enigmatic, the most debated, and the most misunderstood of the Norse ritual practices. The sources describe it as a form of ritual magic involving trance, prophecy, and the manipulation of fate — a practice that was both powerful and socially transgressive, associated primarily with women but also practiced by men who risked social stigma for doing so.

**The Sources on Seiðr:**

The primary textual accounts of seiðr are found in the sagas and in Snorri's *Edda*:

- **The *Vǫluspa*** (The Prophecy of the Seeress) — the opening poem of the *Poetic Edda* — is spoken by a vǫlva (seeress) who addresses Óðinn and the assembled gods, recounting the creation of the world, the first war between the Æsir and Vanir, the coming of the future, and the final battle of Ragnarǫk. The vǫlva's knowledge is presented as the product of seiðr — she "comes from afar" (v. 2), she "remembers" (v. 1), and she "sees" (v. 3) the entire span of cosmic time. The *Vǫluspa* is both the most important source for seiðr and the most problematic: it is a literary composition, not an ethnographic account, and the vǫlva's performance in the poem may be a literary device rather than a description of actual practice.

- **The *Eiríks saga rauða*** (Saga of Erik the Red) contains the most detailed account of a seiðr performance in Old Norse literature. A vǫlva — a Greenlandic seeress named Þórbjǫrg — visits a farmstead during a famine and performs a seiðr ritual to predict the community's future. The description is detailed: the vǫlva sits on a high seat (seiðhjallr), dressed in a blue-black cloak with a catskin hood and lambskin gloves; she carries a staff (a *seiðstafr* with a brass knob set with stones); she eats a special meal of hearts from various animals; and she sings a spell (a *varðlokur*) while a young woman (the farmer's daughter) assists by singing the calling-song. The vǫlva then enters a trance and prophesies: the famine will end, the sick will recover, and the community will prosper. The account is vivid and ethnographically detailed, but it was written in the 13th century by a Christian author, and the extent to which it reflects actual practice rather than literary convention is debated.

- **Snorri's *Ynglinga saga*** describes Óðinn himself as a practitioner of seiðr: "Óðinn knew, and practiced himself, the art which is called seiðr, and from it he could know the fate of men and future events, and cause the death of men or misfortune, and take from men their wit or strength, and give them to others. But this magic, when it is practiced, is accompanied by such great *ergi* [unmanliness, sexual perversion] that men were not without shame to practice it, and it was taught to the goddesses." This account is crucial: it establishes seiðr as a real practice, associates it with Óðinn (the male god who crosses gender boundaries), and explains the social stigma attached to male practitioners (the concept of *ergi* — unmanliness, the accusation that a man who practices seiðr is *argr*, a derogatory term associated with receptive homosexuality and gender transgression).

**The Practice of Seiðr:**

Based on the textual accounts and on comparative evidence from Sámi shamanism and other circumpolar traditions, the 2040 reconstruction of seiðr practice includes the following elements:

1. **The Seiðhjallr (Seiðr Platform).** The vǫlva sits on an elevated platform or seat — the seiðhjallr — during the performance. The platform may have been a simple raised seat, a stool, or a constructed platform; the elevation was ritually significant, placing the practitioner above the ordinary ground level and closer to the divine realm. The seiðhjallr is mentioned in the sagas and in the *Lokasenna* (Loki's flyting), where Loki accuses Óðinn of practicing seiðr "on the platform" (á seiðhjalli).

2. **The Seiðstafr (Seiðr Staff).** The practitioner carries a staff — a long, decorated pole with a knob or basket at the top. The staff is the vǫlva's primary ritual instrument and the most consistent material correlate of seiðr practice. Archaeological finds of staff-like objects in women's burials — particularly the 9th-century Oseberg ship burial in Norway, which contained a richly decorated wooden staff with a bronze knob — have been interpreted as possible seiðr staves, though the identification is not certain.

3. **The Varðlokur (Calling Song).** The seiðr performance included singing — a special song or chant (varðlokur) that facilitated the practitioner's entry into trance. In the *Eiríks saga rauða*, the varðlokur is sung by a young woman who assists the vǫlva; the vǫlva herself may also have sung. The content of the varðlokur is unknown — no texts survive — but comparative evidence from Sámi joik (ritual singing) and from Finnish incantations suggests rhythmic, repetitive, and hypnotic vocalizations.

4. **The Trance and the Prophecy.** The goal of the seiðr performance was to enter an altered state of consciousness — a trance — in which the practitioner could perceive hidden knowledge (the fate of individuals, the future of the community, the location of lost objects or persons) and could manipulate fate (ørlǫg) — the "primal law" or "original destiny" that was laid down by the Norns at a person's birth. The trance was the seiðr's central experience: the practitioner's consciousness was altered, their perception extended beyond the ordinary, and they became a conduit for knowledge that was inaccessible to ordinary awareness.

**Seiðr and Gender — The Problem of Ergi:**

The social dynamics of seiðr are inseparable from the concept of *ergi* — the Norse term for unmanliness, sexual perversion, and gender transgression. A man who practiced seiðr was *argr* (unmanly, perverted) — a charge that, under the law codes, was so serious that it could be legally actionable: a man who called another *argr* could be sued for slander, and a man who was *argr* could be killed with impunity. The association of seiðr with *ergi* created a paradox: Óðinn, the greatest of the gods, practiced seiðr — but his practice was acknowledged as shameful for a male. This paradox has been interpreted as: (a) evidence that Óðinn was a gender-crossing figure who transcended ordinary categories; (b) a reflection of the tension between the Æsir (the warrior gods, associated with the male public sphere) and the Vanir (the fertility gods, associated with the female domestic sphere and with seiðr); (c) a patriarchal stigmatization of a practice originally associated with female authority and power.

The 2040 feminist and queer readings of seiðr (drawing on the work of scholars like Jenny Jochens, Carol Clover, and modern queer theorists) have reinterpreted the *ergi* prohibition not as a description of pre-Christian attitudes but as a projection of post-conversion Christian and patriarchal anxieties onto the pre-Christian past. In this reading, the original practitioners of seiðr were women who held significant social authority as seeresses, prophetesses, and ritual specialists — and the *ergi* accusation was a mechanism by which that authority was delegitimized and suppressed, first by Christian authorities (who condemned seiðr as witchcraft) and then by patriarchal social structures (which denied women ritual authority).

**Seiðr and Sámi Shamanism — The Comparative Question:**

The most productive comparative framework for understanding seiðr is the shamanic practice of the Sámi (the indigenous people of northern Scandinavia). The Sámi noaidi (shaman) practiced a form of ritual trance — accompanied by a drum, a special costume, and a calling song — that shares structural similarities with seiðr: the use of a staff (the Sámi drum-stick), the elevation of the practitioner (the Sámi shaman sometimes sat on a raised platform), the singing of ritual songs (the Sámi joik), and the entry into trance for divination and spiritual journeying. The historical contact between Norse and Sámi peoples in northern Scandinavia — a region of cultural exchange, intermarriage, and mutual influence — makes it plausible that seiðr and Sámi shamanism share a common origin or that seiðr was influenced by Sámi practice. The 2040 consensus is that the connection is real but that seiðr was not simply "Norse shamanism" — it was a distinctively Norse practice with its own social context, cosmological framework, and ritual structure.

## Lecture 6: Vǫlur and Spákonur — The Seeresses

The practitioners of seiðr were primarily women — the vǫlur (seeresses, prophetesses) and spákonur (wise women, seers) who traveled the land, performed seiðr for communities in need, and served as the human interface between the visible world and the hidden knowledge of fate.

**The Vǫlva — Office, Status, and Social Role:**

The vǫlva was a ritual specialist — a woman who performed seiðr professionally, traveling from farmstead to farmstead and from community to community, offering her services as a seer and prophetess. The *Eiríks saga rauða* account of Þórbjǫrg provides the most detailed description of a vǫlva's social role: she is received with respect by the household (a special high seat is prepared for her, a special meal is cooked), she is treated as an honored guest (not a servant or a dependent), and her prophecy is received as authoritative knowledge (the community acts on her predictions). The vǫlva's authority was personal, not institutional — she was not a priestess of a cult, not a functionary of a religious organization, but an individual practitioner whose authority derived from her own abilities and reputation.

The vǫlva's itinerancy — she traveled, she was not permanently attached to a community — is socially significant. In a society where identity was defined by kinship and place (the ætt and the óðal), the vǫlva was a figure who existed outside these structures. She was *heimlaus* — "homeless" — not in the sense of being destitute, but in the sense of not being anchored to a household or a kindred. This rootlessness gave her a liminal, threshold quality: she was both insider (she knew the community's fate) and outsider (she was not bound by its obligations), both welcome (her knowledge was needed) and unsettling (her freedom from social ties was itself transgressive).

**The Oseberg Burial — A Vǫlva's Grave?**

The Oseberg ship burial (c. 834 CE, Vestfold, Norway) is one of the most spectacular archaeological finds of the Viking Age. The burial contained two women — one aged about 50-60, the other about 25-30 — buried in a richly decorated ship with an extraordinary assemblage of goods: a ceremonial wagon, four sleighs, a bed, tapestries, textiles, a bucket with apples and blueberries, and — most importantly for our purposes — a wooden staff with a bronze knob, decorated with a snake's head. The staff has been interpreted as a possible seiðr staff (seiðstafr), and the older woman has been identified by some scholars as a vǫlva — a seeress buried with the instruments of her practice.

The identification is not certain. The Oseberg burial is elite in character — the quality and quantity of the grave goods suggest that the women were of high social rank, possibly royal — and the "vǫlva" interpretation may be reductive (a woman can be both a chieftain and a seiðr practitioner; the categories are not mutually exclusive). But the presence of the staff, the wagon (which may have been used in ritual processions), and the tapestries (which depict ritual scenes including a procession of figures carrying staves) makes the seiðr interpretation plausible. The 2040 consensus is that the Oseberg women were likely of ritual significance, whether or not they were "vǫlur" in the specific sense of traveling seeresses.

**Spá and Spákonur — The Gift of Sight:**

Related to seiðr but distinct from it was the practice of *spá* — prophecy, foretelling, the "sight" (spá is cognate with English "spy" and with the Latin *specio*, "I see"). A *spákona* (seer, wise woman) was a practitioner of spá — a woman who could see the future, perceive hidden things, and offer counsel based on her prophetic knowledge. The distinction between seiðr and spá is not always clear in the sources: some accounts use the terms interchangeably, while others suggest that spá was a less stigmatized, more socially acceptable form of prophetic practice (associated with "seeing" rather than "doing," with passive perception rather than active manipulation of fate).

The *Landnámabók* records several spákonur among the Icelandic settlers — women who were known for their prophetic abilities and whose predictions were treated as authoritative. The *Þiðranda þáttr ok Þórhalls* (The Tale of Þiðrandi and Þórhallr) — a short narrative preserved in the *Óláfs saga Tryggvasonar* — describes a spákona who predicts the coming of Christianity to Iceland, and the response of the dísir (the protective female spirits) to the new religion. The tale is a Christian composition, but it preserves the image of the spákona as a figure whose knowledge extends beyond the immediate community to the cosmic order itself.

**Vǫlur and the Land — The Ritual Geography of Prophecy:**

The vǫlva's practice was not confined to indoor performances. Several accounts describe outdoor seiðr — performances at specific landscape features (mounds, crossroads, hills, grave-mounds) that were associated with the dead and with the hidden powers of the land. The connection between the vǫlva and the landscape reflects the Norse understanding that the land itself was sacred, inhabited by spirits (landvættir), ancestors, and divine forces, and that the vǫlva's knowledge was rooted in her relationship with the land and its powers. The practice of *útiseta* — "sitting out" — involved spending the night at a crossroads, a grave-mound, or another liminal location, in order to receive visions or communicate with the dead. Útiseta is mentioned in the law codes as a prohibited practice, which testifies to its existence and its perceived danger.

## Lecture 7: Galdr and Runic Ritual — The Verbal and Written Magic

Alongside seiðr, the Norse ritual repertoire included two other forms of ritual practice: galdr (incantation, verbal magic) and runic ritual (the carving and activation of runes for magical purposes). These practices were less socially stigmatized than seiðr — they were associated with Óðinn (the god of poetry, runes, and wisdom) and with the male sphere of warfare, law, and craft.

**Galdr — The Chanted Spell:**

Galdr (plural galdri) was a form of verbal magic — a chanted, sung, or spoken incantation that was believed to have direct power over the world. The word *galdr* is related to the verb *gala* — "to crow, to chant" — and it suggests a vocal practice that was rhythmic, alliterative, and formulaic. The surviving examples of galdr in the textual record are:

- **The *Hávamál* spells** — the second half of the *Hávamál* (verses 138-165) contains a series of spells attributed to Óðinn, each beginning with "I know a spell..." (*ek veit einn*...). The spells cover a range of purposes: binding weapons (so they cannot wound), unbinding fetters, quenching fire, turning away an enemy's hatred, making a woman love you, and understanding the speech of birds. The *Hávamál* spells are literary compositions — they are not transcripts of actual magical practice — but they may preserve the structure and the formulaic language of genuine galdr.

- **The *Merseburger Zaubersprüche*** — two Old High German incantations preserved in a 9th-10th century manuscript from the monastery of Merseburg. The second charm describes the gods riding to the forest: Phol (Baldr?) and Wodan (Óðinn); Baldr's horse sprains its foot, and Sinthgunt, Sunna, Friia, and Fulla attempt to heal it; finally Wodan himself chants the healing charm, and the horse is cured. The charm is a *Besprechung* — a spoken healing formula — and it is the closest surviving analogue to a Norse galdr. The structure (listing a series of helpers who fail, followed by the chief god who succeeds) is a common pattern in Indo-European magical charms.

- **The Icelandic *galdrabók* tradition** — a post-medieval tradition of magical books containing spells, sigils, and ritual instructions. The *Galdrabók* (Icelandic Book of Magic, compiled in the 16th-17th centuries) contains spells for raising the dead, finding thieves, winning lawsuits, and causing harm to enemies. The *galdrabók* tradition is post-Christian and syncretic — it blends Norse pagan elements with Christian and Kabbalistic material — but it preserves the structure and the practical orientation of the galdr tradition.

**Runic Ritual — The Carved Power:**

Runes were not merely a writing system; they were also a ritual technology — a means of inscribing power, intention, and magical force onto objects, surfaces, and the landscape itself. The *Hávamál* (verses 139-141) describes Óðinn's acquisition of the runes as a self-sacrifice: he hangs on the World Tree (Yggdrasill) for nine nights, wounded by a spear, without food or drink, until the runes — "not known to any nor understood by any" — reveal themselves to him. The myth of the rune-acquisition establishes runes as a sacred script, a system of signs that connects the visible and invisible worlds.

The archaeological evidence for runic ritual includes:

- **Runic amulets and talismans** — objects inscribed with runes that were worn or carried for protection, healing, or magical effect. The Lindholm amulet (Skåne, Sweden, 4th-5th century) bears the inscription "ek erilaR sa wilagaz hateka" — "I, the erilaz (rune-master), am called Wila-gaz (the cunning one)" — followed by a sequence of runes (aaaaaaaazzznnn-bmuttt) that may be a magical formula rather than a linguistic text.

- **Runic curse inscriptions** — runestones that include explicit protective or punitive formulae. The Björketorp stone (Blekinge, Sweden, 7th century) bears the inscription: "I, the master of the runes, hide here runes of power. Incessantly plagued by maleficence, doomed to insidious death is he who breaks this monument." The curse inscriptions demonstrate that runes were believed to have the power to affect reality — to harm, protect, or bind.

- **The *Hávamál* runic spells** — verses 149-163 describe the carving of runes for specific purposes: carving runes on a horn (to detect poison), on the back of the hand (to win a woman's love), on the fingernails (to protect against harm). The *Sigrdrífumál* (The Lay of Sigrdrífa) provides more specific instructions: "Carve victory-runes on your sword's hilt, on the guard and the blade, and call twice on Tyr's name" (v. 6); "Carve ale-runes on the horn's rim, on the back of the hand, and on the nail — and let the horn bear the sign of the need-fire" (v. 7).

**The 2040 Understanding of Galdr and Runic Ritual:**

Current scholarship interprets galdr and runic ritual as aspects of a broader Norse magical practice that was verbal, written, and performative. The galdr was the spoken component — the incantation that activated the magical force; the runic inscription was the written component — the material inscription that fixed and directed the magical force; the ritual action (the carving, the chanting, the offering) was the performative component — the embodied practice that brought the magical intention into being. These three components were not separate practices but aspects of a single ritual system: the galdr was chanted while the runes were carved, and the carving was accompanied by ritual actions (the staining of the carved runes with blood or ochre, the offering of a sacrifice to activate the inscription).

## Lecture 8: Sacred Space — Hǫrgr, Hof, Vé, and the Natural Sacred

The Norse ritual landscape was not centered on temples in the Mediterranean or Christian sense. The primary sacred spaces of pre-Christian Scandinavia were natural features — groves, springs, hills, lakes, bogs, and distinctive landscape formations — that were identified as places of divine presence. The construction of dedicated cult buildings (hof) was a secondary development, and the existence of such buildings in the pre-Christian period is debated.

**The Hǫrgr — The Natural Altar:**

The *hǫrgr* (plural *hǫrgar*) was a cult site — a natural or constructed feature associated with divine presence and ritual activity. The *Vǫluspa* mentions the hǫrgr as one of the features of the primeval world: "The Æsir met at Idavollr, they who built hǫrgar and hof" (v. 7). The *Hyndluljóð* (The Lay of Hyndla) describes a hǫrgr built by the goddess Freyja's devotee Óttarr: "Ottarr made a hǫrgr for me, he piled up stones; now the stone has become glass, he reddened it with the fresh blood of oxen." The description of the hǫrgr as a pile of stones that was reddened with sacrificial blood is the most specific account of hǫrgr construction and use.

The hǫrgr was probably a cairn or a stone altar — a construction of natural stones piled or arranged to mark a sacred location. The reddening with blood connects the hǫrgr to the blót (the blood of sacrifice was the medium of consecration) and to the broader Norse practice of marking sacred space with sacrificial substances.

**The Hof — The Cult Building:**

The *hof* (temple, shrine) is described in several saga accounts as a dedicated cult building — a structure specifically constructed for ritual purposes. The *Eyrbyggja saga* provides the most detailed description: Þórólfr Mostrarskegg builds a hof at Hofstaðir in Iceland after arriving from Norway. The hof is a large building with a circular or dome-shaped sanctuary (a *hørgr* inside the *hof*), a raised platform where the cult images (goðamyndir) stand, and an altar (stallr) where the sacrificial blood is sprinkled. The hof is also described as having a "sacred place" (vé) around it — an area where violence and defecation were forbidden.

The archaeological evidence for hof buildings is contested. The site of Hofstaðir (Mývatn, Iceland), excavated 1992-2038, revealed a large longhouse (30+ meters) with a history of reuse and modification, but no clear evidence of a dedicated cult space distinct from the domestic living area. The site of Uppåkra (Skåne, Sweden), excavated 1996-2039, revealed a small building (6×6 meters) with a cult assemblage (gold foil figures, weapons, animal bones) that may be a hof — a dedicated cult building within a larger settlement. The 2040 consensus is that cult buildings probably existed in some regions and periods, but that they were not universal, and that most Norse ritual was performed outdoors — at natural sacred sites (groves, springs, hills) or at the assembly sites (þing) where ritual and legal, and political functions were combined.

**The Vé — The Sacred Enclosure:**

The term *vé* (plural *vée*) designated a sacred place — an area set apart from ordinary use and dedicated to the divine. The vé could be a natural feature (a grove, a hill, a spring), a constructed space (the area around a hof or a hǫrgr), or a conceptual boundary (the zone of prohibition around a cult site). The law codes describe the vé as a legally protected area — violence was prohibited within the vé, and violation of the vé's sanctity (by killing, by defecation, by improper behavior) was a serious offense.

The concept of the vé reflects the Norse understanding of sacred space as a boundary phenomenon: the sacred was defined not by its own qualities but by its difference from the profane. A grove was sacred not because it was inherently different from other groves, but because the community had designated it as *vé* — a place where ordinary rules were suspended and divine rules applied. The designation was presumably made through ritual — through the blót, the reddening with blood, the deposition of offerings — and the sanctity was maintained through the continued performance of ritual at the site.

**Liminal Sacred Space — Crossroads, Mounds, and Thresholds:**

Not all sacred spaces were established cult sites. The Norse ritual landscape included a category of liminal sacred spaces — places that were not permanently designated as sacred but that acquired temporary sacred significance through ritual use. These included:

- **Crossroads** — sites where roads met, associated with the dead and with divination. The practice of *útiseta* (sitting out at crossroads for prophecy) was associated with these locations.
- **Grave-mounds** — the burial mounds of ancestors, which were believed to be places where the dead could be contacted and where the living could seek the counsel of their forebears.
- **The threshold of the hall** — the doorway of the chieftain's hall was a ritual boundary: the space between the safe interior and the dangerous exterior, between the social order of the hall and the chaos of the world outside. Oaths were sometimes sworn at the threshold, and the doorway was the location where the groom carried his bride over the threshold in the marriage ritual.

## Lecture 9: Death Rituals — Ship Burials, Mound Burials, and the Journey to the Afterlife

The treatment of the dead was the most archaeologically visible aspect of Norse ritual practice. The Viking Age left a spectacular funerary record — ship burials, chamber graves, inhumation cemeteries, and cremation deposits — that provides direct evidence for the ritual practices surrounding death, burial, and the afterlife.

**The Ship Burial — The Vessel of the Dead:**

The most iconic Norse funerary practice is the ship burial — the interment of the dead in or on a ship, accompanied by grave goods, animals, and (in some cases) human sacrifices. The ship burial was not a universal practice; it was restricted to the elite (the construction or procurement of a ship was a major investment), and it was concentrated in specific regions (Norway, the Norse settlements in Scotland, and the Rus' territories in eastern Europe).

The most famous ship burials include:

- **Oseberg** (c. 834 CE, Vestfold, Norway) — Two women buried in a richly decorated ship with a wagon, sleighs, textiles, and animal sacrifices (horses, oxen, dogs). The Oseberg burial is the richest Viking Age grave find, and the ritual significance of the objects (particularly the staff, the wagon, and the tapestries) suggests a cult context (see Lecture 6).

- **Gokstad** (c. 900 CE, Vestfold, Norway) — A chieftain buried in a large ship with weapons, riding gear, gaming pieces, and the remains of horses, dogs, and a peacock. The Gokstad burial is less ritually elaborate than Oseberg but more "militarily" equipped — the dead man was prepared for a warrior's afterlife.

- **Sutton Hoo** (c. 625 CE, Suffolk, England) — Not Norse (Anglo-Saxon), but structurally similar to the Scandinavian ship burials and providing important comparative evidence. The Mound 1 burial contained a ship (27 meters long), weapons, armor, gold and silver objects, and imported luxuries — the grave of a king or a great warrior.

- **Ibn Fadlan's Rus' funeral** (922 CE, Volga River) — The most detailed contemporary account of a ship burial in progress. The dead chieftain's ship is drawn up on the riverbank; his weapons, clothing, and possessions are placed on the ship; animals (a dog, horses, cattle) are sacrificed and placed on the ship; a slave girl volunteers to accompany her master into death, is ritually prepared, and is strangled (with the assistance of the "Angel of Death," an old woman who supervises the funeral) before being placed on the ship; and the entire assemblage is set on fire. The account is graphic, detailed, and ethnographically precise — and it confirms that ship burial involved the actual burning of a ship with the dead, their possessions, and sacrificial victims.

**Cremation vs. Inhumation:**

The Viking Age saw a shift from cremation (the dominant funerary practice in the early Viking Age and the pre-Viking period) to inhumation (burial of the unburnt body, which became dominant in the 10th-11th centuries). The shift is partly explained by Christian influence (the Church required inhumation), but it may also reflect indigenous changes in belief — a shift from a conception of the afterlife that required the destruction of the body (to release the spirit) to one that required its preservation (for use in the afterlife).

**The Afterlife — Where Do the Dead Go?**

Norse conceptions of the afterlife were multiple and inconsistent — there was no single, uniform "Norse belief" about where the dead went. The sources describe several destinations:

- **Valhǫll (Valhalla)** — Óðinn's hall, where warriors who die in battle are received by the Valkyries, to fight by day and feast by night until Ragnarǫk. Valhǫll is the most famous Norse afterlife destination, but it was probably restricted to a specific subset of the dead — warriors who died in battle — and may not have been the primary afterlife belief for the majority of the population.

- **Hel** — the realm of the goddess Hel, where those who die of sickness or old age are received. Hel is described in the *Gylfaginning* as a grim but not a punitive realm — it is not the Norse equivalent of Christian Hell (the Christian concept of a punitive afterlife was mapped onto the Norse Hel by Christian authors, producing the modern confusion between the two).

- **Fólkvangr** — Freyja's field, where she receives half of the battle-dead (the other half go to Óðinn in Valhǫll). The *Grímnismál* (v. 14) describes Fólkvangr as the place where Freyja "chooses the slain" each day — a parallel to Óðinn's selection of warriors for Valhǫll.

- **The Mound** — the dead could also remain in or near their burial mound, where they continued to interact with the living — receiving offerings, providing counsel, and (in the case of the *draugr* — the animated corpse) sometimes physically harming the living. The mound-dwelling dead were the most accessible and the most ritually significant category of the dead: they were the ancestors who could be consulted, the forebears whose continued presence sustained the kindred's identity and claim to its land.

- **Helgafell** — "Holy Mountain" — a sacred mountain where the dead were believed to reside. The *Eyrbyggja saga* describes Helgafell as a place where the dead go to live with their kinsmen, and where the living should not defecate or look toward the mountain without washing. The Helgafell afterlife is a domestic, kinship-based conception of the afterlife — the dead continue their lives in a community of kin, in a place associated with the sacred landscape of the living community.

## Lecture 10: Ritual Objects and Material Culture

The material culture of Norse ritual — the objects used, worn, displayed, and deposited in ritual contexts — provides archaeological evidence that complements (and sometimes contradicts) the textual accounts.

**Key Ritual Objects:**

- **The Blóthorn (Sacrificial Horn)** — The ceremonial drinking horn, used for the ceremonial ale at the blót and the sumbel. Drinking horns are archaeologically common in elite Viking Age burials; the Oseberg burial included several decorated horns. The horn was both a practical vessel and a ritual object — the specific form of the horn (taken from a large animal) connected the drinker to the sacrificed animal whose horn was being used.

- **The Seiðstafr (Seiðr Staff)** — The vǫlva's staff, discussed in Lecture 6. The staff was the vǫlva's primary ritual instrument and the most distinctive material correlate of seiðr practice. Archaeological finds of staff-like objects in women's burials (Oseberg, Fyrkat, several Swedish finds) suggest that the staff was a real object, not a literary invention.

- **Guldgubbar (Gold Foil Figures)** — Small gold foil figures (2-3 cm tall) depicting embracing couples, single figures, or pairs of figures. Over 3,000 guldgubbar have been found, concentrated in southern Scandinavia (particularly at Sorte Muld on Bornholm, where over 2,500 were recovered). The guldgubbar are consistently found in cult contexts (at cult buildings, at hǫrgr sites, in the post-holes of cult structures) and are interpreted as votive offerings — small, inexpensive but precious objects dedicated to the gods, probably associated with the Vanir cult and with the sacred marriage (hieros gamos) of Freyr and Gerðr.

- **Mjǫllnir Amulets (Thor's Hammer)** — Small hammer-shaped amulets, typically made of silver or iron, found throughout the Viking Age world. Over 50 Mjǫllnir amulets have been recovered, concentrated in the 9th-10th centuries (the period of greatest tension between paganism and Christianity). The Mjǫllnir amulet is the most recognizable symbol of Norse paganism — it was worn as a protective amulet, used in ritual (the sign of the hammer was made over the sacrificial bowl at the blót, as described in *Hákonar saga góða*), and possibly served as a counter-symbol to the Christian cross. The 2040 interpretation emphasizes the Mjǫllnir as a multi-valent symbol — it represented Þórr's protection, the sanctification of the ritual, and (in the context of Christian competition) a declaration of pagan identity.

- **The Vǫluvefr (Seeress's Veil)** — The vǫlva's costume, as described in the *Eiríks saga rauða*: a blue-black cloak, a catskin hood, lambskin gloves, and a pouch containing ritual objects. The costume is a ritual uniform — it marks the wearer as a vǫlva, separates her from ordinary social roles, and equips her for the ritual performance. The archaeological correlates (the Oseberg textiles, the Fyrkat burial objects) suggest that the vǫlva's costume was real and distinctive, not a literary invention.

**The 2040 Material Culture Approach:**

The current archaeological approach to ritual objects emphasizes their *biography* — the life-history of the object from production to deposition. An object is not simply "ritual" or "secular"; it becomes ritual through use, through the contexts in which it is deployed, and through the meanings that are attached to it by its users and its observers. A drinking horn is a secular vessel when used for ordinary drinking; it becomes a ritual object when used at the sumbel; it becomes a grave good when deposited in a burial. The same object can move between categories, and the archaeological challenge is to identify the moments and the contexts in which the transition occurs.

## Lecture 11: The Christianization of Ritual — Continuity, Transformation, and Erasure

The conversion of Scandinavia to Christianity (roughly 950-1100 CE, varying by region) did not simply replace pagan ritual with Christian ritual. The process was complex, uneven, and characterized by three dynamics: **continuity** (the persistence of pagan practices under Christian forms), **transformation** (the modification of pagan practices to fit Christian frameworks), and **erasure** (the active suppression and destruction of pagan ritual).

**Continuity — The Pagan Within the Christian:**

Many Norse ritual practices persisted after the official conversion, often in modified form:

- **The Yule feast** became the Christmas feast, preserving the communal meal, the seasonal timing, and the ritual consumption of specific foods (the Yule ham = the Christmas ham). The Christian feast was layered onto the pagan blót, and the two traditions coexisted for centuries before the pagan elements were fully assimilated.

- **The sumbel** was partially transformed into the Christian banquet — the communal drinking, the toasting, and the oath-making persisted in modified form, stripped of their explicitly pagan references but retaining their social function.

- **The vetrnætr** (Winter Nights) became the feast of Saint Martin (Martinmas, November 11) in some regions, preserving the autumn slaughter and feast under a Christian saint's day.

- **The alfablót** — the private, domestic sacrifice to the elves — persisted in Norwegian and Swedish folk practice as the *alfablot* or *alfoffer* (elf-offering), a household ritual performed at specific times of year, well into the early modern period. The continuity is not direct — the folk practice is not identical to the Viking Age alfablót — but the persistence of the name, the seasonal timing, and the domestic context suggests a genuine continuity of practice.

**Transformation — The Redefinition of the Sacred:**

Christianization also transformed ritual practice by redefining the sacred landscape:

- **Sacred sites** (hǫrgar, vé) were Christianized by building churches on or near the pagan cult sites. The church at Trøndelag (Norway) was built on the site of the pagan blót described in *Hákonar saga góða*; the church at Uppsala was built near the pagan temple described by Adam of Bremen. The Christianization of sacred sites was not merely a practical strategy (using existing sacred spaces for the new religion) but a theological statement: the Christian god was more powerful than the pagan gods, and the proof was that the Christian church stood where the pagan cult site had been.

- **Sacred times** (the seasonal blóts) were replaced by Christian holy days, often at the same times of year. The process was not a simple replacement but a negotiation: the Church accommodated the seasonal timing of the old rituals (the Christmas feast at the time of the Yule blót) while redefining their meaning (the birth of Christ rather than the midwinter sacrifice to Óðinn).

- **Sacred practitioners** (the vǫlur, the goðar) were replaced by Christian priests and clergy. The vǫlva's role as seeress and ritual specialist was taken over by Christian saints and visionaries; the goði's role as cult leader and legal authority was taken over by the parish priest and the royal official.

**Erasure — The Suppression of Pagan Ritual:**

The most dramatic aspect of Christianization was the active suppression of pagan ritual:

- **The law codes** — the post-conversion law codes of Norway, Sweden, and Denmark explicitly prohibited pagan practices: the raising of hǫrgar, the practice of seiðr, the sitting out at crossroads, the eating of horse meat (the horse was a sacrificial animal associated with the blót, and the Christian prohibition on eating horse meat was specifically aimed at suppressing the blót feast). The Norwegian Gulaþingslǫg devotes several chapters to the prohibition of pagan practices, suggesting that they persisted despite official conversion.

- **The destruction of cult sites** — several saga accounts describe the physical destruction of pagan cult sites by Christian kings and missionaries. The *Óláfs saga Tryggvasonar* describes King Óláfr's destruction of the hof at Trøndelag and his forced conversion of the pagan chieftains; the *Adam of Bremen* account describes the destruction of the temple at Uppsala by the Christian king Inge (c. 1080 CE). The destruction was both practical (removing the physical infrastructure of pagan ritual) and symbolic (demonstrating the powerlessness of the pagan gods).

- **The stigmatization of seiðr** — the Christian Church identified seiðr as witchcraft and its practitioners as witches, applying the biblical prohibition on sorcery to the Norse practice. The *ergi* stigma that already attached to male seiðr practitioners was intensified and generalized: under Christian law, any practice of seiðr was a crime, regardless of the practitioner's gender. The Icelandic law codes (post-conversion) prescribe penalties for seiðr, and the *Íslendingasǫgur* depict seiðr practitioners as malevolent and dangerous — a literary tradition that may reflect post-conversion Christian attitudes rather than pre-Christian social reality.

**The 2040 Synthesis:** Current scholarship understands the Christianization of Norse ritual not as a clean break (pagan → Christian) but as a prolonged, uneven process of negotiation, accommodation, and resistance. Pagan practices did not simply disappear; they were transformed, hidden, and sometimes violently suppressed. The traces of pre-Christian ritual that survive in folk practice, in the archaeological record, and in the textual sources are the remnants of a ritual world that was largely — but never entirely — erased.

## Lecture 12: Modern Reconstruction — Ásatrú, Heathenry, and the Revival of Norse Ritual

The modern revival of Norse pagan practice — variously called Ásatrú (Icelandic, "faith in the Æsir"), Heathenry, Forn Sed ("Old Custom"), or simply "the Old Way" — is one of the most significant developments in the history of Norse ritual. Beginning in the 19th-century Romantic revival and accelerating in the 20th and 21st centuries, the modern practice of Norse ritual has created a living tradition that draws on — but is not identical to — the pre-Christian ritual world we have studied in this course.

**The History of the Modern Revival:**

- **19th-century Romantic nationalism** — the rediscovery of the Eddas and the sagas by European Romantics (the Grimm brothers, the Swedish Gothicism movement, the Danish and Norwegian national revivals) created a new interest in Norse paganism as a cultural heritage, but not as a living religious practice. The 19th-century revival was literary and political, not ritual.

- **Early 20th-century occultism** — the Germanic occult movements of the early 20th century (the Germanenorden, the Thule Society, the Armanen Orden) combined Norse mythology with esoteric and völkisch (ethnic nationalist) ideology. These movements were not reconstructions of pre-Christian ritual but syncretic inventions that used Norse symbols for political and occult purposes. The association of Norse symbols with German nationalism and, ultimately, with National Socialism, has left a dark legacy that continues to affect the perception of Norse pagan revival.

- **The 1970s Ásatrú revival** — the modern Ásatrú movement emerged in the early 1970s, independently in Iceland (where Ásatrúarfélagið, the Ásatrú Fellowship, was founded by Sveinbjörn Beinteinsson in 1972 and recognized by the Icelandic government as a religious organization in 1973) and in the United States (where the Ásatrú Free Assembly was founded by Stephen McNallen in 1974). The Icelandic movement was culturally rooted, non-political, and focused on the reconstruction of Icelandic pagan practice; the American movement was more diverse, more political, and more influenced by the American counterculture.

- **The 2020s-2040s: The Era of Reconstruction** — the current period is characterized by the increasing sophistication of ritual reconstruction, the growing collaboration between practitioners and scholars, and the continued diversification of the movement. The University of Yggdrasil's Norse Pagan Studies program (in which this course is taught) is itself a product of the 2040 synthesis of scholarship and practice.

**The Problem of Reconstruction:**

The modern reconstruction of Norse ritual faces a fundamental methodological problem: the sources are fragmentary, mediated by Christian authorship, and insufficient to reconstruct the ritual in its fullness. No amount of scholarly diligence can recover the experience of a 10th-century blót — the smell of the blood, the taste of the horse meat, the sound of the galdr, the feel of the cold winter night, the sense of the divine presence that the practitioners believed they were invoking. The form of the ritual can be reconstructed (with uncertainties); the experience cannot.

This has led to two broad approaches to modern practice:

1. **Reconstructionism** — the attempt to reconstruct pre-Christian ritual practice as closely as the sources allow, using the best available scholarship to guide the reconstruction. Reconstructionists prioritize historical accuracy and are wary of innovation. Their rituals are structured around the source accounts: the blót follows the sequence described in *Hákonar saga góða*; the sumbel follows the structure of the *Beowulf* sumbel and the saga accounts; the seiðr follows the *Eiríks saga rauða* description. The reconstructionist approach acknowledges that the sources are incomplete and that some elements must be inferred or invented, but it insists that the inference be guided by evidence, not by personal preference.

2. **Revivalism** — the attempt to create a living religious practice inspired by, but not strictly bound to, the pre-Christian sources. Revivalists prioritize the living experience of the ritual and are willing to innovate, adapt, and create new practices that respond to the needs and circumstances of modern practitioners. Their rituals may incorporate elements from other traditions (e.g., Wiccan circle-casting, Buddhist meditation, Jungian psychological techniques) alongside the Norse material. The revivalist approach acknowledges that strict reconstruction is impossible (the sources are too fragmentary) and that a living tradition must evolve to survive.

The 2040 consensus in the academic community — and in the University of Yggdrasil's program — is that both approaches are valid and that the choice between them is a matter of personal and communal practice, not of scholarly correctness. The scholar's role is to provide the best possible understanding of what the sources tell us about pre-Christian ritual; the practitioner's role is to decide how to use that understanding in the service of a living faith.

**The Ethics of Modern Practice:**

The modern practice of Norse ritual raises ethical questions that did not exist (or existed differently) in the pre-Christian period:

- **Animal sacrifice** — the blót traditionally involved the sacrifice of animals. Modern practitioners disagree on whether animal sacrifice is appropriate, legal, or ethical. Icelandic Ásatrú does not practice animal sacrifice; some American Heathen groups do, using farm animals that are subsequently consumed. The ethical debate involves questions of animal welfare, religious freedom, cultural authenticity, and the appropriateness of transplanting a 10th-century practice into a 21st-century context.

- **Cultural appropriation** — the use of Norse symbols and rituals by people who are not of Scandinavian descent raises questions of cultural appropriation. The 2040 consensus in the Ásatrú community is that Norse paganism is a religious practice, not an ethnic identity, and that anyone who sincerely practices it is welcome — but the debate continues, particularly around the use of Norse symbols by far-right and white nationalist groups.

- **The far-right appropriation of Norse symbols** — the use of Mjǫllnir, runes, and other Norse symbols by white supremacist and neo-Nazi groups remains a significant problem for the Ásatrú and Heathen communities. The 2040 response has been organized and sustained: anti-racist Heathen organizations (the Troth, Heathens Against Hate, the Declaration of Deeds) have publicly condemned the racist appropriation of Norse symbols and have worked to dissociate Norse paganism from white nationalism. The University of Yggdrasil's Norse Pagan Studies program explicitly affirms that Norse paganism is inclusive, non-racist, and open to practitioners of all backgrounds.
