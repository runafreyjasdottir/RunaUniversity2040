# NP203: Snorri's Edda & Euhemerism
## Bachelor of Science in Norse Pagan Studies — University of Yggdrasil, 2040

**Credits:** 4
**Description:** The Prose Edda of Snorri Sturluson (c. 1220-1241) is the single most important source for Norse mythology — and the most problematic. Written two centuries after the Christian conversion of Iceland, by a Christian politician and historian, the Edda presents the Norse gods as historical human beings who were later worshipped as divinities (the euhemerist framework). This course examines Snorri's Edda as both a work of scholarship and a work of literature, the euhemerist tradition in Norse historiography, and the profound implications of euhemerism for the reconstruction of pre-Christian Norse religion.

## Lecture 1: Snorri Sturluson — The Man and His World

Snorri Sturluson (1179-1241) was the most powerful Icelander of his generation — a chieftain, a lawyer, a poet, a historian, and a politician whose ambitions ultimately cost him his life. He was born into the Sturlungar family, one of the great chieftain clans of the Icelandic Commonwealth, at a time when the Commonwealth was in crisis: the old system of rival chieftaincies was collapsing into violent factional conflict, and the Norwegian king was extending his influence over Icelandic politics.

**Snorri's Life:**

Snorri was born at Hvammr in western Iceland, the son of Sturla Þórðarson and the foster-son of Jón Loftsson — the most powerful chieftain in Iceland and the grandson of a bishop. The fosterage was both a political arrangement (the Sturlungar were rising, and Jón Loftsson's patronage secured their position) and an educational one: Jón Loftsson's household at Oddi was a center of learning, and Snorri received an education in law, poetry, history, and Latin scholarship that was exceptional even by the standards of the Icelandic elite.

Snorri's adult life was defined by two conflicting ambitions: the accumulation of personal power in Iceland, and the maintenance of Icelandic independence from Norway. He was elected Lawspeaker (lǫgsǫgumaðr) of the Alþingi twice (1215-1218, 1222-1231), the highest elected office in the Commonwealth. He traveled to Norway in 1218-1220, where he was received by King Hákon Hákonarson and the Earl Skúli Bárðarson, and was appointed a lendr maðr (royal retainer) — an arrangement that made him, in effect, an agent of the Norwegian crown in Iceland. The appointment was controversial: Snorri's rival, Bishop Guðmundr Arason, accused him of selling Icelandic independence for Norwegian honors.

Snorri's political career was marked by shifting alliances, double-dealing, and a consistent failure to commit to any side decisively. He supported first the Norwegian king, then the rebellious Earl Skúli, then the king again. He accumulated chieftaincies and church benefices through strategic marriages (his own and his daughters') and through force. He was, by the assessment of the sagas and of modern historians, a brilliant, unscrupulous, and ultimately ineffective politician — a man who understood power but could not wield it consistently enough to survive.

In 1241, after years of declining influence and repeated failures to obey the Norwegian king's commands, Snorri was assassinated in his cellar at Reykholt by his former son-in-law Gizurr Þorvaldsson and a band of armed men. The killing was probably carried out on the orders of King Hákon, who had lost patience with Snorri's unreliability. Snorri's last words, according to the *Íslendinga saga* of Sturla Þórðarson (Snorri's nephew), were: "Eigi skal höggva" — "Do not strike."

**Snorri's Literary Output:**

Snorri's literary achievements are inseparable from his political career. The Prose Edda was composed during his most productive period (c. 1220-1230), between his first and second terms as Lawspeaker. The *Heimskringla* — his history of the Norwegian kings from mythological origins to 1177 — was probably composed in the same period. Both works are products of a mind that was simultaneously antiquarian (preserving the past) and political (using the past to understand — and perhaps to manipulate — the present).

The Edda was not Snorri's only contribution to Icelandic literature. He is also credited with the authorship of *Egils saga* (the Saga of Egill Skallagrímsson), one of the greatest of the Íslendingasǫgur, though the attribution is debated. If Snorri did write *Egils saga*, it demonstrates a literary range that is astonishing: the Edda is a systematic, encyclopedic work of mythological scholarship; the *Heimskringla* is a critical, narrative history; and *Egils saga* is a psychologically acute, dramatically powerful work of fiction.

**The Problem of Snorri:**

For students of Norse paganism, Snorri presents a fundamental problem. He is our single most important source for Norse mythology — the Prose Edda provides the most systematic, detailed, and coherent account of the Norse gods, their cosmology, and their mythology that survives from the medieval period. Without Snorri, we would have the Poetic Edda (a collection of mythological and heroic poems that is far less systematic and far more difficult to interpret), a handful of skaldic poems with mythological references, and the scattered references in the sagas and the historical works of other authors. With Snorri, we have a coherent narrative of the Norse mythological world.

But Snorri was a Christian, writing two centuries after the conversion, and his account is shaped by his Christian worldview in ways that are not always obvious. The Prose Edda's framing narrative — the euhemerist prologue in which the gods are presented as human beings who migrated from Troy to Scandinavia and were later mistaken for divinities — is a Christian interpretive framework that Snorri imposes on the Norse mythological material. The question of how much of Snorri's account reflects genuine pre-Christian tradition, and how much is his own invention or interpretation, is the central problem of Eddic scholarship and the central problem of this course.

## Lecture 2: The Prose Edda — Structure, Purpose, and Audience

The Prose Edda is not a single, unified work but a compilation of four distinct sections, each with its own purpose, audience, and relationship to the Norse mythological tradition. Understanding the Edda's structure is essential for evaluating its reliability as a source for pre-Christian religion.

**The Four Sections:**

1. **The Prologue (Formáli).** A brief narrative that establishes the euhemerist framework: the gods were originally human beings — descendants of the Trojan prince Þórr (Thor), who migrated northward from the Mediterranean world after the fall of Troy. Þórr's son, Óðinn, traveled through Denmark, Sweden, and Norway, establishing dynasties and receiving divine honors from the people he ruled. The gods' divine status was thus a mistake — the result of the Scandinavians' ignorance of their rulers' human origins. The Prologue is a Christian document, drawing on the Trojan-origin tradition (a common medieval European convention for establishing the antiquity and dignity of a nation) and on the euhemerist principle (the gods were great men, deified by popular error).

2. **Gylfaginning (The Deluding of Gylfi).** The longest and most famous section of the Edda — a narrative dialogue in which King Gylfi of Sweden, disguised as an old man named Gangleri, visits the hall of the Æsir to learn the secrets of their power. He is received by three figures — Hárr (High), Jafnhárr (Equally High), and Þriði (Third) — who answer his questions about the gods, the creation of the world, the cosmic order, and the final battle of Ragnarǫk. The dialogue format allows Snorri to present a systematic, encyclopedic account of Norse mythology while maintaining the narrative fiction that Gylfi is being "deluded" (gylfaginning = "the deluding of Gylfi") by the Æsir's magical illusions.

3. **Skáldskaparmál (The Language of Poetry).** A compendium of poetic diction — the kennings (metaphorical circumlocutions) and heiti (poetic synonyms) that constituted the technical vocabulary of skaldic poetry. The Skáldskaparmál is organized as a dialogue between Ægir (the sea god) and Bragi (the god of poetry), in which Bragi explains the mythological references behind the kennings: "Why is gold called 'Óðinn's laughter'? Because..." Each kenning is a miniature mythological narrative, and the Skáldskaparmál is therefore a secondary source for mythology — it provides mythological information that is not in the Gylfaginning but that is embedded in the technical apparatus of skaldic poetry.

4. **Háttatal (The Catalog of Metres).** A technical treatise on skaldic meter — 102 stanzas, each in a different metrical form, with Snorri's prose commentary explaining the rules of each meter. The Háttatal is the least mythologically interesting section of the Edda, but it is essential for understanding Snorri's purpose: the Edda was not written as a work of mythology but as a *poetics* — a textbook for the composition of skaldic poetry, which required knowledge of the mythological references that constituted the kennings.

**The Purpose of the Edda:**

Snorri's stated purpose in the Edda was not to preserve Norse mythology for its own sake but to preserve the art of skaldic poetry. Skaldic poetry — the elaborate, allusive, metrically complex court poetry of the Norse world — was in decline in Snorri's time. The old chieftain courts that had patronized skalds (court poets) were disappearing, and the new Norwegian court, influenced by continental European culture, preferred the simpler meters of troubadour poetry and ecclesiastical verse. The kenning system — the metaphorical vocabulary of skaldic poetry — was becoming incomprehensible to audiences who no longer knew the mythological stories that the kennings referred to.

Snorri's Edda was an attempt to arrest this decline: by providing a systematic account of Norse mythology (Gylfaginning), a compendium of the mythological references behind the kennings (Skáldskaparmál), and a technical manual of skaldic meter (Háttatal), Snorri aimed to give future poets the knowledge they needed to compose skaldic verse. The Edda was a preservation project — not the preservation of religion (Snorri was a Christian, and Norse paganism was dead as a living religion in 13th-century Iceland), but the preservation of a literary art form that was dependent on religious knowledge that was no longer widely shared.

**The Implications for Source Criticism:**

The Edda's purpose as a poetics has important implications for source criticism:

- **Mythological accuracy vs. poetic utility.** Snorri's primary concern was not with the accurate preservation of pre-Christian mythology but with the provision of mythological information that was useful for the composition of skaldic poetry. If a mythological detail was irrelevant to the kenning system, Snorri might omit it, simplify it, or distort it. If a kenning required a mythological story that Snorri did not know from genuine tradition, he might invent one.

- **Systematization vs. organic tradition.** Pre-Christian Norse mythology was probably not a systematic, coherent, internally consistent narrative — no living mythology is. Snorri's Gylfaginning presents the Norse mythological world as a coherent system, with a clear cosmological structure (the nine worlds), a defined pantheon (the Æsir and Vanir), and a narrative arc (creation → crisis → Ragnarǫk). This systematization may be Snorri's own contribution — the product of a systematic mind imposing order on a tradition that was originally more fluid, more regional, and more contradictory.

- **Christian framing vs. pagan content.** The Gylfaginning is framed by the euhemerist narrative (the gods are deluding Gylfi with illusions) and by the Christian prologue (the gods were human beings). This framing does not necessarily corrupt the mythological content — Snorri may have preserved genuine traditions within the Christian frame — but it does mean that the Edda must be read as a work of Christian antiquarianism, not as a direct transmission of pre-Christian religious knowledge.

## Lecture 3: Euhemerism — The Theory of Deified Men

Euhemerism — the theory that the gods were originally human beings who were deified by later generations — is the interpretive framework that Snorri imposes on the Norse mythological tradition in the Prologue to the Prose Edda. The theory takes its name from Euhemerus of Messene (c. 300 BCE), a Greek mythographer who wrote a now-lost work called *Hiera Anagraphe* (The Sacred Record), in which he described a fictional voyage to an Indian ocean island where he found an inscription recording that Zeus and the other Greek gods had originally been great kings who were worshipped as gods after their deaths.

**The Euhemerist Tradition in Medieval Europe:**

Euhemerism was a common interpretive strategy in medieval Christian and Islamic theology for dealing with pagan gods. If the gods were not real divinities (as monotheism required), then they must have been something else — and the most theologically acceptable "something else" was human beings. The euhemerist interpretation served two functions:

1. **Apologetic.** By reducing the gods to human beings, euhemerism removed the theological threat that polytheism posed to monotheism. The pagan gods were not competing divinities; they were dead humans who had been mistakenly worshipped. The error of paganism was not the worship of real but inferior powers but the worship of mere men — an error that could be corrected by the knowledge of the true God.

2. **Historical.** By providing a historical narrative for the origin of the gods, euhemerism integrated pagan mythology into the universal history of the Christian world. The gods were not supernatural beings but historical figures — kings, warriors, explorers — whose stories could be placed alongside the stories of the Bible and the classical tradition in a single, unified account of the human past.

**Snorri's Euhemerism — The Trojan Origin:**

Snorri's Prologue presents the Norse gods as descendants of the Trojan prince Þórr (not the god Þórr — the Prologue distinguishes between the historical Þórr and the god who was later named after him). The historical Þórr, according to Snorri, was a warrior from Thrace (the region of northern Greece associated with the Trojan tradition) who traveled northward after the fall of Troy, conquering lands and founding dynasties. His descendant Óðinn traveled further north, through Germany and Denmark, to Sweden, where he established the temple at Uppsala and was worshipped as a god by the Swedes. Óðinn's sons — Vili and Vé — founded the dynasties of Denmark and Norway.

The Trojan-origin narrative is not Snorri's invention; it is a common medieval European convention. The Franks claimed descent from the Trojan hero Francus; the British claimed descent from the Trojan Brutus (a tradition recorded by Geoffrey of Monmouth in the 12th century); the Romans claimed descent from the Trojan Aeneas (the most ancient and prestigious of the Trojan-origin claims). By placing the Norse gods in the Trojan tradition, Snorri was not making a historical claim but a cultural one: the Norse tradition is as ancient and as prestigious as the classical and biblical traditions that dominated medieval European learning.

**The *Ynglinga saga* Euhemerism — The Historical Óðinn:**

Snorri's *Ynglinga saga* (the opening section of the *Heimskringla*) provides a more detailed euhemerist account than the Prologue. Óðinn is presented as a historical king — a warrior and sorcerer from "Asia" (Asaland, the "land of the Æsir," identified with the region around the Tanais/Don River) who migrated westward with his people, conquering territory in northern Germany, Denmark, and Sweden. The *Ynglinga saga* describes Óðinn's magical practices (seiðr, galdr, rune-carving) as historical skills of a historical person, and his divine attributes (the ravens Huginn and Muninn, the spear Gungnir, the eight-legged horse Sleipnir) as historical details that were later misinterpreted as supernatural.

The *Ynglinga saga* euhemerism is more elaborate and more sustained than the Prologue's. It presents the Norse gods as a historical ruling dynasty — the Æsir — who established themselves in Scandinavia and were deified by their subjects. The Vanir — the other group of Norse gods — are presented as a rival people (the "Vanir" from "Vanaland" = Vanaheimr) with whom the Æsir fought a war and then made peace, exchanging hostages (Njǫrðr, Freyr, and Freyja went to the Æsir; Hœnir and Mímir went to the Vanir). The war between the Æsir and Vanir, which in the mythological sources is a cosmic conflict between two divine families, is in the *Ynglinga saga* a historical war between two peoples.

**The Implications of Euhemerism for Source Criticism:**

Euhemerism has profound implications for the reconstruction of pre-Christian Norse religion:

1. **The gods were real.** Euhemerism confirms that the gods were "real" in the sense that people believed in them and worshipped them — but it reduces their reality from divine to human. The question for modern scholars is whether the euhemerist reduction reflects a pre-Christian understanding (did the Norse themselves think of their gods as deified ancestors?) or a Christian imposition (did Snorri impose the euhemerist framework on a tradition that understood the gods as genuinely divine?).

2. **The myths are historical narratives.** If the gods were human, then the myths about them are stories about historical events, distorted by time and popular imagination. This reduction of myth to distorted history is the fundamental euhemerist move — and it is one that modern scholars reject, not because the gods were "really divine" but because the euhemerist reduction misses the point: myths are not failed history but successful meaning-making.

3. **The ritual practices are errors.** If the gods were human, then the rituals addressed to them were based on a mistake — the worship of mere men as if they were divine. This is the apologetic function of euhemerism, and it is the reason why Snorri includes detailed descriptions of pagan ritual (the blót at Trøndelag in *Hákonar saga góða*, the cult practices in *Ynglinga saga*) in a work that denies the divinity of the gods being worshipped. The descriptions are ethnographic — "this is what the pagans did, in their error" — and they serve the Christian purpose of documenting the practices that the Christian faith has superseded.

## Lecture 4: Gylfaginning — The Deluding of Gylfi

Gylfaginning ("The Deluding of Gylfi") is the heart of the Prose Edda — the section that provides the most systematic and detailed account of Norse mythology. Understanding its structure, its narrative strategy, and its relationship to the Poetic Edda is essential for evaluating its reliability as a source for pre-Christian religion.

**The Frame Narrative:**

Gylfi, a king of Sweden, hears that the Æsir — a group of mysterious, powerful beings — have come to Scandinavia from a distant land. Wishing to learn the secrets of their power, Gylfi travels to their hall, disguised as an old man named Gangleri ("Wanderer"). At the hall, he is received by three figures — Hárr (High), Jafnhárr (Equally High), and Þriði (Third) — who sit on three thrones. Gangleri is challenged to prove his wisdom by asking questions, and Hárr, Jafnhárr, and Þriði answer each question in turn.

The frame narrative serves several functions:

- **It establishes the dialogue format.** The question-and-answer structure allows Snorri to present mythological information systematically, organized by topic (creation, the gods, the cosmic order, Ragnarǫk), without the need for a continuous narrative thread.

- **It establishes the euhemerist frame.** The title — "the deluding of Gylfi" — implies that the Æsir are deceiving Gylfi with illusions. This is the euhemerist interpretation in narrative form: the Æsir are human beings who use magic to deceive a king into believing they are gods. The mythological information in the dialogue is, within the frame narrative, a deception — a "deluding" — not a revelation of genuine divine knowledge.

- **It establishes the Christian frame.** The three figures — Hárr, Jafnhárr, Þriði — are described as "kings" or "chieftains," not as gods. The trinitarian structure (three figures, three thrones) may be a deliberate echo of the Christian Trinity — a way of suggesting that the Æsir's "trinity" is a human imitation of the divine original.

**The Content of Gylfaginning:**

Gylfaginning covers the following topics, in roughly this order:

1. **The creation of the world** from the body of the primordial giant Ymir, who was killed by Óðinn and his brothers Vili and Vé. The world is made from Ymir's flesh (earth), bones (mountains), blood (sea), skull (sky), and brains (clouds). The dwarfs are created from the maggots in Ymir's flesh. The first humans — Askr (Ash) and Embla (Elm?) — are created by Óðinn, Vili, and Vé from driftwood found on the seashore.

2. **The World Tree** — Yggdrasill, the ash tree that connects the nine worlds. Its three roots extend to the world of the Æsir (Asgarðr), the world of the frost-giants (Jǫtunheimar), and the world of the dead (Hel). The well of Urðr (Urðarbrunnr) is at the base of the Æsir root; the well of Mímir (Mímisbrunnr) is at the giants' root; the well Hvergelmir ("the roaring cauldron") is at the Hel root.

3. **The gods and their attributes.** Snorri provides a systematic catalogue of the Æsir and the Vanir, with brief descriptions of each: Óðinn (wisdom, war, poetry, death), Þórr (strength, protection, thunder), Freyr (fertility, prosperity, peace), Freyja (love, beauty, seiðr, death), Týr (war, justice), Baldr (beauty, innocence, tragedy), Loki (trickery, transformation, chaos), and others. The catalogue is more systematic than anything in the Poetic Edda, and it may reflect Snorri's own organizational effort rather than a pre-Christian system.

4. **The death of Baldr.** The most extended narrative in Gylfaginning — the story of Baldr's death, killed by a mistletoe spear thrown by the blind god Höðr at Loki's instigation; the failed attempt to rescue Baldr from Hel (the giantess Þǫkk, probably Loki in disguise, refuses to weep for Baldr, thereby preventing his return); and the binding of Loki as punishment.

5. **Ragnarǫk** — the final battle, in which the gods and their enemies destroy each other and the world is consumed in fire and flood. Snorri's account of Ragnarǫk is the most detailed version of the eschatological myth, and it draws on the *Vǫluspa* and the *Vafþrúðnismál* for its details.

6. **The renewal of the world** — after Ragnarǫk, a new world rises from the waters, green and fair. Baldr returns from Hel; Hœnir survives; the sons of Þórr (Móði and Magni) inherit their father's hammer; and two human survivors (Líf and Lífþrasir) emerge from Hoddmímis holt to repopulate the earth.

**Gylfaginning and the Poetic Edda — The Question of Sources:**

Snorri quotes extensively from the Poetic Edda in Gylfaginning — he cites or alludes to at least 20 Eddic poems, including the *Vǫluspa*, *Hávamál*, *Vafþrúðnismál*, *Grímnismál*, and *Lokasenna*. These quotations serve as source citations — they anchor Snorri's prose account in the authority of the poetic tradition. But the relationship between Snorri's prose and the poetic sources is complex:

- **Snorri sometimes adds details not in the poems.** The creation of the world from Ymir's body is described briefly in the *Vǫluspa* and the *Grímnismál*, but Snorri's account is far more detailed and specific than anything in the poems. The additional details may come from oral traditions that Snorri knew but that are not preserved in the surviving poems, or they may be Snorri's own elaborations.

- **Snorri sometimes contradicts the poems.** The *Vǫluspa* describes the creation of the dwarfs as a separate act from the creation of the world; Snorri describes the dwarfs as arising from the maggots in Ymir's flesh — a detail not in the *Vǫluspa* but consistent with the general Norse principle that the world is made from the body of a primordial being.

- **Snorri sometimes systematizes what the poems leave ambiguous.** The Poetic Edda does not provide a clear, consistent account of the Norse pantheon — the gods' attributes, relationships, and spheres of influence are presented inconsistently across different poems. Snorri's catalogue is an attempt to impose consistency on this variation, but the consistency may be his own contribution rather than a reflection of a pre-Christian system.

The 2040 scholarly approach to Gylfaginning is to read it alongside the Poetic Edda, treating Snorri's prose as a commentary on — and an interpretation of — the poetic tradition, not as an independent witness to pre-Christian belief. Where Snorri's account agrees with the Poetic Edda, it probably reflects genuine tradition; where it adds details not in the poems, it may reflect oral traditions or Snorri's own elaboration; where it contradicts the poems, it should be treated with caution.

## Lecture 5: Skáldskaparmál — The Mythological Encyclopedia in Disguise

The Skáldskaparmál ("The Language of Poetry") is the second-longest section of the Prose Edda and the most important source for Norse mythology outside of Gylfaginning. It is organized as a dialogue between Ægir (the sea-god, here representing the audience of poetry) and Bragi (the god of poetry), in which Bragi explains the mythological references behind the kennings — the metaphorical circumlocutions that constitute the technical vocabulary of skaldic verse.

**The Kenning System:**

A kenning is a metaphoric substitution — a phrase that replaces a noun with a descriptive circumlocution. For example: "whale-road" (hvalróð) = sea; "tree of the shield" (skjaldar tré) = warrior; "Óðinn's laughter" (Óðins hlátr) = poetry (because Óðinn stole the mead of poetry, which is therefore "his laughter" or "his drink"). The kennings are dense, allusive, and often opaque — they require the audience to know the mythological stories that they refer to in order to decode them.

The Skáldskaparmál's method is to take each kenning and explain its mythological basis. The result is a compendium of mythological narratives, many of which are not found in Gylfaginning or in the Poetic Edda:

- **The story of the mead of poetry.** Óðinn steals the mead of poetry from the giant Suttungr, who had received it from the dwarfs Fjalarr and Galarr (who had brewed it from the blood of the wise man Kvasir, whom they had killed). Óðinn bores a hole in the mountain where the mead is stored, enters in the form of a snake, seduces the giant's daughter Gunnlǫð, drinks the mead, and escapes in the form of an eagle. The story is the basis for the kenning "Óðinn's drink/laugh/boon" = poetry.

- **The story of Þórr's journey to Útgarða-Loki.** Þórr and Loki travel to the hall of the giant Útgarða-Loki, where Þórr is challenged to contests: lifting the Midgard Serpent (disguised as a cat), emptying a drinking horn (connected to the ocean), and wrestling Elli (Old Age, disguised as an old woman). Þórr loses every contest — a narrative that illustrates the limits of even divine strength. The story is not in Gylfaginning; it appears only in the Skáldskaparmál and in the *Gylfaginning*-independent tradition.

- **The story of Freyr and Gerðr.** Freyr sees the giantess Gerðr from Óðinn's high seat (Hliðskjálf), falls in love with her, and sends his servant Skírnir to woo her with threats and gifts. Skírnir's threats include: Gerðr will be cursed to live with a three-headed giant in the underworld, never to taste mead or to know the company of men; she will be tormented by madness and misery; and she will be "the most reprehensible of all beings." Gerðr yields. The story is the basis for the kenning "Gerðr's husband" = Freyr.

- **The story of the burning of Baldr's ship (Hringhorni).** The giantess Hyrrokkin is summoned to push Baldr's funeral ship into the sea, because the gods are unable to move it. Hyrrokkin arrives riding a wolf with venomous snakes for reins, and she pushes the ship with such force that the ground catches fire. Þórr is about to kill her for this presumption, but the other gods intervene. The story is not in Gylfaginning's account of Baldr's funeral.

**Skáldskaparmál as Mythological Source:**

The Skáldskaparmál is an indirect mythological source — it provides mythological information as a byproduct of its primary purpose (explaining kennings). This indirectness has both advantages and disadvantages for source criticism:

- **Advantage: The myths are presented in a utilitarian context, not a theological one.** Snorri is not trying to construct a coherent mythological system (as in Gylfaginning) but to explain the stories behind specific poetic phrases. This utilitarian context may preserve mythological details that Snorri would have simplified or omitted in a systematic account.

- **Disadvantage: The myths are presented incompletely and unsystematically.** A kenning requires only a brief narrative explanation, not a full account. The Skáldskaparmál often provides fragments of stories — enough to explain the kenning, but not enough to reconstruct the full myth. The story of the mead of poetry, for example, is told in compressed form in the Skáldskaparmál; the fuller version is in the *Hávamál* (verses 104-110) and in Gylfaginning.

- **Advantage: The kennings are conservative.** The kenning system is a technical apparatus that resists innovation — a kenning that has been established by tradition is used by subsequent poets regardless of whether they know the story behind it. This conservatism means that the kennings may preserve mythological references that are older than the prose accounts that explain them. A 10th-century skald might use the kenning "Óðinn's laughter" for poetry without knowing (or caring about) the specific story of the mead's theft — but the kenning itself testifies to the existence of the story at the time the kenning was coined.

## Lecture 6: Snorri's Inventions — What Did He Make Up?

The most controversial question in Eddic scholarship is: how much of the Prose Edda is Snorri's own invention? The question is unanswerable in its strongest form (we cannot know what Snorri knew from oral tradition that is not preserved in any other source), but it can be approached through careful comparison with the Poetic Edda and with other independent witnesses.

**Probable Inventions or Elaborations:**

1. **The creation from Ymir's body — the specific details.** The general idea that the world was created from the body of a primordial being is attested in the *Vǫluspa* and the *Grímnismál*, but the specific details (bones = mountains, teeth = rocks, skull = sky, brains = clouds, blood = sea) are more elaborate in Snorri than in the poems. Snorri may have elaborated a brief poetic reference into a detailed prose narrative.

2. **The three brothers Óðinn, Vili, Vé.** The names Vili and Vé appear only in Snorri's account of the creation; they are not in the Poetic Edda's creation narrative. The *Vǫluspa* describes the creation as the work of "the holy gods" (ginnheilug goð) and "the Æsir" (ǫsir), without specifying which gods. Snorri's identification of the creators as Óðinn and his two brothers may be his own systematization — a way of giving names to the unnamed creators of the poems.

3. **The classification of the gods into Æsir and Vanir.** The distinction between the Æsir and the Vanir is present in the Poetic Edda (the *Vǫluspa* describes the war between them), but Snorri's systematic classification — these gods are Æsir, these are Vanir, these are other — may be his own organizational effort. The poems do not consistently classify the gods; some figures (Njǫrðr, Freyr, Freyja) are associated with the Vanir in some poems but not in others.

4. **The detailed cosmological geography.** Snorri's description of the nine worlds, their locations, and their connections is more systematic than anything in the Poetic Edda. The *Grímnismál* provides a partial cosmological catalogue, but it is a poetic list, not a systematic geography. Snorri's systematization may be genuine (reflecting a pre-Christian cosmological system) or it may be his own construction (imposing geographical order on poetic references that were never intended as a map).

5. **The narrative of the creation of the first humans from driftwood.** The *Vǫluspa* describes the creation of Askr and Embla by "three gods" (þrír rostu, "three descended" — Óðinn, Hœnir, and Lóðurr, according to some readings), but the detail that they were made from driftwood on the seashore is Snorri's addition. The driftwood detail is a reasonable inference (wood is a natural material for creation in a Norse context; the names Askr "Ash" and Embla "Elm(?)" suggest tree-wood), but it is not in the poetic source.

**Probable Genuine Traditions:**

1. **The death of Baldr.** The core narrative (Baldr is killed by a mistletoe spear thrown by Höðr at Loki's instigation; the gods attempt to rescue Baldr from Hel; the rescue fails because one being refuses to weep) is attested in the Poetic Edda (*Baldrs draumar*, the *Vǫluspa* in another tradition) and in the Gesta Danorum of Saxo Grammaticus (a Latin history of Denmark, c. 1200, that provides a euhemerized version of the same story). The convergence of three independent sources (Snorri, the Poetic Edda, Saxo) makes the core narrative highly probable as genuine pre-Christian tradition.

2. **Ragnarǫk.** The eschatological narrative (the death of the gods, the final battle, the destruction and renewal of the world) is attested in the *Vǫluspa*, the *Vafþrúðnismál*, and other Eddic poems, and Snorri's account is broadly consistent with the poetic sources. The specific details of Snorri's Ragnarǫk narrative (the fire-giant Surtr, the wolf Fenrir swallowing the sun, the cosmic winter Fimbulvetr) are attested in the poems and are probably genuine.

3. **Þórr's attributes and exploits.** Þórr's hammer (Mjǫllnir), his goats (Tanngnjóstr and Tanngrisnr), his belt of strength (megingjarðar), and his exploits (the fishing of the Midgard Serpent, the journey to Útgarða-Loki, the battle with Hrungnir) are widely attested in the poetic and skaldic traditions, and Snorri's accounts are broadly consistent with these independent witnesses.

## Lecture 7: Saxo Grammaticus — The Alternative Euhemerist Tradition

Saxo Grammaticus (c. 1150-1220), a Danish cleric and historian, composed the *Gesta Danorum* (Deeds of the Danes) — a vast history of Denmark from mythological origins to Saxo's own time. The *Gesta Danorum* is the most important alternative source for Norse mythology outside of the Icelandic tradition, and it provides a strikingly different version of several myths that Snorri also tells.

**Saxo's Euhemerism:**

Saxo's euhemerism is more aggressive than Snorri's. Where Snorri presents the gods as great men who were mistakenly worshipped, Saxo presents them as cunning deceivers who *deliberately* fraudulently claimed divinity. In Saxo's account, the gods are not noble figures who earned divine honors by their accomplishments — they are tricksters who used magic and illusion to convince a gullible people that they were divine. This more hostile euhemerism reflects Saxo's more aggressively Christian perspective: he was a cleric, not a politician, and his attitude toward paganism is one of unambiguous condemnation.

**The Baldr Narrative in Saxo:**

The most significant difference between Saxo and Snorri is in their accounts of Baldr's death. In Snorri's Gylfaginning, Baldr is the innocent victim of Loki's malice — the beautiful, radiant god who is killed by a mistletoe spear because of a loophole in his invulnerability. In Saxo's *Gesta Danorum*, Baldr (Balderus) is a warrior — a heroic prince who competes with another prince, Hǫðr (Hotherus), for the hand of the princess Nanna. Hotherus is a mortal hero who is aided by forest-maidens (supernatural helpers) and who ultimately kills Balderus in battle. Loki does not appear in Saxo's version.

The differences are profound: Snorri's Baldr is a passive, innocent victim; Saxo's Baldr is an active, heroic warrior. Snorri's narrative is a myth of divine tragedy; Saxo's is a heroic legend of mortal competition. The two versions may reflect different pre-Christian traditions (the Baldr myth may have been told differently in Denmark and in Iceland), or one of them may be a reworking of the other, or both may be Christian reinterpretations of a common pre-Christian original that is now irrecoverable.

**Other Differences:**

- **Þórr (Thor)** in Saxo is a human sorcerer (a "god of the giants" according to Saxo) who uses magic rather than divine strength. In one episode, Thor is outwitted by the hero Thorkillus and is depicted as a figure of ridicule. This hostile portrayal contrasts sharply with Snorri's relatively respectful treatment of Þórr as the strongest of the gods.

- **Óðinn (Othinus)** in Saxo is a cunning king who uses deception and magic to maintain his power. He is not the wise, all-knowing god of Snorri's Gylfaginning but a morally ambiguous ruler who lies, cheats, and manipulates to get his way.

- **The afterlife** in Saxo is not the Valhǫll of Snorri's account but a subterranean realm of the dead, accessed through caves and underground passages. This "underworld" afterlife may reflect a Danish tradition that was different from the Icelandic Valhǫll concept.

**The 2040 Assessment:**

The current scholarly assessment of Saxo is that he is an independent witness to some pre-Christian traditions, but that his Christian hostility makes him an unreliable guide to the pre-Christian meaning of those traditions. Where Saxo's account agrees with Snorri and the Poetic Edda, it probably reflects genuine common tradition; where it differs, it may reflect regional variation, Christian reinterpretation, or Saxo's own literary agenda. The *Gesta Danorum* is most valuable as a control — a source that allows us to test the reliability of the Icelandic tradition by providing an independent (and often contradictory) account of the same myths.

## Lecture 8: The Historia Norwegie and the Chronicon Lethrense — Minor Euhemerist Sources

Beyond Snorri and Saxo, several minor Latin sources provide euhemerist accounts of the Norse gods that are independent of the Icelandic tradition.

**The Historia Norwegie (c. 1150-1220):**

An anonymous Norwegian Latin history, the *Historia Norwegie* provides a brief euhemerist account of the Norse gods that differs in important details from Snorri's version. The *Historia Norwegie* describes the pagan gods as "giants" (gigantes) who were worshipped by the ignorant, and it specifically identifies Þórr as a giant rather than a human king. This "giant" classification is not found in Snorri and may reflect an independent Norwegian tradition.

**The Chronicon Lethrense (c. 1170):**

The *Chronicon Lethrense* (Lejre Chronicle) is a Danish Latin chronicle that provides another independent euhemerist account. It describes Óðinn and Þórr as kings who ruled at Lejre (the traditional royal seat of the Danish kings on Zealand) and who were mistakenly worshipped as gods. The *Chronicon Lethrense* is notable for its localization of the gods at Lejre — a connection that is supported by the archaeological evidence of a great hall at Lejre (excavated 1986-2038, revealing a succession of large halls from the 6th-11th centuries) that may have been the political and ritual center of the Danish kingdom.

**The Saxon and Continental Traditions:**

The continental Germanic traditions — the Old High German and Old Saxon sources — provide further evidence for the euhemerist interpretation of the Norse gods. The *Merseburger Zaubersprüche* (Merseburg Charms) mention Wodan (Óðinn) and Donar (Þórr) by name, and the *Hildebrandslied* (Lay of Hildebrand) preserves a fragment of heroic poetry with mythological references. These continental sources are independent of the Icelandic tradition and confirm that the worship of Óðinn and Þórr was widespread across the Germanic world, not limited to Scandinavia.

## Lecture 9: The Theological Problem — Did the Norse Believe Their Gods Were Divine?

The euhemerist interpretation raises a fundamental theological question: did the pre-Christian Norse understand their gods as genuinely divine beings, or did they share the euhemerist understanding that the gods were deified humans?

**The Case for Pre-Christian Euhemerism:**

Some scholars have argued that elements of euhemerist thinking were present in pre-Christian Norse religion:

- **The cult of dead ancestors.** The Norse practiced ancestor veneration — offerings were made at grave-mounds, the dead were believed to continue their lives in the mound, and some ancestors were attributed supernatural powers. The veneration of powerful dead individuals (chieftains, founders, heroes) is a form of euhemerism — the dead person is elevated to a semi-divine status by the community. If ancestor veneration could produce semi-divine figures, it is not a large step to imagine that the great gods themselves were once powerful ancestors.

- **The Óðinn of the *Ynglinga saga*.** Snorri's account of Óðinn as a migrating warrior-king from Asia may not be entirely his invention. The tradition of Óðinn as a migrating culture-hero — a figure who travels from a distant homeland, establishes institutions, and receives divine honors — is found in several independent sources (the *Historia Norwegie*, the *Chronicon Lethrense*, the Anglo-Saxon royal genealogies that trace the Wessex dynasty to Wōden/Oðinn). The convergence of these independent sources suggests that the tradition of Óðinn as a migrating figure was not Snorri's invention but a genuine element of the pre-Christian or early post-conversion tradition.

- **The *Rígsþula* and the origin of the jarlar.** The Eddic poem *Rígsþula* describes the god Rígr (Heimdallr) as the progenitor of the three social orders — þræll, karl, jarl. The jarlar (the aristocracy) are explicitly descended from a god — a mythological charter for social hierarchy that is also a form of euhemerism in reverse: the gods are the ancestors of the human aristocracy, not the other way around. The *Rígsþula* tradition suggests that the pre-Christian Norse understood the relationship between gods and humans in genealogical terms — gods were the ancestors of human lineages.

**The Case Against Pre-Christian Euhemerism:**

The stronger scholarly consensus is that euhemerism is a post-conversion, Christian interpretive framework that was imposed on the Norse tradition from outside:

- **The gods are supernatural in the Poetic Edda.** The Eddic poems — the closest we can get to pre-Christian textual sources — consistently present the gods as supernatural beings, not as deified humans. Óðinn is a god of wisdom, war, and death, who hangs on the World Tree and speaks with the dead; Þórr is a god of thunder who fights giants and travels to the realm of the gods in a chariot drawn by goats; Freyr is a god of fertility who controls the weather and the harvest. The poetic sources do not present the gods as historical figures who were later deified.

- **The euhemerist framework is theologically Christian.** The euhemerist reduction — "the gods were really men" — is a monotheistic theological move: it eliminates the possibility that the pagan gods are real divinities competing with the Christian God. This move makes no sense in a polytheistic framework, where the existence of multiple divine beings is the default assumption. The pre-Christian Norse had no theological reason to deny the divinity of their gods.

- **The absence of euhemerism in the pre-conversion archaeological record.** The archaeological evidence for pre-Christian Norse religion — the cult sites, the votive offerings, the amulets, the burial practices — is consistent with the worship of supernatural beings, not with the veneration of historical figures. The Mjǫllnir amulets, the gold foil figures, the sacrificial deposits — these are the material remains of a religious system that treated its gods as genuinely divine, not as deified ancestors.

**The 2040 Synthesis:**

The current consensus is that pre-Christian Norse religion did not include euhemerism as a theological principle. The gods were understood as genuinely divine — supernatural beings with real power over the world and over human life. Euhemerism is a post-conversion Christian framework that was imposed on the Norse tradition for apologetic purposes. However, there were pre-Christian elements that were compatible with euhemerism (ancestor veneration, the genealogical connection between gods and humans) and that made the euhemerist interpretation plausible — even attractive — to Christian authors who wanted to integrate the Norse past into the Christian worldview.

## Lecture 10: Snorri's Legacy — The Edda and the Reconstruction of Norse Religion

The Prose Edda has shaped the modern understanding of Norse mythology more profoundly than any other single work. Snorri's systematization of the mythological tradition — his coherent cosmology, his classified pantheon, his narrative arc from creation to Ragnarǫk — has become, for most modern readers, *what Norse mythology is*. The question is whether Snorri's systematization faithfully represents pre-Christian religion or whether it has created a "Snorri's Norse mythology" that is distinct from the genuine pre-Christian tradition.

**The Edda's Influence on Modern Perceptions:**

- **Popular culture.** The Marvel Comics version of Þórr (and the MCU films derived from it) draws more on Snorri's Gylfaginning than on the Poetic Edda — the coherent pantheon, the narrative of Ragnarǫk, the systematic cosmology are all Snorri's contributions to the mythological tradition as it is understood today.

- **Modern Ásatrú and Heathenry.** Most modern Heathen ritual and theology is derived, directly or indirectly, from Snorri's Edda. The blót as practiced in modern Ásatrú follows the structure described in Gylfaginning and *Hákonar saga góða*; the sumbel follows the structure of the Beowulf and saga accounts (mediated through Snorri's presentation); the cosmology (the nine worlds, Yggdrasill, the wells) is Snorri's systematization. Modern Heathenry is, in many respects, a Snorri-derived tradition.

- **Academic scholarship.** The academic study of Norse mythology has, since the 19th century, been organized around the Prose Edda — Snorri's framework provides the structure within which the Poetic Edda and other sources are interpreted. The 2040 scholarly trend is toward a more critical, source-conscious approach that treats Snorri as one witness among many, rather than as the default framework, but Snorri's influence on the scholarly tradition remains immense.

**Beyond Snorri — The 2040 Approach:**

The University of Yggdrasil's Norse Pagan Studies program teaches a source-critical approach to the Prose Edda that emphasizes:

1. **Multi-source triangulation.** No mythological narrative should be accepted on Snorri's authority alone. Wherever possible, Snorri's account should be checked against the Poetic Edda, the skaldic tradition, the archaeological evidence, and the independent literary sources (Saxo, the Latin chronicles, the continental Germanic material).

2. **Awareness of Christian framing.** The euhemerist framework, the narrative structure of Gylfaginning, and the theological assumptions of the Christian author are not neutral containers for pre-Christian content — they shape and sometimes distort the content they contain. Reading "past" the Christian frame to recover the pagan content is a necessary but inherently uncertain operation.

3. **Embracing inconsistency.** Pre-Christian Norse mythology was probably not a coherent, systematic, internally consistent narrative. The inconsistencies and contradictions in the sources (Snorri vs. the Poetic Edda, Snorri vs. Saxo, different Eddic poems vs. each other) may not be problems to be solved but features of a living, variable, regionally diverse tradition. The 2040 approach accepts inconsistency as evidence for the organic character of pre-Christian religion, rather than trying to resolve it into a single "correct" version.

4. **Snorri as interpreter, not transmitter.** Snorri was not a passive transmitter of pre-Christian tradition but an active interpreter who selected, organized, systematized, and sometimes invented mythological material in the service of his literary and pedagogical purposes. His interpretation is valuable — it is the most detailed and systematic account we have — but it is an interpretation, not a transparent window into the pre-Christian world.

## Lecture 11: Euhemerism and Modern Heathenry — The Living Debate

The euhemerist question is not merely an academic problem; it is a living debate within the modern Ásatrú and Heathen communities. How practitioners understand the nature of the gods — as genuinely divine beings, as archetypes, as deified ancestors, or as something else entirely — has profound implications for how they practice their religion.

**The Spectrum of Modern Belief:**

1. **Hard polytheism.** The gods are real, independent, supernatural beings with their own wills, powers, and spheres of influence. Hard polytheism is the most common position in modern Ásatrú, and it is the position that is most consistent with the pre-Christian understanding (as far as it can be reconstructed). Hard polytheists reject euhemerism as a Christian distortion.

2. **Soft polytheism / archetypalism.** The gods are real in the sense that they are psychologically real — they are archetypes of the collective unconscious (in the Jungian sense) or expressions of fundamental human experiences and needs. Archetypalists may find euhemerism partially useful — the gods may have originated as historical figures whose stories were amplified by the collective imagination — but they do not accept the euhemerist reduction of the gods to "merely human."

3. **Ancestor-veneration / euhemerist Heathenry.** A minority position within modern Heathenry holds that the gods were originally great ancestors — powerful human beings who were deified by their communities — and that Heathen practice is fundamentally a form of ancestor veneration. This position is explicitly euhemerist and draws on the Snorri and Saxo traditions as evidence for the "original" understanding of the gods.

4. **Non-literal / cultural Heathenry.** Some practitioners do not believe in the gods as supernatural beings at all — they practice Heathenry as a cultural, ethical, or aesthetic tradition, drawing on Norse mythology as a source of values, stories, and community identity without making metaphysical claims about the gods' existence. Cultural Heathenry is compatible with euhemerism in a practical sense (the gods are cultural symbols, not divine beings) but does not depend on it.

**The University of Yggdrasil Position:**

The University of Yggdrasil's Norse Pagan Studies program does not take a theological position on the nature of the gods. The program teaches the scholarly sources, the source-critical methods, and the range of modern interpretations; students are free to adopt whatever theological position is consistent with their own experience and practice. The program does, however, insist on intellectual honesty: students who claim that euhemerism was the "original" pre-Christian Norse understanding must engage with the evidence against that claim, and students who claim that Snorri's account is an uncorrupted transmission of pre-Christian tradition must engage with the evidence for Snorri's inventions and Christian framework.

## Lecture 12: The Prose Edda in the Digital Age — New Methods, New Questions

The 2040s have brought new computational and digital methods to the study of the Prose Edda, raising new questions about Snorri's sources, methods, and inventions.

**Computational Stylometry:**

Stylometric analysis — the statistical study of authorial style — has been applied to the Prose Edda to determine whether all four sections (Prologue, Gylfaginning, Skáldskaparmál, Háttatal) are by the same author, and whether Snorri's prose can be distinguished from the poetic quotations embedded in it. The 2037 stylometric study by Einarsson and Sigurðsson found that:

- The Prologue is stylistically distinct from the other three sections, consistent with the hypothesis that it is a later addition (possibly by Snorri, possibly by another author).
- Gylfaginning and Skáldskaparmál are stylistically consistent, supporting the traditional attribution of both to Snorri.
- The Háttatal is stylistically distinct from Gylfaginning and Skáldskaparmál, consistent with its different genre (technical verse with prose commentary rather than mythological prose with verse quotations).

**Network Analysis of the Mythological System:**

Network analysis — the mathematical study of relationships between entities — has been applied to Snorri's pantheon to reveal its structural properties. The 2039 study by Lindqvist and Haraldsson constructed a network of the Norse gods based on their relationships as described in Gylfaginning (kinship, enmity, alliance, marriage) and found that:

- The network is a "small-world" network — most gods are connected to each other through a small number of intermediaries, and the network is highly clustered (gods who are connected to the same other gods tend to be connected to each other).
- Óðinn occupies the most central position in the network — he is connected to more other gods, and to more diverse categories of gods, than any other figure.
- The Æsir and Vanir form distinct clusters within the network, connected primarily through the inter-marriage of Njǫrðr, Freyr, and Freyja with the Æsir.

The network analysis confirms Snorri's systematization in a general sense (the gods do form a connected network with Óðinn at the center) but also reveals properties (the small-world structure, the clustering) that may be emergent features of the tradition rather than deliberate constructions by Snorri.

**The Edda Textual Platform:**

The 2040 Edda Textual Platform (ETP) — a collaborative digital project based at the University of Yggdrasil — provides a fully annotated, machine-readable edition of the Prose Edda, with parallel texts from the Poetic Edda, the skaldic corpus, Saxo, and the Latin chronicles. The ETP allows researchers to:

- Search across all Eddic sources for specific mythological motifs, characters, or themes.
- Compare Snorri's prose with the poetic sources side by side, identifying agreements, discrepancies, and innovations.
- Map the distribution of mythological motifs across regions and time periods.
- Trace the transmission and transformation of specific myths through the manuscript tradition.

The ETP is the 2040 tool for Eddic source criticism — it makes possible the kind of systematic, multi-source comparison that was previously the province of individual scholars with decades of reading behind them. The Platform does not replace scholarly judgment — it cannot determine whether a specific detail is Snorri's invention or genuine tradition — but it provides the evidence base for making that judgment in a more systematic and transparent way.
