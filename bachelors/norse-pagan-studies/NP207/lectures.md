# NP207: Afterlife Beliefs — Valhǫll, Hel, Hvergelmir
## Bachelor of Science in Norse Pagan Studies — University of Yggdrasil, 2040

**Credits:** 4
**Description:** Death is not the end. The Norse understanding of the afterlife was complex, multi-directional, and deeply connected to the social order of the living. This course examines the major afterlife destinations — Valhǫll (the slain-hall of Óðinn), Hel (the realm of the dead ruled by Hel), Hvergelmir (the roaring cauldron of the wicked), and the alternative afterlives attested in the sources — including Fólkvangr (Freyja's field of the slain), Rán's hall beneath the waves, and the life-in-the-mound tradition. Drawing on the Eddas, the sagas, the archaeological evidence, and comparative Indo-European studies, students explore how the Norse understood death as a continuation of — and a judgment on — the life that preceded it.

## Lecture 1: The Problem of the Norse Afterlife — Sources, Contradictions, and Interpretive Frameworks

The Norse afterlife is not a single, coherent system. It is a collection of overlapping, sometimes contradictory traditions — the product of a pre-Christian religion that lacked a centralized authority, a canonical scripture, or a systematic theology of death. The sources describe multiple afterlife destinations, multiple criteria for reaching them, and multiple relationships between the dead and the living — and these accounts do not always agree with each other.

**The Major Afterlife Destinations:**

1. **Valhǫll** (Valhǫll, "Hall of the Slain") — The hall of Óðinn in Asgarðr, where half of the warriors who die in battle are brought by the valkyrjur (Valkyries). In Valhǫll, the warriors (einherjar) fight each day and feast each night, preparing for the final battle at Ragnarǫk. Valhǫll is the most famous Norse afterlife, but it was almost certainly not the most common — it was reserved for a specific category of the dead (warriors fallen in battle), and only half of those.

2. **Fólkvangr** (Fólkvangr, "Field of the People" or "Field of Warriors") — The field of the goddess Freyja, where the other half of the battle-slain go. Fólkvangr is mentioned only once in the surviving sources (*Grímnismál*, verse 14), and its significance is debated: is it a separate afterlife destination with its own character, or is it simply Freyja's share of the Valhǫll-bound dead?

3. **Hel** (Hel) — The underworld realm of the dead, ruled by the goddess Hel, daughter of Loki. Hel is the destination for those who die of sickness, old age, or other non-battle causes — the vast majority of the dead. Hel is described in Snorri's Gylfaginning as a gloomy, cold, uncomfortable place, but other sources suggest that Hel was not necessarily a place of punishment — it was simply the realm of the dead, where life continued in a diminished form.

4. **Hvergelmir** (Hvergelmir, "Roaring Cauldron") and **Nástrǫnd** (Nástrǫnd, "Corpse-Shore") — The afterlife of the wicked: perjurers, murderers, oath-breakers, and those who seduce other men's wives. Nástrǫnd is described in the *Vǫluspa* (verses 38-39) as a hall on the Corpse-Shore, where venom drips from the rafters and the dragon Níðhǫggr feeds on the corpses of the damned.

5. **Alternative afterlives** — The sources attest to several afterlife destinations beyond the major four:
   - **Rán's hall** — The sea-goddess Rán was believed to receive those who drowned at sea (attested in the skaldic poetry and the sagas).
   - **Life in the mound** — The dead were believed to continue their existence in their burial mounds, where they could be visited, consulted, and propitiated by their living descendants.
   - **Reincarnation** — Some sources attest to a belief in reincarnation or the continuation of the individual soul through a lineage (the *Helgakviða* poems, the account of the rebirth of the hero Helgi).

**The Sources:**

The primary sources for the Norse afterlife are the Poetic Edda, the Prose Edda, the skaldic poetry, and the sagas. The archaeological evidence — burial practices, grave goods, and sacrificial deposits — provides material confirmation of some textual accounts and challenges others.

The fundamental problem of afterlife studies is that the sources were mostly written by Christians (Snorri, the saga authors) or were preserved in Christian manuscripts (the Poetic Edda), and the Christian authors had their own theological framework for the afterlife (Heaven and Hell, judgment after death, the resurrection of the body) that may have shaped their presentation of the Norse material.

## Lecture 2: Valhǫll — The Hall of the Slain

Valhǫll is the most famous of the Norse afterlives — the hall of Óðinn, where the einherjar (the "one-warriors," the chosen warriors) feast and fight in preparation for Ragnarǫk. But Valhǫll is also, paradoxically, the least representative of the Norse afterlife — a destination reserved for a tiny fraction of the dead (warriors slain in battle, and only half of them), and one whose literary presentation is shaped as much by Christian and warrior-aristocratic values as by genuine pre-Christian tradition.

**The Description of Valhǫll:**

The most detailed description of Valhǫll is in the *Grímnismál* (The Lay of Grímnir), a poem in which Óðinn, disguised as Grímnir, describes the divine realm while being tortured between two fires by King Geirrøðr:

> "Gladshamarr it is called, the fifth,  
> where the gold-bright Valhǫll stretches wide;  
> there Óðinn chooses each day  
> men dead by weapons." (*Grímnismál*, v. 8)

And later:

> "Five hundred doors, and forty more,  
> I think are in Valhǫll;  
> eight hundred einherjar go out through each door  
> when they go to fight the wolf." (*Grímnismál*, v. 23)

The description emphasizes several key features:

- **Valhǫll is a hall** — a great building like a chieftain's hall, the architectural form of the Norse social world. The afterlife is imagined as a continuation of the social life of the hall: feasting, drinking, and the companionship of warriors under the patronage of a chieftain (Óðinn).
- **The einherjar fight each day.** Every morning, the einherjar arm themselves and fight in the courtyard of Valhǫll. Those who are killed rise again at evening, and all return to the hall for the feast. The daily battle is both a training exercise (preparing for Ragnarǫk) and a continuation of the warrior's life — the thing the warrior loved best, continued forever.
- **The einherjar feast each night.** The boar Sæhrímnir is cooked each evening, and the mead flows from the udders of the goat Heiðrún. The eternal feast is the reward of the warrior — the abundance of food and drink that, in mortal life, was never guaranteed.

**The Valkyrjur and the Selection of the Slain:**

The valkyrjur (Valkyries, "choosers of the slain") are the divine female figures who select which warriors die in battle and which live, and who bring the chosen warriors to Valhǫll. The *Vǫluspa* mentions them: "She saw valkyrjur coming from afar, / ready to ride to the people of the gods" (v. 30). Snorri's Gylfaginning describes them as Óðinn's servants who "ride to every battle; they choose the deaths of men and steer the victory."

The valkyrjur represent a theological solution to a practical problem: why do some warriors die in battle while others survive? The answer is that Óðinn, through the valkyrjur, chooses who lives and who dies — not arbitrarily, but according to his need for warriors to fight at Ragnarǫk. The best warriors are taken early (they are more useful to Óðinn in Valhǫll than in Miðgarðr), while weaker warriors are allowed to live longer. This is a theology of death that makes sense of the apparent randomness of battle — the warrior who dies young is not unfortunate but *chosen*.

**The Einherjar as the Army of the Dead:**

The einherjar are not merely the honored dead — they are the army of Óðinn, the host that will fight at his side in the final battle of Ragnarǫk. The *Vǫluspa* describes the einherjar as participants in the cosmic conflict:

> "The einherjar all fight  
> on Óðinn's plains;  
> they break the shields of the jǫtnar." (*Vǫluspa*, v. 24)

The eschatological function of the einherjar gives Valhǫll its theological logic: Óðinn gathers warriors not merely to honor them but to *use* them. The relationship between Óðinn and the einherjar is the relationship between a chieftain and his retinue — the chieftain provides the feast and the companionship; the warriors provide their loyalty and their fighting strength. The exchange is asymmetrical (the warriors give their lives; the chieftain gives eternal life), but it is an exchange — the warriors are not passive recipients of Óðinn's favor but active participants in a reciprocal relationship.

**The Social Logic of Valhǫll:**

The 2040 understanding of Valhǫll emphasizes its social function as a projection of the warrior-aristocratic values of the Viking Age:

- **Valhǫll is the afterlife of the warrior elite.** The einherjar are the "chosen" — the best warriors, the most honorable dead. The criterion for selection is martial: the manner of death (battle), not the moral quality of the life. Valhǫll is not a reward for virtue but a recognition of martial excellence — and a mechanism for perpetuating the warrior ideal beyond death.

- **Valhǫll legitimates the warrior's way of life.** The warrior who follows the chieftain to battle, who risks death each summer, who kills and is killed in turn, is not wasting his life — he is earning a place in Valhǫll. The belief in Valhǫll transforms the warrior's death from a tragedy into an achievement — the ultimate realization of the warrior's identity.

- **Valhǫll is not for everyone.** The vast majority of people — farmers, craftsmen, merchants, women, children, the old, the sick — do not go to Valhǫll. The exclusivity of Valhǫll reflects the social hierarchy of the Viking Age: the warrior elite was, in death as in life, a privileged class.

## Lecture 3: Hel — The Realm of the Dead

Hel is the most widespread and the most fundamental of the Norse afterlives — the destination of the vast majority of the dead, the underworld realm ruled by the goddess Hel. Hel is both a place (the realm of the dead) and a person (the goddess who rules it), and the ambiguity between the two is itself significant: the realm is the ruler, the ruler is the realm, and the dead are identified with the place of their afterlife existence.

**The Goddess Hel:**

Hel is the daughter of Loki and the giantess Angrboða. Snorri's Gylfaginning describes her as a being of terrifying appearance — half blue-black (like a corpse) and half flesh-colored (like the living) — who was cast down into Niflheimr (the world of darkness and cold) by Óðinn and given authority over the dead:

> "Hel he [Óðinn] threw into Niflheimr and gave her authority over nine worlds, so that she should share out all hospitality among those who were sent to her, namely men who die of sickness or of old age." (Gylfaginning, ch. 34)

The description is vivid but problematic. The "half-blue-black, half-flesh-colored" appearance is not attested in any Eddic poem — it may be Snorri's own invention, a visualization of the liminality of the death-goddess (half living, half dead). The nine-world jurisdiction is also Snorri's systematization, imposing a numerical scheme on the underworld realm.

The Poetic Edda mentions Hel less vividly but more authoritatively. The *Vǫluspa* describes Hel as a place where the dead go — "the hall of Hel" (*helju sal*) — and as a destination for the wicked (v. 38-39). The *Baldrs draumar* describes Baldr's journey to Hel after his death, and the failed attempt to rescue him. The Poetic Edda's Hel is less personified than Snorri's but more integrated into the mythological narrative.

**The Geography of Hel — Snorri's Account:**

Snorri gives Hel a detailed geography: it is located in Niflheimr (the world of primal darkness and cold), beneath one of the three roots of Yggdrasill, at the well Hvergelmir. The road to Hel is called *Helveg* (the Hel-Road), and it descends "northward and downward" — a spatial orientation that is cosmologically significant (north = the direction of cold, darkness, and danger in the Norse symbolic system). The bridge to Hel — the Gjallarbrú, guarded by the maiden Móðguðr — spans the river Gjǫll, and the gates of Hel are guarded by the dog Garmr.

The geography of Hel is described in the context of Baldr's death: Hermóðr, Óðinn's son, rides to Hel on Sleipnir (Óðinn's eight-legged horse) to negotiate Baldr's return. The journey takes nine nights (a ritually significant number), and Hermóðr crosses the Gjallarbrú, passes through the gates of Hel, and finds Baldr sitting in the high seat of Hel's hall. The rescue fails because the giantess Þǫkk (probably Loki in disguise) refuses to weep for Baldr, breaking the condition that Hel had imposed for Baldr's return: "All things in the world, living and dead, will weep for him, and then he will go back to the Æsir; but he will remain with Hel if any refuses."

**The Nature of Hel — Punishment or Diminished Existence?**

The question of whether Hel is a place of punishment or a neutral realm of the dead is one of the most debated in Norse afterlife studies. The *Vǫluspa* associates Hel with the wicked — the perjurers, murderers, and oath-breakers on the Corpse-Shore (Nástrǫnd) — but this association may be a specific subset of Hel (the worst part, for the worst dead) rather than a description of Hel as a whole. Snorri's description of Hel as the destination for those who die of sickness or old age suggests that Hel is not a punishment — it is simply the place where the non-warrior dead go.

The 2040 consensus is that Hel was originally a neutral realm of the dead — the place where the dead continued their existence in a diminished but not tormented form. The association of Hel with punishment (Nástrǫnd, Hvergelmir) is probably a secondary development — a moralization of the afterlife that reflects Christian influence (the introduction of a Heaven/Hell binary) and the increasing social differentiation of the Viking Age (the warrior elite's Valhǫll required a contrasting, inferior afterlife for the non-elite).

**The Archaeological Evidence:**

The burial evidence — the grave goods, the burial customs, the treatment of the dead — is consistent with both the Hel and the mound-life traditions. The dead were buried with objects that they would need in the afterlife: weapons for warriors, tools for craftsmen, jewelry for women, food and drink for sustenance. The provision of grave goods assumes that the dead continue to exist and to need the same things they needed in life — an assumption that is consistent with the Hel tradition (life in a diminished form) but not necessarily with the Valhǫll tradition (eternal feasting supplied by Óðinn).

## Lecture 4: Nástrǫnd and Hvergelmir — The Afterlife of the Wicked

Nástrǫnd (the Corpse-Shore) and Hvergelmir (the Roaring Cauldron) are the most horrific elements of the Norse afterlife — the destinations of the wicked, described in the *Vǫluspa* and in Snorri's Gylfaginning in language of vivid and terrible imagery.

**The *Vǫluspa* Account:**

The *Vǫluspa* (verses 38-39) describes Nástrǫnd:

> "A hall she saw standing, far from the sun,  
> on the Corpse-Shore; the doors face north.  
> Drops of venom fall in through the smoke-vent;  
> the hall is woven with serpents' spines.  

> She saw there wading through the heavy streams  
> perjurers and murderers,  
> and the seducer of another man's wife;  
> there Níðhǫggr sucked the corpses of the dead —  
> the wolf tore men apart."

The punishment is both physical (venom dripping from the roof, the dragon tearing corpses) and social: the inhabitants of Nástrǫnd are defined by their crimes — oath-breaking, murder, adultery — rather than by their identity. The wicked are not named individuals but categories of transgression.

**Hvergelmir:**

Hvergelmir is described in the *Vǫluspa* and in Snorri's Gylfaginning as the well at the base of one of Yggdrasill's roots — the well in Niflheimr, where the rivers of the underworld have their source. Snorri describes Hvergelmir as a place of punishment: the wicked dead are immersed in the "roaring cauldron" that is its name.

The relationship between Hvergelmir and Nástrǫnd is unclear — they may be the same place under different names, different regions of the same underworld realm, or distinct afterlife destinations. The 2040 consensus is that Hvergelmir is the well from which the underworld rivers flow, and Nástrǫnd is the hall on the shore of one of those rivers — they are features of the same landscape, not competing afterlives.

**The Dragon Níðhǫggr:**

Níðhǫggr (Níðhǫggr, "Malice-Striker" or "Corpse-Striker") is the dragon who gnaws at the root of Yggdrasill and who feeds on the corpses of the damned on Nástrǫnd. Níðhǫggr is one of the most persistent images of cosmic evil in the Norse tradition — the dragon who threatens the cosmic order, who will be released at Ragnarǫk, and who embodies the destructive forces that oppose the gods.

The *Vǫluspa*'s final stanza — one of the most haunting passages in the Eddic corpus — describes Níðhǫggr rising from the underworld after Ragnarǫk, carrying corpses in his wings, and sinking into the darkness:

> "There comes the dark dragon flying,  
> the gleaming serpent, from beneath the Niðafjǫll;  
> Níðhǫggr bears in his wings  
> corpses flying over the plain — now she must sink."

The final word — *nú man hon søkkva* ("now she must sink") — is the last word of the *Vǫluspa*, the vǫlva sinking back into the earth after delivering her prophecy. The poem ends where it began: with the vǫlva in the earth, the dragon bearing the dead, and the cosmic order turning toward its dissolution.

**The Moralization of the Afterlife:**

The existence of Nástrǫnd and Hvergelmir as destinations for specific categories of the wicked represents a moralization of the afterlife — a departure from the older, simpler model in which the dead go to Hel regardless of their moral character, and the afterlife is a continuation of social identity rather than a judgment on moral conduct.

The moralization of the afterlife is probably a late development in the pre-Christian period, influenced by Christian concepts of heavenly reward and hellish punishment. The crimes that consign the dead to Nástrǫnd — perjury, murder, adultery — are precisely the crimes that Christian theology classifies as mortal sins. The correspondence suggests that the *Vǫluspa*'s moralized afterlife — or at least its explicit formulation — reflects Christian influence on the Norse tradition.

But the crimes are also violations of the Norse social order: perjury is an offense against the legal system; murder (as opposed to killing in battle or in a legal feud) is an offense against the community's peace; adultery is an offense against the kindred's marriage bonds. The moralized afterlife may therefore have a pre-Christian basis — the social order is sacred, and those who violate it are excluded from the community's afterlife as they were excluded from the community's life.

## Lecture 5: Fólkvangr — Freyja's Field of the Slain

Fólkvangr is one of the most enigmatic of the Norse afterlives — mentioned only once in the surviving sources, in a single stanza of the *Grímnismál*:

> "Fólkvangr it is called, the ninth,  
> there Freyja governs the seats in the hall.  
> Half the slain she chooses each day;  
> Óðinn has the other half." (*Grímnismál*, v. 14)

The stanza establishes three things: Fólkvangr is Freyja's hall; it receives half of the battle-slain; and the other half goes to Valhǫll/Óðinn. Beyond these three facts, the sources are silent — no other Eddic poem, no skaldic verse, and no saga elaborates on the nature of Fólkvangr or the character of the afterlife that Freyja provides for her chosen warriors.

**The Name — Fólkvangr:**

The meaning of the name *Fólkvangr* is itself a subject of debate. The most common interpretation is "Field of the People" (*fólk* = people, *vangr* = field), suggesting an open, natural afterlife rather than a great hall. But *fólk* can also mean "host" or "army" (the same root as the Old English *folc*, the German *Volk*), so Fólkvangr might be "Field of the Warrior-Host" — a parallel to Valhǫll, the hall of the warrior-host. The ambiguity reflects the larger ambiguity about Fólkvangr itself: is it a distinct alternative to Valhǫll, or is it simply Freyja's version of the same thing?

**Freyja as Goddess of the Dead:**

Freyja's association with the dead is well-attested beyond the Fólkvangr stanza. She is a practitioner of seiðr — the shamanic magic that involves communication with the dead and the spirits, the entering of trance states, and the journeying between worlds. The *Ynglinga saga* attributes the teaching of seiðr to Freyja (or to the Vanir, her divine family), and the practice is closely associated with vǫlur (female seers) who communicate with the dead and prophesy the future.

Freyja's role as receiver of the dead may be connected to her seiðr function: the goddess who travels between the worlds, who communicates with the spirits, who knows the secrets of the dead, is also the goddess who takes the dead into her own realm. The connection between seiðr and the death-goddess is attested in other Indo-European traditions (the Greek Hecate, the Indic Kālī, the Norse Hel) and may be an inherited feature of the Indo-European conception of the goddess of death.

**Freyja and the Valkyrjur:**

The valkyrjur — the choosers of the slain — are primarily associated with Óðinn in the surviving sources. But some scholars have argued that the valkyrjur tradition was originally associated with Freyja, and that the *Grímnismál* stanza preserves a stage in the tradition at which the slain were divided between Freyja and Óðinn — a division that was later simplified into the Óðinn-monopolization of the valkyrjur tradition.

The argument for Freyja's original association with the valkyrjur rests on the connection between the valkyrjur and the dísir (the female protective spirits, closely associated with Freyja) and on the general pattern of the warrior-death goddess in Indo-European tradition (the Celtic Morrígan, the Indic Durgā). The valkyrjur — the female figures who determine death in battle — are more naturally associated with a goddess like Freyja (who is connected to fertility, sexuality, and death) than with a god like Óðinn (who is connected to wisdom, poetry, and war). The transfer of the valkyrjur from Freyja to Óðinn would reflect the broader shift in Norse religion from the Vanir-centered fertility cult to the Æsir-dominated warrior cult of the Viking Age.

**The 2040 Position:**

The current consensus is that Fólkvangr represents a genuine pre-Christian tradition — an alternative afterlife for the battle-slain, governed by Freyja, that was partially eclipsed by the Óðinn-Valhǫll tradition but that was preserved in the *Grímnismál* stanza. The silence of the other sources about Fólkvangr is not evidence against its existence — it is evidence for the Óðinn-monopolization of the warrior-afterlife tradition in the Viking Age sources, which were composed primarily in the milieu of the warrior aristocracy (the kings' and chieftains' courts).

## Lecture 6: The Soul and the Corpse — The Norse Concept of the Person

The Norse understanding of the afterlife depends on the Norse understanding of the person — what the person is composed of, what survives death, and what is destroyed by it. The Norse concept of the person was complex and multi-partite: the human being was composed of several distinct components, each with its own trajectory after death.

**The Components of the Person:**

1. **Líkamr** — The body, the physical component of the person. The líkamr decays after death (unless preserved by extraordinary circumstances), but the dead could continue to act through their bodies — the draugar (animated corpses) of the sagas are líkamr that have refused to stay dead.

2. **Hamr** — The "shape" or "form" of the person — the external appearance, which could be changed or projected in certain magical practices (hamskipti, "shape-changing"). The hamr was not the same as the líkamr; it could be separated from the body and travel independently, and its appearance could be transformed.

3. **Hugr** — The "mind" or "thought" — the cognitive component of the person, the seat of will, desire, and intention. The hugr was the active principle of the person; it could travel outside the body (as in the *Heldreks saga* account of Óðinn's hugr traveling in the form of a bird), and it persisted after death.

4. **Hamingja** — The "luck" or "fortune" of the person — a quasi-personal force that could be transferred from one person to another, especially at death (the dying chieftain could pass his hamingja to his successor). The hamingja was both personal (it belonged to the individual) and transpersonal (it could be detached from the individual and transferred to someone else).

5. **Fylgja** — The "follower" or "fetch" — a supernatural double, usually female and usually in animal form, that accompanied the person throughout life. The fylgja's appearance at a person's death was an omen of that death; it could be seen by those with second sight, and its form (bear, wolf, bird) reflected the character of the person it accompanied.

6. **Dís** (pl. dísir) — The "dis" or ancestral spirit — the female protective spirit associated with the kindred. The dísir were the spirits of the female ancestors, who protected the kindred and were honored at the dísablót (the sacrifice to the dísir).

**What Survives Death:**

The Norse concept of the person after death is not a single "soul" that survives the body but a distribution of the person's components across different afterlife destinations:

- **The hugr (mind/will)** continues to exist in Hel, Valhǫll, Fólkvangr, or the mound, depending on the manner of death and the social identity of the deceased.
- **The hamingja (luck)** is transferred to the deceased's successor — the chieftain's son, the kindred's heir. The hamingja does not die; it is a resource that passes through the kindred.
- **The fylgja (fetch)** separates from the deceased at death and either dissipates or joins the ancestral dísir.
- **The líkamr (body)** decays in the grave unless animated as a draugr.

The distribution of the person's components after death means that the Norse did not conceive of the afterlife as the survival of a unitary "self" in a single afterlife location. The person was distributed across multiple destinations, multiple communities, and multiple kinds of existence. The self was not a unity but a complex — a network of components whose relationships changed at death but did not entirely dissolve.

## Lecture 7: Life in the Mound — The Dead Among the Living

The mound — the burial mound of the ancestors — was a primary locus of the Norse afterlife. The dead were not merely remembered; they were *present* in their mounds, where they could be visited, consulted, propitiated, and offended by the living. The mound-life tradition was the most ancient and the most persistent form of the Norse afterlife — older than the Valhǫll tradition, more widespread than the Hel tradition, and more socially significant than any of the mythological afterlives.

**The Evidence for Mound-Life:**

The sagas are rich in accounts of encounters with the dead in their mounds:

- **Grettir's encounter with Karr the Old (*Grettis saga*, ch. 18-19).** Grettir descends into the mound of the chieftain Karr, fights the draugr (animated corpse) of Karr, and retrieves a treasure hoard. The draugr is a physical presence — a body that fights, that speaks, that guards its treasure — and the encounter is a heroic adventure.

- **Hervǫr's encounter with Angantýr (*Hervarar saga*).** The shieldmaiden Hervǫr calls on her dead father Angantýr and his brothers to wake from their mound and give her the cursed sword Tyrfingr. The dead rise to bar the mound's entrance, and Angantýr warns his daughter of the sword's curse. The encounter is a dialogue between the living and the dead, in which the dead speak and advise the living.

- **The offerings to the dead in the mound.** The sagas and the archaeological evidence indicate that offerings were made to the dead in their mounds — food, drink, weapons, and valuables placed in or on the mound to sustain the dead and to maintain their favor. The practice of mound-offering (attested from the Bronze Age through the conversion) indicates that the dead were understood to need the same things they needed in life: nourishment, wealth, and the recognition of their descendants.

**The Draugr — The Walking Dead:**

The draugr (pl. draugar) was not a ghost or a spirit but a *body* — the reanimated corpse of the dead person, physically present in the mound and capable of leaving it. The sagas describe the draugr as:

- **Physically powerful.** The draugr is enormously strong — stronger than any living human. Grettir only defeats Karr by wrestling him into submission and striking his head from his body.

- **Malignant.** The draugr is dangerous — it haunts the living, brings sickness, kills livestock, and causes misfortune. The draugr is the dead person who refuses to stay dead, whose will persists in the body and drives it to harm the living.

- **Vulnerable to fire and beheading.** The standard methods of disposing of a draugr are cremation (burning the corpse) or beheading (striking the head from the body and placing it at the feet). Both methods reflect the Norse understanding that the body is the locus of the dead person's continued existence — destroy the body, and you destroy the draugr.

The draugr tradition is consistent with the mound-life tradition: the dead continue to exist in their mounds, and some of them are not content to stay there. The draugr is the mound-life tradition's dark side — the possibility that the dead, if not properly treated, if not properly placated, will return to torment the living.

**The Ancestor as Ally:**

The dead in their mounds were not only threats; they were also resources. The ancestor in the mound was the kindred's connection to the past, the guarantor of its claim to the land, and the source of its hamingja (luck). The ancestor could be consulted for advice, could be propitiated for favor, and could be called on for protection. The mound was the kindred's sacred center — the place where the living and the dead met, where the past and the present intersected, and where the kindred's identity was anchored in the landscape.

## Lecture 8: Reincarnation and Rebirth in the Norse Tradition

The Norse sources attest to a belief in reincarnation — or something like it — that is distinct from the Valhǫll/Hel/mound-life traditions and that may represent an older stratum of Norse afterlife belief.

**The Helgi Tradition:**

The most significant evidence for Norse reincarnation is in the *Helgakviða* (Lay of Helgi) poems of the Poetic Edda:

- **Helgi Hjǫrvarðsson.** The hero Helgi is unnamed at birth — he is a silent child. He is visited by valkyrjur who name him Helgi and give him a sword, declaring his destiny as a great hero. After his death, he is reborn as another Helgi — Helgi Hundingsbani ("Helgi, the Slayer of Hundingr").

- **Helgi Hundingsbani.** The second Helgi is the hero of the *Helgakviða Hundingsbana I* and *II*. He kills the king Hundingr, marries the valkyrja Sigrún, and is killed in battle. After his death, he is received in Valhǫll, but also — and this is the key detail — he returns to his burial mound for one last night with Sigrún.

- **Helgi's return from Valhǫll.** After his death, Helgi returns from Valhǫll to his mound. Sigrún, still alive, enters the mound and spends the night with him — an extraordinary union of the living and the dead, the woman and her dead husband, in the space of the burial mound. Helgi's return from Valhǫll to the mound suggests that the dead can move between afterlives — they are not confined to a single destination.

**The Rebirth of the Helgi-Cycle:**

The prose colophon at the end of *Helgakviða Hundingsbana II* explicitly states:

> "It was the belief in ancient times that people were reborn — but it is now called an old wives' tale. Helgi and Sigrún were reborn; he was then called Helgi Haddingjaskaði, and she was Kára, Hálfdan's daughter."

The colophon is remarkable: it explicitly states the doctrine of rebirth, and it explicitly distances the Christian-era audience from it ("it is now called an old wives' tale"). The doctrine is thus both asserted (as a belief of "ancient times") and dismissed (as superstition). The combination of assertion and dismissal suggests that the rebirth tradition was genuinely pre-Christian — something that the Christian scribe recognized as pagan and felt the need to disavow, even as he recorded it.

**The Transmigration of the Soul:**

Beyond the Helgi-Cycle, there are hints of a rebirth tradition elsewhere in the Norse sources:

- **The *Vǫluspa*'s golden age.** The *Vǫluspa* describes the golden age of the gods, before the coming of the three giantesses (the Norns?) who brought fate and death into the world. The implication is that, before fate, there was no death as we know it — the gods' world was a world of eternal renewal, in which the line between life and death was not yet drawn.

- **The reincarnation of Óðinn's warriors?** The *Hárbarðsljóð* (The Lay of Hárbarðr) suggests that Óðinn's warriors are reborn to fight again — not in Valhǫll but on the earthly battlefield. The idea that the chosen warriors are reborn into new bodies to continue their combat may be an alternative to the Valhǫll tradition — or a parallel tradition that coexisted with it.

- **The naming of the dead.** The Norse practice of naming children after dead ancestors — particularly after ancestors who had recently died — may have been associated with a belief that the dead ancestor was reborn in the child who received their name. The *Vatnsdœla saga* and *Ljósvetninga saga* provide instances of naming a child after a dead relative and attributing the child's character to the influence (or presence) of the dead person.

**The 2040 Interpretation:**

The current interpretation of the Norse rebirth tradition is that it represents an alternative to — and perhaps a predecessor of — the Valhǫll/Hel afterlife system. The rebirth tradition is more ancient (it is "an old wives' tale" by the time of the *Helgakviða* colophon), more family-centered (the dead are reborn into their own kindred), and less concerned with the moral valuation of death (there is no reward or punishment in rebirth — only continuation). The Valhǫll/Hel system, by contrast, is newer, more socially stratified, and more moralized — and it may have displaced the rebirth tradition in the Viking Age, reducing it to the status of a marginal, "old wives'" belief.

## Lecture 9: Dying and Death Rituals — How the Dead Were Made

The Norse understanding of the afterlife was not merely a set of beliefs about what happens after death; it was enacted in the rituals surrounding dying, death, and the disposal of the dead. The way a person died — the manner of death, the rituals performed at death, the treatment of the corpse — determined, in part, where they went after death.

**The Good Death — Dying in Battle:**

The "good death" in the Viking Age was death in battle — the death that earned a place in Valhǫll. The sagas are full of accounts of warriors who, knowing they were about to die, arranged their deaths to maximize their chances of reaching Valhǫll:

- **The *Njáls saga* account of Gunnarr's death.** Gunnarr is attacked at his farm by his enemies. He fights heroically, killing many of his attackers, but his bowstring breaks. He asks his wife Hallgerðr for a strand of her hair to restring his bow; she refuses. Gunnarr laughs and says: "Every man has some gift that is his own." He throws down his weapon and dies fighting with his hands. The detail — the broken bowstring, the refused hair, the final laugh — is a literary construction, but it encodes the ideal of the good death: the warrior dies fighting, with his weapon in his hand, and with the courage to laugh at his own mortality.

- **The *Ragnarssona þáttr* account of Ragnarr loðbrók's death.** Ragnarr is captured by King Ella of Northumbria and thrown into a snake pit. As he dies, he sings a death song (the *Krákumál*) recounting his exploits and anticipating his reception in Valhǫll. The death song transforms the death — from a defeat to a transition, from an end to a beginning. Ragnarr dies not as a victim but as a hero, narrating his own death as the prelude to his afterlife.

**The Marked Death — Dying with Óðinn's Mark:**

The sagas describe a practice in which a dying person, too old or too sick to die in battle, was marked with a spear — the "spear-marking" (geirsoddung) — to dedicate them to Óðinn. The *Ynglinga saga* (ch. 10) describes Óðinn himself as instituting this practice:

> "Óðinn died of sickness in Sweden. But when he was near death, he had himself marked with the point of a spear, and he claimed for himself all men who died of weapons. He said that he was going to Goðheimr [the home of the gods] and would receive his friends there."

The spear-marking — the deliberate wound that simulated a battle death — was a ritual mechanism for redirecting the dying person from Hel (the destination of those who die of sickness) to Valhǫll (the destination of those who die by weapons). The practice indicates that the Norse believed the afterlife destination could be manipulated by ritual — that the manner of death, and the rituals performed at death, could override the natural course of the dying process and redirect the dead person to a different afterlife.

**Cremation and Burial:**

The archaeological evidence shows that cremation and burial coexisted throughout the Norse pre-Christian period, with regional and temporal variation:

- **Cremation** was the dominant rite in the Bronze Age and the early Iron Age, persisting into the Viking Age in some areas (eastern Sweden, the Baltic). The cremation released the dead person's spirit from the body, allowing it to travel to the afterlife. The burning of the corpse was also a ritual of transformation — the body was transformed into smoke and ash, which rose to the sky (the direction of the divine) and fell to the earth (the direction of the ancestors).

- **Burial** became more common in the Viking Age, particularly among the social elite. The burial (in a mound, a ship, or a chamber grave) preserved the body intact, allowing it to continue its existence in the mound. The burial was also a display of status — the size and wealth of the grave reflected the social importance of the deceased, and the mound was a permanent monument to the kindred's presence in the landscape.

The choice between cremation and burial probably reflected both religious belief (different afterlife destinations required different treatments of the body) and social status (different classes and different kindreds had different customs).

## Lecture 10: The Archaeology of Death — Graves, Goods, and Sacrifices

The archaeological evidence provides a material record of the Norse understanding of death and the afterlife that complements, and sometimes challenges, the textual sources. The graves, the grave goods, and the sacrificial deposits are not merely the remains of past rituals; they are the physical traces of a living belief system.

**Grave Goods — Provisioning the Dead:**

The provision of grave goods — objects placed in the grave with the dead — is one of the most consistent features of the Norse archaeological record. The goods varied by region, period, and social status, but they generally included:

- **Weapons** (swords, axes, spears, shields) — for the warrior dead, whose afterlife required the continuation of the warrior's role.
- **Tools** (hammers, knives, sickles, needles) — for the craftsman or the householder, whose afterlife required the continuation of productive work.
- **Jewelry and personal ornaments** (brooches, rings, pendants, beads) — for women and for the elite, whose afterlife required the continuation of social identity and status.
- **Food and drink** (animals, vessels, grain) — for sustenance in the afterlife, on the journey to the land of the dead, or for the feast with the ancestors.
- **Horses and dogs** — companions for the journey to the afterlife, mounts for the dead to ride, or offerings to the gods who received the dead.
- **Sledges, carts, and ships** — vehicles for the journey to the afterlife, or symbols of the social status that the dead would need in the underworld.

The provision of grave goods assumes that the dead need things — that the afterlife is a continuation of the life, and that the same material objects that served the person in life will serve them in death. This assumption is consistent with the Hel tradition (life in a diminished but material form) and with the mound-life tradition (the dead continue to exist in their graves), but it is not obviously consistent with the Valhǫll tradition (the einherjar are fed by the boar Sæhrímnir and the mead of the goat Heiðrún — they do not need their own provisions).

**Ship Graves — The Journey to the Afterlife:**

The ship grave — the burial of the dead in a ship, either on land (under a mound) or at sea (set adrift and burned) — is one of the most dramatic and distinctive features of the Norse archaeological record. The ship graves include:

- **The Oseberg ship** (Norway, c. 834) — a large, elaborately decorated ship containing the bodies of two women (one elderly, of very high status; one younger, possibly a servant or sacrifice), a wealth of grave goods (textiles, furniture, kitchen equipment, animals), and a full complement of oars, mast, and sails. Oseberg is the most spectacular ship grave of the Viking Age — the burial of a queen or a priestess, in a ship that was meant to carry her to the afterlife.

- **The Gokstad ship** (Norway, c. 890) — a large warship containing the body of a man and a rich assortment of grave goods. The Gokstad ship was buried under a mound, on land, suggesting that the ship's function was not literal (carrying the dead to the afterlife by sea) but symbolic (the ship as the vehicle for the journey, regardless of the geographical context of the grave).

- **The Sutton Hoo ship** (England, c. 625) — the most famous ship grave of the early Anglo-Saxon period, containing a rich assemblage of grave goods (a helmet, weapons, jewelry, feasting equipment) and no body (the body may have decayed entirely in the acidic soil, or the grave may have been a cenotaph — a memorial without a corpse).

The ship grave phenomenon is consistent with the Norse understanding of death as a journey — the dead travel to the afterlife, and the ship is the vehicle for that journey. The ship also symbolizes the deceased's social status (only the elite could afford a ship burial) and the deceased's relationship to the sea (the ship is the instrument of the Viking Age's maritime expansion, and the ship grave extends that maritime identity into death).

**Human Sacrifice in the Grave:**

The archaeological evidence attests to the practice of human sacrifice at the burial of high-status individuals — the killing of a servant, a slave, or a captive to accompany the deceased to the afterlife. The Oseberg ship contains the body of a second woman, who may have been sacrificed to accompany the primary deceased. The account of the Rus chieftain's funeral in Ibn Fadlan's *Risāla* (c. 922) describes the sacrifice of a slave girl who volunteers to die with her master — a detailed and disturbing account that is consistent with the archaeological evidence for human sacrifice in the grave.

The practice of human sacrifice in the grave reflects the Norse understanding that the dead needed companions in the afterlife — servants to attend them, warriors to fight for them, companions to share the feast. The sacrifice was also a display of status: the more important the deceased, the more companions they required, and the more elaborate the funeral that provided them.

## Lecture 11: The Christianization of Death — From Valhǫll to Heaven

The conversion of Scandinavia transformed the Norse understanding of death as profoundly as it transformed the understanding of the sacred landscape. The old afterlife destinations — Valhǫll, Hel, Fólkvangr, the mound — were replaced by the Christian Heaven and Hell, and the old death rituals — the blót, the spear-marking, the ship grave — were replaced by the Christian sacraments.

**The Transition — Syncretism and Replacement:**

The Christianization of the Norse afterlife was not a simple replacement of one set of beliefs with another. It was a complex process of negotiation, in which elements of the old tradition were preserved, reinterpreted, or suppressed:

- **Valhǫll and the Christian Heaven.** The Valhǫll tradition did not become the Christian Heaven — it was too closely associated with the warrior-aristocratic values of the old religion, and with the practice of dying in battle (which the Christian religion discouraged). But the concept of a glorious afterlife for the righteous — a place of feasting, companionship, and eternal life — persisted, and it may have eased the transition from the pagan warrior ideal to the Christian ideal of the saint.

- **Hel and the Christian Hell.** The Norse Hel (the neutral realm of the dead) was transformed into the Christian Hell (the place of eternal punishment for the wicked). The linguistic continuity — the same word, *hel*, for both the Norse underworld and the Christian damnation — facilitated the transformation, but it also obscured the difference between the two concepts: the pre-Christian Hel was not a place of punishment (except at Nástrǫnd), but the Christian Hell was defined by punishment.

- **The mound and the churchyard.** The old practice of burial in the family mound was replaced by burial in the churchyard — the consecrated ground of the Christian community. But the transition was gradual: the earliest Christian graves in Scandinavia were often located near the old burial mounds, and the practice of making offerings to the dead (now in the form of Christian prayers and masses for the dead) persisted.

**The Rejection of Valhǫll:**

The Christian rejection of Valhǫll was theological and explicit. The saga accounts of conversion describe the missionary's argument that Valhǫll was false — that Óðinn was not a god but a man, that the dead did not feast in Valhǫll but were judged by the true God, and that the warrior's death was not an achievement but a tragedy (unless the warrior was a Christian, in which case his death was a martyrdom).

The *Óláfs saga Tryggvasonar* describes the missionary Þangbrandr's encounter with the pagan chieftain Hallr á Síðu, who asks: "Where is Óðinn now?" Þangbrandr replies: "Our God has triumphed over Óðinn, and he is now in Hell, bound with chains." The exchange is polemical, but it captures the Christian perspective: the old gods are not just false — they are defeated, imprisoned, and powerless.

**The Persistence of the Folk Tradition:**

Despite the Christianization of the formal theology of death, elements of the old afterlife beliefs persisted in the folk tradition:

- **The landvættir and the álfar** continued to be acknowledged as powers of place, particularly in the rural areas of Scandinavia and Iceland. The dead continued to be believed to reside in their mounds, and offerings continued to be made at the mounds, at least into the 13th century (when the sagas were written) and probably later.

- **The draugr tradition** persisted in Icelandic folklore well into the modern period, with stories of "walking dead" who haunted the living and required the intervention of a priest or a folk-healer to be laid to rest. The Christianization of the draugr is visible in the stories: the draugr was increasingly associated with the devil and with black magic, and the means of disposing of it were increasingly Christian (holy water, the sign of the cross, the burial in consecrated ground).

## Lecture 12: The Afterlife in Modern Heathenry — Reconstruction, Innovation, and Practice

The modern Ásatrú and Heathen movement has engaged with the Norse afterlife traditions in complex and varied ways. Some practitioners accept the Valhǫll/Hel system as a literal description of the afterlife; others interpret it metaphorically, psychologically, or symbolically; and still others reject it entirely in favor of a more general conception of "going to the ancestors."

**The Reconstructionist Position:**

Reconstructionist Heathenry attempts to recover and practice the pre-Christian Norse afterlife beliefs as they were historically understood. Reconstructionists generally accept the Valhǫll/Hel system (often supplemented by other destinations such as Fólkvangr and the mound), and they practice death rituals derived from the saga accounts and the archaeological evidence:

- **The funeral ritual** is often structured around the blót format, with a sacrifice (usually symbolic — mead or ale rather than animal blood) to the god who will receive the deceased.
- **The mound or grave** is marked with a stone or a rune-inscribed memorial, and the dead is honored with a sumbel (ritual toast) at the funeral feast.
- **The ancestor-cult** is practiced through regular offerings to the dead (at the grave, at the hǫrgr, or at a household altar), maintaining the relationship between the living and the dead that was central to the pre-Christian tradition.

**The Metaphorical Position:**

Some modern Heathens interpret the afterlife traditions metaphorically rather than literally:

- **Valhǫll** is not a literal hall in the sky but a metaphor for the honor that the community gives to its warriors (and, more broadly, to those who live courageously, who fight for their values, and who die with dignity).
- **Hel** is not a literal underworld ruled by a goddess but a metaphor for the natural process of death and decay — the return of the individual to the earth, the dissolution of the self into the ancestral past.
- **The mound** is not a literal dwelling of the dead but a symbol of the kindred's continuity — the living remember the dead, and the dead live on in the memory of the living.

The metaphorical position is sometimes criticized by reconstructionists as "cheating" — claiming the mythology without believing in it. But its advocates argue that the metaphorical interpretation is more honest (it does not require belief in propositions that the modern mind cannot accept) and more universal (it makes the Norse afterlife relevant to people who are not Heathens and who do not share the Norse theological framework).

**The Ancestor-Focused Position:**

A growing movement within modern Heathenry de-emphasizes the Valhǫll/Hel system and focuses instead on the ancestor-cult — the relationship between the living and their own dead, the honor due to the ancestors, and the continuation of the kindred across the generations. The ancestor-focused position draws on the mound-life tradition (the dead continue in their mounds) and on the broader Indo-European ancestor-veneration tradition, and it is often combined with an ecological or land-centered spirituality (the ancestors are connected to the land, and honoring them is honoring the land).

**The University of Yggdrasil Position:**

The University of Yggdrasil's Norse Pagan Studies program does not privilege any one modern interpretation over the others. The program teaches the historical sources, the scholarly methods, and the range of contemporary interpretations, and it encourages students to develop their own theological position through critical engagement with the material. The program does, however, insist that all interpretations — reconstructionist, metaphorical, ancestor-focused, and others — be grounded in an honest engagement with the sources and with the historical scholarship, rather than in wishful thinking or in the uncritical acceptance of popular or received versions of the Norse afterlife.
