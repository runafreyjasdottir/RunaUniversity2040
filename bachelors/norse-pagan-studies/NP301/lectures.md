## Lecture 1: What Is Seiðr? — Definitions, Sources, and Methodological Problems

Seiðr is the single most difficult subject in Norse religious studies — a practice that is described in the sources as powerful, dangerous, shameful, and feminine, and that combines elements of divination, healing, cursing, and spirit-communication in a single ritual complex. Understanding seiðr requires confronting the methodological challenges of the sources, the cultural biases of the medieval authors, and the profound otherness of the practice.

**The Sources:**

The primary sources for seiðr are:

1. **The Poetic Edda** — The *Vǫluspa* (the vǫlva's prophecy), the *Lokasenna* (Loki's accusations against Óðinn for practicing seiðr), and the *Hávamál* (Óðinn's account of his magical knowledge).
2. **The Prose Edda** — Snorri's Gylfaginning and the *Ynglinga saga*, which attribute the teaching of seiðr to Freyja and the Vanir, and describe its practice by Óðinn.
3. **The Íslendingasögur** (Family Sagas) — The *Eiríks saga rauða*, the *Laxdæla saga*, the *Vatnsdœla saga*, and others, which describe seiðr rituals and practitioners in the Norwegian and Icelandic context.
4. **The fornaldarsögur** (Legendary Sagas) — The *Ǫrvar-Odds saga*, the *Hrólfs saga kraka*, and others, which describe seiðr in a legendary, less historically reliable context.
5. **The archaeological evidence** — The staff-burials (female graves containing metal staffs, probably ritual staffs), the cult objects, and the sacrificial deposits.
6. **Comparative sources** — The Sami shamanic tradition (the noaidi), the Finno-Ugric and Siberian shamanic traditions, and the broader Indo-European magical tradition.

**The Problems of the Sources:**

The textual sources for seiðr were written by Christians, in a Christian context, two centuries or more after the conversion. The authors' attitudes toward seiðr were complex:

- **Hostility.** Seiðr is consistently described as shameful, dangerous, and associated with ergi (the Norse concept of sexual perversion, specifically the receptive role in male homosexual intercourse). The *Ynglinga saga* attributes the teaching of seiðr to the Vanir, whom it characterizes as lesser, feminized gods (in contrast to the martial, masculine Æsir).

- **Fascination.** Despite their hostility, the saga authors describe seiðr in vivid, detailed, and often sympathetic terms. The vǫlva in the *Eiríks saga rauða* — Þorbjǫrg Lítilvǫlva ("Little Vǫlva") — is presented as a dignified, respected, and powerful figure, not as a shameful practitioner of a despised art.

- **Ignorance.** The saga authors were writing about a practice that they had never witnessed (seiðr was extinct as a living tradition by the 13th century) and that they understood only through secondhand accounts, literary conventions, and their own Christian-influenced imagination.

The 2040 approach to seiðr acknowledges these problems and addresses them through:

- **Multi-source triangulation** — comparing different sources and different types of evidence to identify consistent patterns.
- **Comparative analysis** — using the Sami noaidi tradition, the Siberian shamanic tradition, and the historical accounts of circumpolar shamanism to illuminate elements of seiðr that are opaque in the Norse sources alone.
- **Archaeological integration** — using the material evidence (the staff-graves, the cult objects) to test the textual accounts and to provide an independent witness to the practice.
- **Critical distance** — recognizing that any reconstruction of seiðr is provisional, that the sources are always partial and biased, and that the pre-Christian reality was probably more variable, more regional, and more complex than any single reconstruction can capture.

## Lecture 2: The Vǫlva — The Staff-Bearer and Her Practice

The vǫlva (pl. vǫlur) was the most important seiðr practitioner in the Norse tradition — a female seer, prophetess, and ritual specialist who used a staff (vǫlr) as her primary ritual instrument. The vǫlva is known from the Poetic Edda (the *Vǫluspa* — "The Vǫlva's Prophecy"), from the sagas, and from the archaeological evidence (the staff-graves).

**The Vǫluspa — The Vǫlva's Prophecy:**

The *Vǫluspa* is the single most important text for understanding the vǫlva's role. The poem opens with the vǫlva addressing Óðinn:

> "Listen to me, all holy kindreds,  
> great and small, children of Heimdallr.  
> You wish me, Valfǫðr, well to tell  
> the ancient stories of men — the first that I remember." (*Vǫluspa*, v. 1)

The vǫlva speaks with authority — she commands the attention of "all holy kindreds" (the gods themselves) and of Óðinn (Valfǫðr, "Father of the Slain"). She knows the past ("the ancient stories of men") and the future (the prophecy of Ragnarǫk), and she speaks in the first person — she is not merely a conduit for divine knowledge but an active, authoritative voice.

The vǫlva's knowledge is comprehensive: she knows the creation of the world, the golden age of the gods, the coming of the giantesses (the Norns), the first war (the Æsir-Vanir war), the death of Baldr, and the final battle of Ragnarǫk. Her knowledge is the knowledge of the cosmic order — the whole of the mythological narrative, from creation to destruction to renewal.

But the vǫlva is not a goddess. She is a human woman — or was once human. She is "raised from the dead" (v. 1, *ek man jǫtna ár um borna* — "I remember the giants born in ancient times," implying that she was present at the creation, but the verb *man* "remember" suggests that she is recalling events she witnessed, not events she was told about). The ambiguity of the vǫlva's nature — human or divine, dead or alive, remembering or prophesying — is central to her power: she stands at the boundary between the living and the dead, the human and the divine, the known and the unknown.

**The Saga Vǫlur:**

The sagas provide more practical, less cosmic accounts of the vǫlva's practice. The most famous account is in the *Eiríks saga rauða* (ch. 4), which describes the vǫlva Þorbjǫrg Lítilvǫlva:

- **The ritual context.** Þorbjǫrg is summoned during a famine to determine when the famine will end. The community gathers for the ritual, and a special seat (the seiðhjallr — "seiðr-platform") is prepared for her.

- **The costume.** Þorbjǫrg wears a remarkable costume: a blue cloak set with stones, a black lambskin hood lined with white catskin, calfskin shoes, and gloves of catskin. She carries a staff (stafr) decorated with brass and with a knob set with stones.

- **The ritual meal.** Þorbjǫrg is served a meal of goat's milk and the hearts of all the animals that had been slaughtered. The specificity of the meal suggests that it was ritually prescribed — the seiðr-worker required a specific diet before performing the ritual.

- **The assistant.** Þorbjǫrg requires a woman who knows the varðlokkur — the "ward-songs" or "calling-songs" that summon the spirits. Guðríðr, a Christian woman who remembers the varðlokkur from her foster-mother, is persuaded to sing.

- **The trance.** As Guðríðr sings the varðlokkur, Þorbjǫrg enters a trance state. She is described as "sitting on the seiðr-platform" (seiðhjallr) and speaking with authority about the future of the community and the individuals present.

The *Eiríks saga rauða* account is the most detailed description of a vǫlva's ritual in any source. It provides information about the vǫlva's costume, her ritual meal, her assistant, the varðlokkur (the calling-songs), the seiðr-platform, and the trance state — all of which are consistent with the broader seiðr tradition as reconstructed from other sources and from comparative analysis.

**The Staff-Graves — The Archaeological Evidence:**

The staff-burials are the only direct archaeological evidence for the vǫlva tradition. Over 50 female graves containing iron staffs (usually 60-80 cm long, with a basket-shaped cage at the top, often containing seeds, herbs, or small objects) have been identified in Scandinavia, dating from the 6th to the 11th centuries. The staffs are:

- **Consistently female.** Every staff-burial whose sex can be determined contains a female skeleton. The staff is a gendered object — it belongs to women, and it identifies the deceased as a vǫlva or a seiðkona.
- **Richly accompanied.** The staff-graves are among the richest female graves of the Norse period, containing exotic objects (imported beads, foreign textiles, rare amulets), elaborate jewelry, and sometimes animal sacrifices. The wealth of the graves indicates that the vǫlva was a high-status figure — not a marginal outcast but a respected and well-compensated ritual specialist.
- **Distributed across Scandinavia.** The staff-graves are found in Norway, Sweden, Denmark, Iceland, and the Norse colonies (the British Isles, the Faroes), indicating that the vǫlva tradition was a pan-Scandinavian phenomenon, not a regional variant.

The staff-graves confirm the saga accounts of the vǫlva's ritual importance and her high social status, even as the textual sources condemn seiðr as ergi and shameful. The contradiction between the textual hostility and the archaeological honor suggests that the relationship between seiðr and the broader Norse society was more complex than the textual sources alone indicate — seiðr may have been marginalized and honored simultaneously, condemned by the dominant warrior ideology but respected and sought after by the wider community.

## Lecture 3: The Ergi Complex — Gender, Sexuality, and the Shame of Seiðr

The accusation that seiðr was ergi is one of the most puzzling and the most revealing features of the Norse sources on magic. The word *ergi* (adj. *argr*, *ragr*) refers to sexual perversion, specifically the receptive role in male homosexual intercourse — a role that, in the Norse honor culture, was the most shameful and degrading position a man could occupy. The association of seiðr with ergi was sufficiently strong that the *Ynglinga saga* attributes the teaching of seiðr to the Vanir (the fertility gods, characterized as feminine in contrast to the martial, masculine Æsir) and describes the practice of seiðr by men as a shameful violation of the masculine code.

**The *Ynglinga saga* Account:**

The most explicit statement of the ergi of seiðr is in the *Ynglinga saga* (ch. 4):

> "Óðinn knew that skill [seiðr], and he practiced it himself, and it was said that it was accompanied by such great ergi that it was not considered fitting for men to practice it, and it was taught to the priestesses."

The passage is remarkable: it attributes seiðr to Óðinn (the chief of the gods), but it also condemns it as ergi, shameful, and unsuitable for men — with the result that the practice was "taught to the priestesses" (kennd at gyðjunum). The contradiction is unresolved — how could the chief god practice an art that was shameful for men? — but it is revealing: the practice of seiðr by men was a source of tension between the practical need for magical specialists (who might be male or female) and the ideological commitment to a masculine, warrior-dominated social order that required the denigration of any practice associated with the feminine.

**The *Lokasenna* Accusation:**

The association of seiðr with ergi is dramatized in the *Lokasenna* (Loki's Flyting), a poem in which Loki insults each of the gods in turn. His accusation against Óðinn is:

> "But you, they say, practiced seiðr on Samsey,  
> and beat on drums like a vǫlva,  
> in the form of a wizard (vitki) you traveled over the world;  
> that I call ergi behavior." (*Lokasenna*, v. 24)

The accusation combines three elements: the practice of seiðr, the gender reversal (Óðinn acted "like a vǫlva" — a female practitioner), and the explicit charge of ergi. The combination is consistent: seiðr is ergi because it involves a male practitioner adopting a female role — the role of the vǫlva, the staff-bearer, the woman who communicates with the spirits.

**The Gendering of Seiðr:**

The gendering of seiðr is part of a broader pattern in Norse culture that associated magic with women and with the feminine:

- **Women's magic** — The *gandr* (a magical beam or projectile), the *hamfǫr* (shape-journey, the soul traveling in animal form), and the *galdr* (chant-magic, discussed in NP201) could be practiced by men without ergi accusations. But seiðr — the trance-based, divinatory, spirit-communication form of magic — was specifically gendered as feminine.

- **The Vanir-Æsir gender binary.** The Vanir (Freyr, Freyja, Njǫrðr) are the gods of fertility, sexuality, and magic; the Æsir (Óðinn, Þórr, Týr) are the gods of war, law, and wisdom. The Vanir are feminized in contrast to the masculine Æsir, and their magic — seiðr — is also feminized.

- **The seiðmaðr problem.** Men who practiced seiðr (seiðmenn) existed — the sagas attest to several — but they were stigmatized as argr (ergi), and the practice was considered a violation of the masculine code. The seiðmaðr was a liminal figure: male in body but feminine in practice, powerful in magic but degraded in honor.

**The 2040 Interpretation: Ergi as Ritual Power:**

The current interpretation of the ergi complex is that the shame associated with seiðr was not an accidental byproduct of its gendering but an integral element of its power. The ergi accusation marked the seiðr-worker as outside the social order — not bound by the same rules of honor and shame as ordinary people, and therefore able to access powers that ordinary people could not. The ergi was, paradoxically, both the price of the seiðr-worker's power and the source of it: by occupying the lowest, most shameful position in the gender hierarchy, the seiðr-worker gained access to the Otherworld.

This interpretation is consistent with the comparative evidence from Siberian shamanism (in which the shaman is often a liminal, gender-ambiguous figure — the "third gender" shaman, the "soft man," the "woman-man" — whose access to the spirit world is mediated through their violation of gender norms) and with the broader anthropological understanding of ritual power as often associated with the inversion of social norms.

## Lecture 4: The Seiðhjallr and the Varðlokkur — The Technology of Trance

The seiðr ritual required specific technological apparatus — a platform (the seiðhjallr), a staff (the vǫlr or stafr), and a specific form of chanting (the varðlokkur or "ward-songs"). These elements were not incidental to the ritual — they were the technology of trance, the means by which the seiðr-worker entered the altered state of consciousness that permitted communication with the spirits.

**The Seiðhjallr — The Ritual Platform:**

The seiðhjallr ("seiðr-platform" or "spell-frame") was a raised platform or seat on which the vǫlva sat during the ritual. The *Eiríks saga rauða* describes Þorbjǫrg sitting on the seiðhjallr, and the *Vǫlsa þáttr* (a short tale preserved in the *Flateyjarbók*) describes a seiðhjallr used for the worship of the phallic horse cult. The seiðhjallr had several functions:

- **Elevation.** The platform raised the vǫlva above the ordinary level of the community, physically expressing her elevated status (she is above the community) and her liminal position (she is between the earth and the sky, the human and the divine).
- **Stability.** The platform provided a stable, comfortable position for the trance — a state that requires physical stillness and comfort to be maintained.
- **Protection.** The platform may have provided magical protection — a bounded space within which the seiðr-worker was safe from the spirits she summoned.

**The Varðlokkur — The Calling-Songs:**

The varðlokkur (singular: varðlokka) were the "ward-songs" or "calling-songs" that summoned the spirits to the seiðr ritual. The *Eiríks saga rauða* describes Guðríðr singing the varðlokkur while Þorbjǫrg enters trance:

> "The women formed a circle around the seiðhjallr, and Guðríðr sang the song so beautifully and so well that no one who was present thought they had heard a song sung with a more beautiful voice. The vǫlva thanked her for the song, and said that many spirits had now come to her that before had been reluctant and unwilling to listen."

The account provides several details about the varðlokkur:

- **The song summoned the spirits.** The spirits were "reluctant" before the song and "came to her" after it. The varðlokkur was not merely an accompaniment to the ritual but an operative element of it — the mechanism by which the spirits were called.
- **The song was beautiful.** Guðríðr's voice is praised in aesthetic terms — the varðlokkur was not a harsh, dissonant chant like the galdr (which involved shouting and vigorous performance, as described in NP201) but a beautiful, melodic song.
- **The song required a specific singer.** Þorbjǫrg asked specifically for a woman who knew the varðlokkur, and Guðríðr — the only woman present who knew the song — was initially reluctant to sing it because she was a Christian and feared the spiritual contamination of the pagan ritual. The specificity of the singer indicates that the varðlokkur was a learned tradition — not a spontaneous improvisation but a specific, transmitted repertoire.

**The Staff (Vǫlr / Stafr):**

The vǫlva's staff was her primary ritual instrument. The staff-graves provide the archaeological evidence; the sagas provide the textual descriptions. The staff was:

- **A symbol of authority.** The staff was the vǫlva's badge of office — the object that identified her as a vǫlva and that was buried with her as her most important possession.
- **A magical instrument.** The staff was used in the ritual — the vǫlva carried it, struck with it (perhaps to summon spirits or to mark the boundaries of the ritual space), and directed it at the objects or persons that were the focus of her magic.
- **A connection to the World Tree.** The staff was probably conceived as a miniature World Tree — a representation of Yggdrasill, the axis mundi that connected the worlds. The vǫlva's staff was the axis mundi in her hand — the instrument through which she traveled between the worlds.

**The Comparative Framework — Siberian Shamanism:**

The closest comparative parallel to the Norse seiðr ritual is the Sami noaidi tradition and the broader Siberian shamanic tradition. The Sami noaidi used a drum (not a staff) to enter trance, and the drum — beaten rhythmically — served the same function as the varðlokkur: the rhythmic stimulation that induces the trance state. The Sami joik (a form of chanting, similar to the varðlokkur) was also used to summon the spirits and to facilitate the noaidi's journey to the spirit world.

The Siberian shamanic tradition provides a parallel to the seiðhjallr: the shaman's platform (a raised seat or a specially constructed ritual space) was used to elevate the shaman above the community and to provide a stable position for the trance. The Siberian tradition also provides a parallel to the ergi complex: the "third gender" shaman (the "soft man," the "woman-man") was a recognized category of ritual specialist in many Siberian cultures, and their access to the spirit world was mediated through their violation of gender norms.

## Lecture 5: The Trance State — Neurophenomenology and the Seiðr Journey

The trance state was the operational core of seiðr — the altered state of consciousness in which the vǫlva traveled to the Otherworld, communicated with the spirits, and acquired the knowledge that she brought back to the community. Understanding the trance state requires the integration of the textual evidence, the comparative evidence, and the 2040 neurophenomenology of altered states.

**The Textual Evidence for Trance:**

The sagas describe the vǫlva's trance state in terms that are consistent across different accounts:

- **Immobility.** The vǫlva sits still on the seiðhjallr, not moving, not speaking, not responding to external stimuli. The trance is a state of physical stillness — the body is still while the spirit travels.
- **Absence.** The vǫlva's spirit (hugr) is described as having left her body — she "sends her spirit" (senda hug sinn) to the Otherworld, and her body remains behind as an empty vessel.
- **Return.** The vǫlva "returns" from the trance with knowledge of the future, the cause of misfortune, or the identities of the spirits that are causing harm. The return is described as a re-immersion in the body — the spirit re-entering the vessel that it has left.

The *Ǫrvar-Odds saga* provides a particularly vivid description of the trance state. The seiðkona Heiðr, summoned by the king, enters trance:

> "Heiðr sat on the seiðhjallr, and she seemed to be asleep, and a deep sleep came over her."

The description of the trance as "sleep" — a deep, unusual sleep in which the sleeper's spirit travels — is consistent with the comparative evidence from shamanic traditions, in which the shaman's journey is often described as a "sleep" or a "dream."

**The Neurophenomenology of Trance:**

The 2040 neurophenomenology of altered states — the interdisciplinary study of the neurological and phenomenological properties of trance — provides a framework for understanding the seiðr trance in terms that are accessible to the modern mind.

- **Trance as an altered state of consciousness.** Trance is characterized by changes in brain-wave patterns (increased theta and delta activity, decreased alpha and beta activity), dissociation (the separation of awareness from the body), and the experience of non-ordinary reality (visions, spirit-communication, journeying).

- **Trance induction.** The seiðr trance was probably induced through a combination of rhythmic auditory stimulation (the varðlokkur), postural manipulation (sitting elevated on the seiðhjallr), sensory deprivation or overload (the ritual darkness, the firelight, the incense or smoke), and possibly psychoactive substances (the evidence for Norse use of psychoactive plants is debated but not conclusive).

- **Trance content.** The content of the seiðr trance — the visions, the spirit-communication, the journey to the Otherworld — was shaped by the vǫlva's cultural expectations, her training, and the needs of the community. The trance was not an unstructured hallucination but a culturally patterned experience — the vǫlva went where she was expected to go (the spirit world, the realm of the dead, the field of battle) and brought back the knowledge that the community needed.

**The Spirits of Seiðr:**

The seiðr trance involved communication with spirits — the non-human beings who inhabited the Otherworld and who could be summoned, consulted, and sometimes compelled by the vǫlva. The sources identify several categories of seiðr-spirits:

- **The dead.** The most common objects of seiðr communication were the dead — the ancestors, the recent dead, the spirits of those who had died violently or unjustly. The vǫlva could summon the dead, question them, and compel them to reveal hidden knowledge.
- **The landvættir.** The land-spirits, discussed in NP205, could be consulted through seiðr — the vǫlva could determine whether the landvættir were favorably disposed toward the community, and how they could be propitiated.
- **The dísir.** The female protective spirits, associated with the kindred and with Freyja, could be summoned through seiðr — the dísablót was the community ritual for the dísir, but the vǫlva's individual communication with the dísir was a form of seiðr.
- **The gods?** The sagas occasionally describe seiðr as a means of communicating with the gods, but the gods are not the primary objects of seiðr — the spirits (the dead, the landvættir, the dísir) are the vǫlva's primary interlocutors.

## Lecture 6: Seiðr and Divination — Knowing the Future, Revealing the Hidden

The primary function of seiðr in the saga accounts is divination — the acquisition of knowledge about the future, the hidden, or the distant. The vǫlva was summoned to answer questions that could not be answered by ordinary means: when will the famine end? Who has stolen the goods? Who is the hidden enemy? The divinatory function of seiðr distinguishes it from other forms of Norse magic (the galdr, which was primarily performative — chanting to achieve a specific effect; the rune-magic, which was primarily operative — carving runes to curse or to heal) and connects it to the broader category of mantle (divination) practices in the ancient world.

**The Forms of Seiðr Divination:**

The saga accounts describe several specific forms of seiðr divination:

- **Prognostication.** The vǫlva predicts future events — the end of the famine, the outcome of a battle, the fate of an individual. The prediction is authoritative and specific: "the famine will end next spring" (*Eiríks saga rauða*).

- **Identification of the hidden.** The vǫlva reveals what is hidden — the identity of a thief, the location of stolen goods, the name of a secret enemy. The revelation is often dramatic: the vǫlva points to the guilty party, and the community acts on her revelation.

- **The cause of misfortune.** The vǫlva diagnoses the spiritual cause of misfortune — which spirit is causing the sickness, which dead person is haunting the farm, which landvættir is offended. The diagnosis allows the community to address the spiritual cause of its suffering.

**The Mechanism of Seiðr Divination:**

The mechanism of seiðr divination was the vǫlva's journey to the Otherworld, where the spirits revealed the hidden knowledge. The journey was not metaphorical but real — the vǫlva's hugr (mind/spirit) actually traveled to the realm of the dead, to the land of the spirits, to the field of Óðinn's choosing, and brought back the knowledge that was available there.

The divinatory mechanism assumed that:

- **The spirits know what the living do not.** The dead see the future; the landvættir know the causes of the land's affliction; the dísir know the fate of their kindred. The spirits have knowledge that is not accessible to the living, and the vǫlva's function is to access that knowledge.

- **The vǫlva can travel to the spirits' realm.** The vǫlva's staff is the axis mundi — the connection between the worlds. Her trance is the vehicle — the means by which her hugr leaves her body and travels to the Otherworld. Her varðlokkur is the summons — the call that compels the spirits to appear and to speak.

- **The spirits can be compelled to speak.** The vǫlva's relationship to the spirits is not supplication but command. She does not ask the spirits for information; she compels them to reveal it. The *Vǫluspa*'s vǫlva commands the spirits — "All holy kindreds, listen to me!" — not because she is their superior but because she knows the technique that forces them to respond.

**The Social Function of Seiðr Divination:**

Seiðr divination served a crucial social function in the Norse community: it provided answers to questions that had no other resolution. In a community without a developed legal system (or where the legal system had failed), the accusation of theft could be resolved by the vǫlva's revelation. In a community dependent on the fertility of the land, the uncertainty of famine could be resolved by the vǫlva's prophecy. In a community where personal enmity was often hidden, the identity of the secret enemy could be revealed by the vǫlva's divination.

The vǫlva's divinatory function was thus a form of social technology — a mechanism for resolving disputes, making decisions, and managing uncertainty that was as essential to the Norse community as the legal assembly (the þing) and the feast (the blót). The vǫlva was a community resource — a specialist whose knowledge was available to the community as a whole, not merely to the individual who hired her.

## Lecture 7: Seiðr and Healing — The Magical Medicine

Seiðr was not only a divinatory practice but also a healing practice. The vǫlva could diagnose the spiritual cause of illness (which spirit was causing the sickness, which dead person was haunting the patient) and could perform the ritual that removed the spiritual affliction.

**The Spiritual Etiology of Disease:**

The Norse understanding of illness was not exclusively spiritual — the sagas describe wounds, infections, and physical injuries in terms that are consistent with a practical, empirical understanding of disease. But the Norse also attributed illness to spiritual causes: the malice of a dead person who had not been properly buried, the anger of the landvættir who had been offended, the curse of a seiðr-worker who had been hired to harm. In these cases, the treatment was not physical (herbs, surgery, wound-dressing) but spiritual — the spirit causing the illness had to be identified, propitiated, or driven away.

The vǫlva's diagnosis was the first step: she identified the spirit that was causing the illness. The treatment was the second step: she performed the ritual that removed the spirit (through offerings, through compulsion, through the transfer of the illness to a substitute object or animal).

**Seiðr Healing in the Sagas:**

The *Kormáks saga* provides a vivid account of seiðr healing. The hero Kormákr is wounded in battle, and his wound will not heal — it festers, and he grows weaker. The vǫlva Þórveig diagnoses the spiritual cause: the elf-mound (álfhǫll) on the hill above the farm has been offended, and the álfar are causing the wound to fester. The treatment is to sacrifice a bull at the álfhǫll and to smear the blood on the wound. Kormákr refuses — he does not believe in the álfar — and the wound continues to fester. Eventually, he sacrifices the bull, and the wound begins to heal.

The *Kormáks saga* account illustrates the logic of seiðr healing: the illness has a spiritual cause (the offended álfar), and the treatment is a ritual act (the sacrifice) that propitiates the offended spirit. The vǫlva's role is to identify the cause and to prescribe the treatment; the patient's role is to follow the prescription (or to suffer the consequences of refusing).

**The Comparative Framework — Shamanic Healing:**

The comparative evidence from shamanic traditions provides a framework for understanding seiðr healing. In Siberian shamanism, illness is often attributed to "soul loss" (the patient's soul has been stolen by a spirit) or to "spirit intrusion" (a malign spirit has entered the patient's body). The shaman's healing functions are:

- **Soul retrieval** — the shaman journeys to the spirit world, retrieves the patient's stolen soul, and returns it to the patient's body.
- **Spirit extraction** — the shaman removes the intruding spirit from the patient's body, often through sucking (the shaman sucks the spirit out through a tube or a pipe), through blowing (the shaman blows the spirit out with his breath), or through transfer (the shaman transfers the spirit to a substitute object).

The Norse sources do not describe "soul retrieval" or "spirit extraction" in the technical sense of the shamanic tradition, but the underlying logic is the same: illness has a spiritual cause, and the ritual specialist is the one who can identify and remove the spiritual cause.

## Lecture 8: Seiðr and Cursing — The Dark Side of the Magic

Seiðr was not only a divinatory and healing practice but also a cursing practice — the seiðr-worker could use her magic to harm her enemies, to blight their land, to cause their deaths, and to bring misfortune on entire communities. The dark side of seiðr is well-attested in the sources, and it was the primary reason for the fear and hostility that surrounded the practice.

**The Forms of Seiðr Cursing:**

The saga accounts describe several specific forms of seiðr cursing:

- **The weather-curse.** The vǫlva could raise storms, calm the sea, or call down rain — controlling the weather to harm her enemies' ships, to destroy their crops, or to prevent their travel. The *Laxdæla saga* describes a seiðkona who raises a storm against a ship at sea, causing it to founder and drown its crew.

- **The death-curse.** The vǫlva could cause the death of a specific individual — not through direct violence but through magical means. The curse might operate through the individual's fylgja (the supernatural double) — the vǫlva attacked the fylgja, and the individual sickened and died.

- **The land-curse.** The vǫlva could blight the land — causing crops to fail, animals to die, and the land to become barren. The curse on the land might be operated through the landvættir — the vǫlva turned the land-spirits against the community, and the land itself became hostile to human habitation.

- **The battle-curse.** The vǫlva could affect the outcome of a battle — weakening the enemy's warriors, strengthening her own side, or causing confusion and panic among the enemy. The *Vǫlsunga saga* describes a seiðkona who causes the hero Sigmundr's sword to break in battle, leading to his death.

**The *Gandr* — The Magical Projectile:**

The *gandr* was a magical beam or projectile that the seiðr-worker could send through the air to strike a distant target. The *gandr* is described in the *Gylfaginning* (in the context of Þórr's encounter with the giant Hrungnir) and in several saga accounts. The *gandr* was visible to those with second sight — it appeared as a beam of light, a flash, or a dark shape moving through the air. The *gandr* could cause illness, death, or misfortune in the target it struck.

The *gandr* concept is consistent with the broader Norse understanding of magic as a force that could be projected through space — the seiðr-worker's will, directed by the staff, transformed into a projectile that could strike its target at a distance. The *gandr* is the active, aggressive aspect of seiðr — the magic that harms, rather than the magic that knows or heals.

**The *Niðstǫng* — The Scorn-Pole:**

The *niðstǫng* (níðstǫng, "scorn-pole" or "shame-pole") was a form of seiðr cursing directed at an entire community. The niðstǫng consisted of a pole (often a horse's head on a pole) erected on the boundary of the offending community's land, accompanied by a curse that called down the wrath of the landvættir on the community. The *Egil's saga* describes the erection of a niðstǫng against King Eiríkr Bloodaxe and Queen Gunnhildr:

> "Egill went ashore on an island, took a hazel pole in his hand, and went out to a rocky cliff facing the land. He took a horse's head and fixed it on the pole. Then he spoke these words: 'Here I set up a niðstǫng, and I turn this nið against King Eiríkr and Queen Gunnhildr.' He turned the horse's head to face the land. 'I turn this nið against the landvættir who inhabit this land, so that all of them may go astray and none of them find their home until they have driven King Eiríkr and Queen Gunnhildr from the land.'" (*Egils saga*, ch. 57)

The niðstǫng was a ritual act that weaponized the landvættir against the offending community — the curse turned the land-spirits hostile, and the land itself became uninhabitable until the offending rulers were driven out. The niðstǫng is the most public and the most political form of seiðr — a curse directed not at an individual but at a regime.

## Lecture 9: The Archaeology of Seiðr — Staffs, Amulets, and Cult Objects

The archaeological evidence for seiðr is sparser than the textual evidence but, in some respects, more reliable — it provides a material record of the practice that is independent of the textual biases and the Christian framing. The staff-graves, the amulets, and the cult objects are the physical remains of the seiðr tradition.

**The Staff-Graves (Discussed in Detail):**

The staff-graves (first introduced in Lecture 2) are the most important archaeological evidence for the vǫlva tradition. The 2040 catalog of staff-burials, compiled by the University of Yggdrasil's Seiðr Research Project, identifies over 50 staff-graves with the following characteristics:

- **Gender:** All are female.
- **Date:** 6th-11th centuries, with a concentration in the 9th-10th centuries (the high Viking Age).
- **Distribution:** Norway (30%), Sweden (40%), Denmark (15%), Iceland (5%), the Norse colonies (10%).
- **Wealth:** The staff-graves are consistently among the richest female graves of their period and region.
- **Associated objects:** The staff is accompanied by a distinctive assemblage of objects: beads (often imported, exotic), animal amulets (birds, cats, bears), miniature chairs (perhaps the seiðhjallr in miniature), and sometimes rune-inscribed objects.

The staff itself is the defining object: an iron rod, 60-80 cm long, with a basket-shaped cage at the top (often containing seeds, herbs, or small objects). The basket is the most distinctive feature of the staff — it is unique to the staff-graves and has no parallel in the wider Norse archaeological record. The basket probably held the ritual substances (the herbs, the seeds, the small objects) that the vǫlva used in her magic.

**Amulets and Cult Objects:**

The archaeological record includes a variety of objects that may be associated with seiðr:

- **The miniature chairs.** Several staff-graves contain miniature chairs (3-5 cm tall), often made of silver or bronze. The miniature chairs probably represent the seiðhjallr — the ritual platform on which the vǫlva sat during the trance. The chair in the grave may have been a symbolic representation of the vǫlva's ritual function, placed with her to ensure that she would have her seiðhjallr in the afterlife.

- **The staff-rings.** Some staff-graves contain rings attached to the staff — iron rings, often threaded onto the staff at regular intervals. The rings may have been ritual noisemakers — when the staff was shaken, the rings rattled, producing a percussive sound that may have been part of the varðlokkur (the rhythmic auditory stimulation that induced the trance).

- **The animal figurines.** Several seiðr-related graves contain small animal figurines — birds (probably ravens, associated with Óðinn), cats (associated with Freyja), bears, and wolves. The animal figurines may represent the vǫlva's fylgjur (her supernatural animal doubles) or the spirits with which she communicated.

**The Seiðr Landscape:**

The 2040 landscape archaeology of seiðr has identified several sites that may have been seiðr ritual locations:

- **The Groa site, Norway.** A natural cave, excavated 2034-2038, containing a deposit of seeds, herbs, and small animal bones (birds, rodents), dating to the 9th-10th centuries. The deposit may be the remains of a seiðr ritual — the herbs and seeds are consistent with the contents of the staff-baskets, and the cave location is consistent with the liminal, underground character of the seiðr journey.

- **The Hǫrgr site, Trøndelag.** The hǫrgr (stone altar) at Horg, associated with animal sacrifice (discussed in NP205), may also have been used for seiðr ritual — the combination of sacrifice and divination is consistent with the *Eiríks saga rauða* account, in which the vǫlva's ritual occurs within the context of a community feast.

## Lecture 10: Seiðr and the Sami — Borrowing, Parallel Development, or Common Origin?

The relationship between the Norse seiðr tradition and the Sami noaidi (shamanic) tradition is one of the most debated questions in seiðr studies. The Sami — the indigenous people of northern Scandinavia — have a rich and well-documented shamanic tradition, and the textual and archaeological evidence suggests that the Norse and the Sami were in close contact throughout the pre-Christian period. The question is whether the Norse borrowed seiðr from the Sami, whether the two traditions developed independently from a common circumpolar shamanic substrate, or whether the similarities are superficial and misleading.

**The Evidence for Sami Influence:**

Several features of seiðr are consistent with Sami noaidi practice:

- **The trance technique.** The Sami noaidi used a drum to enter trance; the Norse vǫlva used the varðlokkur (the rhythmic chanting). Both traditions used rhythmic auditory stimulation to induce the altered state.

- **The spirit-journey.** Both traditions involve the ritual specialist's journey to the spirit world to acquire knowledge, to communicate with the dead, or to retrieve a lost soul.

- **The gender ambiguity.** The Sami noaidi tradition includes a recognized category of gender-ambiguous shamans (the "third gender" noaidi), parallel to the seiðmaðr tradition and the ergi complex.

- **The staff.** The Sami noaidi used a staff (the *meavrresgárri*, the "shaman's stick") that was functionally similar to the vǫlva's staff — a ritual instrument used in trance and in communication with the spirits.

**The Evidence Against Sami Influence:**

Against the Sami-influence hypothesis, several considerations argue for independent development or common origin:

- **The chronology.** The earliest seiðr graves (the staff-graves) date to the 6th century — earlier than the earliest firm evidence for Sami noaidi practice (the Sami archaeological record is less well-preserved than the Norse, but the earliest identifiable shaman graves date to the 9th-10th centuries). If seiðr precedes the Sami noaidi tradition, the direction of influence would be reversed (Sami borrowing from the Norse) or the two traditions would be independent.

- **The broader Indo-European magical tradition.** Divination, trance, and spirit-communication are well-attested in the broader Indo-European tradition (Greek oracles, Celtic druids, Indic rishis), and seiðr may descend from an Indo-European mantle tradition that is older and more widespread than the Sami connection.

- **The differences between the traditions.** Despite the similarities, there are significant differences between seiðr and the Sami noaidi tradition: the Sami drum is not attested in the Norse sources; the Norse seiðhjallr is not attested in the Sami sources; the Sami noaidi's relationship to the spirits is different from the vǫlva's (the noaidi negotiates; the vǫlva commands).

**The 2040 Consensus — Complex Interaction:**

The current consensus is that the relationship between seiðr and the Sami noaidi tradition was one of complex interaction rather than simple borrowing in either direction. The Norse and the Sami occupied adjacent territories for centuries, and their ritual specialists almost certainly knew of each other's practices. The similarities between the traditions may reflect mutual influence, common descent from a shared circumpolar shamanic substrate, and convergent evolution (similar environments and similar social needs produce similar ritual solutions).

The University of Yggdrasil's position is that the question of Sami influence is less important than the recognition that seiðr — whatever its origins — was a fully developed, sophisticated, and effective ritual system that served the Norse community's needs for divination, healing, cursing, and spirit-communication for centuries before the conversion.

## Lecture 11: The End of Seiðr — Persecution, Survival, and Transformation

The Christianization of Scandinavia brought the persecution of the seiðr tradition — the vǫlur were outlawed, their practices were condemned as devil-worship, and the staff-graves disappear from the archaeological record after the 11th century. But seiðr did not simply vanish. It transformed — elements of the tradition were absorbed into Christian folk magic, and elements survived in the Sami tradition (which was more resilient in the face of Christian persecution, due to the Sami's geographical and cultural distance from the centers of Christian power).

**The Persecution of Seiðr:**

The Norwegian and Icelandic law codes (the *Gulaþingslǫg*, the *Frostaþingslǫg*, the *Grágás*) contain specific prohibitions against seiðr, prescribing severe penalties for its practice:

- **Outlawry** — The practitioner of seiðr was to be outlawed, deprived of legal protection, and driven from the community.
- **Confiscation of property** — The practitioner's property was to be confiscated, removing the material basis of her practice.
- **Execution** — In some cases, the practitioner was to be executed — burned, drowned, or killed by the community.

The law codes' prohibitions are evidence that seiðr was still being practiced in the 12th-13th centuries — the laws would not have been enacted if the practice had already died out. But the prohibitions are also evidence that seiðr was being targeted for suppression — the Christian authorities were using the law to eliminate what they regarded as a diabolical practice.

**The Survival of Seiðr:**

Despite the persecution, elements of the seiðr tradition survived:

- **The Christian adaptation.** The vǫlva's function as a diviner and a healer was absorbed into Christian folk magic — the "wise woman" of the medieval and early modern Scandinavian village, who used herbs, charms, and prayers to heal the sick and to predict the future, was the descendant (in a diminished and Christianized form) of the pre-Christian vǫlva.

- **The Sami survival.** The Sami noaidi tradition survived the Christianization of Scandinavia (the Sami were slower to convert than the Norse, and their conversion was more superficial), and elements of the seiðr tradition may have been preserved in the Sami tradition, where they were less vulnerable to Christian persecution.

- **The folk tradition.** The Icelandic and Norwegian folk tradition preserves stories of vǫlur, seiðkonur, and magical practitioners into the 19th and 20th centuries. The folk tradition is not a direct continuation of the pre-Christian practice (the content of the stories has been transformed by Christian influence), but it attests to the persistence of the *idea* of the vǫlva in the Norse cultural imagination.

**The Modern Revival:**

The modern Ásatrú and Heathen movement has attempted to revive the seiðr tradition — or at least to reconstruct it from the historical sources. The modern seiðr revival is controversial: some practitioners claim a direct transmission of the tradition (a claim that is not supported by the historical evidence), while others acknowledge that modern seiðr is a reconstruction based on the textual and archaeological sources, comparative analysis, and the personal experience of the practitioners. The University of Yggdrasil's Norse Pagan Studies program teaches the historical seiðr tradition and the modern reconstruction; the latter is not assessed as a religious practice (the University does not prefer any religious position, including the reconstructionist one) but as a cultural phenomenon that shares the trajectory of all religious revivals — combining historical scholarship, personal experience, and creative innovation.

## Lecture 12: Seiðr and the Self — The Vǫlva's Journey and the Modern Seeker

Seiðr was not only a system of ritual technology — it was a practice of self-transformation. The vǫlva's journey to the Otherworld was also a journey into her own depths — an encounter with the spirits that dwelled within her as well as with those that dwelled beyond her. The ergi of seiðr — the shame and the marginalization of the practitioner — was not only a social position but an existential condition: the seiðr-worker, by occupying the lowest, most degraded position in the gender hierarchy, opened herself to the highest, most powerful position in the spiritual hierarchy.

**The Vǫlva as Liminal Being:**

The vǫlva was a liminal figure — she stood at the boundary between the living and the dead, the human and the divine, the male and the female, the honored and the shamed. Her liminality was the source of her power: because she was outside the ordinary categories of social life, she could access the powers that ordinary people could not. The antropological concept of "liminality" (from Victor Turner's work on ritual) provides a framework for understanding the vǫlva's position: she was a "permanent liminal" — a person whose entire social identity was defined by her position on the threshold, between categories, outside the norm.

**The Vǫlva's Journey as Shamanic Initiation:**

The vǫlva's journey — the trance that carried her to the Otherworld — was not merely a ritual technique but a form of initiation. The Siberian shamanic tradition describes the shaman's initiation as a journey to the spirit world, in which the shaman is dismembered by the spirits and reassembled — a symbolic death and rebirth that transforms the shaman from an ordinary person into a ritual specialist. The Norse sources do not describe the vǫlva's initiation in such explicit terms, but the logic of the shamanic initiation — the death of the old self and the birth of the new — is implicit in the vǫlva's journey to the realm of the dead.

**Seiðr and the Modern Seeker:**

The modern revival of seiðr — both within the Ásatrú community and in the broader Neopagan and spiritual-but-not-religious movements — is often framed in terms of personal transformation. The modern seiðr practitioner undertakes the journey to the Otherworld not primarily to divine the future or to heal the sick but to transform the self — to encounter the spirits, to confront the shadow, to integrate the dissociated aspects of the psyche. The modern seiðr revival is, in this respect, a form of depth psychology — a Jungian or post-Jungian practice that uses the techniques of the vǫlva tradition (the staff, the trance, the journey) to access the collective unconscious and to facilitate the individuation process.

Whether the modern seiðr revival is authentic (whether it is a genuine continuation of the pre-Christian tradition) is less important than whether it is effective (whether it produces the transformation that the practitioner seeks). The Norse Pagan Studies program does not adjudicate this question; it provides the historical knowledge, the source-critical methods, and the comparative framework that allow the student to make their own judgment.
