# NP303: Shamanic Elements in Norse Tradition
## Bachelor of Science in Norse Pagan Studies — University of Yggdrasil, 2040

**Credits:** 4
**Description:** Is there a shamanic stratum in Norse religion? This course examines the evidence for shamanic elements in pre-Christian Norse tradition — the techniques of trance, ecstasy, and world-tree journeying; the figure of the ritual specialist (vǫlva, seiðmaðr); the cosmological framework of Yggdrasill as axis mundi; and the relationship between the Norse tradition and the circumpolar shamanic complex. Drawing on the Poetic Edda (especially the *Hávamál* and the *Vǫluspa*), the sagas, the archaeological evidence, comparative shamanic studies, and the 2040 neurophenomenology of trance, students evaluate the shamanic interpretation of Norse religion and its implications for understanding the pre-Christian Norse worldview.

**Prerequisites:** NP201 (Ritual Practice), NP301 (Seiðr, Spá & Vǫlva Practice)
**Corequisite:** NP102 (Norse Cosmology) recommended

## Lecture 1: What Is Shamanism? — Definitions, Debates, and the Problem of Applicability

The term "shamanism" is one of the most contested in the study of religion. Originally a specific term for the ritual practice of the Tungus (Evenki) people of Siberia, "shamanism" has been generalized by scholars, anthropologists, and popular writers to describe a wide range of religious phenomena — trance, spirit-journeying, healing, divination, and ecstatic ritual — that are found across the circumpolar region and beyond. The question for this course is whether the Norse tradition includes shamanic elements, and what it means if it does.

**The Tungus Origin and the Problem of Generalization:**

The word "shaman" comes from the Tungus (Evenki) *šamān*, which refers specifically to the ritual specialist of the Tungus people — a person who enters an altered state of consciousness (trance) to journey to the spirit world, to communicate with spirits, and to acquire knowledge and power on behalf of the community. The Tungus shaman uses a drum, a costume, and the assistance of spirits (animal and ancestral) to make the journey, and the shaman's primary functions are healing, divination, and the escort of the dead to the afterlife.

The generalization of "shamanism" to other traditions has been criticized by some scholars as a form of academic colonialism — imposing a Tungus-specific term on traditions that have their own names, their own concepts, and their own distinct practices. The criticism is valid: the Sami noaidi is not the same as the Siberian shaman, the Norse vǫlva is not the same as the Sami noaidi, and none of them is identical to the Tungus šamān. But the generalization of "shamanism" also has a legitimate basis: the circumpolar traditions (Sami, Siberian, Inuit, Finno-Ugric, and — arguably — Norse) share a cluster of features that distinguish them from the Mediterranean and Near Eastern religious traditions that dominate the study of "religion" in the Western academy.

**The Shamanic Cluster — The Features That Define the Type:**

The current scholarly consensus identifies the following features as the core of the "shamanic" complex:

1. **The trance journey.** The shaman enters an altered state of consciousness (trance, ecstasy) to travel to the spirit world, where he or she communicates with spirits, acquires knowledge, and performs ritual actions (healing, divination, soul retrieval).
2. **The axis mundi.** The cosmos is structured as a vertical axis (a World Tree, a cosmic mountain, a pillar) that connects the upper world (the sky, the gods), the middle world (the earth, the living), and the lower world (the underworld, the dead). The shaman travels along this axis to reach the other worlds.
3. **The spirit allies.** The shaman is assisted by spirits — animal spirits (the shaman's "power animals" or "spirit helpers"), ancestral spirits, and the spirits of the natural world (the land, the water, the sky). The shaman's relationship to the spirits is personal, negotiated, and reciprocal.
4. **The initiatory crisis.** The shaman's calling is often experienced as a crisis — a period of illness, madness, or social marginalization that is interpreted as the spirits' demand that the initiate become a shaman. The crisis is resolved through initiation — the shaman is "reborn" as a ritual specialist.
5. **Healing as primary function.** The shaman's primary social function is healing — diagnosing the spiritual cause of illness and performing the ritual that removes the spiritual affliction.
6. **The drum (or other rhythmic instrument).** The shaman uses a drum, a rattle, or another instrument to produce the rhythmic stimulation that induces the trance state.
7. **The costume.** The shaman wears a distinctive costume — often adorned with animal symbols (feathers, fur, bones), metal pendants, and other objects — that marks the shaman as a ritual specialist and that may serve as a "map" of the spirit world.

**The Application to the Norse Tradition:**

The course will examine the Norse sources for evidence of each of these shamanic features. The trance journey is attested in the seiðr tradition (discussed in NP301); the axis mundi is attested in the cosmology of Yggdrasill (discussed in NP102); the spirit allies are attested in the vǫlva's communication with the dead, the landvættir, and the dísir; the initiatory crisis is attested in the *Hávamál*'s account of Óðinn's hanging on the World Tree and in the vǫlva's death-and-rebirth in the *Vǫluspa*; healing is attested in the seiðr healing tradition; the rhythmic instrument (the varðlokkur and the staff) is attested in the *Eiríks saga rauða*; and the costume is attested in the description of Þorbjǫrg's ritual garments.

The shamanic interpretation of Norse religion does not claim that the Norse vǫlva *was* a Tungus shaman — that would be absurd. It claims that the Norse tradition shares a cluster of features with the circumpolar shamanic traditions that is sufficiently distinctive and systematic to warrant the "shamanic" designation — not as an equivalence but as a comparative framework. The question for the course is whether the shamanic interpretation is illuminating or misleading — whether it helps us understand the Norse tradition better or whether it distorts it by imposing an alien category.

## Lecture 2: Yggdrasill as Axis Mundi — The World Tree and the Shamanic Cosmos

The World Tree — Yggdrasill — is the axis mundi of the Norse cosmos, the vertical axis that connects the upper world (Asgarðr, the realm of the gods), the middle world (Miðgarðr, the realm of humans), and the lower world (Hel, the realm of the dead). The concept of the World Tree as axis mundi is one of the most distinctive features of the circumpolar shamanic complex, and its presence in the Norse tradition is one of the strongest arguments for the shamanic interpretation.

**Yggdrasill in the Poetic Edda:**

The *Vǫluspa* and the *Grímnismál* provide the most detailed descriptions of Yggdrasill:

- **The *Vǫluspa*** describes Yggdrasill as the "measure-tree" (mjǫtviðr) — the tree by which all things are measured, the fixed point of the cosmic order. The tree is watered by the three wells (Urðarbrunnr, Mímisbrunnr, Hvergelmir), its branches spread over the whole world, and it trembles at Ragnarǫk (v. 47: "Yggdrasill trembles, the ash standing upright").

- **The *Grímnismál*** describes the animals that live in Yggdrasill's branches and roots: the eagle at the top, the dragon Níðhǫggr at the bottom, the squirrel Ratatoskr running up and down carrying messages, and the four stags (Dáinn, Dvalinn, Duneyrr, Duraþrór) that gnaw at the tree's branches.

**Yggdrasill as Shamanic Axis:**

The shamanic axis mundi has several features that are paralleled in the Yggdrasill tradition:

- **Verticality.** The axis connects the upper, middle, and lower worlds, and the shaman travels along it to reach the other worlds. The Norse cosmology similarly arranges the worlds vertically (Asgarðr above, Miðgarðr in the middle, Hel below), and the vǫlva's journey to the realm of the dead is a descent along the axis.

- **The tree as cosmic structure.** The World Tree is the most common form of the axis mundi in the circumpolar region (the Sami noaidi's drum depicts the World Tree; the Siberian shaman's ritual often involves a tree or a pole that represents the axis). Yggdrasill is the most fully elaborated World Tree in any circumpolar tradition — the tree with its animals, its wells, its roots, and its cosmological significance.

- **The tree as ritual connection.** The shaman's ritual often involves a tree or a pole that serves as the axis — the shaman climbs the tree (or the pole) to ascend to the upper world, or descends along it to the lower world. The *Hávamál* describes Óðinn hanging on the tree — a ritual act that is both an ordeal (the suffering of the hang) and a journey (the acquisition of the runes from the depths of the tree).

**Óðinn's Hanging on Yggdrasill:**

The *Hávamál* (verses 138-141) describes Óðinn's hanging on Yggdrasill:

> "I know that I hung on the windy tree  
> nine full nights,  
> wounded with a spear and given to Óðinn,  
> myself to myself,  
> on that tree of which no one knows  
> from what roots it rises."

The hanging is a ritual act — Óðinn sacrifices himself to himself, and the reward is the knowledge of the runes: "I looked down and took them, / I screamed and fell from there" (v. 140). The hanging on the tree is a shamanic initiation — the ordeal that transforms Óðinn from a god into the possessor of the runes, the master of magic, the knower of secrets.

The hanging on the tree is also a journey — a descent into the depths of the tree, where the runes are hidden, and an ascent back to the surface, carrying the runes. The tree is the axis — the medium through which the knowledge travels from the depths to the surface, from the hidden to the revealed.

## Lecture 3: Óðinn as Shaman — The Ecstatic God

Óðinn is the most shamanic of the Norse gods — a god of ecstasy, trance, and spirit-journeying, who acquires his power through ordeal and who uses his power to acquire knowledge from the dead and from the spirits. The shamanic interpretation of Óðinn is one of the most widely accepted aspects of the shamanic reading of Norse religion.

**Óðinn's Names and Functions:**

Óðinn's many names (over 200 are recorded) include several that are explicitly shamanic:

- **Valfǫðr** ("Father of the Slain") — Óðinn receives the dead warriors in Valhǫll, analogous to the shaman's function of escorting the dead to the afterlife.
- **Gangleri** ("Wanderer") and **Vegtamr** ("Way-Tamer") — Óðinn travels between the worlds, analogous to the shaman's ecstatic journey.
- **Hangaguð** ("God of the Hanged") and **Hangi** ("Hanged One") — Óðinn's hanging on Yggdrasill is the paradigmatic shamanic ordeal.
- **Galdrafǫðr** ("Father of Galdr") — Óðinn is the master of the chant-magic that summons the spirits and enacts the will.
- **Seiðskeggi** ("Seiðr-Bearded One"?) — a possible reference to Óðinn's practice of seiðr, the magical tradition associated with the vǫlva.

**Óðinn's Shamanic Equipment:**

Óðinn possesses several items that have shamanic associations:

- **The spear Gungnir** — a weapon that is also a ritual instrument (Óðinn is wounded with a spear in the hanging on the tree; the spear is the instrument of the "spear-marking" that dedicates the dying to Óðinn). The spear is the shaman's staff in weaponized form — the axis mundi as a weapon.

- **The ring Draupnir** — a ring that "drips" eight new rings every ninth night, symbolizing abundance and the eternal return. The ring is the shaman's circle — the boundary of the sacred space, the representation of the cosmos, the symbol of eternity.

- **The ravens Huginn and Muninn** — two ravens that fly over the world each day and return to tell Óðinn the news. The ravens are Óðinn's spirit allies — the birds that travel between the worlds, bringing back knowledge from the furthest reaches of the cosmos. The ravens are also Óðinn's externalized consciousness — Huginn ("Thought") and Muninn ("Memory") are Óðinn's own mental faculties, projected into the world and returning to him with their reports.

- **The horse Sleipnir** — an eight-legged horse that carries Óðinn between the worlds. The eight-legged horse is the shaman's mount — the vehicle of the ecstatic journey. The Sami noaidi's drum is sometimes called the "shaman's horse," and the Siberian shaman's drum is the "horse" that carries the shaman to the spirit world. Sleipnir is the drum in equine form.

**Óðinn's Shamanic Journey:**

The *Baldrs draumar* (Baldr's Dreams) describes Óðinn's ecstatic journey to the realm of the dead:

> "Then Óðinn rose and saddled his horse;  
> he rode down to Niflhel.  
> He met a hound that came from Hel,  
> bloody on the chest from the breasts.  
> He rode to the eastern door where he knew  
> the grave of the vǫlva was."

Óðinn rides to the underworld, crosses the bridge to Hel, and reaches the grave of a dead vǫlva, whom he compels to rise from her grave and speak. The vǫlva resists — "Who is that man, unknown to me, who forces me to travel this difficult road?" — but Óðinn's power compels her to answer his questions about Baldr's fate.

The journey is classic shamanic practice: the specialist (Óðinn) travels to the realm of the dead to acquire knowledge that is not available to the living. The journey requires a vehicle (Sleipnir), crosses a boundary (the bridge to Hel), encounters a guardian (the hound), and involves a negotiation with the dead (the vǫlva's resistance and eventual compliance).

## Lecture 4: The Hávamál — Óðinn's Shamanic Initiation and the Acquisition of the Runes

The *Hávamál* (The Sayings of the High One) is a composite poem — a collection of wisdom sayings, practical advice, and magical instruction, framed as the words of Óðinn. The poem's most famous section — the Rúnatals þáttr ("The Section of the Rune-Count") — describes Óðinn's hanging on Yggdrasill and his acquisition of the runes, in a passage that is widely recognized as a shamanic initiation narrative.

**The Rúnatals þáttr (Hávamál 138-145):**

The key verses are:

> Verses 138-139: "I know that I hung on the windy tree / nine full nights, / wounded with a spear and given to Óðinn, / myself to myself..."

The hanging is a sacrifice — Óðinn sacrifices himself to himself, and the reward is the acquisition of the runes. The hanging is also an ordeal — nine nights on the tree, without food or drink (v. 140: "They did not refresh me with bread or with horn"), in a liminal state between life and death.

> Verse 140: "I looked down and took them [the runes], / I screamed and fell from there."

The acquisition of the runes is described as a physical act — "I looked down and took them" — as if the runes were objects hidden in the depths of the tree that Óðinn seized and brought to the surface. The scream is the cry of the initiate — the expression of the pain and the ecstasy of the transformation.

> Verses 141-145: The runes are described as having magical powers — they can heal, they can bind, they can protect, they can curse. The acquisition of the runes is the acquisition of magical power — the power to affect the world through the manipulation of the sacred signs.

**The Shamanic Initiation Parallels:**

The Rúnatals þáttr has several features that are characteristic of the shamanic initiation:

- **The ordeal.** The shaman's initiation often involves a period of suffering — illness, isolation, physical deprivation — that separates the initiate from the ordinary world and prepares them for the encounter with the spirits. Óðinn's nine nights on the tree, without food or drink, is the paradigmatic shamanic ordeal.

- **The dismemberment and rebirth.** Many shamanic traditions describe the initiate's experience of being dismembered by the spirits — the body is torn apart, the bones are scattered, and then reassembled in a new, transformed form. Óðinn's hanging is not explicitly a dismemberment, but it is a death-and-rebirth: he is "given to Óðinn" (sacrificed to himself), he is wounded with a spear, and he "fell from there" — the fall is the return to life, the rebirth as the possessor of the runes.

- **The acquisition of power.** The shaman's initiation culminates in the acquisition of power — the spirits give the shaman knowledge, abilities, and spirit allies that the shaman will use to serve the community. Óðinn's acquisition of the runes is the acquisition of magical power — the runes are the instruments of his magic, and his knowledge of them is the foundation of his authority.

**The Ritual Context of the Rúnatals þáttr:**

The Rúnatals þáttr is not merely a narrative — it is a ritual text. The verses are framed as Óðinn's own words, spoken in the first person, and they are followed by a catalogue of magical songs (the Ljóðatal, *Hávamál* 146-163) that Óðinn claims to know. The poem as a whole is a magical instruction — the initiate who learns the songs acquires the power that Óðinn acquired through his hanging.

The ritual context of the Rúnatals þáttr has implications for the shamanic interpretation: the *Hávamál* is not merely a description of shamanic initiation but a vehicle for it — the poem is meant to be performed, and the performance is itself a form of initiation. The reader or hearer who engages with the Rúnatals þáttr ritually participates in Óðinn's hanging, his acquisition of the runes, and his transformation.

## Lecture 5: Animal Spirits and Shape-Shifting — The Hamfǫr and the Fylgja

The Norse tradition is rich in conceptions of the relationship between humans and animal spirits — the *hamfǫr* (shape-journey), the *fylgja* (the supernatural animal double), and the broader complex of shape-shifting and animal transformation. These concepts have strong shamanic parallels, and they illuminate the Norse understanding of the permeable boundary between the human and the non-human.

**The Hamfǫr — The Shape-Journey:**

The *hamfǫr* (ham-fǫr, "shape-journey") was the practice of the soul traveling outside the body in the form of an animal. The *Ynglinga saga* (ch. 7) describes Óðinn's practice of the hamfǫr:

> "Óðinn could change his form; then his body lay as if asleep or dead, but he was a bird or a beast, a fish or a serpent, and he traveled in an instant to distant lands on his own errands or those of other men."

The hamfǫr involved the separation of the hugr (mind/spirit) from the líkamr (body), and its projection in an animal form to a distant location. The body remained behind, inert ("as if asleep or dead"), while the spirit traveled. The animal form was not arbitrary — it reflected the character, the abilities, and the intentions of the person whose spirit was traveling.

The hamfǫr is a shamanic concept — the shaman's spirit-journey in animal form is one of the core features of the circumpolar shamanic complex. The Sami noaidi's journey to the spirit world in the form of a bear, a bird, or a fish is the closest parallel to the Norse hamfǫr, and the Siberian shaman's transformation into an animal (the shaman's "power animal") is the broader context.

**The Fylgja — The Supernatural Animal Double:**

The fylgja (pl. fylgjur, "follower" or "fetch") was a supernatural double — usually female and usually in animal form — that accompanied the person throughout life. The fylgja was not the person's soul in animal form (that was the hamfǫr) but a separate being — a spirit that was attached to the person and that reflected the person's character. The fylgja's animal form varied with the person's social status and personality:

- **The bear, wolf, or boar** — the fylgjur of warriors and chieftains, reflecting their strength, ferocity, and social dominance.
- **The bird** — the fylgja of those with second sight, the prophetic gift, or the ability to travel in spirit.
- **The ox or the horse** — the fylgjur of farmers and householders, reflecting their connection to the land and to domestic wealth.

The fylgja was visible to those with second sight, and its appearance at a person's death was an omen of that death. The fylgja could also be transferred from one person to another — the dying chieftain might pass his fylgja (and the hamingja, the "luck," that accompanied it) to his successor.

The fylgja is a shamanic concept — the shaman's spirit allies, the "power animals" that assist the shaman in the journey to the spirit world, are the closest parallel. The fylgja is not, however, under the person's control — it is a given, not an acquired, spirit — and it does not serve the person in the same way that the shaman's power animal serves the shaman.

**Shape-Shifting in the Sagas:**

The sagas describe several instances of shape-shifting, some of which are associated with seiðr and some of which are independent:

- **The *Vǫlsunga saga*** describes the hero Sigmundr and his son Sinfjǫtli putting on wolf-skins and becoming wolves — a shape-shift that is literal (they actually become wolves, physically and behaviorally) and that is associated with the berserkr tradition (the warrior who fights in a trance-like fury, often described as being "not like a man" but like a beast).

- **The *Kormáks saga*** describes a seiðkona who shape-shifts into a bird to spy on her enemies.

- **The *Egils saga*** describes the shape-shifting abilities of the poet-warrior Egill, who is said to have inherited his gift from his shape-shifting ancestor Úlfr (whose name means "wolf").

## Lecture 6: The Sami Noaidi — The Circumpolar Context

The Sami noaidi (the shaman of the Sami people) is the closest geographical and cultural parallel to the Norse vǫlva, and the relationship between the two traditions is central to the shamanic interpretation of Norse religion.

**The Sami Noaidi Tradition:**

The Sami are the indigenous people of northern Scandinavia (the *Sápmi* region, spanning northern Norway, Sweden, Finland, and the Kola Peninsula of Russia). The Sami noaidi was the ritual specialist of the pre-Christian Sami religion — a figure who used a drum (the *goavddis* or *meavrresgárri*), a distinctive costume, and a technique of trance to journey to the spirit world, communicate with spirits, heal the sick, and divine the future.

The key features of the Sami noaidi tradition include:

- **The drum (goavddis).** The Sami drum was a frame drum (a wooden frame with a reindeer-skin head, sometimes painted with cosmological symbols — the World Tree, the sun, the moon, the spirits). The drum was the primary instrument of the noaidi's trance — the rhythmic beating of the drum induced the altered state of consciousness that allowed the noaidi to journey to the spirit world.

- **The joik.** The joik is the traditional Sami form of singing — a highly personal, often wordless, melodic expression that could be used to summon spirits, to honor the dead, to describe a person or a place, or to enter a trance state. The joik is the Sami parallel to the Norse varðlokkur — the rhythmic, melodic vocalization that facilitates the trance.

- **The spirit allies.** The noaidi was assisted by spirit allies — the *sáiva* (the spirits of the underworld, associated with lakes and mountains), the *háldi* (the spirits of specific places), and the *noaidi-gážzi* (the noaidi's personal spirit helpers, often in animal form).

- **The division of labor.** The Sami tradition distinguished between several categories of ritual specialists: the *noaidi* (the shaman who could journey to the spirit world), the *guvllár* (the diviner, who could answer questions without entering a full trance), the *sieidi* (the keeper of a sacred site, responsible for offerings to the spirits of that site), and others.

**The Contact Between the Norse and the Sami:**

The Norse and the Sami were in contact throughout the pre-Christian period and into the Christian era. The *Óttarr's voyage* (the account of the Norwegian trader Óttarr, recorded in King Alfred's Old English *Orosius*, c. 890) describes the Sami as the northern neighbors of the Norse, and the archaeological evidence (Norse objects in Sami contexts, Sami objects in Norse contexts) confirms the existence of sustained contact.

The sagas attest to the Norse perception of the Sami as a people of powerful magic — the *Vatnsdœla saga* and the *Fóstbrœðra saga* describe the Sami as practitioners of magic (often hostile magic) against the Norse, and the *Historia Norwegie* describes the Sami as "extremely skilled in the magic arts" (magicæ artis peritissimi).

The relationship between Norse seiðr and Sami noaidi practice was probably one of mutual influence rather than simple borrowing in either direction. The Norse and the Sami were adjacent and interacting cultures, and their ritual specialists would have known of each other's practices. The similarities between the traditions may reflect mutual borrowing, common descent from a shared circumpolar substrate, or parallel development in similar environments.

## Lecture 7: The Archaeology of Shamanism — Drums, Bones, and Ritual Objects

The archaeological evidence for shamanic practice in the Norse world is less direct than the textual evidence, but it provides material confirmation of some elements of the shamanic interpretation.

**The Absence of the Drum:**

The most striking absence from the Norse archaeological record is the drum — the primary instrument of the circumpolar shaman, but not attested in any Norse grave, settlement, or cult site. The absence of the drum is a problem for the shamanic interpretation: if the Norse vǫlva was a shamanic figure in the circumpolar sense, why does the archaeological record not include drums?

The absence of the drum may be explained by several factors:

- **The staff as drum-substitute.** The vǫlva's staff (the vǫlr) may have served the rhythmic function of the drum — the staff could be shaken (the rings on the staff would rattle) or beaten against the ground, producing the rhythmic stimulation that induced the trance. The staff was a multiple-use instrument — it served as the axis mundi, as the weapon, and as the percussive instrument — and it replaced the specialized drum of the Sami and Siberian traditions.

- **The varðlokkur as drum-substitute.** The rhythmic function of the drum may have been performed by the varðlokkur — the calling-songs that the assistant sang to summon the spirits and to facilitate the vǫlva's trance. The varðlokkur was not a percussive instrument in the strict sense, but it provided the rhythmic auditory stimulation that the drum provided in the Sami tradition.

- **Organic materials.** The drum may have been made of organic materials (wood, skin) that decay in the archaeological record. If the Norse drum existed but was not preserved, its absence from the archaeological record would not be evidence against its existence.

**The Staff-Graves Revisited:**

The staff-graves (discussed in NP301) are the primary archaeological evidence for the vǫlva tradition. In the context of the shamanic interpretation, several features of the staff-graves are significant:

- **The basket on the staff.** The basket-shaped cage at the top of the vǫlva's staff (containing seeds, herbs, and small objects) is a unique feature of the Norse tradition, with no direct parallel in the Sami or Siberian shamanic traditions. The basket may represent the vǫlva's gathering function — the collection of spiritual substances from the Otherworld and their return to the human world.

- **The animal figurines.** The animal figurines found in the staff-graves (birds, cats, bears) may represent the vǫlva's spirit allies — the animal spirits that assisted her in the journey to the Otherworld. The figurines are the material embodiment of the vǫlva's relationship with the animal powers.

- **The miniature chairs.** The miniature chairs found in some staff-graves probably represent the seiðhjallr — the ritual platform on which the vǫlva sat during the trance. The chair is the symbol of the vǫlva's function — the seat from which she journeyed, the throne from which she spoke.

**The Animal Burials:**

The archaeological record includes several animal burials that may be associated with shamanic practice. The most significant is the *Guldgubber* (gold foil figures) from the Uppåkra cult site (Skåne, Sweden), which include numerous animal figures — birds, boars, bears — that may represent the spirit allies of the ritual specialists who operated at the site.

## Lecture 8: Berserkr and Úlfheðnar — The Ecstatic Warriors

The berserkr (pl. berserkir, "bear-shirt") and the úlfheðinn (pl. úlfheðnar, "wolf-coat") were ecstatic warriors who fought in a trance-like fury, immune to pain, fire, and iron. The berserkr tradition has strong shamanic parallels, and the relationship between the ecstatic warrior and the ecstatic ritual specialist is one of the most debated questions in the shamanic interpretation of Norse religion.

**The Berserkr in the Sources:**

The *Ynglinga saga* (ch. 6) describes the berserkir as Óðinn's warriors:

> "Óðinn's men went into battle without mail-coats, and they were mad as dogs or wolves; they bit their shields and were strong as bears or bulls. They killed men, but neither fire nor iron could harm them. This is called 'going berserk.'"

The berserkr's battle-trance is characterized by:

- **Fury (berserksgangr).** The berserkr enters a state of uncontrollable rage, in which he attacks friend and foe alike, and in which he is impervious to pain and to ordinary weapons.
- **Shape-shifting.** The berserkr is described as being like a bear or a wolf — he does not literally change shape (the shape-shift is metaphorical, or it is a psychological transformation rather than a physical one), but his behavior is no longer human.
- **The post-trance exhaustion.** After the berserksgangr, the berserkr collapses in exhaustion — a physical and psychological crash that is consistent with the aftermath of a trance state.

**The Shamanic Parallels:**

The berserkr tradition has several features that are characteristic of the shamanic complex:

- **The trance state.** The berserkr's battle-fury is an altered state of consciousness — a trance state induced by ritual preparation (fasting, isolation, the donning of the animal skin) and by the rhythms of the battle (the war-cries, the clash of weapons, the physical exertion). The berserkr's trance is not the same as the vǫlva's trance (the berserkr's trance is aggressive and violent; the vǫlva's is passive and receptive), but both are altered states that involve the transformation of the person into a non-human mode of being.

- **The animal identification.** The berserkr identifies with a specific animal — the bear or the wolf — and his battle-fury is characterized by the animal's qualities (strength, ferocity, immunity to pain). The animal identification is a form of the hamfǫr — the spirit traveling in animal form — but the berserkr's hamfǫr is performed in the body, not outside it.

- **The initiatory function.** The berserksgangr may have been an initiatory experience — the young warrior proved his fitness for the warrior band by entering the battle-trance and fighting with the fury of the beast. The initiation was both social (the warrior was accepted into the band) and spiritual (the warrior was transformed by the experience of the berserksgangr).

**The Berserkr and the Shaman — Two Branches of the Same Tree?**

Some scholars have argued that the berserkr and the vǫlva represent two branches of the same shamanic complex — the "male" or "aggressive" branch (the berserkr, the ecstatic warrior) and the "female" or "receptive" branch (the vǫlva, the ecstatic ritual specialist). The argument is that the pre-Christian Norse recognized two forms of trance-based, ecstatic practice — one oriented toward violence and the other toward knowledge — and that both are descended from a common circumpolar shamanic substrate.

The "two branches" hypothesis is attractive but speculative. The sources do not explicitly connect the berserkr and the vǫlva, and the two traditions are presented in different contexts (the berserkr in the warrior band, the vǫlva in the community ritual). The relationship between the two traditions is a question for further research — archaeological, textual, and comparative.

## Lecture 9: The Shamanic Interpretation of Norse Religion — The Debate

The shamanic interpretation of Norse religion — the claim that the pre-Christian Norse tradition includes a shamanic stratum, and that the shamanic framework is the most illuminating comparative lens for understanding Norse ritual practice — has been the subject of intense debate in the 2030s-2040s. The debate has clarified the issues, sharpened the evidence, and produced a more nuanced understanding of the relationship between the Norse tradition and the circumpolar shamanic complex.

**The Arguments for the Shamanic Interpretation:**

1. **The trance journey.** The seiðr tradition involves the vǫlva's trance-induced journey to the Otherworld — a practice that is characteristic of the circumpolar shamanic complex and that has no close parallel in the Mediterranean or Near Eastern religious traditions.
2. **The axis mundi.** Yggdrasill, the World Tree, is a fully elaborated axis mundi — a vertical cosmic axis that connects the upper, middle, and lower worlds — a feature that is characteristic of circumpolar cosmology.
3. **The animal spirits.** The Norse tradition of the fylgja, the hamfǫr, and the animal spirit allies is consistent with the circumpolar shamanic tradition of the shaman's power animals.
4. **The initiatory ordeal.** Óðinn's hanging on Yggdrasill — the nine-night ordeal, the wounding with the spear, the acquisition of the runes — is a classic shamanic initiation narrative.
5. **The drum-substitutes.** The vǫlva's staff (with its rattling rings) and the varðlokkur (the rhythmic calling-songs) perform the rhythmic function of the shamanic drum, even if the drum itself is absent from the Norse sources.

**The Arguments Against the Shamanic Interpretation:**

1. **The absence of the drum.** The drum is the defining instrument of the circumpolar shaman, and its absence from the Norse sources is a significant counter-evidence.
2. **The cultural distance.** The Norse were an Indo-European people, not a Uralic or circumpolar people, and the Indo-European religious tradition (Greek, Roman, Indic, Celtic) does not include shamanic elements in the circumpolar sense. The Norse tradition should be understood in the Indo-European framework rather than the circumpolar one.
3. **The Christian bias of the sources.** The textual sources for seiðr were written by Christians who may have exaggerated the "magical" and "diabolic" elements of the practice for polemical purposes. The seiðr of the sagas may be a Christian caricature, not a reliable account of pre-Christian ritual.
4. **The risk of circularity.** The shamanic interpretation was developed by scholars who were familiar with the circumpolar shamanic traditions and who saw similarities in the Norse sources. But the similarities may be artefacts of the scholarly lens — the result of looking for shamanic features and finding them, rather than the result of genuine historical relationship.

**The 2040 Synthesis:**

The current consensus is a qualified acceptance of the shamanic interpretation: the Norse tradition includes shamanic elements, and the shamanic framework is a useful comparative lens, but the Norse tradition is not "shamanic" in the full circumpolar sense. The Norse vǫlva was not a Sami noaidi or a Tungus šamān; she was a distinctively Norse ritual specialist who shared some features with her circumpolar counterparts and differed from them in others. The shamanic interpretation is most useful as a comparative framework, not as a claim of identity — it illuminates the Norse tradition by showing what it shares with, and how it differs from, its circumpolar neighbors.

## Lecture 10: Shamanic Elements Beyond Seiðr — Galdr, Runes, and the Sacred

The shamanic interpretation of Norse religion has focused primarily on the seiðr tradition, but shamanic elements may also be present in other aspects of Norse ritual practice — the galdr (chant magic), the runes, and the sacred landscape.

**Galdr as Shamanic Chant:**

The galdr (plural: galdrar) was a form of chant-magic, performed in a loud, vigorous voice (galdr is related to the verb gala, "to crow," suggesting the loud, rhythmic, repetitive character of the chant). The galdr was used for healing, cursing, protection, and the manipulation of the spirits, and it was performed by both men and women.

The galdr's rhythmic, repetitive, loud character suggests a connection to the shamanic drum — the galdr may have induced a trance state in the performer (not through rhythmic percussion but through rhythmic vocalization) that allowed the performer to access the spiritual powers that the chant addressed.

**The Runes as Shamanic Symbols:**

The runes — the sacred signs of the Norse tradition — have a shamanic dimension. The *Hávamál* describes Óðinn's acquisition of the runes through his hanging on Yggdrasill — a shamanic initiation in which the runes are the gift of the spirits. The runes are not merely an alphabet but a system of sacred symbols, each with its own meaning, its own power, and its own relationship to the cosmic order. The runes are the shaman's tools — the instruments of magical operation, the signs of power, the keys to the spirit world.

The rune-magic tradition — the carving of runes on amulets, weapons, and ritual objects — may have been a shamanic practice: the rune-master, like the shaman, manipulated the sacred signs to affect the world, to summon the spirits, and to protect the community.

**The Sacred Landscape as Shamanic Map:**

The Norse sacred landscape — the hǫrgr, the vé, the álfhǫll — was not merely a collection of sacred sites but a map of the spirit world. The landscape was alive with spirits (the landvættir, the álfar, the dísir), and the ritual specialist — the vǫlva, the goði, the householder — navigated this landscape in both physical and spiritual terms. The sacred landscape was the shaman's map — the representation of the Otherworld in the material world, the grid of sacred sites that made the spirit world accessible to the living.

## Lecture 11: Modern Shamanic Heathenry — Reconstruction, Innovation, and Critique

The modern Ásatrú and Heathen movement has engaged with the shamanic interpretation of Norse religion in complex ways. Some practitioners embrace the shamanic interpretation and practice forms of "Norse shamanism" (often called "seiðr" or "shamanic Heathenry"); others reject the shamanic interpretation as an alien imposition on the Norse tradition.

**Norse Shamanism — The Modern Practice:**

The modern "Norse shamanism" movement draws on the historical seiðr tradition, the comparative shamanic literature, and the personal experience of the practitioners. Modern seiðr practice typically includes:

- **The seiðhjallr** — a literal or symbolic platform, representing the vǫlva's elevated seat.
- **The staff** — a reproduction of the vǫlva's staff, used as a ritual instrument.
- **The varðlokkur** — improvised or traditional chanting, often in a collective setting (the community sings to support the seiðr-worker's trance).
- **The trance journey** — the seiðr-worker enters a trance state and journeys to the Otherworld, seeking knowledge, healing, or spirit communication.
- **The divination** — the seiðr-worker answers questions from the community, based on the knowledge acquired in the trance.

The modern practice is a reconstruction — based on the historical sources but adapted to the modern context, with modern expectations (the "workshop" format, the emphasis on personal transformation, the openness to participants from non-Heathen backgrounds).

**The Critique of Modern Norse Shamanism:**

Modern Norse shamanism has been criticized on several grounds:

- **Historical inaccuracy.** The modern practice may not accurately reflect the pre-Christian seiðr tradition — the sources are too fragmentary to support a full reconstruction, and the modern practice fills in the gaps with material from other shamanic traditions (Sami, Siberian, Native American) that may not be appropriate to the Norse context.

- **Cultural appropriation.** The incorporation of Sami and Siberian elements into modern Norse shamanism has been criticized as a form of cultural appropriation — the use of indigenous traditions by non-indigenous practitioners without the permission or the participation of the indigenous communities.

- **The loss of the ergi.** The modern practice often de-emphasizes (or entirely omits) the ergi dimension of seiðr — the shame, the marginalization, the gender-transgression — that was central to the pre-Christian tradition. The modern practice's "cleaned-up," "respectable" seiðr may be missing the very feature that gave the tradition its power.

**The University of Yggdrasil Position:**

The Norse Pagan Studies program teaches the historical seiðr tradition, the comparative shamanic framework, and the modern reconstruction; it does not teach a specific practice of modern seiðr. Students who wish to practice seiðr are encouraged to do so with critical awareness of the historical sources, the comparative framework, and the ethical issues (cultural appropriation, historical accuracy, the ergi complex) that the modern practice raises.

## Lecture 12: The Shamanic Legacy — What the Norse Taught the World About the Spirit

The shamanic interpretation of Norse religion is not merely an academic exercise. It is a recovery of a dimension of the Norse tradition that was suppressed by the Christian conversion and that has been obscured by the Christian-influenced scholarship of the medieval and modern periods. The shamanic interpretation recovers the ecstatic, the transformative, the spirit-communicating dimension of the Norse religious world — the dimension that the Christian sources condemned as diabolical and that the modern, secular scholarship often dismissed as superstition.

The shamanic interpretation also recovers what is universal in the Norse tradition — the human capacity for trance, for spirit-communication, for journeying between the worlds — that is attested across the circumpolar region and beyond. The Norse tradition was not an isolated, sui generis phenomenon; it was part of a circumpolar complex of shamanic practices that extended from Siberia to Scandinavia, and that connected the Norse to their Sami, Finnish, and Arctic neighbors in a shared tradition of ecstatic ritual.

The legacy of the shamanic interpretation for modern Heathenry is a challenge: to recover the ecstatic dimension of the Norse tradition without romanticizing it, to engage with the historical sources critically, and to practice the reconstructed tradition with respect for the indigenous communities from whom the comparative framework is drawn. The shamanic Heathenry of the 2040s is a work in progress — a tradition in the making, drawing on the past but living in the present, and open to the future.
