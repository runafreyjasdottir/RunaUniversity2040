# NP305: Christianity & Norse Paganism — Contact & Conversion
## Bachelor of Science in Norse Pagan Studies — University of Yggdrasil, 2040

**Credits:** 4
**Description:** The encounter between Christianity and Norse paganism was not merely a religious conflict — it was a transformation of the social, political, and cultural order of Scandinavia. This course examines the Christianization of Scandinavia from the earliest missionary contacts in the 8th century to the formal conversion of the Scandinavian kingdoms in the 11th-12th centuries. Topics include: the pre-Christian Norse encounter with Christianity (through trade, raiding, and settlement); the missionary methods and their adaptation to the Norse context; the political dynamics of conversion (the kings as Christianizers, the chieftains as resisters); the theological and ritual transformations of the conversion; the syncretic period (the coexistence and blending of pagan and Christian practices); and the long-term survival of pagan elements in Scandinavian Christian culture.

**Prerequisites:** NP201 (Ritual Practice), NP102 (Norse Cosmology)
**Corequisite:** NP203 (Snorri's Edda & Euhemerism) recommended

## Lecture 1: Before the Missionaries — The Norse Encounter with Christianity Before Conversion

The Christianization of Scandinavia did not begin with the arrival of the first missionaries. The Norse had been in contact with Christian peoples — the Anglo-Saxons, the Franks, the Irish — for centuries before the conversion, through trade, through raiding, and through settlement. The pre-conversion encounter with Christianity shaped the Norse perception of the new religion, provided the context for the missionaries' efforts, and created a hybrid cultural zone in which pagan and Christian elements coexisted.

**Trade and the Cross:**

The Norse trading networks extended from the Baltic to the Black Sea, from the North Atlantic to the Mediterranean. In the course of their travels, Norse traders encountered Christian communities — the Anglo-Saxons in England, the Franks in the Carolingian Empire, the Byzantines in Constantinople, the Rus in Kiev. The encounters were commercial, diplomatic, and personal, and they exposed the Norse to Christian beliefs, Christian practices, and Christian material culture (crosses, icons, liturgical objects).

The *prim-signing* (prímsigning — "prime-signing," the preliminary sign of the cross that marked a person as a catechumen, a candidate for baptism) was often performed on Norse traders who wished to do business with Christian communities. The prim-signing was not a full conversion — it did not require the renunciation of the old gods or the abandonment of pagan practice — but it created a category of "half-Christian" Norse who were welcome in Christian markets and who might, over time, move toward fuller Christian commitment.

**The Viking Raids and the Christian Perception of the Norse:**

The Viking raids on Christian monasteries (Lindisfarne, 793; Iona, 795, 802, 806; Noirmoutier, repeatedly) brought the Norse into violent contact with the institutional centers of Christianity. The raids were not religiously motivated — the Vikings raided monasteries because they were wealthy, undefended, and located on accessible coasts — but they created a Christian perception of the Norse as the "scourge of God," the heathen enemy who attacked the holy places and murdered the servants of Christ.

The Christian perception of the Norse as the pagan enemy shaped the missionary effort: the missionaries were not only converting individuals but defeating a demonic religion, and the conversion was not only a spiritual victory but a civilizational one — the replacement of chaos (the pagan raids) with order (the Christian kingdom).

**The Norse Perception of Christianity:**

The Norse perception of Christianity before the conversion was complex and variable. Some Norse regarded Christianity with contempt — the White Christ (Hvítakristr) was a weak god, unable to defend his servants from the Norse warriors' attacks. Others regarded Christianity with curiosity — the new religion had powerful rituals (the mass, the baptism), impressive buildings (the stone churches, the monasteries), and a rich material culture (the illuminated manuscripts, the gold and silver liturgical vessels) that were unlike anything in the Norse tradition.

The Norse also encountered Christianity through the Christian slaves (thralls) who were captured in the raids and brought back to Scandinavia. The slaves brought their Christian beliefs with them, and the presence of Christian slaves in Norse households meant that the new religion was not only an external phenomenon but an internal one — a religion that was practiced, however covertly, within the Norse domestic sphere.

## Lecture 2: The First Missionaries — Ansgar, Rimbert, and the Frankish Mission

The first organized missionary effort in Scandinavia was the Frankish mission, centered on the archbishopric of Hamburg-Bremen, and its most famous figure was Ansgar (c. 801-865), the "Apostle of the North."

**Ansgar's Mission:**

Ansgar, a Frankish monk from the monastery of Corbie (later the archbishop of Hamburg-Bremen), made two missionary journeys to Scandinavia: the first to Denmark (c. 823-828), where he established a school and baptized some converts, and the second to Sweden (c. 829-831), where he established a mission at Birka (the major trading center in the Mälaren valley). Ansgar's mission was modest in its achievements — he made few converts, his churches were destroyed by pagan raids, and the Christian presence in Scandinavia after his death was minimal.

But Ansgar's mission established a model for the Christianization of Scandinavia that the later missionaries would follow:

- **The royal connection.** Ansgar sought the permission and the patronage of the kings — the Danish king Harald Klak, the Swedish king Bjǫrn at Haugi. The missionary effort was always mediated through the political elite — the kings who could grant access, protect the missionaries, and enforce the conversion (or refuse it).

- **The trading-center strategy.** Ansgar targeted the trading centers (Hedeby in Denmark, Birka in Sweden) where the Norse were already in contact with Christian traders and where the Christian presence was least disruptive to the existing social order.

- **The slave-church model.** Ansgar built churches and established congregations, but the initial converts were often slaves (thralls) — captives from Christian lands who had been enslaved by the Norse and who welcomed the Christian mission as a source of spiritual consolation and social identity.

**The Vita Anskarii:**

Ansgar's life and mission are recorded in the *Vita Anskarii* (Life of Ansgar), written by his successor Rimbert (c. 865-876). The *Vita Anskarii* is a hagiography — a saint's life, written to promote Ansgar's canonization and to support the claims of the archbishopric of Hamburg-Bremen to jurisdiction over the Scandinavian mission. The *Vita* is therefore not a neutral historical account but a polemical document — it exaggerates Ansgar's achievements, minimizes his failures, and frames the mission as a heroic struggle against pagan resistance and demonic opposition.

The *Vita Anskarii* is, nonetheless, an invaluable source for the early Christianization of Scandinavia — it preserves details of the missionary encounter (the debates, the conversions, the conflicts) that are not available from any other source, and it provides the earliest detailed description of Norse pagan practice from a Christian perspective.

**The Later Carolingian Mission:**

Ansgar's successors — Rimbert, Unni, Adaldag — continued the Frankish mission throughout the 9th and 10th centuries, with mixed results. The mission was interrupted by the Viking raids (the sack of Hamburg by the Danes in 845 destroyed the archbishopric's seat), by the political instability of the Carolingian Empire (which undermined the mission's political support), and by the resistance of the Norse chieftains (who saw the Christian mission as a threat to their authority).

## Lecture 3: The Anglo-Saxon Mission and the Conversion of Norway

The conversion of Norway in the late 10th and early 11th centuries was the work of the Anglo-Saxon mission, operating through the Norwegian kings who had been baptized during their exiles in England. The Anglo-Saxon mission was more successful than the Frankish mission — it produced the formal conversion of the Norwegian kingdom — but it was also more violent and more politically entangled.

**Hákon the Good (c. 920-961):**

Hákon Aðalsteinsfóstri ("Hákon, fostered by Æthelstan") was the son of Haraldr hárfagri (Harald Fairhair), raised at the court of the English king Æthelstan (hence his foster-name). Hákon was baptized in England, and when he returned to Norway to claim the kingdom, he attempted to introduce Christianity. The *Hákonar saga góða* (Snorri's saga of Hákon the Good) describes his efforts:

> "Hákon was a good Christian when he came to Norway... He had priests brought from England, and he had churches built and priests appointed to them. But when he went to the Þrœndalǫg [Trøndelag, the stronghold of pagan resistance], the farmers rose against him."

The farmers of Trøndelag, led by the pagan chieftains, compelled Hákon to participate in the pagan blót — the sacrifice at the hof at Mære. Hákon was forced to eat the horse-meat (a pagan practice) and to drink the sacrificial toasts to Óðinn, Þórr, and Freyr. The *Hákonar saga* describes Hákon's humiliation and his compromise: he remained a Christian in private, but he participated in the pagan rituals in public, a hybrid practice that satisfied neither the Christians (who demanded the renunciation of paganism) nor the pagans (who demanded the full observance of the old rituals).

Hákon's failure illustrates the limits of royal Christianization: the king could not impose Christianity on a population that was attached to the old religion and that was led by chieftains whose authority was bound up with the pagan cult. The conversion of Norway would require a more forceful king and a more systematic campaign.

**Óláfr Tryggvason (c. 995-1000):**

Óláfr Tryggvason was a Norwegian king (r. 995-1000) who made the Christianization of Norway his primary project. Óláfr had been baptized in England (or in the Scilly Isles, according to some traditions), and he returned to Norway with a missionary mandate: to convert the kingdom, by persuasion if possible, by force if necessary.

The *Óláfs saga Tryggvasonar* describes Óláfr's missionary methods:

- **The royal progress.** Óláfr traveled through Norway, holding assemblies (þing) at which he demanded that the farmers accept baptism. Those who refused were threatened, attacked, or killed.
- **The destruction of the cult sites.** Óláfr destroyed the hof, the hǫrgar, and the sacred groves — the material infrastructure of the pagan cult. The destruction was both a practical measure (removing the sites at which the pagan rituals were performed) and a symbolic one (demonstrating the defeat of the old gods).
- **The execution of the resisters.** Óláfr executed the pagan chieftains who refused baptism, including the famous seiðmaðr Rǫgnvaldr réttilbeini and the pagan goði Þorleifr spaki.

Óláfr's methods were effective: by the end of his reign, Norway was nominally Christian — the population had been baptized, the cult sites had been destroyed, and the Christian Church had been established. But Óláfr's conversion was also shallow: the population had accepted baptism under compulsion, not out of conviction, and the pagan practices persisted in private and in the remote regions.

**Óláfr Haraldsson (St. Óláfr, r. 1015-1028):**

Óláfr Haraldsson (later St. Óláfr) completed the Christianization of Norway that Hákon had begun and Óláfr Tryggvason had advanced. St. Óláfr's methods were similar to his predecessor's — the royal progress, the destruction of the cult sites, the execution of the resisters — but he also established the institutional infrastructure of the Christian Church (the bishoprics, the tithe system, the ecclesiastical law) that made the conversion permanent.

St. Óláfr's death at the Battle of Stiklestad (1030) — killed by a coalition of pagan and Christian chieftains who opposed his centralizing rule — was the occasion for his canonization and for the establishment of his cult as the patron saint of Norway. The combination of the king's sanctity and the Church's institutional presence made the Christianization of Norway irreversible.

## Lecture 4: The Conversion of Denmark — Harald Bluetooth and the Jelling Dynasty

The conversion of Denmark in the late 10th century was the work of the Jelling dynasty — the kings Gormr the Old, Haraldr Bluetooth (Haraldr blátǫnn), and Sveinn Forkbeard (Sveinn tjúguskegg) — who used Christianity as a tool of state-building and dynastic legitimation.

**Haraldr Bluetooth (r. c. 958-986):**

Haraldr Bluetooth was the first Christian king of Denmark. The Jelling rune-stone (c. 965), erected by Haraldr at Jelling (Jutland), declares:

> "Haraldr king ordered this monument made in memory of Gormr, his father, and in memory of Þyrvé, his mother — that Haraldr who won for himself all Denmark and Norway and made the Danes Christian."

The Jelling stone is the earliest Danish source that explicitly states the Christianization of the kingdom, and it presents the conversion as a royal achievement — Haraldr "made the Danes Christian," as he "won for himself all Denmark and Norway." The conversion is a political act — the king imposes the new religion on the kingdom, as he imposes his rule on the territory.

Haraldr's conversion was probably motivated by a combination of factors:

- **Political calculation.** The Christian kingdoms to the south (the Ottonian Empire) were more powerful than Denmark, and Haraldr's conversion was a diplomatic gesture — a bid for recognition and protection from the Christian emperor.
- **Dynastic legitimation.** Haraldr's father Gormr was a pagan, and Haraldr's conversion distanced the Jelling dynasty from the pagan past and aligned it with the Christian future. The conversion was a way of asserting the dynasty's superiority over the rival chieftain families who remained attached to the old religion.
- **Administrative centralization.** The Christian Church provided an administrative apparatus (the bishoprics, the parishes, the written records) that the pagan cult could not, and Haraldr's adoption of Christianity was a step toward the centralization of the Danish kingdom.

**The Poppo Legend:**

The *Widukind's Res gestae Saxonicae* (c. 970) describes a miracle that was said to have converted Haraldr: the missionary Poppo carried a red-hot iron in his hand without being burned, proving the power of the Christian God. Haraldr, convinced by the miracle, accepted baptism. The Poppo legend is a conversion narrative — a story that explains the king's conversion in terms of a dramatic, decisive event — but the historical reality was probably more gradual and more political than the legend suggests.

**The Completion of the Danish Conversion:**

Haraldr's son Sveinn Forkbeard (r. 986-1014) and his grandson Knútr the Great (r. 1018-1035) completed the Christianization of Denmark. Knútr, who was also king of England (from 1016) and of Norway (from 1028), was a Christian king who patronized the Church, built churches and monasteries, and made a pilgrimage to Rome (1027). By the end of Knútr's reign, Denmark was fully Christian — the pagan cult had been suppressed, the Church had been established, and the Danish kingdom was integrated into the Christian political order of Europe.

## Lecture 5: The Conversion of Sweden — Resistance and Gradual Transformation

The conversion of Sweden was slower, more contested, and less complete than the conversion of Denmark or Norway. The Swedish kingdom was less centralized than the Danish or Norwegian, and the pagan cult — centered on the temple at Uppsala — was more deeply rooted in the Swedish social and political order. The conversion of Sweden was a gradual process, extending from the 9th to the 12th centuries, and the pagan cult persisted in some regions into the 13th century.

**The Uppsala Cult:**

The temple at Uppsala (Gamla Uppsala, Old Uppsala) was the most important pagan cult center in Scandinavia. Adam of Bremen (c. 1070) describes the temple:

> "This people has a most famous temple called Uppsala, not far from the town of Sigtuna. In this temple, entirely decorated with gold, the people worship the statues of three gods: the most powerful of them, Þórr, occupies a seat in the middle of the room; Wodan and Fricco have seats on either side."

Adam's description (discussed in NP205) is problematic — he never visited Uppsala, and his account includes elements (the golden chains, the specific triad of gods) that may be literary inventions. But the existence of a major cult center at Uppsala is confirmed by the archaeological evidence (the great hall, the royal mounds, the sacrificial deposits) and by the place-name record.

The Uppsala cult was the center of the Swedish fertility religion — the worship of Freyr (the god of fertility and prosperity), the sacrificial feast (the blót), and the seasonal rituals that ensured the fertility of the land. The Uppsala cult was also the center of the Swedish kingship — the king's authority was bound up with his role in the cult, and the conversion of the king was a threat to the cult's survival.

**The Christian Kings of Sweden:**

The first Christian king of Sweden was Óláfr skǫtkonungr (r. c. 995-1022), who was baptized by the English missionary Sigfrid. Óláfr's conversion did not extend to the rest of the kingdom — the pagan chieftains, particularly in the Uppland region (the heartland of the Uppsala cult), resisted the Christian mission, and Óláfr was forced to compromise: he remained a Christian, but he allowed the pagan cult to continue at Uppsala.

The subsequent Swedish kings — Ǫnundr Jakob, Emundr the Old, Stenkil — continued the gradual Christianization of the kingdom, but the Uppsala cult persisted. The final suppression of the pagan cult at Uppsala did not occur until the reign of Ingi the Elder (r. c. 1080-1110), who was driven from the þing by the pagan farmers when he refused to perform the blót, but who later returned with an army and destroyed the temple.

**The Persistence of Paganism in Sweden:**

The archaeological evidence indicates that pagan burial practices (cremation, mound burial, the provision of grave goods) persisted in Sweden into the 12th century, and the textual evidence indicates that pagan rituals (the blót, the offerings to the landvættir) continued in some regions into the 13th century. The Christianization of Sweden was not a clean replacement of paganism by Christianity but a long period of coexistence, syncretism, and gradual transformation.

## Lecture 6: The Conversion of Iceland — The Alþingi Decision of 999/1000 CE

The conversion of Iceland is unique in the history of the Christianization of Scandinavia: it was achieved not by a king (Iceland had no king) but by the community's own decision, at the Alþingi (the national assembly) of 999 or 1000 CE (the exact year is debated). The Alþingi decision — the adoption of Christianity as the public religion of Iceland, while allowing private pagan practice to continue — is one of the most extraordinary episodes in the history of religious change.

**The *Íslendingabók* Account:**

The *Íslendingabók* (Book of the Icelanders), written by Ari Þorgilsson the Wise (c. 1125-1132), provides the earliest account of the conversion:

> "The king of Norway, Óláfr Tryggvason, sent the priest Þangbrandr to Iceland to preach Christianity. Þangbrandr traveled through the country, baptizing those who were willing and killing those who opposed him... At the Alþingi, the Christians and the pagans were on the point of fighting, and it seemed that the country would be split by civil war. Then the Lawspeaker, Þorgeirr Ljósvetningagoði, lay down under a cloak for a day and a night and spoke not a word. Then he rose and declared that all men should be Christian and should believe in one God... But the exposure of children and the eating of horse-meat should be allowed to continue, and the pagan sacrifices should be performed in private, not in public."

The Alþingi decision had several key elements:

- **The conversion was voluntary.** The community, through its representative assembly, decided to accept Christianity — it was not imposed by a foreign king or a conquering army.
- **The conversion was pragmatic.** The decision was motivated by the desire to avoid civil war — the Christians and the pagans were on the brink of conflict, and the Lawspeaker's compromise avoided the bloodshed.
- **The conversion was incomplete.** The private practice of pagan ritual was allowed to continue, and the pagan customs (the exposure of children, the eating of horse-meat) were explicitly preserved. The Alþingi decision was not a renunciation of paganism but a shift in the public religion of the community.

**The Role of Þorgeirr:**

Þorgeirr Ljósvetningagoði (the goði of Ljósavatn) was the Lawspeaker — the highest elected official of the Icelandic Commonwealth, responsible for reciting the law at the Alþingi and for advising on legal questions. Þorgeirr was a pagan (he was a goði, a chieftain-priest of the pagan cult), and his decision to advocate for Christianity was the decisive moment in the conversion.

The *Íslendingabók* account of Þorgeirr's deliberation — lying under a cloak for a day and a night, speaking not a word, then rising to deliver his decision — is a ritual act: the Lawspeaker enters a liminal state (the withdrawal from ordinary life, the silence, the covering with the cloak) to receive the knowledge that will decide the community's fate. The ritual framing of the decision suggests that the conversion was not merely a political compromise but a religious act — the Lawspeaker, acting in his capacity as the community's ritual specialist, determined the community's relationship to the divine.

**The Þangbrandr Mission and the Norwegian Connection:**

The conversion of Iceland was precipitated by the missionary activity of Þangbrandr, a Saxon or Flemish priest sent by King Óláfr Tryggvason of Norway. Þangbrandr's mission was violent and divisive — he killed several pagans who opposed him, and he was eventually outlawed by the Alþingi and forced to leave Iceland. But his mission had created a Christian faction in Iceland, and the conflict between the Christian and pagan factions was the immediate cause of the crisis at the Alþingi.

The Norwegian connection was crucial: the Icelandic chieftains who had been baptized (either by Þangbrandr or during their visits to Norway) were aligned with the Norwegian king, and the adoption of Christianity by the Alþingi was a way of maintaining Iceland's relationship with Norway (the dominant power in the region) and of avoiding Norwegian intervention (Óláfr Tryggvason was pressuring the Icelanders to convert, and the decision was, in part, a response to that pressure).

## Lecture 7: The Conversion of Greenland and the Norse Colonies — The Periphery

The conversion of the Norse colonies — Greenland, the Faroe Islands, the Hebrides, the Isle of Man, and the Norse settlements in Ireland and England — followed different patterns, determined by the local political conditions, the relationship between the Norse settlers and the indigenous populations, and the proximity to the centers of Christian power.

**Greenland:**

Greenland was settled by the Norse in the 980s (Eiríkr rauði led the settlement from Iceland), and the colony was Christianized in the early 11th century. The conversion of Greenland was closely connected to the conversion of Iceland (Greenland was settled from Iceland, and the Greenlanders attended the Alþingi of Iceland for their legal needs). The *Eiríks saga rauða* describes the conversion of Greenland as a more gradual, less conflictual process than the conversion of Iceland — the settlers brought their Christianity with them from Iceland, and the pagan cult was never as firmly established in Greenland as it was in the mother country.

The archaeological evidence from Greenland — the church at Brattahlíð (the farm of Eiríkr rauði's family), the graveyard, the scattered Christian objects — indicates that the Christianization of the colony was complete by the mid-11th century.

**The Norse in the British Isles:**

The Norse settlers in the British Isles — the Danelaw in England, the Norse kingdom of Dublin, the Norse settlements in the Hebrides, Orkney, and Shetland — were in close contact with Christian populations (the Anglo-Saxons, the Irish, the Picts), and their conversion was often rapid and relatively peaceful. The Norse in the Danelaw were baptized within a generation of their settlement (the treaty between Alfred the Great and Guthrum, c. 886, included Guthrum's baptism), and the Norse in Dublin were baptized in the 10th century, often as a condition of political alliance with the Irish kings.

The conversion of the Norse colonies in the British Isles illustrates the importance of the local political context: where the Norse were a minority among a Christian population, they adopted Christianity quickly (they were absorbed into the Christian culture); where they were a majority, they were slower to convert (or they converted on their own terms, maintaining elements of the pagan tradition within the Christian framework).

## Lecture 8: Theological Conflict and Transformation — The Old Gods and the New

The Christianization of Scandinavia was not only a political and social process but a theological one — the encounter between two religious systems that made competing claims about the nature of the divine, the structure of the cosmos, and the destiny of the human soul. The theological conflict was fought in the assemblies, in the law courts, in the sagas, and in the minds of the people who lived through the transition.

**The Christian Critique of the Old Gods:**

The Christian missionaries criticized the Norse gods on several grounds:

- **The gods were not gods.** They were men (the euhemerist argument), or they were demons (the demonological argument), or they were imaginary (the skeptical argument). In any case, they were not worthy of worship.
- **The gods were immoral.** The *Lokasenna* (which may have been composed or edited in the Christian period) presents the gods as adulterers, murderers, oath-breakers, and cowards — a parody of the pagan pantheon that served the Christian polemical purpose.
- **The gods were powerless.** The Christian God had triumphed over the old gods — the churches were built on the sites of the hof, the cross had replaced the hammer, and the Christian kings had defeated the pagan chieftains. The gods' powerlessness was demonstrated by the success of the Christian mission itself.

**The Pagan Critique of Christianity:**

The pagan response to the Christian critique is less well-preserved (the sources are all Christian), but the sagas preserve some pagan arguments:

- **The old gods were the ancestors' gods.** To abandon the old gods was to abandon the ancestors — the kindred's connection to the past, the source of its hamingja (luck), and the foundation of its identity.
- **The Christian God was a weak god.** The White Christ could not defend his servants from the Norse warriors; the monasteries were pillaged, the churches were burned, and the Christian priests were killed. The Christian God's powerlessness was demonstrated by the Vikings' success.
- **Christianity was a slave's religion.** The early Christian converts were slaves and the socially marginal; the free men, the warriors, and the chieftains remained pagan. Christianity was associated with social inferiority, and the conversion was resisted as a threat to the social order.

**The Resolution — The Triumph of the New God:**

The resolution of the theological conflict was not a victory of argument but a victory of power. The Christian kings enforced the conversion by law and by force; the Christian Church provided the institutional infrastructure that the pagan cult could not; and the Christian God "proved" his power by the success of the Christian mission. The old gods did not "lose" the argument — they were defeated, suppressed, and replaced.

But the triumph of Christianity did not eliminate the theological questions that the conversion had raised. The problem of theodicy — the justice of God in a world of suffering — was sharpened by the conversion: if the Christian God was all-powerful and all-good, why had he allowed the old gods to exist at all? Why had he allowed the Norse people to languish in paganism for so many centuries? The answer — that the old gods were demons, that the pagan period was a divine punishment, that the conversion was an act of divine mercy — was a Christian answer, but it did not satisfy everyone, and the theological tensions of the conversion persisted in the Christian culture of post-conversion Scandinavia.

## Lecture 9: Syncretism and Continuity — The Blending of Pagan and Christian

The conversion of Scandinavia was not a clean break with the pagan past. The Christianization of the sacred landscape (churches on the sites of hǫrgar, settlements renamed with Christian toponyms), the transformation of the ritual calendar (Christian feasts replacing the seasonal blóts), and the survival of pagan elements in the Christian folk tradition indicate that the conversion was a gradual process of blending and transformation, in which the old religion was not so much replaced as absorbed.

**The Christianization of the Sacred Landscape:**

The physical landscape of Scandinavia was Christianized through the construction of churches on — or near — the sites of the pre-Christian cult. The GIS-based study by Lundqvist and Magnusson (2038, discussed in NP205) found that over 40% of the medieval churches in the Mälaren valley are located within 500 meters of a pre-Christian sacred place-name. The churches were not replacing the pagan sacred sites but appropriating them — the sacredness of the location was preserved, but its meaning was transformed.

**The Christianization of the Ritual Calendar:**

The seasonal blóts of the pre-Christian calendar — the dísablót (the spring sacrifice to the dísir), the miðsumarsblót (the midsummer sacrifice), the haustblót (the autumn sacrifice for a good harvest), the jól (the midwinter festival) — were absorbed into the Christian calendar:

- **Dísablót → Candlemas (February 2).** The spring sacrifice to the female protective spirits was replaced by the Christian feast of the purification of the Virgin.
- **Miðsumarsblót → St. John's Eve (June 23).** The midsummer bonfires, the fertility rituals, and the gathering of herbs for magical purposes were absorbed into the Christian feast of St. John the Baptist.
- **Haustblót → Michaelmas (September 29).** The autumn harvest sacrifice was replaced by the feast of St. Michael and All Angels.
- **Jól → Christmas (December 25).** The midwinter feast — the most important festival of the pagan year — was absorbed into the Christian feast of the Nativity. The name *jól* was retained (the modern Scandinavian words for Christmas — *jul*, *jol* — derive from the pagan *jól*), and many of the customs of the jól (the feast, the drinking, the gift-giving, the burning of the yule log) persisted in the Christian festival.

**The Survival of Pagan Elements in the Christian Folk Tradition:**

The Christianization of Scandinavia did not eliminate the belief in the álfar, the landvættir, the draugar, and the other non-Christian beings of the Norse tradition. These beings continued to be acknowledged — and propitiated — in the Christian folk tradition, where they were reinterpreted as "hidden folk" (huldufólk, in Icelandic folk belief) or as "nature spirits" whose powers were independent of the Christian God.

The Christian Church's attitude toward these surviving pagan elements was ambivalent: some clergymen condemned them as diabolical, while others tolerated them as harmless superstition or accommodated them within the Christian framework (the landvættir were reinterpreted as angels; the álfar were reinterpreted as the souls of the unbaptized dead).

## Lecture 10: Law and Conversion — The Legal Framework of Christianization

The conversion of Scandinavia was not merely a religious process but a legal one. The post-conversion law codes — the *Gulaþingslǫg* and the *Frostaþingslǫg* in Norway, the *Grágás* in Iceland, the *Skaanske Lov* and the *Jyske Lov* in Denmark — encoded the Christian religion as the law of the land, prohibited pagan practice, and established the institutional infrastructure of the Christian Church.

**The Prohibition of Pagan Practice:**

The Norwegian law codes specifically prohibited the practice of pagan ritual:

- **The prohibition of the blót.** The *Gulaþingslǫg* prohibited the sacrifice to the pagan gods, the eating of horse-meat (a practice associated with the pagan blót), and the participation in pagan rituals.
- **The prohibition of seiðr.** The *Gulaþingslǫg* and the *Frostaþingslǫg* prohibited the practice of seiðr, prescribing outlawry and confiscation of property for the practitioner.
- **The prohibition of the mound-offering.** The *Grágás* prohibited the offering of food and drink to the dead (the haugbúi, the mound-dweller) and the veneration of the burial mounds.

The prohibitions were enforced through the legal system — the offenders were prosecuted, fined, outlawed, or executed — but the enforcement was uneven, and the pagan practices persisted in some regions and in private.

**The Establishment of the Christian Church:**

The law codes also established the institutional infrastructure of the Christian Church:

- **The tithe.** The *Gulaþingslǫg* required the payment of the tithe — a tenth of all produce — to the Church, providing the economic basis for the Church's institutional existence.
- **The bishoprics and parishes.** The law codes defined the ecclesiastical geography — the bishoprics, the parishes, the churches — and the obligations of the population to support them.
- **The Christian calendar.** The law codes specified the Christian feasts and fasts that the population was required to observe, replacing the pagan ritual calendar with the Christian liturgical year.

**The *Kristinréttr* — The Christian Law:**

The *Kristinréttr* (the Christian Law) was a distinct section of the Norwegian and Icelandic law codes that dealt specifically with religious matters. The *Kristinréttr* defined the obligations of the Christian population (attendance at church, the payment of the tithe, the observance of the Christian feasts and fasts), the prohibitions on pagan practice (the blót, the seiðr, the mound-offering), and the penalties for violation (fines, outlawry, excommunication).

The *Kristinréttr* was a legal and administrative tool of Christianization — the Christian religion was not merely preached but *legislated*, and the force of the law was used to enforce the conversion.

## Lecture 11: The Persistence of the Old Ways — Pagan Survivals in Christian Scandinavia

The Christianization of Scandinavia did not eliminate the old religion. Elements of the pre-Christian tradition — the beliefs, the practices, the stories, the gods themselves — survived in the Christian culture, transformed and disguised, but not forgotten.

**The Gods as Euhemerized Heroes:**

The euhemerist tradition (discussed in NP203) preserved the memory of the gods as historical figures — Óðinn as the migrating king, Þórr as the warrior-hero, Freyr as the founder of the Uppsala dynasty. The euhemerist tradition was Christian in its theology (the gods were not gods) but pagan in its content (the stories of the gods were preserved, albeit in a transformed frame).

**The Gods as Demons:**

The demonological tradition reinterpreted the pagan gods as demons — the pagan cult was a form of devil-worship, and the pagan priests (the goðar) were the devil's servants. The demonological interpretation was the darkest form of pagan survival — the old gods were still real (they existed), but they were evil, and their worship was a sin that could damn the soul to eternal punishment.

**The Gods as Folk Figures:**

The folk tradition preserved the gods as characters in stories — Óðinn as the Wild Huntsman, leading his ghostly army through the stormy sky; Þórr as the thunder-giant, fighting the trolls with his hammer; Freyja as the witch-woman, practicing seiðr and shape-shifting. The folk tradition demoted the gods from divine to supernatural, but it preserved their names, their attributes, and their stories, which were told in the Scandinavian countryside long after the conversion.

**The Archaeological Evidence for Continuity:**

The archaeological evidence confirms the persistence of pagan elements in the Christian period:

- **The Mjǫllnir amulets.** The hammer-amulets continued to be worn into the 11th and 12th centuries, often alongside the Christian cross. The combination of the hammer and the cross is evidence of a syncretic period in which the old religion and the new coexisted in the material culture.
- **The Thor's hammer graves.** Some Christian-period graves contain Mjǫllnir amulets, suggesting that the deceased (or the survivors) continued to identify with the old religion even as they were buried in the Christian manner.
- **The pagan burial customs.** Cremation, the provision of grave goods, and the burial in the family mound persisted in some regions into the 12th century, indicating that the Christian burial rite had not entirely displaced the pagan.

## Lecture 12: The Conversion and Modern Heathenry — Reclaiming the Past

The Christianization of Scandinavia is not merely a historical event; it is a living issue for modern Heathens, who understand the conversion as a loss — the suppression of their ancestral religion, the destruction of their sacred places, and the appropriation of their gods. The modern Heathen movement's relationship to the conversion is complex: it includes the mourning of the loss, the study of the historical process, and the attempt to reverse the conversion — to reclaim the gods, to reconstruct the rituals, and to re-establish the Norse pagan tradition as a living religion.

**The Conversion as Trauma:**

For many modern Heathens, the conversion is experienced as a trauma — a violent rupture with the past, a forced abandonment of the ancestral ways, and a spiritual dispossession that has lasted for a thousand years. The conversion is not merely a historical event but a continuing condition — the Christian dominance of Scandinavian culture, the marginalization of Heathenry, and the prejudice against the old gods are legacies of the conversion that persist into the present.

The concept of the conversion as trauma is psychologically powerful but historically problematic: the Norse who converted were not passive victims of a foreign invasion but active participants in a process of religious change that they understood, at least in part, as a positive transformation. The modern Heathen use of the conversion as a narrative of victimhood may simplify a complex historical process and may project modern experiences (the post-colonial critique of Christian missions) onto the pre-modern past.

**The Reconstruction of the Pre-Christian Tradition:**

The modern Heathen movement has reconstructed the pre-Christian tradition — not by a direct line of transmission (the tradition was broken by the conversion) but by the study of the historical sources, by comparative analysis, and by the creative imagination. The reconstruction is a form of "reclaiming" — taking back what was lost, restoring what was broken — and it is also a form of "creation" — building a new tradition from the fragments of the old.

**The Dialogue with Christianity:**

The modern Heathen movement's relationship with Christianity is complex. Some Heathens are explicitly anti-Christian — they regard Christianity as a hostile, foreign religion that has damaged the Norse cultural heritage and that should be rejected — while others are more accommodating, seeking dialogue and cooperation with Christian communities on shared values (environmental stewardship, social justice, the dignity of the human person). The University of Yggdrasil's Norse Pagan Studies program does not take a position on the Heathen-Christian dialogue, but it teaches the historical information, the theological issues, and the ethical considerations that inform the dialogue.
