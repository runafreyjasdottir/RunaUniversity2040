# NP307: Ecological Philosophy in Norse Paganism
## Bachelor of Science in Norse Pagan Studies — University of Yggdrasil, 2040

**Credits:** 4
**Description:** The pre-Christian Norse understood the land as sacred, alive, and demanding of respect. This course explores the ecological dimensions of Norse paganism — the concept of the landvættir (land-spirits), the cosmology of Yggdrasill as an ecological model, the ethics of sustainability and reciprocity in the Norse relationship to the natural world, and the contemporary application of Norse ecological philosophy to the environmental crises of the 21st century. Drawing on the Eddas, the sagas, the archaeological evidence, landscape archaeology, environmental anthropology, and the 2040 ecological turn in Heathen theology, students examine whether the Norse tradition offers resources for an alternative, non-exploitative relationship to the natural world.

## Lecture 1: Was Norse Paganism "Ecological"? — Framing the Question

The question at the heart of this course — was pre-Christian Norse religion "ecological"? — requires careful framing. The Norse were not environmentalists in the modern sense. They exploited their environment: they deforested Iceland, hunted the great auks to extinction, and reshaped the landscape through agriculture, grazing, and settlement. The concept of "ecology" as a scientific discipline and as a political movement did not exist in the Viking Age. To ask whether the Norse were "ecological" is to risk anachronism — projecting modern values onto the pre-modern past.

But the question is not meaningless. The Norse had a theology of the land — a set of beliefs, practices, and values that governed their relationship to the natural world. They understood the land as sacred, as alive with spirit-presences (the landvættir, the álfar, the dísir), and as demanding respect and reciprocity. They practiced rituals (the blót, the mound-offering, the land-taking) that acknowledged the land's non-human powers and that sought to maintain the right relationship between the human community and the non-human world. Whether this theology was "ecological" in the modern sense is less important than whether it offers resources for an alternative relationship to the natural world — a relationship that is not based on domination (the Cartesian model of humanity as master of nature) or on indifference (the market model of nature as a collection of resources).

**The 2040 Ecological Turn:**

The 2040s have seen a growing interest in the ecological dimensions of Norse paganism, driven by several factors:

- **The environmental crisis.** Climate change, biodiversity loss, and ecological collapse have made the question of humanity's relationship to the natural world urgent and unavoidable. The search for alternative models of human-nature relationship has led some scholars and practitioners to the pre-Christian traditions, including the Norse.

- **The ecological turn in Heathen theology.** The modern Ásatrú and Heathen movement has increasingly emphasized the ecological dimensions of the Norse tradition, developing a "green Heathenry" that interprets the landvættir as ecological principles, the blót as an act of ecological reciprocity, and Yggdrasill as a model of the interconnectedness of all life.

- **The archaeological and landscape-archaeological evidence.** The growing body of evidence for the Norse sacred landscape (discussed in NP205) has provided a material basis for the ecological interpretation — the Norse did not merely have ideas about the land; they enacted those ideas in their arrangement of the landscape, their treatment of sacred sites, and their ritual practices.

**The Limitations of the Ecological Interpretation:**

The ecological interpretation has limitations that must be acknowledged from the outset:

- **The Norse were not conservationists.** They transformed their environment — sometimes sustainably, sometimes destructively. The ecological interpretation cannot whitewash the Norse environmental record, but it can illuminate the values and beliefs that governed the Norse relationship to the land.
- **The sources are partial.** The textual sources for Norse ecology are fragmentary and biased — they were written by Christians who were not interested in (and often hostile to) the pagan understanding of the land. The archaeological evidence can supplement the texts, but it cannot tell us what the Norse *believed* about the land.
- **The modern application is contested.** The application of Norse ecological philosophy to the modern environmental crisis is not straightforward — the pre-Christian Norse lived in a different world, with different technologies, different population densities, and different environmental pressures. The Norse tradition cannot be directly "applied" to the 2040s, but it can be drawn on as a resource.

## Lecture 2: Yggdrasill as Ecological Model — The World Tree and Interconnectedness

Yggdrasill, the World Tree, is the most powerful image in the Norse cosmological tradition. It is also — in the 2040 ecological reading — a model of ecological interconnectedness: a living system in which all beings are connected to all others, in which the health of the whole depends on the health of each part, and in which the destruction of one element threatens the collapse of the entire structure.

**Yggdrasill as Living System:**

The *Grímnismál* and the *Vǫluspa* describe Yggdrasill as a living tree — it is not a static structure but a dynamic, growing, vulnerable organism. The tree is attacked by the animals that gnaw at its branches (the four stags) and at its roots (the dragon Níðhǫggr), and it is sustained by the water from the three wells (Urðarbrunnr, Mímisbrunnr, Hvergelmir). The tree's health is precarious — it trembles at Ragnarǫk (the *Vǫluspa*: "Yggdrasill trembles, the ash standing upright"), and its destruction is the end of the cosmic order.

The description of Yggdrasill as a system under threat — a living organism that is attacked, sustained, and ultimately vulnerable — is a model of ecological interconnectedness. The tree is not a metaphor for the cosmos; it *is* the cosmos, and the cosmos is a living system, subject to the same threats (parasitism, resource depletion, catastrophic collapse) as any other living system.

**The Animals of Yggdrasill — An Ecological Community:**

The animals that live in Yggdrasill's branches and roots constitute an ecological community — a network of interdependent beings, each with its own role in the system:

- **The eagle at the top** — the apex predator, the overseer, the being that sees the whole.
- **The dragon Níðhǫggr at the bottom** — the parasite, the consumer, the being that threatens the tree's existence.
- **The squirrel Ratatoskr** — the messenger, the conduit of communication between the top and the bottom, the being that carries the insults and the provocations that sustain the conflict between the eagle and the dragon.
- **The four stags** — the herbivores, the beings that feed on the tree's leaves, drawing their sustenance from the tree and returning their waste to the soil.

The community of Yggdrasill is not harmonious — it is a system of conflict, consumption, and communication, in which each being pursues its own interests but is contained by the network of relationships that constitutes the tree. The tree's health is the health of the community, and the community's destruction (at Ragnarǫk) is the tree's destruction.

**Yggdrasill and the Modern Ecological Imagination:**

The image of Yggdrasill as a living, interconnected system has been powerful in the modern ecological imagination, inspiring thinkers and activists who see in the World Tree a model of the biosphere — the global ecosystem, under threat from human activity, whose health depends on the recognition of the interconnectedness of all life.

The 2040 ecological reading of Yggdrasill does not claim that the pre-Christian Norse understood the tree as an "ecological model" in the modern sense. It claims that the tree — as a living, interconnected, vulnerable system — resonates with modern ecological consciousness, and that the resonance is not accidental: the pre-Christian Norse understood the world as alive, as interconnected, and as vulnerable, in ways that anticipate and illuminate the modern ecological understanding.

## Lecture 3: The Landvættir — Spirits, Ecology, and Environmental Ethics

The landvættir (land-spirits, land-wights) are the most immediate and the most practical expression of the Norse ecological understanding. The landvættir are the spirits of the land — the non-human beings who inhabit the hills, the waters, the forests, and the specific features of the landscape — and their relationship to the human community is reciprocal: the landvættir protect the community in exchange for the community's respect and acknowledgment.

**The Landvættir as Ecological Guardians:**

The landvættir can be read as ecological guardians — supernatural beings whose function is to regulate the relationship between the human community and the natural environment. The prohibitions on offending the landvættir — cutting trees in their groves, polluting their springs, building on their hills — are, in ecological terms, restrictions on unsustainable resource use. The recommendation to propitiate the landvættir — with offerings, with respectful behavior — is, in ecological terms, a principle of reciprocity: the community gives to the land, and the land gives back.

The 2040 ecological reading of the landvættir is not reductive — it does not claim that the landvættir were "really" ecological principles, and that the Norse who believed in them were "really" practicing ecology. It claims that the landvættir tradition encoded ecological knowledge in the language of the sacred — the knowledge that the land is a living system, that the human community depends on the land, that the land can be depleted or offended by human activity, and that the right relationship between the community and the land is one of reciprocity and respect.

**The Four Landvættir of Iceland — Guardians of the Land:**

The four great landvættir of Iceland — the dragon of the east, the eagle (or griffin) of the north, the bull of the west, and the mountain-giant (bergrisi) of the south — are the most dramatic example of the landvættir tradition. The *Íslendingabók* describes how the Danish king Haraldr Gormsson sent a wizard to Iceland to spy on the land, and how the wizard was met at each quarter by a fearsome landvættir who prevented him from landing. The four landvættir are the guardians of Iceland — they protect the island from invasion, and their presence is the sign that the land is alive and defended.

The four landvættir appear on the coat of arms of Iceland (the dragon, the eagle/griffin, the bull, and the giant), where they continue to symbolize the nation's connection to its land and its heritage. The survival of the landvættir into the modern national symbolism is evidence of the persistence of the Norse understanding of the land as alive, sacred, and protected.

**The Landvættir and Modern Environmental Ethics:**

The landvættir tradition has been invoked in modern environmental debates in Iceland and Scandinavia. The protests against the construction of dams, roads, and industrial facilities on sites that are believed to be inhabited by landvættir or by the huldufólk (the "hidden folk," the Icelandic folk-tradition descendants of the álfar) have drawn on the Norse sacred landscape tradition — the land is alive, and the beings who inhabit it must be respected. The protests are not "religious" in the conventional sense (the protesters do not necessarily believe in the literal existence of the landvættir), but they draw on the cultural memory of the Norse sacred landscape to articulate an environmental ethic that is rooted in the specific history and identity of the Scandinavian people.

## Lecture 4: The Blót as Ecological Exchange — Sacrifice, Reciprocity, and the Gift Economy

The blót — the sacrifice at the hǫrgr or the hof — was not merely a ritual of worship but an act of ecological exchange: the community gave to the gods (and to the land) in the expectation that the gods (and the land) would give back. The blót was a gift economy, in which the relationship between the human and the non-human was structured by the principles of reciprocity, obligation, and mutual dependence.

**The Gift Economy of the Blót:**

The anthropological concept of the "gift economy" — developed by Marcel Mauss in *The Gift* (1925) — provides a framework for understanding the blót as an ecological practice. In a gift economy, the exchange of gifts creates and sustains social relationships: the giver gives a gift, which creates an obligation in the receiver, who reciprocates with a return gift, which creates a new obligation, and the cycle continues, sustaining the relationship across time.

The blót was a gift economy between the human community and the divine/natural world:

- **The human community gave:** the sacrificed animal, the offerings of food and drink, the blood sprinkled on the hǫrgr and on the participants.
- **The divine/natural world gave back:** the fertility of the land, the health of the animals, the victory in battle, the peace of the community.

The blót was not a transaction (a payment for services rendered) but a relationship — a cycle of giving and receiving that sustained the community's connection to the powers on which it depended. The blót was the mechanism by which the community maintained its membership in the larger-than-human community of the land.

**The Blót and the Seasonal Cycle:**

The blóts were tied to the seasonal cycle — the dísablót (spring), the miðsumarsblót (midsummer), the haustblót (autumn), the jól (midwinter) — and each blót addressed the specific ecological needs of the season:

- **The dísablót** — the sacrifice to the dísir (the female protective spirits) at the beginning of the growing season, asking for the fertility of the land and the protection of the kindred.
- **The miðsumarsblót** — the sacrifice at the height of the summer, giving thanks for the growth of the crops and asking for a successful harvest.
- **The haustblót** — the sacrifice at the harvest, giving thanks for the gathered produce and asking for the survival of the livestock through the winter.
- **The jól** — the midwinter sacrifice, the most important blót of the year, asking for the return of the sun and the renewal of the cycle of growth.

The seasonal blóts linked the human community to the rhythms of the natural world — the rituals acknowledged the community's dependence on the land, expressed gratitude for the land's gifts, and petitioned for the land's continued generosity.

**The Blót and the Modern Ecological Consciousness:**

The blót has been revived in the modern Ásatrú and Heathen movement, often with an explicitly ecological emphasis. The modern blót is a ritual of connection — the community gathers at a sacred site (or at a constructed hǫrgr), makes offerings (mead, grain, fruit) to the gods and to the landvættir, and acknowledges the community's dependence on the natural world. The modern blót is not a re-enactment of the historical practice (the animal sacrifice, the blood-sprinkling, the horse-meat feast are generally omitted) but a creative adaptation of the historical tradition to the modern ecological consciousness.

## Lecture 5: The Ethics of Sustainability — Norse Resource Use and Its Limits

The Norse relationship to the natural world was not static — it changed over time, in response to environmental pressures, technological innovations, and cultural transformations. The archaeological and environmental evidence provides a record of Norse resource use that reveals both the sustainability and the unsustainability of the Norse economy.

**The Forest and the Farm:**

The Norse economy was based on a combination of agriculture, animal husbandry, hunting, fishing, and gathering. The settlement of Iceland — the most dramatic Norse environmental transformation — involved the deforestation of the island to create pasture for sheep and cattle and to provide timber for building and fuel. The deforestation of Iceland was rapid (the island was mostly forested when the Norse arrived in the 9th century; it was mostly treeless by the 12th century) and its effects were severe: soil erosion, the loss of biodiversity, and the degradation of the landscape that has persisted to the present.

The deforestation of Iceland is the most visible evidence of the unsustainability of the Norse economy — the Norse settlers transformed their environment in ways that were destructive and irreversible. But the Icelandic case is exceptional — the settlers were moving into an unfamiliar environment (Iceland is a volcanic island with fragile soils and a short growing season) and they did not have the ecological knowledge (the *local* knowledge of the specific environment) that would have allowed them to manage the island sustainably.

**The Outfield and the Infield:**

The Norse farm was organized around the distinction between the infield (the tún, the cultivated area close to the farmhouse) and the outfield (the úthagi, the uncultivated land beyond the infield). The infield was intensively managed — manured, irrigated, and cultivated for grain and vegetables. The outfield was extensively used — grazed by livestock, hunted for game and fowl, gathered for wild plants and timber.

The infield-outfield system was a form of sustainable resource management — the infield was carefully maintained to provide the staple food supply, while the outfield was exploited at a level that the ecosystem could sustain. But the distinction between the infield and the outfield was not merely practical — it was also sacred. The farm's boundary (the tún-garðr) was a ritual boundary as well as a physical one: the infield was the domesticated, human-controlled space; the outfield was the wild, spirit-inhabited space, the domain of the landvættir and the álfar.

**The Sacred Constraints on Resource Use:**

The Norse sacred landscape tradition imposed constraints on resource use that may have contributed to the sustainability of the Norse economy. The prohibitions on offending the landvættir (cutting trees in their groves, polluting their springs, building on their hills) were, in effect, protected-area designations — specific locations that were off-limits to exploitation, preserved as sacred sites. The prohibition on sailing to Iceland with a dragon-head (so as not to frighten the landvættir) was a ritual acknowledgment of the land's non-human inhabitants — a recognition that the land was not empty but occupied, and that the human settlement of the land required the landvættir's consent.

## Lecture 6: Animism and the Norse World — The Living Land

The Norse understanding of the land as alive is the most fundamental ecological insight of the pre-Christian tradition. The concept of *animism* — the belief that the world is full of persons, only some of whom are human — provides a framework for understanding the Norse relationship to the non-human world.

**What Is Animism?**

Animism, in the modern anthropological sense (as developed by Graham Harvey and others), is not a "belief that everything has a soul" (the 19th-century definition) but a way of relating to the world that recognizes the personhood of non-human beings — animals, plants, rocks, rivers, mountains, the land itself. Animism is not a doctrine but a practice — a set of behaviors (greeting, offering, respecting the boundaries) that constitute a relationship with the non-human persons who share the world with human persons.

The Norse tradition is profoundly animist. The landvættir, the álfar, the dísir, the draugar, the gods themselves — all are persons (beings with their own will, their own interests, their own capacity for relationship) who are not human but who inhabit the same world as humans and who must be treated with the respect that is due to persons.

**The Practice of Animism in the Norse World:**

The Norse practice of animism is visible in the rituals, the prohibitions, and the everyday behaviors that governed the relationship between humans and non-humans:

- **Greeting the land.** The *Landnámabók* describes the ritual of land-taking — the settler walked around the boundaries of the land he claimed, lighting fires to mark the boundary and to announce his presence to the landvættir. The land-taking was not a unilateral declaration of ownership but a communication — the settler was introducing himself to the land's non-human inhabitants and asking (or demanding) their acceptance.
- **Offering to the land.** The blót, the mound-offering, the álfhǫll sacrifice — these were gifts to the non-human persons who inhabited the land, expressing gratitude, seeking favor, and maintaining the relationship.
- **Respecting the boundaries.** The prohibitions on offending the landvættir — cutting trees in their groves, polluting their springs, building on their hills — were the behavioral expressions of the recognition that the land was inhabited and that the non-human inhabitants had rights to their specific places.

**Animism and the Modern Ecological Consciousness:**

The animist interpretation of the Norse tradition has been influential in the modern ecological movement, which has drawn on the concept of animism to articulate a relationship to the natural world that is based on respect, reciprocity, and the recognition of the personhood of non-human beings. The Norse animist tradition is not a blueprint for modern environmental policy, but it is a resource for the imagination — a way of thinking about the human relationship to the land that is an alternative to the dominant models of exploitation and indifference.

## Lecture 7: Ragnarǫk as Ecological Catastrophe — The End of the World and the Warning of the Gods

Ragnarǫk — the final battle, the destruction of the gods, the burning of the world — is the most terrifying image in the Norse mythological tradition. But Ragnarǫk is not merely a myth of cosmic destruction; it is also, in the 2040 ecological reading, a warning about the consequences of the disruption of the cosmic order and a reflection of the Norse experience of environmental catastrophe.

**Ragnarǫk as System Collapse:**

The events of Ragnarǫk — the cosmic winter (Fimbulvetr), the breaking of the bonds that contain the monsters, the fire-giant Surtr's burning of the world, the sinking of the earth into the sea — describe a system collapse: the breakdown of the order that sustains the world, triggered by the accumulation of violations (the binding of Loki, the breaking of oaths, the violation of the sacred) and resulting in the destruction of everything.

Ragnarǫk is the ecological catastrophe at the cosmic scale: the World Tree trembles, the sun turns black, the stars fall from the sky, the earth sinks into the sea. The destruction is total — only the memory of the old world survives, and the new world that rises from the sea is a fresh creation, not a restoration of the old.

**Ragnarǫk and the Norse Environmental Experience:**

The 2040 reading of Ragnarǫk as an ecological myth connects the apocalyptic narrative to the Norse environmental experience — the volcanic eruptions of Iceland (the Eldgjá eruption of c. 934, the Laki eruption of 1783-84), the climatic fluctuations of the North Atlantic, the deforestation and soil erosion that transformed the Icelandic landscape. The Norse lived in an environment that was volatile, unpredictable, and capable of catastrophic collapse, and Ragnarǫk may encode that experience — the knowledge that the world is fragile, that the order that sustains it can break, and that the collapse, when it comes, is absolute.

**Ragnarǫk and the Modern Climate Crisis:**

The image of Ragnarǫk has been taken up by the modern climate movement as a myth of ecological catastrophe — the "Fimbulvetr" as the climate crisis, the "Surtr's fire" as the burning of the forests and the rising of the seas, the "Ragnarǫk" as the collapse of the global ecosystem. The Ragnarǫk myth does not predict the climate crisis in any literal sense, but it provides a narrative framework for understanding the crisis — the violation of the sacred order (the exploitation of the earth), the accumulation of violations (the centuries of industrialization), and the catastrophic consequence (the collapse of the biosphere).

The Ragnarǫk myth also provides a narrative of renewal — after the destruction, a new world rises from the sea, green and fair, where Baldr returns and the survivors rebuild. The renewal is not a restoration but a new creation — the old world is gone, and the new world is different, built on different principles.

## Lecture 8: Green Heathenry — The Modern Ecological Movement in Ásatrú

The modern Ásatrú and Heathen movement has increasingly emphasized the ecological dimensions of the Norse tradition, developing a "green Heathenry" that interprets the Norse sacred landscape, the landvættir tradition, and the ethics of the blót as resources for environmental activism and sustainable living.

**The Principles of Green Heathenry:**

The 2040 Declaration of Deeds (the ethical statement of the international Ásatrú community) includes a section on "The Land and the Community" that articulates the principles of green Heathenry:

- **The land is sacred.** The earth is not a resource to be exploited but a sacred community of beings — human and non-human — with whom the Heathen community is in relationship.
- **The landvættir are real.** The land-spirits are not metaphors but persons — non-human beings with their own will, their own interests, and their own capacity for offense and for blessing.
- **Reciprocity is the law.** The relationship between the human community and the land is structured by reciprocity — the community gives to the land, and the land gives back.
- **The environmental crisis is a spiritual crisis.** The destruction of the earth is not merely an economic or a political problem but a spiritual one — a violation of the sacred order, a desecration of the divine, a failure of the human community's obligation to the non-human persons who share the world.

**Green Heathenry in Practice:**

Green Heathenry is practiced through:

- **Ecological blóts** — rituals that explicitly acknowledge the ecological dimensions of the sacrifice, making offerings to the landvættir and the gods with the intention of maintaining the health of the land and the community's relationship to it.
- **Land-protection activism** — participation in environmental campaigns, protests against the destruction of sacred sites (the álfhǫllir, the hǫrgar, the vé locations), and advocacy for the legal protection of the sacred landscape.
- **Sustainable living** — the adoption of sustainable practices (organic agriculture, renewable energy, low-impact consumption) as expressions of the Heathen commitment to the health of the land.

**The Critique of Green Heathenry:**

Green Heathenry has been criticized on several grounds:

- **Anachronism.** The pre-Christian Norse were not environmentalists — they exploited their environment as vigorously as any pre-modern people. Green Heathenry is a modern projection, not a recovery of the pre-Christian tradition.
- **The loss of the sacrificial dimension.** The modern ecological blót omits the animal sacrifice that was the core of the historical blót — the killing of the animal, the sprinkling of the blood — and the omission transforms the blót from a sacrificial ritual into a symbolic gesture.
- **The instrumentalization of the tradition.** Green Heathenry uses the Norse tradition as a vehicle for modern environmental politics, reducing the gods and the landvættir to symbols and the ritual to a political statement.

The University of Yggdrasil's Norse Pagan Studies program does not adjudicate these criticisms; it provides the historical, theological, and ethical framework for students to make their own judgments about the relationship between the Norse tradition and the modern environmental movement.

## Lecture 9: Comparative Ecologies — Norse, Sami, Celtic, and Indigenous Traditions

The Norse ecological tradition is not unique. The Sami, the Celtic, and the indigenous circumpolar traditions share similar understandings of the land as alive, the spirits of place as real, and the human-nature relationship as reciprocal. The comparative study of these traditions illuminates both what is distinctive about the Norse tradition and what it shares with the broader circumpolar ecological complex.

**The Sami Ecological Tradition:**

The Sami noaidi tradition (discussed in NP303) includes a rich ecological dimension. The Sami relationship to the land is mediated by the spirits of place (the *háldi*), the spirits of the underworld (the *sáiva*), and the ritual specialists (the *noaidi*) who communicate with the spirits. The Sami reindeer-herding economy is a form of ecological management that is integrated with the noaidi's spiritual practice — the noaidi's ritual ensures the health of the reindeer, the fertility of the grazing lands, and the right relationship between the human community and the land.

**The Celtic Ecological Tradition:**

The pre-Christian Celtic tradition — preserved in the Irish and Welsh literary sources and in the archaeological evidence — includes a similar understanding of the land as sacred and the spirits of place as real. The Celtic *sidhe* (the fairy mounds, the dwelling places of the spirits) are parallels to the Norse álfhǫllir, and the Celtic sacred springs, groves, and hills are parallels to the Norse vé, hǫrgr, and sacred landscape features.

**The Indigenous Circumpolar Traditions:**

The indigenous peoples of the circumpolar region — the Inuit, the Yupik, the Chukchi, the Evenki, and others — have ecological traditions that emphasize the reciprocity between the human community and the non-human world, the personhood of animals and the land, and the ritual obligations of the community to the spirits. The circumpolar traditions are the broader context of the Norse ecological tradition — the Norse were part of a circumpolar cultural complex that extended from Siberia to Scandinavia, and their ecological understanding was shaped by their participation in that complex.

**The 2040 Comparative Framework:**

The comparative study of the Norse ecological tradition does not reduce the Norse tradition to a variant of a broader circumpolar pattern, but it illuminates what is shared and what is distinctive. The Norse ecological tradition shares with the Sami, the Celtic, and the indigenous circumpolar traditions the understanding of the land as alive, the spirits of place as real, and the human-nature relationship as reciprocal. But the Norse tradition is distinctive in its cosmology (Yggdrasill as the axis mundi), its gods (the Æsir and the Vanir as the patrons of the sacred landscape), and its ritual system (the blót as the mechanism of the gift economy between the human and the non-human).

## Lecture 10: The Environmental Record — What the Norse Actually Did

The archaeological and environmental evidence provides a record of what the Norse actually did to their environment, as distinct from what they believed about it. The record is mixed — the Norse were capable of both sustainable management and destructive exploitation, and the balance between the two varied by region, period, and circumstance.

**The North Atlantic Colonies:**

The Norse colonies in the North Atlantic — Iceland, Greenland, the Faroes, the Shetlands, Orkney — provide the most detailed environmental record of the Norse settlement. The cores of lake sediments, the pollen analysis, and the soil profiles reveal the environmental impact of the Norse settlement:

- **Iceland** — The deforestation of the island (the loss of 95% of the original forest cover) was rapid and irreversible. The introduction of sheep and cattle (alien species in the Icelandic ecosystem) transformed the vegetation, and the resulting soil erosion (the loss of the fragile volcanic soils) degraded the landscape.
- **Greenland** — The Norse colony in Greenland (established c. 985, abandoned c. 1450) was a marginal environment from the beginning, and the Norse settlers struggled to maintain their European agricultural economy in an Arctic environment. The colony's collapse was probably due to a combination of climatic deterioration (the Little Ice Age), environmental degradation (overgrazing, soil erosion), and the failure to adapt to the changing conditions.
- **The Faroes** — The Faroe Islands were deforested by the Norse settlement, but the environmental impact was less severe than in Iceland (the Faroes are smaller, and the environmental pressures were less intense).

**The Scandinavian Homeland:**

In Scandinavia (Denmark, Norway, Sweden), the environmental impact of the Norse settlement was less dramatic than in the North Atlantic colonies. The Scandinavian environment was more resilient than the Icelandic, and the Norse settlement in Scandinavia was older and more established — the settlers were not pioneers in an unknown land but inhabitants of a landscape that had been shaped by human activity for millennia.

But the Norse in Scandinavia also transformed their environment — the expansion of agriculture, the draining of wetlands, the clearance of forests, and the exploitation of mineral resources (iron, copper, silver) left their mark on the Scandinavian landscape. The Norse were not conservationists, but they were also not indifferent to the land — the sacred landscape tradition indicates that the Norse understood the land as more than a collection of resources, even if they did not always act on that understanding.

## Lecture 11: Re-wilding and the Norse Imagination — The Return of the Wild

The 2040s have seen a growing interest in "re-wilding" — the restoration of wild ecosystems, the reintroduction of extinct or endangered species, and the creation of spaces where the non-human world can flourish without human control. The Norse ecological tradition — particularly the concept of the outfield, the domain of the landvættir — provides a cultural framework for the re-wilding movement in Scandinavia.

**The Outfield as the Wild:**

The Norse outfield — the úthagi, the land beyond the farm's boundary — was the wild, the space that was not cultivated, not domesticated, not under human control. The outfield was the domain of the landvættir, the álfar, and the other non-human persons, and the human relationship to the outfield was one of respect, caution, and ritual acknowledgment.

The concept of the outfield provides a cultural framework for the re-wilding movement — a way of understanding the restoration of wild ecosystems as a restoration of the domain of the landvættir, a re-creation of the space where the non-human world is allowed to exist on its own terms.

**The Reintroduction of the Wolf and the Bear:**

The reintroduction of wolves and bears to Scandinavia — species that were hunted to local extinction in the 19th and early 20th centuries — has been a controversial issue, opposed by farmers (who fear for their livestock) and supported by environmentalists (who advocate the restoration of the natural ecosystem). The Norse ecological tradition provides a perspective on the reintroduction: the wolf and the bear were feared and respected in the Norse tradition (the wolf was Óðinn's animal, the bear was the berserkr's totem), and their return to the landscape is a return of the wild powers that the Norse recognized as part of the community of beings.

**The Sacred Landscape as Re-wilded Space:**

The Norse sacred landscape — the hǫrgar, the vé, the álfhǫllir — were spaces that were set apart from human exploitation, preserved as the domains of the non-human persons who inhabited them. The re-wilding of the Norse sacred landscape — the restoration of the groves, the springs, the hills to their pre-agricultural state — is a form of ecological restoration that is also a religious act: the restoration of the sacred space, the renewal of the relationship between the human community and the landvættir.

## Lecture 12: The Future of Norse Ecological Philosophy — From Tradition to Transformation

The Norse ecological tradition is not a fixed body of doctrine that can be "recovered" and "applied" to the modern world. It is a living tradition — or, more precisely, a tradition that is being revived, reconstructed, and adapted to the circumstances of the 21st century. The future of Norse ecological philosophy depends on the creativity of the Heathen community — their willingness to engage with the historical sources, with the modern ecological science, and with the practical challenges of living sustainably in a damaged world.

**From Recovery to Creation:**

The recovery of the Norse ecological tradition — the reconstruction of the pre-Christian understanding of the land as sacred, the identification of the landvættir as real, the revival of the blót as an act of ecological reciprocity — is the first step. But the recovery is not enough. The Norse tradition was developed for a world that no longer exists — a world of small-scale farming communities, of local economies, of a population density that is a fraction of the modern. The Norse tradition cannot simply be "applied" to the modern world; it must be transformed — adapted to the urban, industrial, globalized context of the 2040s.

**The Transformation:**

The transformation of the Norse ecological tradition involves:

- **The integration of modern ecological science.** The Norse tradition's ecological knowledge was local and practical; the modern ecological crisis requires global and theoretical knowledge. The Heathen community must integrate the Norse tradition with the best available ecological science — not as a replacement for the tradition but as a complement to it.
- **The expansion of the community.** The Norse ecological community was the local kindred — the farm, the village, the assembly. The modern ecological community is the global — the biosphere, the human species, the community of all beings. The Heathen community must expand its sense of community to include the non-human world at all scales, from the local hill to the global climate.
- **The political engagement.** The Norse ecological tradition was apolitical in the modern sense — the blót was a ritual, not a policy. The modern ecological crisis requires political engagement — the Heathen community must advocate for environmental policies, participate in the democratic process, and hold accountable the corporations and governments that are responsible for the destruction of the earth.

**The Hope of the New World:**

The *Vǫluspa*'s image of the new world after Ragnarǫk — the green earth rising from the sea, the return of Baldr, the survivors rebuilding — is the hope that sustains the ecological engagement. The Norse tradition does not promise that the world will be saved; it promises that the world will be renewed — after the catastrophe, after the destruction, after the old order has collapsed. The hope of the new world is what makes the ecological engagement meaningful: the work of protecting the earth is not futile, even if it fails; it is the work of preparing the ground for the renewal that will come.
