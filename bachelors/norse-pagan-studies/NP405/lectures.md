# NP405: Independent Research Project II
## Bachelor of Science in Norse Pagan Studies — University of Yggdrasil, 2040

**Credits:** 4
**Description:** The Independent Research Project is the culmination of the Norse Pagan Studies major — an opportunity for the student to design, conduct, and present an original research project on a topic of their choice, under the supervision of a faculty advisor. NP405 is the second of two independent research courses (following NP305 which is a taught course, and NP405 which is the project); the project may be a traditional research paper, a digital reconstruction, a ritual analysis, a fieldwork report, or any other format approved by the advisor. **Prerequisites:** Senior standing, NP401 (Advanced Seminar), and approval of the faculty advisor
**Corequisite:** NP405 is the capstone project for the Norse Pagan Studies major

## Lecture 1: Designing the Research Project — Questions, Methods, and Scope

The first and most important step in the independent research project is the formulation of the research question — the question that the project will answer, the problem that it will solve, the gap in the scholarship that it will fill. A good research question is specific, answerable, significant, and original.

**The Characteristics of a Good Research Question:**

- **Specific.** The question must be narrow enough to be answerable within the scope of a 4-credit, semester-long project. "What did the Norse believe about the afterlife?" is too broad; "How does the representation of Hel in the *Baldrs draumar* compare to its representation in Snorri's Gylfaginning?" is specific.
- **Answerable.** The question must be answerable with the available evidence. "What did the pre-Christian Norse really believe?" is not answerable (the evidence is too fragmentary); "What does the archaeological evidence of the staff-graves tell us about the social status of the vǫlva?" is answerable.
- **Significant.** The question must matter — it should contribute to the scholarship, advance the understanding of the Norse tradition, or provide a new perspective on a known problem.
- **Original.** The question should not simply repeat what has already been done. The student should identify a gap in the scholarship — a topic that has not been adequately treated, a question that has not been answered, a perspective that has not been applied.

**The Research Design:**

Once the research question is formulated, the student must design the research:

- **The evidence.** What evidence will be used to answer the question? The textual sources (the Eddas, the sagas, the skaldic poetry), the archaeological evidence (the graves, the cult sites, the objects), the place-name evidence, the comparative evidence?
- **The method.** What method will be applied to the evidence? Textual analysis, archaeological analysis, comparative analysis, theoretical analysis (structuralist, feminist, postcolonial, cognitive)?
- **The scope.** What is the scope of the project? A 25-page research paper, a digital reconstruction, a ritual analysis, a fieldwork report?

**The Proposal:**

The research proposal (5-7 pages) must include:

1. **The research question** — clearly stated.
2. **The significance** — why does the question matter?
3. **The evidence** — what sources will be used, and how will they be accessed?
4. **The method** — how will the evidence be analyzed?
5. **The scope** — the format of the final project and the expected length.
6. **The bibliography** — a preliminary list of the primary and secondary sources.

### Required Reading
- Booth, W.C. et al., *The Craft of Research* (2037 edition), ch. 1-4
- The NP405 Research Proposal Guidelines (course packet)
- Course textbook, ch. 1-3

**Sample Research Questions — Successful NP405 Projects 2035-2040:**

The following are examples of strong research questions from previous NP405 projects:
- "How does the representation of the valkyrjur in the *Vǫluspa* and the *Grímnismál* reflect the changing role of elite women in the Viking Age?"
- "What does the spatial distribution of hof place-names in Trøndelag reveal about the political geography of pre-Christian Norway?"
- "Can the cognitive science of religion explain the cross-cultural persistence of the 'dying god' motif, as exemplified in the Baldr myth?"
- "How did the conversion-era Icelandic law codes negotiate the tension between Christian prohibition and the persistence of pagan burial customs?"

These questions are specific, answerable, significant, and original — they identify a gap in the scholarship and propose a method for filling it.

2. What are the most common mistakes in research question formulation? How can they be avoided?
3. How does the choice of method (textual analysis, archaeological analysis, theoretical analysis) affect the research question?

## Lecture 2: Literature Review — Mapping the Scholarship

The literature review is the foundation of the research project — it maps the existing scholarship on the topic, identifies the gaps that the project will fill, and establishes the context for the student's original contribution.

**The Purpose of the Literature Review:**

- **To demonstrate mastery.** The literature review shows that the student knows the scholarship — the major works, the key arguments, the current debates.
- **To identify the gap.** The literature review identifies what has not been done — the question that has not been answered, the evidence that has not been examined, the perspective that has not been applied.
- **To position the project.** The literature review positions the student's project in relation to the existing scholarship — it shows how the project builds on, challenges, or extends the work that has been done.

**The Method of the Literature Review:**

1. **Search the databases.** The University of Yggdrasil's library databases (the Norse Studies Database, the Edda Textual Platform, the runic corpus, the SLP database) are the starting point.
2. **Read the major works.** The key monographs, the canonical articles, the reference works — these are the foundation of the literature review.
3. **Follow the citations.** The bibliographies of the major works lead to the secondary literature — the articles, the book chapters, the dissertations — that fill in the detail.
4. **Identify the debates.** The literature review should identify the debates — the points on which the scholars disagree, the competing interpretations, the unresolved questions.
5. **Write the review.** The literature review is not a list of summaries but an argument — it should organize the scholarship around the student's research question, showing how the question has been addressed (or not), and how the student's project will contribute.

### Required Reading
- Fink, A., *Conducting Research Literature Reviews* (2037 edition), ch. 3-5
- Course textbook, ch. 4-6

**The University of Yggdrasil's Digital Resources for Literature Review:**

The University provides several digital resources specifically designed for literature review in Norse Pagan Studies:
- **The Norse Studies Database (NSD)** — a comprehensive, searchable database of all scholarly publications in Norse Pagan Studies from 1800 to the present, with abstracts, keywords, and citation information. The NSD allows filtering by topic, period, methodology, and source type.
- **The Edda Textual Platform (ETP)** — the fully annotated, machine-readable edition of the Eddas, with parallel texts, commentaries, and links to the secondary literature (see NP203).
- **The Runic Corpus Database** — a searchable database of all known runic inscriptions, with transliterations, translations, and archaeological context.
- **The Sacred Landscape Project (SLP) Database** — the GIS database of pre-Christian Norse sacred sites (see NP205, NP403).

These resources are the starting point for any NP405 literature review. Students should be proficient in their use before beginning the project.

### Discussion Questions
1. How do you distinguish between the major works and the secondary literature? What makes a work "major"?
2. How do you identify the gap in the scholarship? What are the signs of a gap?
3. How should the literature review be organized — chronologically, thematically, methodologically?

## Lecture 3: Primary Source Analysis — Working with the Evidence

The primary sources — the Eddas, the sagas, the skaldic poetry, the runic inscriptions, the archaeological reports — are the raw material of the research project. This lecture reviews the principles of primary source analysis: the evaluation of the source (its date, its authorship, its bias), the extraction of the evidence, and the use of the evidence to answer the research question.

**The Principles of Source Criticism:**

- **Date.** When was the source written or created? How close is it to the events or the practices it describes?
- **Authorship.** Who wrote or created the source? What was their perspective, their bias, their agenda?
- **Genre.** What kind of source is it — a poem, a saga, a law code, an archaeological report? How does the genre affect the interpretation?
- **Context.** What was the historical, social, and cultural context in which the source was created? How does the context affect the content?

**The Use of the Primary Sources:**

The primary sources are not "facts" that can be extracted and compiled; they are complex documents that must be interpreted. The interpretation involves:

1. **The literal reading.** What does the source actually say (or, for archaeological sources, what does it contain)?
2. **The contextual reading.** What does the source mean in its context — what did it mean to the people who created and consumed it?
3. **The critical reading.** What does the source conceal, distort, or omit? What are the biases, the agendas, the silences?

**The Documentation of the Sources:**

All claims must be documented — the source for each claim must be cited in the text (using the University of Yggdrasil's citation format: Author-Year-Page). The bibliography must include all sources cited, and the citation must allow the reader to locate the source and to evaluate the evidence for themselves.

### Required Reading
- The University of Yggdrasil's *Guide to Source Citation* (course packet)
- Course textbook, ch. 7-9

**The Archive of Norse Pagan Studies:**

The University of Yggdrasil maintains the Archive of Norse Pagan Studies — a physical and digital repository of primary-source materials, including facsimiles of the major manuscripts (the Codex Regius of the Poetic Edda, the Flateyjarbók, the Möðruvallabók), archaeological reports from the major excavations (Uppåkra, Tissø, Gamla Uppsala, Hofstaðir), and the collected field notes of the Sacred Landscape Project. The Archive is open to all registered students, and NP405 students are encouraged to consult the Archive's holdings when the published sources are insufficient.

**Ethical Considerations in Primary Source Use:**

The use of primary sources in Norse Pagan Studies raises ethical considerations that the student must address:
- **The treatment of the dead.** The archaeological evidence includes human remains — the staff-graves, the bog bodies, the cremation deposits. The student who uses this evidence must do so with respect — acknowledging the humanity of the dead, not treating them as objects or as data.
- **The sacred texts.** The Eddas and the sagas are literary documents for the scholar, but they are sacred texts for many Heathens. The student's analysis should be rigorous but should not dismiss or denigrate the religious significance that the texts hold for contemporary practitioners.
- **The living tradition.** The Norse Pagan tradition is not only a subject for historical study but a living religion practiced by thousands of people today. The student's research should be informed by an awareness of the contemporary Heathen community and a respect for its beliefs and practices.

### Discussion Questions
1. How do you evaluate the reliability of a primary source? What criteria should be applied?
2. How does the genre of the source (poem, saga, law code) affect its interpretation?
3. How should the student handle contradictory sources — two sources that report different versions of the same event or practice?

## Lecture 4: Argument Construction — Thesis, Evidence, and Logic

The research project is an argument — a claim, supported by evidence, organized by logic. This lecture reviews the principles of argument construction: the formulation of the thesis, the organization of the evidence, and the logic of the argument.

**The Thesis:**

The thesis is the central claim of the project — the answer to the research question. A good thesis is:

- **Specific.** The thesis should state precisely what the project will argue.
- **Arguable.** The thesis should be a claim that can be debated — not a statement of fact but an interpretation that requires evidence and argument to support it.
- **Original.** The thesis should contribute something new — a new interpretation, a new use of evidence, a new perspective.

**The Organization of the Evidence:**

The evidence should be organized to support the thesis:

- **The strongest evidence first.** The most convincing evidence should be presented first, to establish the argument.
- **The counter-evidence acknowledged.** The evidence that contradicts the thesis should be acknowledged and addressed — the argument is stronger if it shows that the counter-evidence has been considered and found wanting.
- **The logical structure.** The argument should have a clear logical structure — each paragraph should advance the argument, each section should address a specific aspect of the thesis, and the conclusion should summarize the argument and its implications.

**The Logic of the Argument:**

The argument should be logical — the evidence should actually support the thesis, and the reasoning should be transparent. Common logical fallacies to avoid:

- **The hasty generalization.** Drawing a general conclusion from insufficient evidence.
- **The false cause.** Assuming that because A preceded B, A caused B.
- **The appeal to authority.** Citing an authority as evidence for a claim that the authority did not actually support.
- **The straw man.** Misrepresenting an opposing argument to make it easier to refute.

### Required Reading
- Booth, W.C. et al., *The Craft of Research*, ch. 7-11
- Course textbook, ch. 10-12

### Discussion Questions
1. How do you formulate a thesis that is arguable, specific, and original?
2. How should the counter-evidence be handled? Should it be refuted, acknowledged, or incorporated?
3. What are the most common logical fallacies in research papers? How can they be avoided?

## Lecture 5: Writing the Research Paper — Structure, Style, and Revision

The research paper is the most common format for the NP405 project. This lecture reviews the principles of academic writing: the structure of the paper, the style of the prose, and the process of revision.

**The Structure of the Research Paper:**

The standard structure is:

1. **Introduction.** The research question, the thesis, the significance, and the roadmap (the structure of the paper).
2. **Literature review.** The existing scholarship, the gap, and the contribution.
3. **Evidence and analysis.** The primary sources, the interpretation, and the argument.
4. **Conclusion.** The summary of the argument, the implications, and the limitations.

**The Style of Academic Prose:**

Academic prose should be:

- **Clear.** The meaning should be immediately apparent — no jargon, no obscurity, no unnecessary complexity.
- **Precise.** The words should mean exactly what they say — no vagueness, no ambiguity, no hand-waving.
- **Concise.** The prose should be as short as it can be while still being clear and precise — no padding, no filler, no repetition.
- **Engaging.** The prose should be interesting to read — the writer should care about the topic, and the reader should be able to tell.

**The Process of Revision:**

Revision is not proofreading — it is the re-thinking of the argument, the re-organization of the evidence, the re-writing of the prose. The revision process should include:

1. **Structural revision.** Does the paper have a clear structure? Does each section support the thesis?
2. **Evidential revision.** Is the evidence sufficient? Is it correctly interpreted?
3. **Stylistic revision.** Is the prose clear, precise, concise, and engaging?
4. **Proofreading.** Are there errors of spelling, grammar, or citation?

### Required Reading
- Williams, J.M., *Style: Lessons in Clarity and Grace* (2037 edition), ch. 1-4
- Course textbook, ch. 13-15

### Discussion Questions
1. How do you write an introduction that engages the reader and establishes the argument?
2. What are the most common problems in academic prose? How can they be fixed?
3. How do you revise effectively? What are the stages of revision?

## Lecture 6: Alternative Formats — Digital, Performative, Creative

The NP405 project need not be a traditional research paper. This lecture surveys the alternative formats: the digital reconstruction, the ritual analysis (the documentation and interpretation of a ritual performance), the fieldwork report, and the creative project (a poem, a story, a musical composition based on the Norse tradition).

**The Digital Reconstruction:**

A digital reconstruction of a Norse sacred site (building on NP403) may be submitted as the NP405 project. The reconstruction must be accompanied by a written report (15-20 pages) documenting the evidence base, the reconstruction decisions, and the interpretive conclusions. The digital reconstruction must comply with the London Charter principles (discussed in NP403).

**The Ritual Analysis:**

A student who has participated in or observed a modern Heathen ritual (with the approval of the advisor and the ritual's organizers) may submit a ritual analysis — a detailed description and interpretation of the ritual, using the analytical frameworks from NP201 (Ritual Practice) and from the broader ritual studies literature.

**The Fieldwork Report:**

A student who has visited a Norse sacred site (in Scandinavia, Iceland, or the Norse colonies) may submit a fieldwork report — a description and analysis of the site, including the archaeological features, the landscape context, and the modern experience of the site.

**The Creative Project:**

A student who wishes to engage with the Norse tradition creatively may submit a poem, a story, a musical composition, or another creative work based on the Norse mythological corpus. The creative project must be accompanied by a written commentary (15-20 pages) that explains the creative decisions, the engagement with the sources, and the interpretive perspective.

## Lecture 7: The Advisor Meeting — Feedback and Revision

The faculty advisor is the most important resource for the NP405 project. This lecture prepares the student for the advisor meeting: the presentation of the work-in-progress, the reception of the feedback, and the revision of the project.

**The Advisor's Role:**

The advisor is not a co-author but a guide — their role is to:

- **Provide feedback.** The advisor reads the draft and provides comments on the argument, the evidence, the structure, and the style.
- **Identify problems.** The advisor identifies the problems that the student may not see — the gaps in the argument, the weaknesses in the evidence, the errors in the logic.
- **Suggest solutions.** The advisor suggests solutions to the problems — additional sources, alternative interpretations, better organization.
- **Maintain standards.** The advisor ensures that the project meets the standards of the Norse Pagan Studies program — the mastery of the sources, the engagement with the scholarship, the quality of the argument.

**The Student's Responsibility:**

The student is responsible for:

- **Preparing the draft.** The draft should be as complete as possible — the advisor cannot provide useful feedback on a fragmentary or incomplete document.
- **Receiving the feedback.** The feedback is not a personal criticism but a professional assessment — the student should receive it with openness and without defensiveness.
- **Revising the project.** The feedback is not optional — the student must incorporate the advisor's comments (or must explain, in writing, why a specific comment was not followed).

### Required Reading
- The advisor meeting guidelines (course packet)
- Course textbook, ch. 19-21

### Discussion Questions
1. How should the student prepare for the advisor meeting? What should be brought to the meeting?
2. How should the student receive critical feedback? What is the difference between "criticism" and "feedback"?
3. What should the student do if they disagree with the advisor's feedback? How should the disagreement be handled?

## Lecture 8: The Oral Presentation — Communicating the Research

The NP405 project is presented orally to the faculty and the student's peers. This lecture prepares the student for the oral presentation: the structure of the presentation, the use of visual aids, and the handling of questions.

**The Structure of the Oral Presentation:**

The 20-minute presentation should include:

1. **The research question.** What is the question, and why does it matter?
2. **The literature review.** What has been done, and what is the gap?
3. **The evidence and the argument.** What sources were used, and what was the argument?
4. **The conclusions.** What is the answer to the research question, and what are the implications?

**The Visual Aids:**

Visual aids (slides, images, diagrams) should support the presentation, not dominate it:

- **Slides should be simple.** Each slide should convey one idea, in a clear, uncluttered format.
- **Images should be relevant.** The images should illustrate the argument, not merely decorate the presentation.
- **Text should be minimal.** The slides should not contain long passages of text; the text should be summarized, and the speaker should elaborate.

**The Questions:**

The presentation is followed by a question period (10 minutes). The student should:

- **Listen carefully.** Make sure you understand the question before answering.
- **Answer directly.** Answer the question that was asked, not the question you wish had been asked.
- **Acknowledge uncertainty.** If you do not know the answer, say so — it is better to acknowledge uncertainty than to bluff.

### Required Reading
- The oral presentation guidelines (course packet)
- Course textbook, ch. 22-24

**Designing Effective Slides for Norse Pagan Studies:**

Presentations in Norse Pagan Studies often benefit from specific visual strategies:
- **Use the archaeological evidence visually.** Photographs of excavations, 3D renderings of sacred sites, and maps of the sacred landscape communicate the spatial and material dimensions of the research more effectively than text descriptions.
- **Use the primary sources directly.** Rather than paraphrasing the Eddas or the sagas, display the key passage on the slide (in translation, with the original Old Norse if relevant) and read it aloud. The audience can see and hear the source simultaneously.
- **Use comparative visuals.** When comparing the Norse tradition with the Sami, Celtic, or Indo-European traditions, side-by-side images (the Norse staff and the Sami drum, the Norse hof and the Celtic nemeton) communicate the comparison instantly.
- **Avoid the "wall of text."** The slide is not a teleprompter — it should support the presentation, not replace it. If the slide contains full paragraphs, the audience will read the slide instead of listening to the speaker.

**Handling the Q&A:**

The question period is the most important part of the presentation — it is where the student demonstrates their mastery of the material. Strategies for handling questions:
- **Anticipate the questions.** Think in advance about the most likely questions and prepare answers.
- **Pause before answering.** Take a moment to think — it is better to pause and give a good answer than to rush and give a bad one.
- **Connect the question to the argument.** Show how the question relates to the thesis — the answer should advance the argument, not merely respond to the question.
- **Acknowledge the limits.** If the question touches on something outside the scope of the project, say so — "That's an important question, but it's beyond the scope of this project. Here's what I can say..."

### Discussion Questions
1. How do you design a presentation that communicates the argument effectively in 20 minutes?
2. What are the most common mistakes in oral presentations? How can they be avoided?
3. How do you handle a hostile question — a question that challenges the fundamental premise of your project?

## Lecture 9: Peer Review Workshop — Giving and Receiving Feedback

The NP405 cohort meets for a peer review workshop, in which each student's project is reviewed by two peers. This lecture prepares the students for the workshop: the principles of constructive feedback, the assessment criteria, and the revision process.

**The Principles of Constructive Feedback:**

- **Be specific.** "This is unclear" is not helpful; "The argument in paragraph 3 does not follow from the evidence in paragraph 2 because..." is helpful.
- **Be balanced.** Identify the strengths as well as the weaknesses — the feedback should not be entirely negative.
- **Be constructive.** The feedback should suggest solutions, not merely identify problems.
- **Be respectful.** The feedback should be about the project, not about the person.

**The Assessment Criteria:**

- **The research question.** Is the question clear, specific, and significant?
- **The use of evidence.** Is the evidence sufficient, relevant, and correctly interpreted?
- **The argument.** Is the argument logical, well-supported, and original?
- **The writing.** Is the prose clear, precise, concise, and engaging?
- **The presentation.** Is the oral presentation well-structured, well-delivered, and well-supported?

### Required Reading
- The peer review guidelines (course packet)
- Course textbook, ch. 25-27

### Discussion Questions
1. How do you give feedback that is both honest and constructive?
2. How do you receive feedback that is critical without becoming defensive?
3. How do you distinguish between a serious problem (the argument is flawed) and a minor problem (the citation format is incorrect)?

## Lecture 10: Revision and Final Submission — Polishing the Project

The final stage of the NP405 project is the revision and the submission of the final version. This lecture reviews the revision process and the submission requirements.

**The Revision Process:**

The revision is not a single pass but a series of passes:

1. **The structural revision.** Does the argument hold together? Is the structure clear?
2. **The evidential revision.** Is the evidence sufficient? Are the sources correctly cited?
3. **The stylistic revision.** Is the prose clear, precise, concise, and engaging?
4. **The proofreading.** Are there errors of spelling, grammar, or citation?

**The Submission Requirements:**

The final project must include:

- **The main text** (25 pages for a research paper; 15-20 pages for the written component of an alternative format).
- **The bibliography** (all sources cited, in the University of Yggdrasil citation format).
- **The appendices** (any additional material — tables, figures, images, the digital model, the ritual documentation).

## Lecture 12: The Future of the Research — Where Do You Go From Here?

The NP405 project is the end of the Norse Pagan Studies major, but it is also the beginning — the beginning of a scholarly career, a ritual practice, a creative engagement with the Norse tradition. This final lecture surveys the possibilities: graduate study (the University of Yggdrasil's M.A. and Ph.D. programs in Norse Pagan Studies), publication (the *Yggdrasil Journal of Norse Pagan Studies*, the *Edda* journal), ritual practice (the Ásatrú and Heathen communities), and personal engagement (the life-long relationship with the Norse gods and the Norse tradition).

### Required Reading
- The University of Yggdrasil's Graduate Studies Handbook (online)
- Course textbook, ch. 31-36

**Graduate Study Pathways:**

The University of Yggdrasil offers several graduate programs for NP405 graduates:
- **M.A. in Norse Pagan Studies** — a one-year program (six courses plus a thesis) that deepens the student's knowledge of the Norse tradition and provides the research skills for doctoral work.
- **Ph.D. in Norse Pagan Studies** — a four-year program (coursework, comprehensive examinations, and a dissertation) that prepares the student for an academic career in the field.
- **Joint M.A. in Norse Pagan Studies and Digital Heritage** — a two-year program that combines the study of the Norse tradition with the digital heritage skills developed in NP403.
- **Joint M.A. in Norse Pagan Studies and Environmental Studies** — a two-year program that combines the study of the Norse ecological tradition (NP307) with the University's Environmental Science program.

**Publication Opportunities:**

The University of Yggdrasil's *Yggdrasil Journal of Norse Pagan Studies* is the premier peer-reviewed journal in the field, publishing original research on all aspects of the pre-Christian Norse tradition. The journal accepts submissions from graduate students and from exceptional undergraduate researchers. NP405 projects of high quality are encouraged to submit to the journal after revision and expansion.

**Ritual Practice and Community Engagement:**

Graduates of the Norse Pagan Studies program are not required to be practitioners of Ásatrú or Heathenry, but many are, and the program prepares them for leadership roles in the Heathen community — as goðar (ritual specialists), as educators (teaching courses and workshops in the community), and as advocates (representing the Heathen community in interfaith dialogues and in public forums).

The program also prepares graduates for careers in cultural heritage management (working with museums, archaeological sites, and heritage organizations), in digital media (creating content about Norse Paganism for online platforms), and in the creative arts (writing, music, visual art inspired by the Norse tradition).

**The Life-Long Relationship:**

The Norse Pagan Studies major is an education, but it is also a formation — the student who completes the program should be not only knowledgeable about the Norse tradition but transformed by it. The gods, the myths, the rituals, and the sacred landscape are not merely objects of study but realities to be engaged — for a lifetime, in whatever form that engagement takes.
