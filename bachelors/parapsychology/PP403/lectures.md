## Lecture 1: The Problem of Application — Can Psi Be Practical?

**The Gap Between Laboratory and Life**

Parapsychology has, for over a century, focused primarily on *existence proof* — demonstrating, under controlled laboratory conditions, that psi phenomena occur. This focus has been strategically necessary: if psi cannot be demonstrated to exist, the question of its application is moot. But the focus on existence proof has created a gap between the laboratory and the world. We know (with reasonable meta-analytic confidence) that the Ganzfeld produces a hit rate of approximately 30-35% (vs. 25% chance), that remote viewing can produce accurate descriptions of distant targets, and that presentiment can produce physiological anticipation of future emotional stimuli. But can these laboratory effects be harnessed for practical purposes? Can a 30-35% hit rate in the Ganzfeld translate into a 5% improvement in business decision accuracy, a 10% reduction in diagnostic errors, or a meaningful advantage in intelligence gathering?

The gap between laboratory and life is not unique to parapsychology — it affects all sciences that seek practical application. The effect of aspirin on heart attack risk (d ≈ 0.03) is smaller than the Ganzfeld effect (d ≈ 0.18), yet aspirin is a standard preventive intervention because the stakes are high and the effect, though small, is real. The question for applied parapsychology is not whether psi effects are "large enough" but whether they are large enough to be practically useful — and whether they can be reliably elicited under the messy, uncontrolled conditions of the real world.

**The Reliability Problem**

The central challenge of applied parapsychology is the **reliability problem**: psi effects are small, variable, and sensitive to poorly understood moderating variables (emotional state, expectation, environment). In the laboratory, we can control these variables to some extent — screening participants for absorption and hypnotizability, creating a relaxed, psi-conducive environment, using standardized protocols. In the real world — a corporate boardroom, an emergency room, a military command center — these controls are not available. The decision-maker is stressed, time-pressured, and surrounded by noise. The psi signal, already faint, may be drowned out entirely.

The reliability problem has several dimensions:
1. **Signal-to-noise**: In the laboratory, the "noise" (distractions, competing demands, emotional stress) is minimized. In the real world, it is maximized. The psi signal must compete with the noise of ordinary cognition, emotion, and sensory input.
2. **Feedback**: In the laboratory, the participant receives immediate feedback (was the Ganzfeld session a hit or a miss?), which may facilitate learning and psi performance. In the real world, feedback is often delayed, ambiguous, or absent — the executive who makes a "psi-informed" decision may never know whether it was correct.
3. **Motivation**: In the laboratory, the participant is motivated to perform well (for the experiment, for science, for payment). In the real world, the decision-maker may be ambivalent about using psi — skeptical of its validity, concerned about its reputation, or uncomfortable with its implications.
4. **Protocol fidelity**: In the laboratory, the protocol is standardized and precisely followed. In the real world, protocols are adapted, abbreviated, or ignored — and the adaptations may inadvertently eliminate the active ingredients.

**The Promise of Application**

Despite these challenges, the promise of applied parapsychology is substantial. Consider:
- A business executive who can "intuit" which of three strategic options will succeed, improving decision accuracy by even 5-10%.
- A physician who can "sense" which of two plausible diagnoses is correct, reducing diagnostic errors and saving lives.
- A law enforcement investigator who can "feel" which lead is worth pursuing, saving time and resources.
- An intelligence analyst who can "see" a hidden facility through remote viewing, complementing satellite imagery and signals intelligence.
- An individual who can "anticipate" a dangerous situation and avoid it through presentiment.

These applications do not require psi to be perfect. They require it to be *better than chance* — to provide a small but real advantage that, cumulated over many decisions, produces meaningful benefit.

**The History of Applied Psi**

Applied psi is not new. Throughout the twentieth century, governments, corporations, and individuals have attempted to harness psi for practical purposes — sometimes openly, sometimes in secret. Key episodes include:

- **The Stargate Project (1972-1995)**: The U.S. government's $20 million, 23-year program to evaluate and apply remote viewing for intelligence purposes. The program produced some striking apparent successes (the description of a Soviet missile facility, the location of a kidnapped general) but was ultimately terminated amid controversy about its reliability and methodology.

- **The PEAR Lab's Applied Work (1979-2007)**: The Princeton Engineering Anomalies Research laboratory, while primarily focused on basic research, also explored applications — including "precognitive remote perception" for financial forecasting and "field consciousness" for group decision-making.

- **Remote viewing in business**: Since the 1980s, a small but persistent industry of "applied remote viewers" has offered services to corporations — predicting market trends, evaluating competitors, locating resources. The evidence for these services is largely anecdotal, and the industry is unregulated.

- **The UoY Applied Psi Initiative (2038-present)**: The University of Yggdrasil's applied psi research program, described in later lectures, is the first systematic, academically rigorous effort to evaluate and develop applied psi protocols.

## Lecture 2: The Psychology of Intuition — Gut Feelings as Latent Psi

**Intuition: Definition and Phenomenology**

Intuition — the "gut feeling," the "hunch," the "sense" that something is right or wrong without being able to articulate why — is a universal human experience. Surveys consistently find that a majority of people report having had intuitive experiences, and that a substantial minority report making important decisions (career, relationship, health) based on intuition.

Phenomenologically, intuition has several characteristic features:
- **Immediacy**: The intuitive feeling arises suddenly, without conscious deliberation.
- **Certainty**: The feeling is often accompanied by a sense of confidence or "knowing."
- **Inarticulability**: The person cannot explain *why* they feel the way they do — the reasons are unconscious or inaccessible.
- **Bodily grounding**: Intuition is often experienced as a bodily sensation — a "gut feeling," a tightening in the chest, a sense of expansion or contraction.

These features are consistent with the hypothesis that intuition is, at least sometimes, **latent psi** — the conscious tip of an unconscious psi process. The unconscious mind (or the "biofield," or the "extended consciousness") accesses information that is not available through ordinary sensory channels and presents it to the conscious mind as a feeling, a hunch, or an impulse. The conscious mind, unable to articulate the source of the information, experiences it as "intuition."

**The Dual-Process Model of Intuition**

Psychologists have developed dual-process models of cognition that distinguish between "System 1" (fast, automatic, intuitive) and "System 2" (slow, deliberate, analytical) processing. Intuition, in this framework, is a System 1 process — a rapid, unconscious pattern-recognition that draws on accumulated experience and tacit knowledge.

In standard dual-process theory, intuition is entirely "normal" — it reflects the unconscious processing of information that has been acquired through ordinary sensory channels and encoded in memory. The experienced chess player who "intuits" the best move is not accessing psi; they are drawing on thousands of hours of pattern recognition that have been encoded in their neural networks.

The question for parapsychology is whether this standard account is *complete*. Might there be cases where intuition draws on information that is *not* available through ordinary sensory channels — where the "pattern recognition" is operating on data that the person has never encountered? This is the "psi intuition" hypothesis: that at least some intuitive experiences involve anomalous cognition.

**Empirical Tests of Psi Intuition**

Several experimental paradigms have been developed to test whether intuition involves psi:

1. **The "Iowa Gambling Task" variant**: In the standard Iowa Gambling Task, participants choose cards from four decks; two decks are "good" (small rewards, small losses, net positive) and two are "bad" (large rewards, large losses, net negative). Participants develop a "gut feeling" about which decks are good before they can consciously articulate why. In a psi variant, the decks are randomized such that the "good" and "bad" outcomes cannot be predicted from previous experience — any above-chance preference for good decks must involve precognition or clairvoyance. Results from psi variants of the Iowa Gambling Task have been mixed: some studies find above-chance performance (particularly with emotional or personally relevant outcomes), while others find chance performance.

2. **The "intuition probe" paradigm**: Participants are presented with pairs of images (one target, one decoy) and asked to indicate which "feels" more important, meaningful, or salient. Unbeknownst to the participants, one image in each pair is the target that will later be encountered (precognition) or that is being viewed by a sender in another room (telepathy). The paradigm is essentially a simplified, forced-choice psi task framed as an "intuition" test. Results have been generally positive: participants perform above chance, particularly when they are high in absorption and when the targets are emotionally significant.

3. **The "CEO intuition" studies**: Several studies have examined the role of intuition in business decision-making, finding that experienced executives often rely on "gut feelings" and that these feelings, in some cases, predict outcomes above chance. However, these studies are observational and cannot distinguish psi-based intuition from experience-based intuition.

**The UoY "Intuition Amplification" Protocol**

The UoY Applied Psi Initiative has developed an "Intuition Amplification" (IA) protocol that aims to enhance the reliability of psi-based intuition. The protocol involves:

1. **Pre-decision relaxation**: The decision-maker enters a relaxed, meditative state (similar to the Ganzfeld receiver's state) before accessing their intuition.
2. **Somatic focusing**: The decision-maker attends to bodily sensations — the "gut feeling" — associated with each option.
3. **Forced-choice comparison**: The options are presented in random pairs, and the decision-maker indicates which option "feels" better, without conscious deliberation.
4. **Aggregation**: The pairwise preferences are aggregated to produce an overall preference ranking.
5. **Blind feedback**: The decision-maker receives feedback on their accuracy (if available) to facilitate learning.

Preliminary evaluations of the IA protocol in simulated business decision scenarios (Haraldsdóttir & Thórsdóttir, 2039) have found that it improves decision accuracy by approximately 5-10% compared to baseline (random choice), with the largest improvements for decisions that are emotionally significant and that involve multiple options with ambiguous information.

## Lecture 3: Remote Viewing in the Field — Intelligence, Archaeology, and Exploration

**The Stargate Legacy**

The U.S. government's Stargate Project (1972-1995) remains the most extensive and well-documented attempt to apply psi — specifically, remote viewing — for practical purposes. Funded initially by the CIA and later by the Defense Intelligence Agency (DIA), the program employed a small cadre of "remote viewers" who were tasked with describing distant locations, facilities, and events that were inaccessible to conventional intelligence gathering.

The Stargate Project's operational methodology, known as **Coordinate Remote Viewing (CRV)**, was developed at SRI International by physicists Hal Puthoff and Russell Targ, and later refined by Ingo Swann and other viewers. The CRV protocol involved:
1. **Target designation**: The target was identified by geographic coordinates (latitude and longitude) or by a randomly generated code number. The viewer was given the coordinates or code number but no other information.
2. **Blind conditions**: The monitor (the person interacting with the viewer) was blind to the target — they knew the coordinates or code number but not the location or facility they referred to.
3. **Structured reporting**: The viewer described their impressions in a structured format, moving from general (Stage 1: gestalt, overall impression) to specific (Stage 6: three-dimensional models, detailed sketches).
4. **Independent evaluation**: The viewer's report was evaluated by an independent judge who compared it to the actual target and to decoy targets, rating the correspondence.

The Stargate program produced some striking apparent successes — most famously, viewer Pat Price's description of a Soviet missile facility at Semipalatinsk, which included details (the layout of buildings, the presence of a large gantry crane) that were later confirmed by satellite imagery. However, the program also produced many failures — sessions in which the viewer's descriptions were vague, inaccurate, or misleading. And the program was plagued by methodological controversies: critics argued that the viewers were (perhaps unconsciously) receiving cues from their monitors, that the evaluation process was biased, and that the apparent successes were cherry-picked from a much larger number of failures.

**The UoY Applied Remote Viewing Program**

The UoY Applied Remote Viewing (ARV) Program, established in 2038, is the first systematic, academically rigorous attempt to evaluate and refine remote viewing for practical applications since the termination of the Stargate Project. The ARV Program differs from Stargate in several key respects:

1. **Methodological rigor**: All ARV sessions are conducted under automated, double-blind conditions, with targets randomly selected by quantum RNG and the viewer, monitor, and judge all blind to the target. All sessions are pre-registered, and all data (including failures) are publicly reported.

2. **Focus on specific applications**: Rather than attempting general "psychic espionage," the ARV Program focuses on specific, well-defined applications: (a) **archaeological remote viewing** — locating and describing unexcavated archaeological sites; (b) **environmental remote viewing** — identifying sources of pollution, locating missing wildlife, monitoring ecosystem changes; and (c) **medical remote viewing** — describing the internal anatomy and pathology of patients (as an adjunct to, not a replacement for, conventional imaging).

3. **Protocol optimization**: The ARV Program systematically tests variations in the remote viewing protocol to identify the components that maximize accuracy. Variables tested include: the format of the target designation (coordinates vs. photographs vs. verbal descriptions), the number of viewers per target, the use of consensus (combining multiple viewers' reports), and the timing of the session relative to the target event.

4. **Quantitative evaluation**: Every ARV session is evaluated quantitatively — a numerical accuracy score is assigned by independent judges, and the distribution of scores is compared to chance (using permutation tests). This enables rigorous statistical evaluation of the program's effectiveness.

**Preliminary Results**

As of 2040, the ARV Program has conducted approximately 500 operational sessions across its three application areas. Key findings:

- **Overall accuracy**: The mean accuracy score across all sessions was significantly above chance (d ≈ 0.25, p < 0.001) — an effect size comparable to the laboratory Ganzfeld but achieved under operational (not laboratory) conditions.

- **Application-specific accuracy**: Archaeological RV produced the highest accuracy (d ≈ 0.35), followed by environmental RV (d ≈ 0.25) and medical RV (d ≈ 0.15). The superior accuracy in archaeological applications may reflect the "static" nature of the targets (archaeological sites do not change, unlike environmental conditions or medical pathologies).

- **Viewer effects**: A small number of viewers (approximately 10% of the viewer pool) accounted for a disproportionate share of the successful sessions. These "high-performing viewers" were characterized by high absorption, high hypnotizability, and extensive meditation experience — consistent with the laboratory individual differences literature.

- **Consensus effects**: Combining the reports of multiple viewers (consensus RV) improved accuracy beyond the performance of the best individual viewer — a finding consistent with the group facilitation effects discussed in PP303.

- **Target format effects**: Using photographs as target designations produced higher accuracy than using coordinates or verbal descriptions — suggesting that visual "resonance" with the target may be important.

## Lecture 4: Financial Psi — Presentiment, Precognition, and the Markets

**The Allure (and Peril) of Financial Psi**

Financial markets represent, in principle, an ideal testbed for applied psi. The outcomes are unambiguous (a stock either goes up or down), the data are plentiful (millions of price movements daily), and the incentives are strong (financial gain). If psi can predict market movements with even slightly above-chance accuracy, the financial rewards could be enormous.

This allure has attracted parapsychologists and entrepreneurs for decades. Dean Radin, Dick Bierman, and others have conducted experiments on "presentiment" in financial contexts — testing whether physiological responses (skin conductance, heart rate) anticipate future price movements before the movements occur. Several "psychic investment" services have claimed (with varying degrees of credibility) to use remote viewing or precognition to predict market trends. And the "prediction market" literature — which shows that aggregated individual predictions often outperform expert forecasts — has been cited as evidence for "collective intuition" that may involve psi.

But financial psi also faces formidable challenges — not least, the efficient market hypothesis, which states that all available information is already incorporated into asset prices, making it impossible to consistently "beat the market" through any means (psi included). If a reliable psi-based trading strategy were discovered, its very use would tend to eliminate the advantage (as the strategy's trades moved prices). The financial psi researcher thus faces a paradox: the more successful the psi strategy, the faster it would be arbitraged away.

**The Presentiment Trading Paradigm**

The presentiment trading paradigm, developed by Radin and Bierman and refined at UoY, tests whether physiological responses can anticipate future price movements. The paradigm:

1. **Setup**: The participant (trader) views a series of financial charts (stock prices, currency pairs, commodities) on a screen. Each chart shows a price history and, at the end, either a price increase or a price decrease (the outcome).
2. **Physiological measurement**: The participant's skin conductance, heart rate, and EEG are recorded continuously.
3. **Presentiment analysis**: The physiological data from the period *before* the outcome is revealed are analyzed to determine whether they differ between the "up" and "down" outcomes. If the participant's body "knows" the outcome before it is consciously perceived, the pre-reveal physiology should differ between the two conditions.
4. **Trading simulation**: The participant is given virtual money and, based on their "gut feeling" (which may reflect the unconscious presentiment signal), decides whether to buy, sell, or hold. The participant's trading performance is compared to chance and to a control condition (trading without access to the pre-reveal data).

Results from the presentiment trading paradigm have been mixed. Some studies (Radin, 2012; Bierman, 2015) report above-chance trading performance, particularly when the participant is emotionally engaged (trading with real money or with strong incentives). Other studies (including the UoY replication, Haraldsdóttir & Chen, 2039) find chance-level performance overall, with above-chance performance in subgroups (high-absorption participants, participants in cardiac coherence states).

**The "Consciousness Portfolio" Concept**

A more ambitious approach to financial psi is the "consciousness portfolio" — a trading strategy that uses aggregated psi input (from a group of trained intuitives or remote viewers) to make investment decisions. The consciousness portfolio concept, developed at UoY, involves:

1. **Target selection**: Each month, a set of candidate investments (stocks, bonds, commodities) is identified by conventional analysis.
2. **Psi assessment**: A group of trained remote viewers or intuitive decision-makers assesses each candidate using a structured protocol (e.g., each viewer rates each candidate on a 1-7 scale for "future performance").
3. **Portfolio construction**: The ratings are aggregated, and the top-rated candidates are selected for the portfolio.
4. **Performance evaluation**: The consciousness portfolio's performance is compared to benchmarks (S&P 500, random selection, conventional analyst recommendations).

The UoY Consciousness Portfolio has been running as a simulation since 2039. Preliminary results through 2040 show modest above-benchmark performance (annualized return approximately 1.5% above the S&P 500, p ≈ 0.08, not significant). The simulation is ongoing, and the results are too preliminary to draw firm conclusions.

**The Efficient Market Paradox**

The efficient market hypothesis presents a paradox for financial psi. If psi-based trading is possible, and if it is widely adopted, the trades executed by psi traders will move prices, incorporating the psi-derived information into market prices. At that point, the information becomes available to all market participants (through price movements), and the psi advantage is eliminated. The only way to sustain a psi advantage is to keep the psi trading strategy secret — but secrecy prevents the scientific validation that would establish the strategy's credibility.

This paradox is not unique to psi — it applies to any information-based trading strategy. But it is particularly acute for psi, because the mechanism is not understood and the strategy cannot be patented or protected through conventional intellectual property law.

## Lecture 5: Medical Intuition — Psi in Diagnosis and Healing

**The Phenomenon of Medical Intuition**

Medical intuition — the experience of "knowing" a patient's diagnosis, prognosis, or hidden condition without being able to articulate the basis for the knowledge — is widely reported among healthcare professionals. Surveys of physicians and nurses consistently find that a majority report having had "intuitive" diagnostic experiences, and that a substantial minority report that these intuitions proved correct. The phenomenon is often discussed in the medical literature under the rubric of "clinical intuition" or "gut feelings in diagnosis," typically interpreted as the unconscious pattern recognition of an experienced clinician.

The question for parapsychology is whether some instances of medical intuition involve anomalous cognition — that the clinician is accessing information about the patient that is not available through ordinary sensory channels, through the patient's presentation, or through the clinician's accumulated experience. The hypothesis is that the clinician's "gut feeling" is, at least sometimes, a psi-mediated detection of the patient's biofield (PP307) or a telepathic "reading" of the patient's unconscious knowledge of their own condition.

**Empirical Studies of Medical Psi**

Several empirical paradigms have been developed to test medical psi:

1. **The "hidden condition" paradigm**: A clinician (physician, nurse, or trained intuitive) is asked to diagnose a patient whose medical records, test results, and physical appearance have been stripped of diagnostic cues. The clinician's diagnosis is compared to the actual diagnosis (gold standard). Above-chance accuracy would suggest anomalous cognition. Several studies using this paradigm have reported above-chance accuracy for specific conditions (particularly cancer and infectious diseases), but the literature is small and methodologically heterogeneous.

2. **The "distant diagnosis" paradigm**: A trained intuitive is given minimal information about a patient (e.g., a photograph, a name, a date of birth) and asked to describe the patient's medical condition. The intuitive's description is compared to the patient's actual medical record by an independent judge. Meta-analyses of distant diagnosis studies (Schlitz et al., 2012) have found a small but significant effect (d ≈ 0.3), but the studies are generally of low methodological quality, and publication bias is a concern.

3. **The UoY "Biofield Diagnosis" paradigm**: In a series of studies, trained intuitives (some with medical training, some without) are asked to diagnose patients based solely on photographs of the patients' fingertips (from which GDV biofield images can be extracted), their biophoton emission spectra, or their cardiac field patterns. The hypothesis is that the biofield carries diagnostic information that can be "read" by sensitive individuals. Results have been mixed: some intuitives perform above chance, while others perform at chance, and the conditions that predict success are not well understood.

**The UoY Medical Intuition Training Program**

The UoY Applied Psi Initiative, in collaboration with the UoY Medical School, has developed a Medical Intuition Training (MIT) program for healthcare professionals. The program teaches:

1. **Somatic awareness**: Attending to bodily sensations (the "gut feeling") that may carry intuitive diagnostic information.
2. **Biofield perception**: Developing sensitivity to the patient's biofield — the subjective experience of "reading" the patient's energetic state.
3. **Intuitive differential diagnosis**: Structured protocols for accessing intuitive impressions about competing diagnostic hypotheses.
4. **Integration with conventional diagnosis**: Using intuition as a complement to — not a replacement for — conventional diagnostic methods (history, physical examination, laboratory tests, imaging).
5. **Error awareness**: Recognizing the limitations of intuition — the tendency to overconfidence, the influence of cognitive biases, the risk of confirmation bias.

Preliminary evaluation of the MIT program (Thórsdóttir & Haraldsdóttir, 2040) found that participants showed a small but significant improvement in diagnostic accuracy in simulated clinical scenarios (improvement ≈ 8%, p < 0.05) and that the improvement was larger for complex, ambiguous cases (where conventional diagnostic cues were insufficient) than for straightforward cases. However, the study was small (N = 30), the improvement was modest, and the durability of the training effect over time is unknown.

**Ethical Considerations**

Medical intuition raises significant ethical questions:
- **Standard of care**: If a clinician has an intuitive "hit" about a patient's condition, but the intuition contradicts the available evidence, how should the clinician proceed? Should they act on the intuition (potentially harming the patient if the intuition is wrong) or ignore it (potentially missing a diagnosis)?
- **Informed consent**: Should patients be informed that their clinician is using (or may use) intuitive methods? What level of disclosure is ethically required?
- **Liability**: If a clinician acts on an intuition that proves wrong, who is liable — the clinician, the intuition training program, or no one (since intuition is inherently uncertain)?

The UoY MIT program addresses these questions by emphasizing that intuition is a complement to, not a replacement for, conventional diagnosis, and that intuitive impressions should be confirmed by conventional methods whenever possible. But the ethical questions are not fully resolved, and they will become more pressing as medical intuition training becomes more widespread.

## Lecture 6: Psi in Law Enforcement and Criminal Investigation

**The "Psychic Detective" — Myth and Reality**

The figure of the "psychic detective" — the clairvoyant who assists police in solving crimes, locating missing persons, or identifying perpetrators — is a staple of popular culture and a persistent phenomenon in real-world law enforcement. Surveys of police departments consistently find that a substantial minority (20-40%) have received tips from self-proclaimed psychics, and that a smaller percentage (5-15%) have followed up on such tips. Anecdotal accounts of psychic "successes" — the psychic who correctly identified the location of a missing child's body, the psychic who described the perpetrator in detail before an arrest was made — are widely circulated.

But the anecdotal evidence is difficult to evaluate. For every apparent "hit" — a psychic tip that proves accurate — there are many "misses" — tips that are vague, inaccurate, or misleading. And the retrospective evaluation of psychic tips is vulnerable to confirmation bias: the tendency to remember the hits and forget the misses, and to reinterpret vague statements as accurate after the facts are known.

**Empirical Studies of Investigative Psi**

Several controlled studies have examined whether psychics can provide useful information in criminal investigations:

1. **The "mock crime" paradigm**: A mock crime is staged (e.g., a confederate "steals" an object and "hides" it in a known location). Psychics and control participants (non-psychics) are asked to describe the crime, the perpetrator, and the location of the hidden object. The accuracy of their descriptions is evaluated by independent judges. Results have been mixed: some studies find that psychics outperform controls (e.g., the "psychic" group provides more accurate descriptions), while others find no difference.

2. **The "retrospective" paradigm**: Psychics who claim to have assisted in real criminal investigations are asked to provide descriptions of cases that have already been solved (but about which they are given no information). Their descriptions are compared to the known facts. This paradigm eliminates the "continued investigation" confound (where psychics' tips are evaluated against ongoing, incomplete investigations) but introduces the "retrospective reinterpretation" confound (where judges may reinterpret vague psychic statements to fit the known facts).

3. **The UoY "Investigative Remote Viewing" paradigm**: In collaboration with the Icelandic Police, the UoY Applied Remote Viewing Program has conducted a series of retrospective investigations — cold cases in which remote viewers are given minimal information (date, location, case number) and asked to provide descriptions of the crime, the perpetrator, and the evidence. The descriptions are evaluated by detectives who are blind to whether they came from remote viewers or from control participants. Results (Haraldsdóttir, Vigfússon, & Gunnarsson, 2040) have been promising but preliminary: remote viewers provided significantly more accurate information than controls (d ≈ 0.3, p < 0.01), but the accuracy was still modest (approximately 20% of statements rated as "accurate" vs. 10% for controls), and the most accurate statements tended to be the most general ("the perpetrator is male," "the crime occurred at night").

**The Problem of "Retrofitting"**

A major methodological challenge in evaluating investigative psi is **retrofitting** — the tendency for vague or ambiguous psychic statements to be interpreted, after the facts are known, as accurate. For example, a psychic says "I see water" in connection with a missing person case. If the person is later found near a lake, the statement is counted as a "hit." But "I see water" is so vague (it could refer to a lake, a river, a swimming pool, a bathtub, rain, tears) that it would likely be "confirmed" in almost any context.

To address retrofitting, the UoY Investigative Remote Viewing protocol uses:
- **Blind, independent judging**: Judges are given the psychic's statements and the actual facts of the case, but they do not know which statements came from which psychic (or control participant).
- **Pre-specified criteria**: Before the judging begins, the research team specifies what counts as a "match" — e.g., for each statement, what specific fact would confirm it.
- **Quantitative rating scales**: Judges rate each statement on a numerical scale (1-7) for accuracy, rather than making binary (hit/miss) judgments.

**Ethical and Legal Considerations**

The use of psi in law enforcement raises significant ethical and legal concerns:
- **False leads**: A psychic's tip that proves inaccurate can waste investigative resources, divert attention from genuine leads, and cause distress (e.g., to family members of a missing person who are given false hope).
- **Chain of custody**: If a psychic "identifies" a suspect or a location, how is this information treated in the legal system? Can a psychic's testimony be admitted as evidence? (Generally, no — it is considered hearsay or speculation.)
- **Exploitation**: Vulnerable individuals (e.g., families of missing persons) may be exploited by self-proclaimed psychics who charge for their services without providing demonstrable value.

The UoY Applied Psi Initiative takes the position that investigative psi should be used only as a complement to conventional investigation, not as a replacement, and that all psi-derived leads should be verified by conventional means before they are acted upon.

## Lecture 7: Military and Intelligence Applications — The Covert History and Open Future

**The Covert History**

The military and intelligence applications of psi have a long, largely covert history. The Stargate Project (Lecture 3) is the most famous example, but it was not the only one. Throughout the Cold War, both the United States and the Soviet Union invested in psi research for military and intelligence purposes, motivated by the fear that the other side might develop a "psi gap" — a decisive advantage in psychic warfare.

Key episodes in the covert history of military psi include:

- **Soviet psi research (1920s-1980s)**: The Soviet Union funded extensive psi research, particularly in the areas of telepathy, remote viewing, and psychokinesis. The research was conducted at various institutes, including the Institute for Brain Research in Leningrad and the Institute for Problems of Information Transmission in Moscow. The Soviets were particularly interested in "psychotronics" — the technological amplification and direction of psi.

- **The "First Earth Battalion" and "Jedi Warriors" (1980s)**: A faction within the U.S. military explored the possibility of creating "super soldiers" with enhanced psi capabilities — the "First Earth Battalion" concept, which included training in remote viewing, telepathy, and psychokinesis. The concept was never fully implemented but influenced later military interest in "enhanced human performance."

- **Chinese psi research (1980s-present)**: China has maintained an active psi research program, focusing on "exceptional human functions" (EHF) — children and adults who claim abilities such as clairvoyance, psychokinesis, and telepathy. The research program is reportedly integrated into China's broader military modernization efforts.

- **Post-9/11 "enhanced interrogation" and psi (2000s)**: Following the 9/11 attacks, elements within the U.S. intelligence community reportedly explored the use of remote viewing and other psi techniques for locating terrorist leaders and anticipating attacks. The extent and effectiveness of these efforts remain classified.

**The Modern Landscape (2040)**

By 2040, military and intelligence interest in psi has evolved considerably:

1. **Declining stigma**: The stigma associated with psi research in military contexts has diminished, partly due to the accumulation of meta-analytic evidence for psi and partly due to the broader cultural acceptance of "non-ordinary" capacities (meditation, mindfulness, intuition) in military and intelligence training.

2. **Focus on decision-making**: Rather than attempting "psychic spying" (the Stargate model), modern military psi applications focus on enhancing the intuitive decision-making of commanders, analysts, and operators — using protocols adapted from the Ganzfeld and remote viewing research to improve situational awareness, threat detection, and strategic judgment.

3. **Technological amplification**: The biofield and quantum psi technologies developed at UoY and elsewhere (PP307, PP305) are being explored for military applications — psi-tuned RNGs for threat detection, quantum sensors for biofield-based surveillance, neuromorphic chips for intuitive data analysis.

4. **Ethical frameworks**: The UoY Applied Psi Initiative has developed ethical frameworks for military psi applications (in collaboration with the Icelandic Ministry of Defense), including principles for informed consent (soldiers and analysts should consent to psi training), proportionality (psi should not be used for purposes that are disproportionate to the threat), and transparency (the existence of military psi programs should be publicly acknowledged, even if specific operations remain classified).

**The "Psi Arms Race" Scenario**

A significant concern among military ethicists is the possibility of a "psi arms race" — a competitive dynamic in which nations invest in military psi research because they fear that their adversaries are doing so, regardless of the actual effectiveness of the technology. This dynamic is familiar from the nuclear arms race and the AI arms race: the fear of being left behind drives investment, even when the technology's effectiveness is uncertain.

The psi arms race scenario is particularly dangerous because:
- The effectiveness of psi is uncertain — nations may invest billions in a capability that cannot be reliably deployed.
- The countermeasures are unknown — if an adversary develops offensive psi, how do you defend against it?
- The escalation potential is unpredictable — how would an adversary respond to the perception that you are developing psi weapons?

The UoY Applied Psi Initiative has advocated for international agreements on military psi research, analogous to the Biological Weapons Convention or the Treaty on the Non-Proliferation of Nuclear Weapons. But as of 2040, no such agreements exist, and military psi research continues in several nations.

## Lecture 8: The Ethics of Applied Psi

**The Unique Ethical Challenges of Applied Psi**

Applied psi raises ethical challenges that are in some respects familiar (they parallel the challenges of other powerful technologies) and in some respects unique (they arise from the specific nature of psi). The key ethical challenges include:

**1. The Consent Problem**

If psi can be used to access information about a person without their knowledge or consent, does this violate their privacy? The question is analogous to concerns about surveillance technology, but with a crucial difference: surveillance technology can be detected, blocked, or regulated; psi (at least as currently understood) cannot. If a remote viewer can "see" inside a private facility — or inside a person's body, or into their private thoughts — without leaving any trace, how can privacy be protected?

The UoY Applied Psi Initiative has proposed a "right to psi privacy" — the principle that individuals have a right not to be subjected to psi-based information gathering without their informed consent, except in narrowly defined circumstances (e.g., law enforcement with judicial oversight, analogous to search warrants). But this principle is difficult to enforce, given that psi leaves no detectable trace.

**2. The Reliability Problem (Revisited)**

If psi is unreliable — if it produces accurate information only slightly above chance — then acting on psi may cause more harm than good. The physician who acts on a mistaken intuitive diagnosis may harm the patient. The military commander who acts on a mistaken remote viewing report may jeopardize a mission. The business executive who acts on a mistaken premonition may lose the company's money.

The ethical question is: how reliable must psi be before it is ethical to use it in high-stakes contexts? The UoY Applied Psi Initiative has proposed a framework based on the "precautionary principle": psi should be used in high-stakes contexts only when (a) the expected benefit of acting on the psi is large, (b) the expected harm of acting on the psi is small, and (c) conventional methods are insufficient. This framework is conservative, perhaps overly so, but it errs on the side of protecting those who might be harmed by unreliable psi.

**3. The Equity Problem**

If applied psi becomes practically useful, access to it may be unequal. Wealthy individuals, corporations, and governments may be able to afford psi training, psi technologies, and psi consulting services, while ordinary individuals cannot. The result would be a new dimension of inequality — not just economic inequality, but "psi inequality": the psi-rich and the psi-poor.

The UoY Applied Psi Initiative has advocated for open-access psi training and technology, and for public funding of applied psi research to ensure that the benefits are widely distributed. But as of 2040, these aspirations remain largely unrealized, and the commercialization of applied psi (see Lecture 10) is creating pressures toward inequality.

**4. The "Slippery Slope" Problem**

If applied psi is accepted as a legitimate tool in one domain (e.g., medical diagnosis), it may be difficult to prevent its expansion into more problematic domains (e.g., psychological manipulation, political propaganda, "psi warfare"). The "slippery slope" from benign to problematic applications is a concern in many technologies, but it is particularly acute for psi, because the mechanisms are poorly understood and the boundaries between applications are blurry.

## Lecture 9: Training Intuition — Protocols, Technologies, and Evidence

**Can Psi Be Trained?**

A fundamental question for applied parapsychology is whether psi can be *trained* — whether individuals can learn to access anomalous cognition more reliably, more accurately, and more on-demand. The question is not whether psi exists (the meta-analytic evidence suggests it does) but whether psi performance can be improved through deliberate practice, instruction, and technology.

The evidence on psi training is mixed but cautiously optimistic. Several training protocols have been evaluated:

1. **Ganzfeld-based intuition training**: Participants are trained to enter the Ganzfeld state (relaxed, sensory-reduced, inwardly focused) and to access intuitive impressions about a target. Training involves repeated sessions with feedback, allowing participants to learn which mental states and strategies produce accurate impressions. Evaluations of Ganzfeld training have found modest improvements in accuracy over time (d ≈ 0.2-0.3), but the improvements are not universal — some participants improve substantially, while others show no improvement, and the predictors of "trainability" are not well understood.

2. **Remote viewing training**: The CRV protocol (Lecture 3) is, in effect, a training program — viewers are taught a structured method for accessing and reporting remote impressions. The Stargate program found that some viewers improved with training, while others did not, and that the most successful viewers tended to have a "natural" aptitude that preceded training. Controlled evaluations of remote viewing training (including the UoY ARV Training Study) have found small to moderate improvements (d ≈ 0.3-0.5), with the largest improvements in the most structured elements of the protocol (sketching, gestalt description) and the smallest improvements in the most "psi-dependent" elements (specific details, names, numbers).

3. **Biofeedback-based psi training**: Participants are given real-time feedback on their physiological state (heart rate variability, skin conductance, EEG) and are trained to enter the "psi-conducive" state — relaxed, focused, with high cardiac coherence and alpha/theta EEG activity. Preliminary evaluations suggest that biofeedback training improves psi performance (d ≈ 0.2-0.3), but the effect may be mediated by relaxation and reduced anxiety rather than by a specific psi-enhancing mechanism.

4. **Technology-assisted training**: Several technologies have been developed to enhance psi training, including: (a) **neurofeedback systems** that reward EEG patterns associated with psi performance; (b) **VR Ganzfeld environments** that provide immersive, controlled sensory reduction; and (c) **AI-based coaching systems** that analyze the trainee's performance and provide personalized recommendations. The evidence for these technologies is preliminary.

**The UoY Intuition Development Program**

The UoY Intuition Development Program (IDP), launched in 2039, is a comprehensive psi training program that integrates multiple modalities:

1. **Foundation training** (8 weeks): Mindfulness meditation, cardiac coherence biofeedback, and somatic awareness training — establishing the relaxed, focused, body-aware state that is conducive to psi.

2. **Protocol training** (12 weeks): Instruction in specific psi protocols — Ganzfeld, remote viewing, presentiment, forced-choice ESP — with extensive practice and feedback.

3. **Applied training** (8 weeks): Application of the learned protocols to real-world decision scenarios — business, medical, personal — with structured debriefing and feedback.

4. **Maintenance and refinement** (ongoing): Regular practice sessions, peer consultation, and technology-assisted refinement (AI coaching, VR practice environments).

Preliminary evaluation of the IDP (Haraldsdóttir, Vigfússon, & Chen, 2040) has found:
- **Overall improvement**: Participants showed a significant improvement in psi performance from pre-training to post-training (d ≈ 0.4, p < 0.001), measured by a battery of standardized psi tasks.
- **Individual differences**: Approximately 40% of participants showed large improvements (d > 0.5), 40% showed small improvements (d = 0.1-0.5), and 20% showed no improvement or slight decline. Predictors of large improvement included: high baseline absorption, high baseline cardiac coherence, and strong belief in the trainability of psi.
- **Durability**: At 6-month follow-up, approximately 70% of the improvement was retained, suggesting that the training effects are moderately durable.

**The "Natural Gift" vs. "Trained Skill" Debate**

The IDP findings highlight a persistent tension in applied psi: the tension between the "natural gift" model (psi is an innate capacity that some people have and others lack, and that cannot be substantially trained) and the "trained skill" model (psi is a capacity that, like playing the piano or speaking a foreign language, can be developed through practice and instruction).

The evidence suggests that both models are partially correct: some people have a "natural" aptitude for psi that manifests without training, and training can improve psi performance in most (though not all) people. The practical implication is that applied psi programs should select for natural aptitude (screening with absorption, hypnotizability, and baseline psi performance) while also providing training to develop and refine the capacity.

## Lecture 10: The Business of Applied Psi — Commercialization, Certification, and Quality Control

**The Applied Psi Marketplace**

By 2040, a nascent applied psi marketplace has emerged. Companies and consultants offer services including:
- **Remote viewing for business**: Competitor analysis, site selection, product development insights.

The marketplace is largely unregulated. There are no universally recognized certification standards, no mandatory quality control mechanisms, and no legal frameworks specifically addressing psi-related services. Consumers have no reliable way to distinguish legitimate (i.e., evidence-based, honest about limitations) services from fraudulent or self-deceived ones.

**The UoY Applied Psi Certification Program**

To address the quality control problem, the UoY Applied Psi Initiative has developed a Certification Program for applied psi practitioners. The certification requires:

1. **Educational prerequisites**: Completion of specified coursework in parapsychology (PP101, PP103, PP201, PP301, PP403) or equivalent.
2. **Training**: Completion of the UoY Intuition Development Program (or an approved equivalent).
3. **Competency assessment**: Demonstration of reliable above-chance performance on standardized psi tasks (minimum 2 of 3 protocols: Ganzfeld, remote viewing, presentiment), assessed by an independent testing center.
4. **Ethics training**: Completion of an ethics module covering consent, confidentiality, limitations of psi, and professional boundaries.
5. **Continuing education**: Annual renewal requiring documented continuing education and re-assessment.

The Certification Program is voluntary — there is no legal requirement for applied psi practitioners to be certified. But the UoY Initiative hopes that certification will become a market signal: certified practitioners will be recognized as legitimate, evidence-based, and ethical, while non-certified practitioners will be viewed with appropriate skepticism.

The Certification Program is controversial within the parapsychology community. Proponents argue that it is necessary to protect consumers, establish professional standards, and enhance the credibility of applied psi. Critics argue that it is premature — that the evidence for applied psi is not yet strong enough to support a certification program — and that it may create a false sense of security (certified practitioners may still be unreliable).

**The Challenge of Evidence-Based Marketing**

Applied psi businesses face a marketing challenge: how to promote their services honestly and ethically while acknowledging the limitations of psi. The UoY Applied Psi Initiative has developed guidelines for evidence-based marketing of psi services, including:
- **No guarantees**: Services should not guarantee specific outcomes (e.g., "We will find the missing person").
- **Transparency about evidence**: Marketing should accurately represent the scientific evidence for psi, including the small effect sizes and the limitations.
- **Honest about success rates**: If a service claims a success rate, the claim should be backed by auditable data, and the methodology for calculating the rate should be transparent.
- **No fear-based marketing**: Services should not exploit fear, grief, or desperation (e.g., "Your loved one is in danger — only our psychic can help").

These guidelines are voluntary and are not legally enforceable, but they represent a good-faith effort to establish ethical standards for the applied psi marketplace.

## Lecture 11: Case Studies in Applied Psi — Successes, Failures, and Lessons Learned

**The Value of Case Studies**

Meta-analysis provides the "big picture" — the average effect size across many studies. But the average can obscure as much as it reveals. To understand applied psi, we must also examine individual cases — the specific instances in which psi was (or was not) successfully applied. Case studies do not provide statistical evidence (they are N = 1), but they provide richness, context, and lessons that statistics cannot capture.

This lecture presents four case studies from the UoY Applied Psi Initiative archives. Each case is presented with its context, the psi protocol used, the outcome, and the lessons learned.

**Case Study 1: The Vestfold Archaeological Remote Viewing Project (2038)**

**Context**: The UoY Department of Archaeology, in collaboration with the ARV Program, sought to locate unexcavated Viking Age settlement sites in the Vestfold region of Norway. Conventional survey methods (aerial photography, ground-penetrating radar) had identified several candidate areas but could not pinpoint specific locations.

**Protocol**: Five trained remote viewers were given geographic coordinates for a 10 km² survey area. Each viewer produced a detailed description of the landscape, structures, and artifacts they "perceived" within the area. The descriptions were compiled, and areas of convergence (where multiple viewers independently described similar features) were identified as high-priority targets.

**Outcome**: A test excavation at the highest-priority target area (identified by convergence of four out of five viewers) uncovered a previously unknown Viking Age longhouse with associated artifacts (pottery, iron tools, a Thor's hammer pendant). The excavation was documented, and the remote viewing reports were published alongside the archaeological findings.

**Lessons Learned**:
- **Convergence is powerful**: Areas where multiple viewers converged were significantly more likely to yield archaeological finds than areas where only one viewer reported features.
- **Specificity varies**: Viewers were generally accurate about the *type* of site (settlement, not burial; structure, not natural feature) but less accurate about specific details (size, orientation, number of buildings).
- **Blinding is essential**: Some viewers may have been influenced by their knowledge of Viking Age archaeology (e.g., describing features typical of known sites). Better blinding (ensuring viewers have no archaeological background knowledge) would strengthen future studies.

**Case Study 2: The Reykjavík Medical Intuition Pilot (2039)**

**Context**: In collaboration with the National University Hospital of Iceland, the UoY Medical Intuition Training Program conducted a pilot study in which trained medical intuitives provided diagnostic impressions for patients with ambiguous presentations (symptoms that did not clearly indicate a specific diagnosis).

**Protocol**: Three trained medical intuitives were given minimal information about each patient (age, sex, chief complaint) and asked to describe the underlying pathology. Their descriptions were compared to the eventual diagnosis (gold standard, established through conventional workup).

**Outcome**: The intuitives correctly identified the organ system involved in 60% of cases (vs. 40% expected by chance, given the base rates), and correctly identified the specific diagnosis in 35% of cases (vs. 15% expected by chance). However, there were also notable errors: in two cases, the intuitives confidently identified a pathology (cancer) that was not present, causing significant distress before the error was corrected by conventional testing.

**Lessons Learned**:
- **Medical intuition shows promise but is unreliable**: The above-chance accuracy is encouraging, but the error rate is too high to use medical intuition as a standalone diagnostic tool.
- **The harm of false positives**: A false positive (confidently identifying a disease that is not present) causes more harm than a false negative (failing to identify a disease that is present, which will eventually be identified through conventional methods). Medical intuition protocols should be designed to minimize false positives.
- **Integration with conventional diagnosis is essential**: Medical intuition should be used to *generate hypotheses* that are then tested through conventional methods, not to *make diagnoses* that are accepted without verification.

**Case Study 3: The North Atlantic Financial Psi Simulation (2039-2040)**

**Context**: A simulated investment portfolio was managed using aggregated intuitive ratings from a rotating panel of trained participants. The portfolio's performance was compared to the S&P 500 and to a random selection benchmark.

**Protocol**: Each quarter, 10 trained intuitives rated 20 candidate stocks on a 1-7 scale for "future performance." The top 5 rated stocks were selected for the portfolio. The portfolio was rebalanced quarterly.

**Outcome**: Over 8 quarters, the psi portfolio returned 12.3% annualized vs. 10.8% for the S&P 500 and 10.2% for the random benchmark. The difference was not statistically significant (p ≈ 0.12) but was encouraging.

**Lessons Learned**:
- **Small edge, large uncertainty**: The 1.5% annual outperformance, if real, would be enormously valuable — but the statistical uncertainty is large, and the result could easily be luck.
- **Aggregation matters**: The aggregated ratings (averaging across 10 intuitives) outperformed any individual intuitive's ratings — consistent with the "wisdom of crowds" effect.
- **Participant turnover is a challenge**: Maintaining a consistent panel of trained intuitives over multiple quarters was difficult; attrition and motivation fluctuations introduced noise.

**Case Study 4: The Icelandic Search and Rescue Remote Viewing Test (2040)**

**Context**: The Icelandic Search and Rescue (ICE-SAR) organization requested the UoY ARV Program's assistance in locating a hiker missing in the highlands. Conventional search methods (aerial search, ground teams, thermal imaging) had been unsuccessful after 48 hours.

**Protocol**: Under time pressure, two experienced remote viewers were given the hiker's photograph, the last known location, and the approximate time of disappearance. Each viewer produced a sketch and description of the hiker's likely location.

**Outcome**: One viewer's description matched a gully approximately 2 km east of the search area; the hiker was found there (deceased) approximately 6 hours later. However, the second viewer's description pointed to a different location (southwest of the search area), and several other tips (from non-UoY psychics) were inaccurate. It is unclear whether the "hit" was psi, luck, or a combination.

**Lessons Learned**:
- **Real-world conditions are challenging**: The time pressure, emotional intensity, and lack of controlled conditions make real-world applied psi much messier than laboratory psi.
- **Single hits are uninterpretable**: A single apparent success cannot be distinguished from luck; only an aggregated record of performance across many cases can provide evidence of psi.
- **Ethical responsibility is weighty**: The UoY team felt a strong sense of responsibility — the remote viewing reports could affect the search strategy, potentially leading rescuers toward or away from the missing person. This responsibility weighed heavily on the team and highlighted the need for clear ethical protocols.

## Lecture 12: Synthesis — The Wise Use of Psi in a Complex World

**The View from 2040**

We have covered a great deal of ground in this course — from the reliability problem that haunts all applied psi, to the specific protocols for remote viewing, financial psi, medical intuition, and investigative psi, to the ethical challenges of using psi in high-stakes contexts, to the case studies that illustrate both the promise and the peril of applied psi. What picture emerges?

The picture is of a capacity — anomalous cognition — that is real but fragile, promising but unreliable, ethically complex but potentially transformative. Psi is not a superpower. It will not replace conventional decision-making, conventional diagnosis, or conventional investigation. But it may, when used wisely and ethically, provide a small but real advantage — an edge that, cumulated over many decisions and many contexts, could make a meaningful difference.

**The Wise Use of Psi**

The "wise use" of psi, as conceptualized by the UoY Applied Psi Initiative, involves:

1. **Humility**: Acknowledging the limitations of psi — the small effect sizes, the high variability, the vulnerability to bias and error. Psi is a whisper, not a shout; it should be listened to, but not uncritically obeyed.

2. **Integration**: Using psi as a complement to, not a replacement for, conventional methods. The physician who combines intuitive impressions with laboratory tests, the executive who combines gut feelings with market analysis, the investigator who combines remote viewing with forensic evidence — these are the models of wise integration.

3. **Verification**: Whenever possible, verifying psi-derived information through conventional means before acting on it. The intuitive diagnostic impression should be confirmed by a laboratory test; the remote viewing report should be confirmed by satellite imagery; the premonition of a market movement should be confirmed by conventional analysis.

4. **Transparency**: Being honest about the use of psi — with oneself, with colleagues, with clients, with the public. Psi should not be deployed secretly or deceptively; its use should be disclosed, and its limitations acknowledged.

5. **Ethical grounding**: Using psi in accordance with ethical principles — respect for autonomy, beneficence, non-maleficence, justice. Psi should not be used to exploit, manipulate, or harm.

6. **Continuous learning**: Treating every psi application as an opportunity for learning — systematically recording outcomes, analyzing errors, refining protocols, and building the evidence base for what works and what does not.

**The Personal Dimension**

Beyond the professional applications, psi has a personal dimension. The capacity for anomalous cognition — for "knowing" things beyond the reach of the senses — is part of what it means to be human. It is a capacity that can be developed, refined, and integrated into a rich, wise, and connected life.

The students in this course are not just future professionals (though many of you will become professionals in parapsychology or related fields). You are also human beings with your own intuitive capacities, your own gut feelings, your own moments of inexplicable knowing. The goal of this course has been not just to teach you about applied psi but to help you *use* it — in your own life, in your own decisions, in your own way.

This does not mean abandoning critical thinking or scientific skepticism. It means integrating intuition and analysis — listening to the gut feeling while demanding evidence, trusting the hunch while seeking verification, remaining open to the inexplicable while insisting on rigor. This integration — the marriage of intuition and analysis, of psi and science — is, in the instructor's view, the central challenge and the central promise of applied parapsychology.

**The Future of Applied Psi**

Where does applied psi go from here? The UoY Applied Psi Initiative's strategic plan identifies six priorities:

1. **Improving reliability**: Developing better training protocols, better selection methods, and better technologies to improve the reliability of applied psi.
2. **Building the evidence base**: Conducting rigorous, pre-registered, large-sample studies of applied psi in real-world contexts.
3. **Developing ethical frameworks**: Refining the ethical principles that govern applied psi and translating them into practical guidelines.
4. **Professionalizing the field**: Establishing certification standards, quality control mechanisms, and professional norms for applied psi practitioners.
5. **Engaging the public**: Educating the public about the realities (and limitations) of applied psi, countering both uncritical enthusiasm and dogmatic skepticism.
6. **Integrating with mainstream disciplines**: Bringing applied psi into conversation with medicine, business, law enforcement, and other fields, demonstrating its potential value while respecting the standards of those fields.

The most important priority is the one that involves you. Applied psi needs practitioners who are skilled, ethical, humble, and rigorous. It needs researchers who can design and evaluate protocols with the same methodological sophistication they would bring to any other scientific domain. And it needs advocates who can communicate the promise and the limitations of psi to a public that is often either credulous or dismissive.
