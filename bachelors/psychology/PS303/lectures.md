## Lecture 1: What Is Cognitive Neuroscience? — Methods, Assumptions, and the Mind-Brain Relationship

Cognitive neuroscience is the field that investigates how the brain enables the mind. It rests on the fundamental assumption — supported by an overwhelming convergence of evidence — that mental processes are brain processes. Every thought, every memory, every decision, every perception, every emotion is, at some level, a pattern of neural activity. This assumption — **materialism** or **physicalism** — is not new (it was argued by Hippocrates in the 5th century BCE), but it has been established with a specificity and precision that were unimaginable even fifty years ago. The task of cognitive neuroscience is to map the relationships between cognitive processes and neural processes — to identify the brain structures and networks that implement specific cognitive functions, to understand the neural computations that underlie them, and to explain how patterns of neural activity give rise to the subjective experience of a unified, conscious mind.

The **methods** of cognitive neuroscience constitute a family of techniques, each with its own strengths, limitations, and interpretive challenges. **Structural magnetic resonance imaging (sMRI)** provides high-resolution images of brain anatomy — the distribution of grey matter, white matter, and cerebrospinal fluid — and enables the quantification of regional brain volume, cortical thickness, and white-matter integrity (via **diffusion tensor imaging — DTI**, which measures the diffusion of water molecules along axonal tracts, providing a window into the brain's structural connectivity). **Functional MRI (fMRI)** measures brain activity indirectly, by detecting changes in blood oxygenation (the **BOLD signal** — blood-oxygen-level-dependent contrast) that accompany neural activity. When a brain region is active, local blood flow increases, delivering more oxygenated haemoglobin than is immediately consumed; the ratio of oxygenated to deoxygenated haemoglobin changes the magnetic properties of the tissue, and the MRI scanner detects this change. The BOLD signal is correlated with neural activity (primarily with local field potentials — the summed synaptic activity of thousands of neurons — rather than with spiking activity per se), but the correlation is imperfect, the temporal resolution is poor (the haemodynamic response lags neural activity by 2–6 seconds and peaks at 5–8 seconds), and the signal is noisy (only about 1–2% of the total MRI signal). These limitations must be borne in mind when interpreting fMRI results.

**Electroencephalography (EEG)** records electrical activity at the scalp, generated by the synchronised postsynaptic potentials of large populations of cortical pyramidal neurons. EEG has excellent temporal resolution (milliseconds) but poor spatial resolution (the signal is distorted by the skull and scalp, and the source of a given scalp potential cannot be determined uniquely — the **inverse problem**). **Event-related potentials (ERPs)** — the average EEG waveform time-locked to a specific stimulus or response — provide a millisecond-by-millisecond record of the brain's processing of that event, and the characteristic ERP components (the N170 — a face-sensitive negativity at 170 ms; the P300 — a positivity at 300 ms associated with attention and context-updating; the N400 — a negativity at 400 ms associated with semantic processing) index specific cognitive processes. **Magnetoencephalography (MEG)** records the magnetic fields generated by neural currents, with the same excellent temporal resolution as EEG but somewhat better spatial resolution (magnetic fields are less distorted by the skull than electric potentials). **Transcranial magnetic stimulation (TMS)** uses a magnetic pulse applied to the scalp to induce an electrical current in the underlying cortex — transiently disrupting (or, with certain parameters, enhancing) neural processing. TMS provides a causal test of structure-function relationships: if disrupting a brain region impairs a cognitive function, that region is necessary (not merely correlated) for that function. **Functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS)** is a portable, non-invasive optical imaging technique that measures cortical haemodynamic responses — it is less spatially precise than fMRI but can be used in naturalistic settings (e.g., while the participant walks, talks, or interacts socially), opening cognitive processes to study that would be impossible in the scanner.

The core **interpretive challenge** of cognitive neuroscience is **reverse inference** (Poldrack, 2006): the inference from the activation of a brain region to the engagement of a cognitive process. When the amygdala is activated in an fMRI study, is the participant experiencing fear? Not necessarily — the amygdala is activated by novelty, salience, arousal, and uncertainty, as well as by fear. When the dorsolateral prefrontal cortex is activated, is the participant engaged in cognitive control? Not necessarily — dlPFC is activated by a wide range of demanding cognitive tasks. Reverse inference is logically fallacious (the inference from "if P then Q" and "Q" to "P" is deductively invalid — it is the fallacy of affirming the consequent) but is routinely practised in the neuroimaging literature. The responsible cognitive neuroscientist avoids reverse inference by: (a) designing tasks that isolate the cognitive process of interest (e.g., subtracting a control condition that shares all processes with the experimental condition except the one of interest — the **cognitive subtraction** method, though even this assumes pure insertion — that adding a cognitive process does not alter the processes already engaged, an assumption that is frequently violated); (b) using converging operations (multiple methods — fMRI, EEG, TMS, lesion studies — provide converging evidence for a structure-function relationship); and (c) acknowledging the limitations of the inference and the alternative interpretations that are compatible with the data.

## Lecture 2: The Architecture of the Brain — From Neurons to Networks

The human brain is the most complex object in the known universe — approximately 86 billion neurons, each forming an average of 1,000–10,000 synapses, yielding something on the order of 100 trillion connections, organised into a hierarchically nested, small-world network that integrates information across spatial and temporal scales from the millisecond firing of a single neuron to the decades-long consolidation of a personal identity. Understanding cognition requires understanding the architecture of the brain — the components from which it is built and the principles that govern their organisation and interaction.

The **neuron** is the fundamental signalling unit of the brain. The neuron doctrine — proposed by Santiago Ramón y Cajal in the late 19th century, using Golgi's silver-staining method — established that the nervous system is composed of discrete cells (neurons), not a continuous syncytium (as Golgi himself believed — Cajal and Golgi shared the Nobel Prize in 1906, each advocating the opposite interpretation of the same data). The neuron consists of: **dendrites** (branched extensions that receive synaptic input from other neurons), the **cell body** (soma — containing the nucleus and metabolic machinery), the **axon** (a long, thin extension that conducts electrical impulses — action potentials — away from the soma to the axon terminals), and the **axon terminals** (which form synapses with the dendrites, soma, or axon of the postsynaptic neuron). The **action potential** — the all-or-none electrical impulse that travels along the axon — is generated by the opening and closing of voltage-gated ion channels (sodium channels open, depolarising the membrane; potassium channels open, repolarising it; the sodium-potassium pump restores the resting potential). The action potential triggers the release of **neurotransmitters** — chemical messengers — from the presynaptic terminal into the synaptic cleft; the neurotransmitters bind to receptors on the postsynaptic membrane, opening (or closing) ion channels and altering the postsynaptic neuron's likelihood of firing.

The **cerebral cortex** — the convoluted outer layer of the brain, approximately 2–4 mm thick, containing approximately 16 billion neurons — is the seat of the higher cognitive functions. The cortex is organised into **layers** (six layers, distinguished by cell type and density — the **neocortex**, or "new cortex," is six-layered; older cortical regions — the hippocampus, the olfactory cortex — have fewer layers) and **columns** (vertical arrays of neurons, approximately 0.5 mm in diameter, that share functional properties — the columnar organisation is most clearly established in primary sensory cortices and remains a topic of research and debate in higher-order association cortices). The cortex is divided into **lobes**: the **frontal lobe** (anterior to the central sulcus — involved in motor control, executive functions, working memory, decision-making, and personality); the **parietal lobe** (posterior to the central sulcus, superior to the temporal lobe — involved in somatosensory processing, spatial attention, and the integration of sensory information); the **temporal lobe** (inferior to the lateral fissure — involved in auditory processing, memory — the hippocampus and surrounding medial temporal structures — language comprehension, and object recognition); and the **occipital lobe** (the most posterior region — involved in visual processing).

The **subcortical structures** — deep brain regions that lie beneath the cortex — include: the **basal ganglia** (caudate nucleus, putamen, globus pallidus, subthalamic nucleus, substantia nigra — involved in motor control, habit learning, and action selection — the basal ganglia are the primary site of pathology in Parkinson's disease and Huntington's disease, and are implicated in OCD, addiction, and Tourette syndrome); the **thalamus** (a relay station — most sensory information passes through the thalamus en route to the cortex, and the thalamus is involved in attention, arousal, and the gating of information flow); the **hypothalamus** (small — about the size of an almond — but critical, regulating hunger, thirst, sex, sleep, body temperature, and the autonomic nervous system and endocrine system via the pituitary gland); the **amygdala** (in the medial temporal lobe — critical for emotional processing, particularly fear, and for the modulation of memory by emotion); and the **hippocampus** (in the medial temporal lobe — critical for the formation of new declarative memories — memories for facts and events — and for spatial navigation — the hippocampus contains place cells, grid cells, and other spatially tuned neurons that constitute the neural basis of the cognitive map).

The **large-scale organisation** of the brain is increasingly understood in terms of **networks** — sets of brain regions whose activity is correlated over time (functional connectivity) and that are anatomically connected by white-matter tracts (structural connectivity). The major functional networks — identified by **resting-state fMRI** (scanning the brain while the participant lies quietly, not performing any task) — include: the **default mode network (DMN)** (medial prefrontal cortex, posterior cingulate cortex, angular gyrus, medial temporal lobes — active at rest, deactivated during externally focused tasks, associated with self-referential thought, autobiographical memory, and future simulation); the **salience network** (anterior insula, dorsal anterior cingulate cortex — involved in detecting and orienting to behaviourally relevant stimuli — the "alarm system" that signals when something important is happening); the **central executive network (CEN)** (dorsolateral prefrontal cortex, posterior parietal cortex — involved in working memory, cognitive control, and decision-making — active during demanding cognitive tasks, anticorrelated with the DMN); and the **dorsal and ventral attention networks** (dorsal: intraparietal sulcus, frontal eye fields — involved in top-down, goal-directed attention; ventral: temporoparietal junction, ventral frontal cortex — involved in bottom-up, stimulus-driven attention — the "circuit-breaker" that redirects attention to unexpected, salient events). These networks are not isolated modules but interacting components of a **dynamic, integrated system**, and understanding how their interactions produce cognition is the central challenge of contemporary cognitive neuroscience.

## Lecture 3: Perception — How the Brain Constructs the Visual World

Perception is the process by which the brain interprets sensory information to construct a representation of the external world. It is the foundation of cognition — before we can remember, decide, or act, we must perceive. The study of visual perception has been the paradigmatic domain of cognitive neuroscience — vision is the best-understood sensory system, and the principles discovered in visual neuroscience have illuminated the neural mechanisms of perception more broadly. This lecture surveys the neural architecture of the visual system, from the retina to the cortex, and the theoretical frameworks — hierarchical processing, predictive coding, and the Bayesian brain — that organise contemporary research.

The **visual pathway** begins in the **retina**, where photoreceptors (rods — for low-light, achromatic vision; cones — for high-acuity, colour vision) transduce light into neural signals. The retinal ganglion cells — the output neurons of the retina — project via the **optic nerve** to the **lateral geniculate nucleus (LGN)** of the thalamus, and from there to the **primary visual cortex (V1)** in the occipital lobe. The organisation of the visual system is **retinotopic** — adjacent points in the retina project to adjacent points in the LGN and V1, preserving the spatial layout of the visual field — and **hierarchical**: information flows from V1 (which encodes simple features — orientation, spatial frequency, direction of motion, binocular disparity) through a series of extrastriate visual areas (V2, V3, V4, MT/V5) to the **inferior temporal (IT) cortex** (which encodes complex objects and faces) and the **posterior parietal cortex** (which encodes spatial location and guides visually guided action). This hierarchical organisation is encapsulated in the **two-streams hypothesis** (Ungerleider & Mishkin, 1982; Goodale & Milner, 1992): the **ventral stream** (occipito-temporal — the "what" pathway) processes object identity; the **dorsal stream** (occipito-parietal — the "where/how" pathway) processes spatial location and guides action. The double dissociation between visual agnosia (inability to recognise objects — ventral stream damage) and optic ataxia (inability to guide actions to objects — dorsal stream damage) provides compelling evidence for this functional segregation.

The **receptive field** — the region of visual space (or, at higher levels, the set of stimuli) to which a neuron responds — is the fundamental unit of visual processing. Hubel and Wiesel (1959, 1962) — who won the Nobel Prize in 1981 — demonstrated that neurons in V1 have elongated receptive fields and respond selectively to lines or edges of a particular orientation. They classified V1 neurons into **simple cells** (responding to a line of a specific orientation at a specific location) and **complex cells** (responding to a line of a specific orientation over a larger region of visual space, and — in some cases — to a line moving in a particular direction). The hierarchical model proposes that simple cells combine the outputs of LGN cells with aligned receptive fields; complex cells combine the outputs of simple cells with the same orientation preference but different locations; and higher-level cells combine the outputs of lower-level cells to extract increasingly complex features — edges, contours, shapes, objects, faces. The discovery of the **fusiform face area (FFA)** — a region of the fusiform gyrus that responds selectively to faces — and the **parahippocampal place area (PPA)** — a region that responds selectively to scenes and places — provided dramatic confirmation of the principle of functional specialisation, and the debate about whether these regions are truly domain-specific (selective for faces and places, respectively) or process-specific (selective for the cognitive operations required to process faces — expert within-category discrimination — and places — spatial layout) has been one of the most productive debates in cognitive neuroscience.

The **predictive coding** framework — the most influential integrative theory of perception and brain function in 2040 — proposes that the brain is not a passive receiver of sensory input but an active constructor of perceptual experience. The core idea: the brain generates predictions (top-down signals from higher cortical areas) about the sensory input it expects to receive; these predictions are compared with the actual input (bottom-up signals from sensory receptors); the discrepancy — the **prediction error** — is propagated forward through the hierarchy, updating the predictions; perception is the process of minimising prediction error across all levels of the hierarchy, which is equivalent to inferring the most likely causes of the sensory input (Friston, 2005, 2010; Clark, 2013, 2033; Hohwy, 2013). The predictive coding framework explains a wide range of perceptual phenomena: **binocular rivalry** (when different images are presented to the two eyes, perception alternates — the brain cannot settle on a single interpretation — because the prediction errors from the two images cannot be simultaneously minimised); **the McGurk effect** (the perception of speech is influenced by visual information — what you see overrides what you hear — because the brain integrates auditory and visual prediction errors); and **hallucinations** (perception in the absence of sensory input — the brain's predictions are not constrained by prediction error, and the person perceives what the brain expects). Predictive coding is a specific implementation of the broader **Bayesian brain** hypothesis — the idea that the brain performs probabilistic inference, combining prior expectations (the probability of a hypothesis before observing the data, given past experience) with sensory evidence (the likelihood of the data given the hypothesis) to compute the posterior probability (the probability of the hypothesis given the data).

## Lecture 4: Attention — The Selection of Information for Conscious Processing

The world presents the brain with far more information than it can fully process. At any moment, the retina is bombarded with millions of photons, the ears with a cacophony of sound waves, the skin with a symphony of pressure, temperature, and pain signals. The brain must select — attend to the information that is relevant to current goals, suppress the information that is not, and monitor the unattended channels for signals (the cry of a baby, the screech of tyres) that demand immediate attention. **Attention** is the family of processes that accomplish this selection. This lecture surveys the cognitive neuroscience of attention — the neural mechanisms of selective attention, the distinction between top-down and bottom-up attention, and the consequences of attentional selection for perception, memory, and conscious awareness.

Attention can be **overt** (accompanied by movements of the eyes, head, or body toward the attended stimulus) or **covert** (without observable movement — the "spotlight" of attention moves independently of the eyes). The neural bases of covert attention have been studied most extensively using the **Posner cueing paradigm** (Posner, 1980): a cue (an arrow at fixation, or a flash of light at a peripheral location) directs attention to a location; a target appears at the cued location (valid trial) or at the opposite location (invalid trial); the participant responds as quickly as possible. The **validity effect** — faster responses on valid than invalid trials — indexes the benefit of attention and the cost of reorienting. Neuroimaging studies of the Posner paradigm reveal that attention enhances the neural response to stimuli at the attended location and suppresses the response to stimuli at unattended locations, and that these effects occur in sensory cortex — even as early as V1 — demonstrating that attention modulates the earliest stages of perceptual processing.

The **frontoparietal attention network** — the dorsal attention network — is the neural substrate of top-down, goal-directed attention. It includes the **intraparietal sulcus (IPS)** and **superior parietal lobule (SPL)**, the **frontal eye fields (FEF)**, and the **supplementary eye fields (SEF)**. These regions are activated when attention is voluntarily directed to a location, a feature, or an object, and they generate the top-down signals that bias sensory processing in favour of the attended stimulus. The **ventral attention network** — including the **temporoparietal junction (TPJ)** and the **ventral frontal cortex (VFC)** (parts of the inferior frontal gyrus and middle frontal gyrus) — is the neural substrate of bottom-up, stimulus-driven attention. It is activated by unexpected, behaviourally relevant stimuli (the "oddball" in a stream of identical stimuli, one's own name in an unattended auditory channel), and its function is to interrupt ongoing processing and reorient attention to the novel event — the "circuit-breaker" (Corbetta & Shulman, 2002; Corbetta, Patel & Shulman, 2008). The dorsal and ventral attention networks interact dynamically: the dorsal network maintains the current attentional set (e.g., "look for red cars"); the ventral network detects stimuli that match the current set or that are sufficiently salient to warrant interruption; and the two networks together determine what is attended and what is not.

The **biased competition model** (Desimone & Duncan, 1995) is the most influential theoretical framework for understanding the neural mechanisms of attention. When multiple stimuli are present in the visual field, they compete for neural representation — they suppress each other's responses in the visual cortex. Attention resolves this competition by biasing processing in favour of the attended stimulus: neurons that respond to the attended stimulus increase their firing rates; neurons that respond to the unattended stimulus decrease their firing rates; and the attended stimulus "wins" the competition for representation and control of behaviour. The biased competition model is supported by single-unit recordings in monkeys (when two stimuli are presented in a neuron's receptive field, the neuron's response is dominated by the attended stimulus, and the unattended stimulus has little effect — Reynolds, Chelazzi & Desimone, 1999) and by fMRI studies in humans (in visual cortex, the response to a stimulus is enhanced when it is attended and suppressed when it is ignored, and this modulation is most pronounced when multiple stimuli compete for representation — Kastner & Ungerleider, 2000).

Attention has profound consequences for perception, memory, and consciousness. The **attentional blink** (Raymond, Shapiro & Arnell, 1992) — when two targets are presented in rapid succession, the second target is often missed if it appears within 200–500 ms of the first — demonstrates the temporal limits of attention: attending to the first target consumes the limited capacity of the attentional system, and the second target is not processed to the level of conscious awareness. **Change blindness** (Simons & Levin, 1997) — the failure to detect a change in a visual scene when the change occurs during a brief disruption (a blank screen, an eye blink, a cut in a film) — demonstrates that attention is necessary for conscious perception: without attention, we do not perceive even large changes in our visual environment. **Inattentional blindness** (Mack & Rock, 1998) — the failure to perceive an unexpected stimulus when attention is engaged elsewhere (the famous "invisible gorilla" experiment — Simons & Chabris, 1999) — demonstrates that attention is necessary for conscious awareness: without it, we are effectively blind, even though our eyes are open and the stimulus is clearly visible.

## Lecture 5: Memory — Encoding, Consolidation, and Retrieval in the Brain

Memory is not a single faculty but a collection of systems, each with its own neural substrates, operating principles, and developmental trajectory. The major distinction — between **declarative (explicit) memory** (memory for facts and events that can be consciously recalled — subdivided into **episodic memory** — memory for personally experienced events, rich in contextual detail — and **semantic memory** — memory for facts, concepts, and general knowledge, abstracted from the contexts in which they were learned) and **non-declarative (implicit) memory** (memory that is expressed through performance rather than conscious recall — including **procedural memory** for skills and habits, **priming** — the facilitated processing of a stimulus due to prior exposure, **classical conditioning**, and **non-associative learning** such as habituation and sensitisation) — is among the most important and best-validated distinctions in cognitive neuroscience.

The **medial temporal lobe (MTL) memory system** — the hippocampus and the surrounding entorhinal, perirhinal, and parahippocampal cortices — is critical for the formation of new declarative memories. The evidence for this claim comes from several sources. (a) The study of the amnesic patient **H.M.** (Henry Molaison), who underwent bilateral resection of the medial temporal lobes (including most of the hippocampus) for the treatment of intractable epilepsy in 1953. H.M. was left with a profound **anterograde amnesia** — the inability to form new declarative memories — while his intelligence, working memory, procedural memory, and memory for events prior to the surgery were largely intact (Scoville & Milner, 1957; Corkin, 2013). H.M. could learn new motor skills (mirror drawing, pursuit rotor) at a normal rate, even though he had no conscious memory of having performed the task before — a dissociation that provided powerful evidence for the distinction between declarative and procedural memory. (b) Neuroimaging studies consistently show that the hippocampus and surrounding MTL structures are activated during the encoding of new memories (particularly when the encoding is deep — semantic, elaborative — rather than shallow — perceptual, rote) and during the retrieval of recent memories. (c) Animal studies — particularly the development of the **Morris water maze** (Morris, 1981) — demonstrate that hippocampal lesions impair spatial memory, supporting the broader role of the hippocampus in relational memory — the binding of disparate elements of an experience into a coherent, integrated representation.

The **standard model of memory consolidation** (Squire & Alvarez, 1995; Squire & Wixted, 2011) proposes that the hippocampus is critical for the initial formation and short-term retention of declarative memories but that, over time, memories become independent of the hippocampus and are stored in the neocortex — a process called **systems consolidation**. The evidence for this model includes: **temporally graded retrograde amnesia** — patients with hippocampal damage have impaired memory for events that occurred in the years immediately before the damage but relatively preserved memory for events from the more distant past (the memories have been consolidated and no longer depend on the hippocampus); and **sleep-dependent consolidation** — memories are reactivated and strengthened during sleep (particularly during slow-wave sleep — the replay of hippocampal place-cell sequences during sleep, discovered by Wilson & McNaughton, 1994, is thought to drive the gradual strengthening of cortical representations). The standard model has been challenged by the **multiple trace theory** (Nadel & Moscovitch, 1997) and the **trace transformation theory** (Winocur & Moscovitch, 2011), which propose that the hippocampus is always involved in the retrieval of episodic memories — even very remote ones — and that the distinction between hippocampal-dependent and cortical-dependent memories is between rich, contextually detailed episodic memories and schematic, gist-like semantic memories, not between recent and remote memories per se.

**Working memory** — the system that holds and manipulates information in mind over short periods (seconds) — is critical for reasoning, comprehension, and the guidance of behaviour. The **Baddeley-Hitch model** (Baddeley & Hitch, 1974; Baddeley, 2000, 2012) proposes a multi-component system: the **phonological loop** (stores and rehearses verbal information — the "inner voice"), the **visuospatial sketchpad** (stores and manipulates visual and spatial information — the "inner eye"), the **central executive** (an attentional control system that coordinates the slave systems, allocates attention, and manages the interface between working memory and long-term memory), and the **episodic buffer** (a limited-capacity, multimodal store that integrates information from the slave systems and long-term memory into coherent episodes). The neural substrates of working memory include: the **dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC)** — critical for the maintenance and manipulation of information, particularly under conditions of distraction; the **ventrolateral prefrontal cortex (vlPFC)** — involved in the retrieval and selection of information from long-term memory; and the **posterior parietal cortex** — involved in the storage of information in the visuospatial sketchpad. The **persistent activity** of dlPFC neurons during the delay period of a working-memory task — the neuron fires during the presentation of the stimulus, continues to fire during the delay while the information must be held in mind, and stops firing after the response — is thought to be the neural basis of the active maintenance of information in working memory (Fuster & Alexander, 1971; Goldman-Rakic, 1995). However, alternative models propose that working memory is maintained by **activity-silent mechanisms** — rapid, transient changes in synaptic weights that can be read out by a non-specific attentional signal (the **synaptic theory of working memory** — Mongillo, Barak & Tsodyks, 2008; Stokes, 2015) — and the debate between the persistent-activity and activity-silent models is one of the most active in contemporary cognitive neuroscience.

## Lecture 6: Language — The Neural Organisation of Speech, Meaning, and Grammar

Language is the most distinctively human of cognitive capacities — no other species possesses a communication system with the generativity, structure-dependence, and expressive power of human language. The cognitive neuroscience of language has been dominated since the 19th century by the **classical model** — Broca's area (left inferior frontal gyrus) for speech production, Wernicke's area (left superior temporal gyrus) for speech comprehension, and the arcuate fasciculus (the white-matter tract connecting them) for the repetition of speech — and by the **lesion-deficit method**: the language function of a brain region is inferred from the deficits produced by damage to that region. The classical model is elegant, historically important, and — in its broad outlines — correct. But it is also a substantial oversimplification: language processing involves a distributed network of cortical and subcortical regions, extending far beyond the classical perisylvian language areas, and the functional organisation of this network is more complex — and more dynamic — than the classical model suggests.

**Paul Broca's** (1861) description of the patient "Tan" — who could understand language but could produce only a single syllable ("tan"), and whose brain at autopsy revealed a lesion in the left inferior frontal gyrus — established the principle of lateralisation (language is primarily a left-hemisphere function) and the localisation of speech production. **Carl Wernicke's** (1874) description of patients who could speak fluently but incomprehensibly, and who could not understand spoken or written language, and whose brains showed lesions in the left superior temporal gyrus — established the localisation of speech comprehension and the concept of **disconnection syndromes**: damage to the connections between regions can produce deficits that are not attributable to the loss of either region alone. The classical model — Broca's area for production, Wernicke's area for comprehension, the arcuate fasciculus for repetition — was elaborated by Lichtheim (1885) and Geschwind (1965) into the **Wernicke-Lichtheim-Geschwind model**, which dominated clinical neurology and cognitive neuropsychology for most of the 20th century.

The **contemporary model** of the neural organisation of language — informed by functional neuroimaging, electrocorticography (recording directly from the cortical surface in patients undergoing surgery for epilepsy or tumour), and the critical re-examination of the classical aphasia syndromes — reveals a more complex picture. Language processing involves a **dual-stream architecture**, analogous to the two visual streams (Hickok & Poeppel, 2004, 2007). The **ventral stream** (middle and inferior temporal gyri, extending to the anterior temporal lobe) maps sound onto meaning — it is the "what" pathway for speech, involved in lexical access and semantic processing. The **dorsal stream** (posterior superior temporal gyrus, extending via the arcuate fasciculus to the posterior inferior frontal gyrus — Broca's area — and the premotor cortex) maps sound onto articulatory representations — it is the "how" pathway for speech, involved in phonological processing and speech production. The division of labour between the streams is not absolute — they interact extensively — but the dual-stream framework provides a more adequate account of the functional anatomy of language than the classical model.

The **neurobiology of semantics** — the representation of word meaning — has been a topic of intense research and debate. The **sensorimotor (embodied) semantics** hypothesis proposes that word meaning is grounded in the sensorimotor systems that are engaged by the referents of words: the word "kick" activates the motor cortex representations of the leg; the word "red" activates the colour-processing regions of the visual cortex; the word "telephone" activates the auditory cortex. This hypothesis is supported by neuroimaging studies showing category-specific activation patterns (words denoting actions activate the motor cortex; words denoting visual features activate the visual cortex — Pulvermüller, 2005; Hauk, Johnsrude & Pulvermüller, 2004) and by TMS studies showing that stimulation of the motor cortex facilitates the processing of action words. But the hypothesis has been challenged: the effects are small, are not consistently replicated, and may reflect post-comprehension imagery rather than the core representation of meaning. The alternative — the **amodal (symbolic) semantics** hypothesis — proposes that word meaning is represented in an abstract, symbolic format in the anterior temporal lobe, a "hub" that integrates information from modality-specific "spokes" (Patterson, Nestor & Rogers, 2007; Lambon Ralph, Jefferies, Patterson & Rogers, 2017). The hub-and-spoke model is supported by the study of **semantic dementia** — a form of frontotemporal dementia characterised by progressive, pan-category loss of semantic knowledge, associated with atrophy of the anterior temporal lobe — and by TMS studies showing that stimulation of the anterior temporal lobe impairs semantic processing across modalities. The debate between embodied and amodal semantics is one of the most active and consequential in cognitive neuroscience, with implications for theories of concepts, the relationship between language and perception/action, and the architecture of the semantic system.

**Dyslexia** — a specific impairment in reading that cannot be attributed to inadequate instruction, sensory impairment, or general intellectual disability — has been studied extensively as a window into the neural basis of reading. The dominant theory is the **phonological deficit hypothesis**: dyslexia arises from a core deficit in phonological processing — the ability to segment, manipulate, and represent the sound structure of speech — which impairs the learning of grapheme-phoneme correspondences (the mapping between letters and sounds) that is the foundation of reading. Neuroimaging studies of dyslexia have identified: (a) underactivation of the left temporoparietal and occipitotemporal regions (the "reading network") during reading tasks; (b) abnormalities in the white-matter tracts (particularly the arcuate fasciculus) that connect these regions; and (c) reduced or absent activation of the **visual word form area (VWFA)** — a region of the left fusiform gyrus that responds selectively to written words and that is thought to be the gateway to the reading network (Dehaene & Cohen, 2011). The VWFA is not innately specialised for reading — reading is a cultural invention, too recent for dedicated neural circuitry to have evolved — but is a region of the ventral visual stream, originally involved in object and face recognition, that is "recycled" for the processing of written words through the experience of learning to read (the **neuronal recycling hypothesis** — Dehaene, 2009). The discovery that the VWFA is activated by reading in literate adults and not in illiterate adults, that its development can be tracked longitudinally in children learning to read, and that its dysfunction is associated with dyslexia is one of the triumphs of cognitive neuroscience — a demonstration that cultural experience shapes brain function in specific, predictable, and consequential ways.

## Lecture 7: Executive Functions and the Prefrontal Cortex

The **prefrontal cortex (PFC)** — the most anterior portion of the frontal lobes — is the brain region most expanded in humans relative to other primates, and it is the neural substrate of the most distinctively human cognitive capacities: planning, reasoning, decision-making, cognitive control, and the regulation of thought, emotion, and behaviour in accordance with internal goals. Damage to the PFC does not impair basic sensory, motor, or cognitive functions — the patient with PFC damage can see, hear, move, remember, and speak — but it impairs the capacity to organise these functions in the service of long-term goals, to adapt behaviour to changing circumstances, and to regulate impulses and emotions in accordance with social norms and personal values. The classic case is **Phineas Gage** — the 19th-century railway foreman whose frontal lobes were penetrated by a tamping iron in an explosion, and whose personality was transformed from responsible, socially adept, and temperate to impulsive, profane, and incapable of following through on plans (Harlow, 1868; Damasio, Grabowski, Frank, Galaburda & Damasio, 1994).

**Executive functions** — also called **cognitive control** — are the set of processes that enable goal-directed behaviour: (a) **working memory** — the active maintenance and manipulation of information in mind (see Lecture 5); (b) **inhibitory control** — the suppression of prepotent, automatic, or impulsive responses in favour of goal-appropriate ones (the classic measure is the **Stroop task** — naming the ink colour of a colour word when the word and the colour are incongruent — "RED" printed in green — requires inhibiting the automatic tendency to read the word, and the cost of this inhibition — the Stroop effect — is a measure of cognitive control); (c) **cognitive flexibility** — the ability to shift between tasks, mental sets, or strategies in response to changing demands (the **Wisconsin Card Sorting Test** — the participant must sort cards according to a rule — colour, shape, number — that changes without warning, and the participant must discover the new rule — is the classic measure; patients with PFC lesions perseverate — they continue to sort by the old rule despite feedback that it is wrong); and (d) **planning and decision-making** — the ability to formulate a sequence of actions to achieve a goal, to evaluate alternatives, and to choose among them (the **Tower of London** task — the participant must rearrange a set of coloured balls on pegs to match a target configuration, in the minimum number of moves — is a measure of planning, and patients with PFC lesions are impaired).

The PFC is not a homogeneous structure — it consists of multiple, functionally distinct regions. The **dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC)** is critical for working memory, planning, and the top-down control of attention. It is the region most consistently activated by demanding cognitive tasks, and its dysfunction is implicated in the cognitive deficits of schizophrenia, ADHD, and depression. The **ventrolateral prefrontal cortex (vlPFC)** is involved in the retrieval and selection of information from memory, the inhibition of irrelevant or inappropriate responses, and — in the left hemisphere — aspects of language processing (semantic retrieval, phonological processing). The **orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)** and **ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC)** are involved in the evaluation of reward and punishment, the representation of the expected value of outcomes, and the regulation of emotion and social behaviour. Patients with OFC/vmPFC damage — the most famous is **Phineas Gage**, but the condition has been studied systematically in the **Iowa Gambling Task** (Bechara, Damasio, Damasio & Anderson, 1994) — make impulsive, shortsighted decisions that prioritise immediate reward over long-term consequences, fail to learn from negative feedback, and exhibit impaired social behaviour (disinhibition, inappropriate familiarity, disregard for social conventions). The **anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)** — particularly the dorsal ACC — is involved in the monitoring of conflict (between competing responses, between expected and actual outcomes, between goals and performance) and the signalling of the need for increased cognitive control. The ACC is activated by errors (the **error-related negativity — ERN**, an ERP component that peaks approximately 100 ms after an error, generated by the ACC), by response conflict (the simultaneous activation of incompatible responses), and by the anticipation of cognitively demanding tasks.

The **unity and diversity of executive functions** is an ongoing debate. Factor-analytic studies (Miyake et al., 2000; Friedman & Miyake, 2017) suggest that executive functions are both **diverse** — working memory updating, inhibition, and shifting are separable at the latent-variable level — and **unified** — they share a common factor (often interpreted as the maintenance and management of goals, or the capacity for active, controlled processing). The diversity implies that executive functions are not a single faculty and that impairments in one executive function do not necessarily imply impairments in others (a person may have poor inhibitory control but intact working memory). The unity implies that there is a general factor — common executive function — that contributes to performance across all executive tasks, and that is highly heritable (h² ≈ 90% in some studies), stable across the lifespan, and predictive of real-world outcomes (academic achievement, occupational success, health behaviour, and — in one longitudinal study — criminal offending, with an effect size comparable to that of IQ and socioeconomic status — Moffitt et al., 2011).

The **rostro-caudal gradient** of the PFC — the proposal that the PFC is organised hierarchically along its anterior-posterior axis, with more anterior regions representing more abstract, temporally extended, and complex goals — is an influential framework for understanding PFC organisation (Koechlin & Summerfield, 2007; Badre & Nee, 2018). The caudal PFC (posterior frontal, near the motor cortex) represents concrete, immediate actions (press the left button); the mid-PFC represents intermediate goals and task sets (sort by colour); the rostral PFC (anterior PFC, the most anterior region of the frontal lobe) represents the most abstract, long-term goals and the context in which tasks are performed (participate in the experiment, earn course credit). The gradient is supported by neuroimaging studies showing that more anterior PFC regions are activated by tasks that require more abstract, higher-level control, and by the finding that the structural and functional connectivity of the PFC — how it connects to the rest of the brain — maps onto this gradient.

## Lecture 8: Social Cognitive Neuroscience — The Neural Basis of Social Cognition

Human beings are the most social of the primates — we live in complex, multi-layered social networks, we cooperate and compete with extraordinary sophistication, we attribute mental states to others (and to ourselves), and our brains have been shaped by the demands of social life to an extent that is only now being appreciated. **Social cognitive neuroscience** — the study of the neural mechanisms underlying social cognition — has been one of the most rapidly growing and productive areas of cognitive neuroscience since the early 2000s, and it has illuminated the neural basis of theory of mind, empathy, moral judgement, self-knowledge, and the uniquely human capacity to navigate the social world.

**Theory of mind (ToM)** — also called **mentalising** or **mindreading** — is the capacity to attribute mental states (beliefs, desires, intentions, emotions) to others and to understand that others' mental states may differ from one's own. The classical test of ToM is the **false-belief task** (Wimmer & Perner, 1983): the child watches as a character (Sally) places an object (a marble) in a basket and leaves the room; another character (Anne) moves the marble to a box; Sally returns; the child is asked where Sally will look for the marble. Children below about 4 years of age typically say that Sally will look in the box (where the marble really is), failing to appreciate that Sally holds a false belief. Children above 4 typically pass the task, demonstrating that they can represent another person's mental state, even when it conflicts with reality. The development of ToM — from the early precursors (joint attention, pretend play, the understanding of desire and intention) through the emergence of false-belief understanding to the sophisticated mentalising of adult social interaction — is a central topic of developmental psychology, and its disruption (or delay) is a core feature of autism spectrum disorder.

The **neural basis of ToM** involves a network of regions — often called the **mentalising network** or the **default mode network** (because these regions are active at rest and deactivate during externally focused cognitive tasks): the **medial prefrontal cortex (mPFC)** (particularly the dorsal mPFC — involved in thinking about the mental states of others who are dissimilar to oneself, and in the integration of social information with self-knowledge), the **temporoparietal junction (TPJ)** (particularly the right TPJ — involved in the attribution of mental states and in the distinction between self and other), the **posterior cingulate cortex (PCC)** and **precuneus** (involved in self-referential processing and the retrieval of autobiographical memories that may inform social cognition), and the **anterior temporal lobe** (involved in the representation of social knowledge — scripts, schemas, person knowledge). The involvement of the DMN in social cognition is striking: the brain network that is most active when we are "at rest" — not engaged in any external task — is the same network that we use to think about other people and about ourselves. This suggests that social cognition is the brain's default mode — what the brain does when it is not doing anything else is to think about the social world.

**Empathy** — the capacity to share and understand the emotional states of others — has been studied using paradigms that elicit empathic responses (watching another person in pain, observing emotional facial expressions, reading emotional narratives) and measuring the neural response. The **anterior insula (AI)** and **anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)** — regions involved in the perception of one's own bodily states (interoception) and in the subjective experience of pain and emotion — are consistently activated both when a person experiences pain and when they observe another person experiencing pain (Singer et al., 2004; Lamm, Decety & Singer, 2011). The **inferior frontal gyrus (IFG)** and **inferior parietal lobule (IPL)** — the human mirror neuron system — are activated both when a person performs an action and when they observe another person performing the same action, and they are thought to be involved in the simulation of others' actions and emotions that underlies empathy. However, empathy is not a simple, automatic resonance — it is modulated by: the **perceived fairness** of the other person (empathy for pain is reduced when the person in pain has behaved unfairly — Singer et al., 2006), **group membership** (empathy is stronger for ingroup than outgroup members — Xu, Zuo, Wang & Han, 2009), and **contextual factors** (the presence of others, the perceived responsibility of the person for their suffering). The modulation of empathy by social factors suggests that empathy is not a fixed, automatic response but a flexible, context-sensitive process that is shaped by the perceiver's motivations, beliefs, and values.

**Self-knowledge** — the representation of one's own personality, abilities, preferences, and history — involves the **cortical midline structures (CMS)**: the mPFC, the PCC/precuneus, and the ACC. These regions are consistently activated when people reflect on their own traits and preferences, when they retrieve autobiographical memories, and when they make judgements about themselves. The mPFC, in particular, has been the focus of research on the self: it is activated by self-referential processing (thinking about whether a trait describes oneself), it shows a gradient of self-relatedness (more ventral regions are involved in the processing of self-relevant information; more dorsal regions in the processing of other-relevant information — Denny, Kober, Wager & Ochsner, 2012), and its activity predicts the extent to which people incorporate feedback into their self-concept. The overlap between the neural systems involved in self-knowledge and those involved in theory of mind — both engage the mPFC, the PCC, and the TPJ — suggests that the representation of self and the representation of others are not entirely distinct but share common neural resources, and that the development of self-knowledge may be scaffolded by the mechanisms that evolved for social cognition — a hypothesis with implications for understanding disorders of self (depersonalisation, schizophrenia, borderline personality disorder) and for the philosophical problem of self-knowledge.

## Lecture 9: The Cognitive Neuroscience of Emotion and Motivation

Emotion and motivation are not separate from cognition — they are integral to it. The traditional view — from Plato through Descartes to the mid-20th century — held that emotion and reason are opposing forces, and that the good life consists in the subordination of emotion to reason. The cognitive neuroscience of the past three decades has overturned this view: emotion and motivation are not disruptors of rational thought but essential contributors to it, and the neural systems that process emotion and motivation are deeply integrated with the systems that process perception, memory, and decision-making.

The **amygdala** — a small, almond-shaped structure in the medial temporal lobe — is the most extensively studied brain region in affective neuroscience. The amygdala is critical for the processing of emotional stimuli, particularly those that signal threat: it is activated by fearful faces, by conditioned fear stimuli, and by stimuli that are novel, ambiguous, or salient; its activation is rapid (within 100–200 ms of stimulus presentation — the "low road" from the thalamus to the amygdala, bypassing the cortex — LeDoux, 1996), automatic (it can be activated by stimuli that are presented subliminally or that are not attended), and associated with the modulation of memory (emotionally arousing events are better remembered than neutral events, and this enhancement is mediated by the amygdala's modulation of the hippocampus — the **emotional enhancement of memory** — Cahill & McGaugh, 1998). The classic demonstration of the amygdala's role in fear comes from studies of **fear conditioning**: a neutral stimulus (a tone) is paired with an aversive stimulus (a shock); after conditioning, the tone alone elicits a fear response (freezing, increased heart rate, potentiated startle). Lesions of the amygdala abolish fear conditioning; neuroimaging studies show amygdala activation during conditioning; and the neural circuitry of fear conditioning — the convergence of CS and US information in the lateral amygdala, the plasticity (LTP) that strengthens the CS-US association, and the projection from the central amygdala to brainstem and hypothalamic targets that produce the fear response — is the best-understood circuit in the mammalian brain (LeDoux, 2000; Maren, 2001; Fanselow & Poulos, 2005).

The **prefrontal regulation of emotion** is the neural basis of emotion regulation (see PS207, Lecture 8). The **ventromedial prefrontal cortex (vmPFC)** and **orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)** are involved in the **extinction** of conditioned fear — when the CS is repeatedly presented without the US, the fear response diminishes, and this extinction depends on the vmPFC (which inhibits the amygdala). The failure of extinction — the persistence of fear despite the absence of threat — is a hallmark of anxiety disorders, and the dysfunction of the vmPFC-amygdala circuit is implicated in their pathophysiology. The **dorsolateral prefrontal cortex (dlPFC)** is involved in the deliberate, effortful regulation of emotion — when a person is instructed to reappraise a negative stimulus ("think about it in a way that reduces its emotional impact"), dlPFC activation increases, and amygdala activation decreases. The **anterior cingulate cortex (ACC)** — particularly the subgenual ACC (sACC) — is involved in the modulation of mood; it is hyperactive in depression (and normalises with successful treatment), and it is the target of deep brain stimulation (DBS) for treatment-resistant depression.

The **reward system** — the neural circuitry that processes reward, pleasure, and motivation — centres on the **mesolimbic dopamine pathway** from the ventral tegmental area (VTA) to the nucleus accumbens (NAc) in the ventral striatum, with projections to the prefrontal cortex (the mesocortical pathway) and the amygdala. The dopamine signal — as described in PS203, Lectures 2–3 — encodes **reward prediction error**: dopamine neurons fire when a reward is better than expected (positive prediction error), are silent when the reward is exactly as expected (no prediction error), and decrease their firing when the reward is worse than expected (negative prediction error). This signal is the neural implementation of the teaching signal of reinforcement learning models, and it is the foundation of the computational neuroscience of learning and decision-making. The reward system is activated by: primary rewards (food, sex, social interaction), secondary rewards (money, praise, status), and — pathologically — by drugs of abuse (which produce supraphysiological dopamine release, "hijacking" the reward system — see PS201, Lecture 8). The **orbitofrontal cortex (OFC)** represents the **expected value** of outcomes — the subjective value that the person assigns to a potential reward, integrating multiple dimensions (magnitude, probability, delay, effort) into a common currency — and the OFC is critical for decision-making: patients with OFC damage make impulsive, shortsighted choices that fail to take account of long-term consequences (the Iowa Gambling Task — Bechara et al., 1994).

The **integration of emotion and cognition** — the recognition that they are not separate systems but deeply interdependent — is one of the most important insights of contemporary cognitive neuroscience. Emotion influences: **attention** (emotional stimuli capture attention — the "emotional Stroop" effect — it takes longer to name the ink colour of emotionally charged words), **perception** (fear enhances the perception of threat-related stimuli — a fearful face is detected faster than a neutral face), **memory** (emotionally arousing events are better remembered — the amygdala modulates hippocampal consolidation), and **decision-making** (the **somatic marker hypothesis** — Damasio, 1994 — proposes that emotional signals — "gut feelings" — guide decision-making, and that the OFC is critical for the generation and use of these signals). Conversely, cognition influences emotion: appraisal processes determine which emotions are elicited by an event (Lazarus, 1991), and emotion regulation strategies (reappraisal, suppression, distraction) modulate the intensity and duration of emotional responses (Gross, 2015).

## Lecture 10: Consciousness — The Hard Problem and the Search for Neural Correlates

Consciousness — subjective experience, the "what-it-is-like-ness" of being — is the most familiar and the most mysterious phenomenon in the universe. It is the medium of all experience, the precondition of all knowledge, and — as the philosopher David Chalmers (1995) famously argued — the "hard problem" of science. The **easy problems** of consciousness (Chalmers's term, somewhat tongue-in-cheek — they are not easy, but they are, in principle, tractable by standard scientific methods) are to explain the neural mechanisms that underlie specific conscious functions: the difference between wakefulness and sleep, the ability to report on the contents of experience, the integration of information across sensory modalities, the control of behaviour by conscious states. The **hard problem** is to explain why and how these neural mechanisms give rise to subjective experience — why there is "something it is like" to be a conscious organism, rather than nothing at all. The cognitive neuroscience of consciousness has made substantial progress on the easy problems; the hard problem remains hard.

The **neural correlates of consciousness (NCC)** — the minimal set of neural events sufficient for a specific conscious percept — have been sought using a variety of paradigms. **Binocular rivalry**: when different images are presented to the two eyes, perception alternates between them — the physical stimulus is constant, but the conscious percept changes. By contrasting the neural activity when the participant perceives one image with the activity when they perceive the other, researchers can identify the neural activity that correlates with the content of consciousness, independent of the physical stimulus (Logothetis, 1998; Tong, Meng & Blake, 2006). The results suggest that the NCC for visual stimuli involves the ventral visual stream (particularly the fusiform gyrus and the inferior temporal cortex) and the prefrontal-parietal network, though the relative contributions of these regions — and whether prefrontal activation is a correlate, a consequence, or a prerequisite of conscious perception — remain controversial. **Continuous flash suppression (CFS)** — a variant of binocular rivalry in which one eye is presented with a static image and the other with a dynamic, high-contrast pattern that suppresses the static image from awareness for extended periods — has been used to study unconscious processing: stimuli that are not consciously perceived nonetheless activate the visual cortex (including, in some studies, the fusiform face area and the parahippocampal place area), demonstrating that substantial neural processing occurs outside of awareness.

The major **theories of consciousness** compete for explanatory supremacy in 2040. **Global workspace theory (GWT)** (Baars, 1988; Dehaene & Changeux, 2011; Dehaene, 2014) proposes that consciousness arises when information is "broadcast" — made globally available — to a distributed network of brain regions, particularly the prefrontal-parietal network. Unconscious processing is modular — information is processed in specialised, encapsulated systems (the visual system processes shapes and colours; the auditory system processes sounds) without being accessible to other systems. Conscious processing occurs when information is selected by attention and amplified into the global workspace, where it can be accessed by a wide range of cognitive systems — memory, language, decision-making, motor planning — enabling the flexible, context-sensitive control of behaviour. GWT is supported by neuroimaging studies showing that conscious (vs. unconscious) perception is associated with: (a) sustained, high-amplitude activation in the prefrontal-parietal network; (b) increased functional connectivity among distant brain regions; and (c) the **P3b** — a late, positive ERP component at approximately 300–600 ms that is thought to index global ignition.

**Integrated information theory (IIT)** (Tononi, 2004, 2008; Oizumi, Albantakis & Tononi, 2014) takes a more radical approach: consciousness is not a function that the brain performs but an intrinsic property of certain kinds of information processing. IIT proposes that consciousness is **integrated information** — information that is generated by a system that cannot be reduced to the information generated by its parts. The quantity of consciousness — Φ (phi) — is the amount of integrated information generated by the system; the quality of consciousness is specified by the "shape" of the integrated information — the particular set of distinctions and relations that constitute the experience. IIT makes predictions that, in principle, can be tested: high Φ should be associated with consciousness; low Φ with unconsciousness (sleep, anaesthesia, coma); and Φ should be high in systems that are integrated and differentiated (the waking brain) and low in systems that are fragmented (the anaesthetised brain) or homogeneous (a digital camera, which processes information but with low integration). IIT has been praised for its mathematical rigour and its ambition — it is the only theory that attempts to explain, not merely to correlate, the relationship between brain activity and consciousness — but it has been criticised for its computational intractability (Φ cannot be calculated exactly for systems larger than a few neurons), its counterintuitive predictions (a grid of simple logic gates could, in principle, have high Φ and therefore be conscious, which most people find implausible), and the difficulty of testing its predictions empirically.

**Predictive processing** (Friston, 2010; Clark, 2013, 2033; Hohwy, 2013) — introduced in Lecture 3 — offers a third perspective: consciousness is the process of inferring the causes of sensory input, and the contents of consciousness are the brain's best hypotheses about the world. Conscious perception is not a passive reflection of sensory input but an active construction — a "controlled hallucination" — that is constrained by prediction errors. The quality of consciousness — what it feels like — reflects the structure of the brain's generative model: the dimensions of experience (space, time, objecthood, selfhood) are the dimensions along which the brain predicts and infers. Predictive processing has the advantage of integrating consciousness with the broader framework of Bayesian brain function, and it provides a natural account of psychopathology — hallucinations and delusions are the consequences of aberrant precision-weighting (the brain assigns too much weight to its predictions relative to sensory evidence, or vice versa). But it has been criticised for not addressing the hard problem — it explains the contents of consciousness but not the existence of consciousness — and for being insufficiently constrained by neural data.

## Lecture 11: The Predictive Brain — From Perception to Action and Beyond

The **predictive processing** framework — also called the **free-energy principle** — has been the most influential integrative theory in cognitive neuroscience since the 2010s. Its core claim: the brain is fundamentally a **prediction engine** — a hierarchical generative model that continuously generates predictions about its sensory input and updates those predictions based on the prediction errors (the discrepancies between predicted and actual input) that are propagated forward through the hierarchy. Perception, action, attention, learning, and — in its most ambitious formulations — consciousness are all manifestations of the single, unifying imperative: to minimise prediction error (or, equivalently, to minimise free energy — an information-theoretic quantity that bounds the surprise of sensory input).

The **hierarchical generative model** is the computational architecture of the predictive brain. At each level of the cortical hierarchy, the brain encodes predictions about the activity at the level below. The predictions are propagated downward (top-down); the actual input from the level below is propagated upward (bottom-up); the difference — the prediction error — is computed and propagated upward, where it is used to update the predictions. The system settles into a state in which prediction errors are minimised across all levels — which is equivalent to the brain's having found the most probable explanation for its sensory input (the posterior distribution, in Bayesian terms). The hierarchy is crucial: lower levels encode predictions about simple features (edges, orientations, frequencies); higher levels encode predictions about increasingly abstract, invariant features (objects, faces, scenes, concepts); and the highest levels — the prefrontal cortex — encode predictions about the most abstract and temporally extended regularities (goals, plans, narratives). The predictive processing framework provides a unifying account of a wide range of neural and psychological phenomena, including: (a) **receptive fields** — the classical receptive field of a V1 neuron (it responds to an oriented edge at a specific location) is the stimulus feature that minimises prediction error for that neuron, given the prediction from the level above; (b) **attention** — attention is the process of increasing the **precision** (the inverse variance, or the reliability) of the prediction errors associated with the attended stimulus. By increasing the precision of some prediction errors (and, implicitly, decreasing the precision of others), attention ensures that the brain's model of the world is dominated by the sensory evidence that matters most for current goals (Feldman & Friston, 2010); (c) **action** — in the predictive processing framework, action is the process of minimising prediction error by changing the sensory input to match the predictions, rather than changing the predictions to match the input. When the brain predicts that the arm will be in a certain position, and the proprioceptive input indicates that it is not, the brain can minimise the prediction error either by updating the prediction (passive perception) or by moving the arm to the predicted position (active inference). In this view, action is not a separate system from perception but the same system operating in reverse — perception minimises prediction error by updating the model; action minimises prediction error by fulfilling the predictions; (d) **learning** — learning, in the predictive processing framework, is the optimisation of the parameters of the generative model (the synaptic weights) to minimise prediction error over the long term. Perception and action minimise prediction error in the moment (by optimising the current state estimates and by selecting actions that fulfil predictions); learning minimises prediction error over time by changing the structure of the model itself.

The **implications for psychopathology** are a major focus of predictive processing research in 2040. The **aberrant precision-weighting** hypothesis proposes that psychological disorders arise from disturbances in the balance between top-down predictions and bottom-up prediction errors. In **psychosis** (schizophrenia), the precision of prior beliefs is abnormally low, and the precision of sensory evidence is abnormally high — the brain is flooded with prediction errors that cannot be explained away by existing priors, leading to the updating of priors that would normally be stable (producing delusions — false beliefs that are resistant to evidence) and to the perception of patterns in noise (producing hallucinations — perceptions in the absence of external stimuli) (Fletcher & Frith, 2009; Corlett, Honey & Fletcher, 2016). In **autism**, the precision of sensory evidence is abnormally high, and the precision of prior beliefs is abnormally low — the world is experienced as a chaotic, overwhelming flux of sensory detail that cannot be integrated by high-level priors, producing the sensory hypersensitivity, insistence on sameness, and difficulty with social interaction (which requires the flexible updating of high-level priors about others' mental states) that characterise autism (Pellicano & Burr, 2012; Van de Cruys et al., 2014). In **depression**, the precision of negative prior beliefs is abnormally high — the brain's predictions are dominated by negative schemas that are resistant to updating by positive sensory evidence, producing the pervasive negative affect and the cognitive biases (attention to negative information, interpretation of ambiguous events as negative) that characterise depression (Barrett, Quigley & Hamilton, 2016; Clark, Watson & Friston, 2018). The computational psychiatry framework — the application of predictive processing principles to the understanding and treatment of mental disorders — is one of the most active and promising frontiers in clinical neuroscience.

The **free-energy principle (FEP)** — the most general formulation of the predictive processing framework — proposes that any self-organising system that maintains a boundary between itself and its environment (a cell, an organism, a brain) must minimise free energy — a quantity that bounds the surprise (the improbability) of its sensory states (Friston, 2010). Minimising free energy is equivalent to resisting the tendency to disorder (the second law of thermodynamics) by maintaining the system within the limited range of states that are compatible with its continued existence. The FEP is ambitious — it purports to unify perception, action, learning, evolution, and life itself under a single mathematical principle — and it has been both hailed as a "grand unified theory of brain function" and criticised as unfalsifiable, too general to be useful, and mathematically obscure. The Yggdrasil Theoretical Neuroscience Group takes the position that the FEP is a valuable guiding framework — it generates testable hypotheses, it integrates a wide range of phenomena, and it provides a common language for researchers across subfields — but that it is not (yet) a theory in the strict sense of making precise, quantitative predictions that could falsify it.

## Lecture 12: The Future of Cognitive Neuroscience — Neurotechnology, AI, and the Ethics of Brain Science

Cognitive neuroscience in 2040 stands at a threshold. The methods that have driven the field for the past four decades — fMRI, EEG, TMS, the study of lesion patients — have been refined to an extraordinary degree and have yielded a rich, if incomplete, map of the neural basis of cognition. But the field is being transformed by new technologies — brain-computer interfaces, optogenetics (in animal models), high-density electrophysiology (Neuropixels and their successors, recording from thousands of neurons simultaneously), and the integration of AI with neuroscience — that promise to move cognitive neuroscience from correlation to causation, from mapping to manipulation, and from description to mechanistic explanation. This lecture surveys the frontiers of the field and the ethical challenges they raise.

**Brain-computer interfaces (BCIs)** — devices that record neural activity and translate it into commands for external devices — have advanced from proof-of-concept demonstrations in the 2000s to clinically deployed devices in the 2040s. The Yggdrasil NeuroLink — an implantable BCI that records from the motor cortex and uses machine learning to decode intended movements — enables paralysed individuals to control robotic limbs, communicate through computer interfaces, and — in the case of one participant — to fly a drone using thought alone. The technology is transformative for individuals with severe motor impairment, but it raises ethical questions that extend beyond the clinical context: if BCIs can read intentions from the brain, what are the limits of neural privacy? If BCIs can write information into the brain (by electrically stimulating specific regions), what are the limits of neural autonomy? The Yggdrasil Neuroethics Board (established 2032) has developed guidelines for BCI research and clinical use: **neural data are personal data** (subject to the same protections as other sensitive personal information); **neural autonomy must be respected** (individuals have the right to control their own neural activity and to refuse neural intervention); and **access to neurotechnology should be equitable** (the benefits of BCI should not be limited to those who can afford them).

**AI and neuroscience** are increasingly integrated. **Deep neural networks** — the architecture underlying the AI revolution of the 2010s–2040s — were originally inspired by the hierarchical, parallel architecture of the visual system, and they have, in turn, become the most powerful models of brain function available. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) achieve human-level performance on object recognition tasks and exhibit patterns of activity that closely match the ventral visual stream (Khaligh-Razavi & Kriegeskorte, 2014; Yamins et al., 2014). Recurrent neural networks (RNNs) and transformer architectures capture aspects of working memory, language processing, and sequential decision-making. The use of AI models as **models of brain function** — testing whether a model that performs a cognitive task does so using the same computational principles as the brain — is a major research programme, and the Yggdrasil AI-Neuroscience Lab's "Model-Brain Convergence" project (2038–2048) systematically compares the representations learned by AI models with those measured in the brain. The integration of AI with neuroscience also enables: **brain decoding** — the reconstruction of perceived or imagined stimuli from patterns of brain activity (using AI to learn the mapping from brain activity to stimulus features); **brain-computer interfaces** (AI decodes the user's intentions from neural signals); and **closed-loop neurostimulation** (AI analyses real-time neural activity and adjusts the stimulation parameters to optimise the therapeutic effect — used in DBS for Parkinson's disease and depression, and in responsive neurostimulation — RNS — for epilepsy).

The **ethics of cognitive neuroscience** extend beyond BCIs to encompass: (a) **neural privacy** — as brain-decoding technology advances, the boundary between the private contents of the mind and the publicly observable neural signals that encode them is eroding. What protections should neural data have? Should law enforcement be permitted to use brain-based lie detection? Should employers or insurers be permitted to use neural data to predict performance or risk? The Yggdrasil position is that neural data are the most intimate of personal data, and that their collection, storage, and use should require explicit, informed consent and should be subject to the highest standards of privacy protection; (b) **cognitive enhancement** — TMS, tDCS, pharmacological agents (modafinil, methylphenidate, psychedelics in sub-therapeutic doses), and BCIs can enhance cognitive function in healthy individuals. Is cognitive enhancement ethically permissible? Is it a legitimate form of self-improvement, analogous to education and exercise, or is it a form of cheating that undermines the value of effort and the fairness of competition? The Yggdrasil position is that cognitive enhancement is not intrinsically unethical but should be evaluated case by case, with attention to safety, fairness, coercion (e.g., pressure on employees or students to enhance), and the effects on the distribution of social goods; (c) **the neuroscience of responsibility** — as the neural mechanisms of decision-making, impulse control, and moral reasoning are elucidated, the legal and moral notion of responsibility is challenged. If a criminal act is associated with a brain abnormality that impairs impulse control, is the person fully responsible? Partially responsible? Not responsible at all? The Yggdrasil NeuroLaw Project (established 2034) — a collaboration between the Departments of Psychology, Law, and Philosophy — conducts research on the intersection of neuroscience and criminal responsibility and provides expert testimony and policy guidance.

The final message of this course — and of the cognitive neuroscience sequence — is that the study of the brain is not merely an academic pursuit. It is a journey into the biological basis of who we are — our perceptions, our memories, our decisions, our emotions, our selves. The knowledge you have gained in this course is provisional and incomplete — much of what is taught will be revised, refined, or overturned in the decades ahead — but the habits of mind you have cultivated — the capacity to reason about brain-behaviour relationships, to evaluate evidence critically, to recognise both the power and the limits of neuroscientific methods — will endure. The brain is the most complex, the most beautiful, and the most mysterious object in the known universe. Treat it with the wonder it deserves.

### Written Examination Format
The written examination is three hours, closed-book. You will be asked to answer **four of eight** essay questions. Each essay should demonstrate: (a) accurate understanding of the relevant neural systems, methods, and theoretical frameworks; (b) ability to integrate cognitive and neural levels of analysis; (c) critical engagement with the strengths and limitations of cognitive neuroscience methods and theories; and (d) clear, well-organised, and evidence-supported writing. **Sample essay questions**:
1. Compare and contrast the methods of fMRI and EEG/MEG. What does each method reveal about brain function that the other does not? In what circumstances would you choose one over the other, and why?
2. The standard model of memory consolidation proposes that memories become independent of the hippocampus over time. Critically evaluate the evidence for and against this model, with reference to both human and animal studies.
3. The dual-stream model of language (Hickok & Poeppel, 2007) proposes a ventral "what" pathway and a dorsal "how" pathway for speech processing. Discuss the evidence for this model and compare it to the classical Wernicke-Lichtheim-Geschwind model. What does the dual-stream model explain that the classical model does not?
4. The predictive processing framework proposes that perception, action, attention, and learning are all manifestations of the single imperative to minimise prediction error. Discuss the evidence for and against this framework. Is it a genuine theoretical unification, or does it redescribe known phenomena in new language?
5. The prefrontal cortex is argued to be organised along a rostro-caudal gradient of abstraction — more anterior regions represent more abstract, temporally extended goals. Critically evaluate this proposal with reference to neuroimaging, lesion, and computational evidence.
6. The neural basis of theory of mind overlaps substantially with the default mode network. What does this overlap suggest about the function of the brain "at rest"? What are the implications for understanding autism?
7. Global workspace theory, integrated information theory, and predictive processing offer competing accounts of consciousness. Compare any two of these theories, and discuss their strengths, limitations, and empirical support.
8. Brain-computer interfaces, brain decoding, and cognitive enhancement raise profound ethical challenges. Discuss one of these technologies in depth — its current capabilities, its potential benefits and harms, and the ethical principles that should govern its development and use.

Over the course of the semester, you will complete a neuroimaging data analysis project using curated datasets from the Yggdrasil Neuroimaging Data Platform.
