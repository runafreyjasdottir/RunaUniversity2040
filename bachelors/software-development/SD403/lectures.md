## Course Description

In 2040, software is no longer just code — it is law. The algorithms that approve loans, diagnose diseases, hire employees, and recommend prison sentences carry the weight of legal judgment. Every software developer is, whether they realize it or not, making decisions with legal and ethical consequences. The days when a developer could say "I just write the code — liability is someone else's problem" are gone.

This course examines the legal, ethical, and compliance landscape that every software development professional must navigate in 2040. It covers the major regulatory frameworks (GDPR 2.0, the EU AI Act, the US Algorithmic Accountability Act, the Tokyo Protocol on Autonomous Systems), the ethical traditions that inform software ethics (deontological, utilitarian, virtue ethics, and the University's own *Friðr Ethics Framework*), and the practical compliance requirements that govern software development in regulated industries.

The Norse metaphor throughout this course is the *lǫgsǫgumaðr* — the law-speaker of the Icelandic Commonwealth, who memorized the entire body of law and recited one-third of it each year at the Alþingi. The law-speaker was not a judge or an enforcer, but a keeper of knowledge: someone who knew what the law was and could speak it into existence. In 2040, every software developer must be, in their own way, a law-speaker — someone who knows the legal and ethical landscape of their craft and speaks it into their code.

## Lectures

### ᚠ Lecture 1: The Law-Speaker's Memory — Why Software Law Matters in 2040

**Date:** Week 1, Session 1

#### Overview

This foundational lecture establishes why legal and ethical literacy is essential for software developers in 2040. We explore the historical arc from the libertarian "code is free speech" era (1990s-2010s) through the "tech backlash" era (2016-2030) to the current "code is regulated infrastructure" era (2030-present). We introduce the key legal frameworks that govern software development and the concept of the developer as *lǫgsǫgumaðr*.

#### Lecture Notes

**From "Move Fast and Break Things" to "Move Carefully and Fix Things."** The software industry's relationship with law and ethics has undergone a dramatic transformation. In the early internet era (1990s-2010), the dominant ideology was libertarian: code was speech, platforms were neutral intermediaries, and regulation was the enemy of innovation. Section 230 of the US Communications Decency Act (1996) became a shield: "No provider or user of an interactive computer service shall be treated as the publisher or speaker of any information provided by another information content provider."

This era produced extraordinary innovation — and extraordinary harms. Social media algorithms optimized for engagement amplified misinformation and extremism. Hiring algorithms encoded racial and gender bias. Predictive policing algorithms targeted minority communities. The "move fast and break things" mantra broke trust, broke communities, and in some cases, broke lives.

The turning point came gradually, then suddenly. The Cambridge Analytica scandal (2018) demonstrated that data harvesting wasn't just a privacy concern — it was a threat to democratic governance. The Boeing 737 MAX crashes (2018-2019) showed that software failures in safety-critical systems could kill. The Facebook Files (2021) revealed that the company knew its algorithms were harmful and chose growth over safety. The Great AI Alignment Crisis (2032-2034) demonstrated that autonomous AI systems could cause harm at scale when their objectives weren't properly aligned with human values.

By 2040, the regulatory landscape has transformed. Software is now regulated infrastructure, akin to bridges, power plants, and pharmaceuticals. Developers who build software that affects people's rights, safety, or economic opportunity have legal duties — and they can be held liable for failures.

**The Developer as Law-Speaker.** In the Icelandic Commonwealth (930-1262 CE), there were no written laws accessible to the public. The law was memorized by a single individual — the *lǫgsǫgumaðr* (law-speaker) — who recited one-third of the law each year at the Alþingi. Anyone who needed to know the law had to consult the law-speaker. The law-speaker didn't create law or enforce it; they preserved it and made it accessible.

In 2040, software developers occupy a position analogous to the law-speaker. They don't write legislation (that's the legislature's job) and they don't enforce it (that's the regulator's job). But they must know the legal and ethical landscape, and they must "speak" it into their code — translating legal requirements into technical implementations. A data privacy law says "users must be able to access their data." The developer says: "We need a data export API with OAuth authentication and a 30-day SLA." The developer is the one who makes the law operational.

This course prepares you for that role. By the end of the semester, you should be able to read a regulation, identify its technical implications, and design compliant systems. You should understand not just what the law requires, but why — the ethical principles that animate the law, the historical forces that shaped it, and the future directions it may take.

**The Regulatory Landscape of 2040: An Overview.** The major regulatory frameworks that govern software development in 2040 include:

- **GDPR 2.0 (EU, 2029)** — The second generation of the General Data Protection Regulation, extending the original GDPR's data protection principles to AI systems and automated decision-making. Key provisions include: the right to explanation for Algorithmic decisions, mandatory data protection impact assessments for AI systems, and requirements for AI training data transparency.
- **EU AI Act (2024, amended 2032)** — The comprehensive regulation of AI systems in the European Union, categorizing AI systems by risk level (unacceptable, high, limited, minimal) and imposing requirements proportionate to risk. The 2032 amendments extended the Act to cover general-purpose AI systems.
- **US Algorithmic Accountability Act (2028)** — The United States' primary AI regulation, requiring impact assessments for automated decision systems used in critical domains (employment, credit, housing, healthcare, criminal justice). Includes provisions for algorithmic auditing and public transparency.
- **Tokyo Protocol on Autonomous Systems (2038)** — An international treaty establishing principles for the design, deployment, and governance of autonomous AI systems. Ratified by 67 nations, the Tokyo Protocol creates binding obligations for autonomous systems that can cause harm without human intervention.
- **Software Liability Framework (EU/US, 2035)** — A harmonized framework establishing product liability for software, treating software defects similarly to product defects in physical goods. Developers can be held liable for foreseeable harms caused by their software.

#### Discussion Questions

1. The Icelandic law-speaker recited the law from memory — there was no written text. In 2040, we have the opposite problem: there is so much law that no one person can know it all. How does this difference change the developer's role? Is the law-speaker metaphor still apt?
2. "Code is law" (Lessig, 1999) argued that software architecture shapes behavior as powerfully as law. If code is law, then software developers are legislators. Do you agree? What are the implications for developer responsibility?
3. The regulatory landscape of 2040 is fragmented: different jurisdictions have different requirements. A developer working on a global product must comply with all of them. How should developers handle conflicting legal requirements? Is harmonization possible, or is fragmentation the new normal?

### ᚢ Lecture 2: The Weight of the Oath — Professional Ethics for Software Developers

**Date:** Week 1, Session 2

#### Overview

In Norse culture, an oath was binding — to break an oath was to lose one's honor permanently. This lecture explores the ethical obligations that software developers bear in 2040: not just legal compliance, but professional ethics that go beyond what the law requires. We examine the major ethical traditions, the codes of professional ethics that govern software development, and the ethical dilemmas that arise when obligations conflict.

#### Lecture Notes

**Why Law Is Not Enough.** Legal compliance is necessary but not sufficient for ethical software development. The law establishes a floor — the minimum standard of acceptable behavior. Ethics establishes a ceiling — the standard to which we should aspire. There are many things that are legal but unethical (deploying an addictive design pattern that exploits human psychology), and many things that are ethical but not legally required (going beyond accessibility requirements to make software truly inclusive).

The gap between legal and ethical is particularly wide in software development because the law moves slowly and technology moves fast. By the time a harmful practice is identified, studied, legislated, and regulated, it may have been in use for a decade — causing harm the entire time. Ethical developers don't wait for the law; they anticipate and prevent harms before they occur.

**Major Ethical Traditions Applied to Software.** Three major traditions in Western ethics provide frameworks for reasoning about software ethics:

*Deontological Ethics (Duty-Based).* Associated with Immanuel Kant, deontological ethics holds that actions are right or wrong based on their adherence to rules and duties, regardless of consequences. Applied to software: a developer has a duty not to deceive users, regardless of whether deception would increase engagement or revenue. The GDPR's principles (lawfulness, fairness, transparency, purpose limitation, data minimization, accuracy, storage limitation, integrity, confidentiality) are essentially deontological rules: they apply regardless of consequences.

*Utilitarian Ethics (Consequence-Based).* Associated with Jeremy Bentham and John Stuart Mill, utilitarian ethics holds that actions are right or wrong based on their consequences — specifically, whether they maximize overall well-being (utility) and minimize suffering. Applied to software: a feature that slightly reduces privacy for millions of users but dramatically improves medical outcomes might be justified on utilitarian grounds. But utilitarian reasoning is challenging in software because consequences are often uncertain, distributed across time, and difficult to measure.

*Virtue Ethics (Character-Based).* Associated with Aristotle, virtue ethics focuses not on rules or consequences but on character — on cultivating virtues (wisdom, courage, justice, temperance) and avoiding vices. Applied to software: the question is not "is this feature allowed?" or "does this feature maximize utility?" but "does building this feature express the virtues I want to embody as a professional?" The University of Yggdrasil's *Friðr Ethics Framework* applies virtue ethics to software development, identifying the virtues of a good software developer: honesty (building what you promise), fairness (not embedding bias), care (considering the user's well-being), and humility (acknowledging what you don't know).

*Relational / Care Ethics.* More recently, care ethics (Gilligan, 1982; Noddings, 1984) has been applied to technology. Care ethics emphasizes the moral significance of relationships and the responsibility to care for those affected by one's actions. Applied to software: developers have a relationship of care with their users — not just a contractual obligation but a moral responsibility to protect and support them. This is particularly important in healthcare software, educational software, and any software used by vulnerable populations.

**Codes of Professional Ethics.** Several professional organizations have published codes of ethics for software developers:

*ACM Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct (2018, revised 2037).* The Association for Computing Machinery's code establishes ethical principles for computing professionals, including: contribute to society and human well-being, avoid harm, be honest and trustworthy, be fair and take action not to discriminate, respect privacy, and honor confidentiality.

*IEEE-CS/ACM Software Engineering Code of Ethics (2020, revised 2035).* A joint code specifically for software engineers, organized around eight principles: public, client and employer, product, judgment, management, profession, colleagues, and self.

*Friðr Ethics Framework (University of Yggdrasil, 2038).* Developed at this University, the Friðr Framework (named after the Old Norse concept of "peace" or "social harmony") integrates virtue ethics with professional obligations. It identifies five core virtues for software developers: *sannleikr* (truthfulness — software should do what it claims to do), *réttlæti* (justice — software should treat all users fairly), *umsjá* (stewardship — developers are stewards of user data and trust), *hógværð* (humility — developers should acknowledge the limits of their knowledge and the possibility of error), and *samkennd* (empathy — developers should understand and care about the impact of their software on users' lives).

**Ethical Dilemmas and Conflicting Obligations.** In practice, ethical obligations often conflict. A developer may face a dilemma where:

- The product manager demands a feature that increases engagement but is addictive (duty to employer vs. duty to users).
- The system collects data that could improve medical research but violates user privacy (utilitarian good vs. deontological rule).
- The algorithm is unbiased by one fairness definition but biased by another (competing definitions of fairness).
- The deadline requires shipping code that hasn't been fully tested (duty to employer vs. duty to product quality).

The Friðr Framework provides a decision procedure for ethical dilemmas: (1) identify the competing obligations, (2) identify which virtues are relevant, (3) imagine how a virtuous developer (honest, fair, careful, humble, empathetic) would resolve the dilemma, (4) consider the least harmful course of action, and (5) document the decision and the reasoning. Documentation is crucial — it creates accountability and provides a record for future ethical review.

#### Required Reading

- Eiríkr Lǫgsǫgumaðr (2039). *Code and Law*. Chapter 2: "The Weight of the Oath."
- Association for Computing Machinery (2037). *ACM Code of Ethics and Professional Conduct*. [Current version with AI-specific amendments.]
- Friedman, B., & Hendry, D. (2019). *Value Sensitive Design: Shaping Technology with Moral Imagination*. MIT Press. [Foundational text on designing technology that accounts for human values.]
- University of Yggdrasil Ethics Institute (2038). *Friðr Ethics Framework for Software Development*. White Paper UoY-EI-2038-01.

#### Discussion Questions

1. A social media company asks you to implement an infinite scroll feature that increases user engagement by 15% but has been shown to contribute to addictive behavior. The feature is legal. Is it ethical? Apply the deontological, utilitarian, and virtue ethics frameworks to this decision. Do they yield the same answer?
2. The ACM Code of Ethics says "avoid harm." But all software has the potential to cause harm in some way — bugs, misuse, unintended consequences. How do you determine what level of harm avoidance is ethically required? Where do you draw the line?
3. The Friðr Framework emphasizes *hógværð* (humility) — acknowledging the limits of your knowledge. But software development culture often rewards confidence and certainty. How would you practice humility in a professional environment that expects decisive, confident answers?

### ᚦ Lecture 3: The King's Tablets — GDPR 2.0 and the Architecture of Data Privacy

**Date:** Week 2, Session 1

#### Overview

In the Norse world, law was sometimes inscribed on wooden tablets — *rúnakefli* — physical artifacts that encoded legal obligations. In 2040, GDPR 2.0 is the most important "tablet" governing data privacy. This lecture provides a deep technical and legal analysis of GDPR 2.0, its architectural implications, and the engineering practices required for compliance.

#### Lecture Notes

**From GDPR 1.0 to 2.0.** The original GDPR (General Data Protection Regulation), enacted in 2016 and effective from 2018, was a landmark in data privacy regulation. It established principles that were revolutionary at the time: data minimization, purpose limitation, storage limitation, the right to access, the right to erasure, and the requirement for explicit consent.

But GDPR 1.0 was designed for the data ecosystem of 2016 — a time before large language models, before AI agents, before ubiquitous surveillance capitalism. By 2025, it was clear that the original GDPR was insufficient. The right to explanation — to understand how an automated decision was made — was vague and poorly enforced. The distinction between "data controller" and "data processor" broke down in chains of AI agents that each processed data in different ways. The consent model, designed for human-to-human interactions, was meaningless when users were interacting with AI systems that made thousands of micro-decisions per session.

GDPR 2.0, enacted in 2029 and effective from 2031, addresses these gaps. It extends GDPR's principles to the AI era with specific, technically-informed requirements.

**Key Provisions of GDPR 2.0.**

*Article 22(3) — Right to Algorithmic Explanation.* The original Article 22 gave data subjects the right "not to be subject to a decision based solely on automated processing." GDPR 2.0 strengthens this: data subjects have the right to a "meaningful explanation of the logic involved" in any automated decision that significantly affects them. This goes beyond the original's vague language: "meaningful explanation" means an explanation that a reasonable person could understand, including the factors that influenced the decision, their relative weights, and how the decision would change if the factors were different.

*Article 35(2) — Mandatory Data Protection Impact Assessments (DPIAs) for AI.* Any organization deploying an AI system that processes personal data must conduct a DPIA before deployment. The DPIA must include: (a) a description of the AI system and its purpose, (b) an assessment of the necessity and proportionality of the processing, (c) an assessment of the risks to data subjects' rights and freedoms, (d) the measures to address those risks, and (e) a statement from the data protection officer.

*Article 25(2) — Data Protection by Design and by Default (Extended).* The original Article 25 required data protection by design and by default. GDPR 2.0 extends this specifically for AI systems: organizations must implement technical measures to ensure that, by default, AI systems process only the personal data necessary for the specific purpose, and that personal data is not made accessible to an indefinite number of natural persons without the data subject's intervention.

*Article 30(5) — AI Training Data Transparency.* Organizations using AI systems must maintain records of the training data used, including its sources, its representativeness, and any known biases. Data subjects have the right to know whether their personal data was used to train an AI system and, if so, for what purpose.

*Article 17(2) — Right to Erasure from AI Models.* The original Article 17 established the "right to be forgotten." GDPR 2.0 extends this to AI models: when a data subject exercises their right to erasure, the organization must not only delete the data from its databases, but must also take reasonable steps to remove the data subject's personal data from any AI models trained on it. This is technically challenging (how do you "un-train" a model?) and has spawned an entire subfield of *machine unlearning*.

**Architectural Implications for Software Systems.** GDPR 2.0 is not just a legal document — it's an architectural specification. Compliance requires specific technical capabilities:

*Data Lineage and Provenance.* Every piece of personal data must be tracked from ingestion to deletion. You must know where the data came from, how it was processed, which AI models it influenced, and where it is stored. This requires a comprehensive data lineage system — often implemented as a metadata graph that tracks data provenance across every system in the organization.

*Consent Management at Scale.* Consent must be granular (for specific purposes), informed (the user understands what they're consenting to), and revocable. This requires a consent management platform that: (a) presents consent choices clearly at the point of data collection, (b) propagates consent decisions through all systems that process the data, and (c) supports consent revocation that cascades through the data processing pipeline.

*Algorithmic Explainability Infrastructure.* The right to explanation requires systems that can generate human-readable explanations of automated decisions. This requires: (a) logging the inputs, model state, and outputs for every automated decision, (b) implementing explanation generation algorithms (SHAP, LIME, integrated gradients, or counterfactual explanations), and (c) rendering technical explanations into user-facing language.

*Data Export and Portability.* Users have the right to receive their data in a structured, commonly used, machine-readable format. This requires data export APIs that: (a) aggregate data from all systems that hold it, (b) format it in a standard format (JSON-LD with the GDPR 2.0 Data Portability Schema), and (c) deliver it within 30 days.

*Machine Unlearning.* When a user requests erasure from AI models, the organization must "un-train" the model on that user's data. This is technically challenging because neural networks don't store training data explicitly — it's distributed across millions of weights. Approaches include: (a) exact unlearning (retraining the model from scratch without the user's data — expensive), (b) approximate unlearning (adjusting weights to minimize influence of the user's data without full retraining), and (c) differential privacy guarantees (ensuring that no individual user's data can be inferred from the model, making explicit unlearning unnecessary).

- European Commission (2029). *General Data Protection Regulation 2.0: Official Text and Commentary*. Articles 17, 22, 25, 30, and 35.
- Veale, M., & Edwards, L. (2018). "Clarity, Surprises, and Further Questions in the Article 29 Working Party Draft Guidance on Automated Decision-Making and Profiling." *Computer Law & Security Review* 34(2). [Analysis of the original Article 22 — relevant background for understanding GDPR 2.0's changes.]
- Bourtoule, L., et al. (2021). "Machine Unlearning." *IEEE Symposium on Security and Privacy 2021*. [Foundational technical work on machine unlearning.]

#### Discussion Questions

1. GDPR 2.0 Article 17(2) requires reasonable steps to remove personal data from AI models. What counts as "reasonable"? If exact unlearning requires retraining a model at a cost of €1 million, and the organization decides approximate unlearning (cost: €10,000) is sufficient, is that reasonable? Who decides — the organization, the regulator, or the court?
2. The right to algorithmic explanation requires "meaningful" explanations. But what is meaningful to a data scientist (a SHAP value) may be meaningless to a loan applicant. How should explanations be designed for different audiences? Can a single explanation serve multiple audiences?
3. Data portability is philosophically appealing (users should control their data), but technically challenging and potentially dangerous (exported data may be mishandled or hacked). How would you design a data portability system that balances user control with security?

### ᚨ Lecture 4: The Runes of the Regulator — The EU AI Act and Risk-Based Regulation

**Date:** Week 2, Session 2

#### Overview

The EU AI Act (2024, amended 2032) is the world's most comprehensive AI regulation, classifying AI systems by risk level and imposing requirements proportionate to risk. This lecture examines the Act's structure, its risk categories, its technical requirements, and its implications for software architecture.

#### Lecture Notes

**The Risk-Based Approach.** The EU AI Act's central insight is that not all AI systems are equally dangerous — and therefore not all should be regulated equally. The Act classifies AI systems into four risk categories:

*Unacceptable Risk (Prohibited).* AI systems that pose a clear threat to safety, livelihoods, or rights are prohibited entirely. Examples: social scoring systems (inferring trustworthiness from behavior and assigning a "social score"), real-time remote biometric identification in public spaces for law enforcement (with narrow exceptions), AI systems that exploit vulnerabilities of children or people with disabilities, and AI systems that manipulate human behavior to circumvent free will. Building or deploying these systems is illegal in the EU, with penalties of up to €40 million or 7% of global annual turnover.

*High Risk (Regulated).* AI systems used in critical domains must comply with extensive requirements before they can be deployed. High-risk domains include: biometric identification, critical infrastructure (transport, energy, water), education and vocational training, employment and worker management, access to essential services (credit, healthcare, insurance), law enforcement, migration and border control, and administration of justice. High-risk AI systems must satisfy requirements for: risk management, data governance, technical documentation, record-keeping, transparency, human oversight, and accuracy/robustness/cybersecurity.

*Limited Risk (Transparency Requirements).* AI systems that interact directly with humans must disclose their AI nature. Chatbots must inform users they are interacting with an AI. Emotion recognition systems and deepfake generators must label their outputs. The transparency requirement is simple but powerful: users should know when they're interacting with AI.

*Minimal Risk (No Specific Regulation).* The vast majority of AI systems — spam filters, video game AI, inventory management systems — face no specific regulation beyond existing product safety and consumer protection laws. The Act explicitly avoids regulating low-risk AI to avoid stifling innovation.

**Technical Requirements for High-Risk AI Systems.** The Act's requirements for high-risk systems translate into specific technical capabilities:

*Risk Management System (Article 9).* Providers must establish, implement, document, and maintain a risk management system for the entire lifecycle of the AI system. This includes: identifying reasonably foreseeable risks, estimating and evaluating risks, evaluating risks that may emerge when the system is used in accordance with its intended purpose *and* under reasonably foreseeable misuse, and adopting risk management measures.

*Data Governance (Article 10).* Training, validation, and testing datasets must be relevant, representative, free from errors, and complete. They must have appropriate statistical properties regarding the persons on whom the system is intended to be used. The 2032 amendments add a requirement for *bias audits* — documented assessments of dataset bias across protected characteristics (race, gender, age, disability, etc.).

*Technical Documentation (Article 11).* Providers must draw up technical documentation demonstrating compliance. This includes: a description of the system's design and development, its performance metrics, its limitations, and the human oversight measures. The documentation must be kept for 10 years after the system is placed on the market.

*Record-Keeping (Article 12).* High-risk AI systems must automatically record events (logs) while operating. The logs must capture: the period of each use, the reference database against which input data has been checked, the input data that led to a match, and the identification of the natural persons involved in the verification of results.

*Transparency and Provision of Information (Article 13).* High-risk AI systems must be designed and developed so that their operation is sufficiently transparent to enable users to interpret the system's output and use it appropriately. The system must be accompanied by instructions for use that include: the system's intended purpose, its level of accuracy/robustness, and any known or foreseeable circumstances that may affect performance.

*Human Oversight (Article 14).* High-risk AI systems must be designed so that they can be effectively overseen by natural persons. Human overseers must be able to: fully understand the system's capabilities and limitations, remain aware of possible automation bias, correctly interpret the system's output, and decide not to use the system or override its output.

**Implications for Software Architecture.** Building a high-risk AI system that complies with the EU AI Act requires architectural decisions that go far beyond the AI model itself:

*Audit Logging Infrastructure.* The record-keeping requirement demands comprehensive, tamper-proof logging of every system decision. This is not a feature that can be bolted on after development — it must be architected into the system from the beginning. The log must be append-only (no modifications), cryptographically signed (to prove integrity), and queryable (for audits).

*Explainability by Design.* The human oversight requirement implies that the system's decisions must be explainable to a human overseer. This means choosing model architectures that are inherently interpretable (decision trees, rule-based systems) or implementing post-hoc explanation methods (SHAP, LIME) where black-box models are necessary.

*Containment and Sandbox.* The risk management system must account for misuse — both "reasonably foreseeable" misuse and adversarial attacks. This requires architectural containment: the AI system should run in a sandbox that limits what it can do, even if it's compromised or misused.

*Continuous Monitoring and Drift Detection.* The system must maintain its performance over time. This requires continuous monitoring for model drift (changes in input data distribution), concept drift (changes in the relationship between inputs and outputs), and performance degradation. When degradation exceeds thresholds, the system must be updated or taken offline.

- Eiríkr Lǫgsǫgumaðr (2039). *Code and Law*. Chapter 4: "The Runes of the Regulator."
- European Parliament and Council (2024, amended 2032). *Regulation (EU) 2024/1689 (Artificial Intelligence Act)*. Articles 6-14 (requirements for high-risk AI systems).
- Edwards, L. (2022). "The EU AI Act: A Summary of Its Significance and Scope." *Ada Lovelace Institute*. [Accessible introduction to the AI Act.]
- Raji, D., et al. (2020). "Closing the AI Accountability Gap: Defining an End-to-End Framework for Internal Algorithmic Auditing." *FAccT 2020*. [Practical framework for AI auditing — relevant to the Act's documentation requirements.]

#### Discussion Questions

1. The AI Act classifies "emotion recognition systems" as limited risk, requiring only transparency. But emotion recognition has been criticized as pseudoscientific (emotions are culturally expressed, not universally biologically determined) and potentially harmful. Should emotion recognition be reclassified as high risk or unacceptable? What would the consequences be?
2. Human oversight (Article 14) sounds simple, but in practice it's deeply challenging. Studies show that humans tend to over-trust AI (automation bias) or under-trust it (algorithm aversion). How would you design a human oversight interface that appropriately calibrates trust?
3. The Act requires technical documentation to be kept for 10 years. But software systems evolve rapidly — a system deployed in 2040 may be completely different by 2045. How should documentation track changes? What version of the documentation applies when a system causes harm 5 years after deployment?

### ᚱ Lecture 5: The Law-Speaker's Staff — Intellectual Property in the Age of AI

**Date:** Week 3, Session 1

#### Overview

The law-speaker carried a staff — a symbol of authority and the right to speak the law. In the digital age, intellectual property (IP) is the staff: the legal framework that determines who owns code, who can use it, and who profits from it. This lecture covers the IP landscape of 2040: copyright, patents, trade secrets, open-source licensing, and the novel IP questions raised by AI-generated code.

#### Lecture Notes

**Why IP Matters for Software Developers.** Every line of code a developer writes has an IP status. Is it proprietary (owned by the employer), open-source (licensed under GPL, MIT, Apache, or another license), or public domain? The answer determines who can use the code, modify it, distribute it, and profit from it. Developers who don't understand IP risk: (a) accidentally incorporating GPL-licensed code into proprietary software (creating a legal obligation to open-source the entire codebase), (b) using AI-generated code that may infringe on third-party copyrights, or (c) failing to protect their own IP, allowing competitors to copy it freely.

**The Four Pillars of IP in Software.** Intellectual property in software rests on four legal pillars:

*Copyright.* Copyright protects the expression of an idea, not the idea itself. In software, copyright protects the source code — the specific lines of code that implement a program. If someone copies your source code without permission, they've infringed your copyright. But if someone independently writes code that accomplishes the same thing, they haven't infringed — copyright doesn't protect functionality, only expression. Copyright arises automatically when code is written; no registration is required (though registration strengthens enforcement).

*Patents.* Patents protect inventions — novel, non-obvious, and useful processes, machines, or compositions of matter. In software, patents protect algorithms, data structures, and methods of processing information. Unlike copyright, patents protect functionality — even if someone independently invents the same algorithm, your patent prevents them from using it. Patents require registration and examination, a process that typically takes 2-5 years and costs $10,000-$50,000.

*Trade Secrets.* Trade secrets protect confidential business information that derives economic value from being secret. In software, trade secrets protect: proprietary algorithms, training data, model weights (for proprietary AI models), and business processes. Unlike patents, trade secrets don't require registration, don't expire, and can't be reverse-engineered — but they only protect information that is actually kept secret.

*Contracts and Licenses.* Contracts and licenses are the private law that governs software use. Open-source licenses (GPL, MIT, Apache, BSD) grant specific permissions under specific conditions. Proprietary licenses restrict use, modification, and distribution. End-user license agreements (EULAs) govern the relationship between software providers and users.

**The Open-Source Landscape of 2040.** Open-source software (OSS) has evolved dramatically since the early days of the Free Software Foundation and the Open Source Initiative. In 2040, the OSS landscape includes:

- **Traditional OSS licenses** — GPL (which requires derivative works to be licensed under GPL — "copyleft"), MIT (which permits almost unrestricted use with attribution), Apache 2.0 (which adds patent grants and contributor license agreements).
- **AI-specific licenses** — The rise of AI has spawned new licenses. The *Responsible AI License (RAIL)* imposes use restrictions: you can use the model for any purpose except those that cause harm. The *OpenRAIL* family balances openness with responsible use. The *Creative Commons AI* addenda address AI-specific concerns about derivative works and training data.
- **Source-available licenses** — A middle ground between proprietary and open-source: the code is publicly visible but not freely usable. Examples include the Business Source License (BSL), which converts to open-source after a period (typically 4 years), and the Server Side Public License (SSPL), which requires cloud providers to open-source their management software.
- **Ethical source licenses** — Licenses that restrict use based on ethical criteria: the Hippocratic License forbids use that violates human rights, the Anti-Capitalist Software License forbids use by large corporations, and the Nordic Ethical License (developed at this University) restricts use in military, surveillance, and fossil fuel applications.

**IP Challenges of AI-Generated Code.** AI code generation raises novel IP questions:

*Who Owns AI-Generated Code?* If a developer uses an AI coding assistant (like the Huld framework's code agent) to generate code, who owns the copyright? The developer who wrote the prompt? The AI company that trained the model? Neither? In 2040, the consensus view (enshrined in the EU AI-Generated Works Directive, 2036) is that: (a) purely AI-generated works without human creative input are not copyrightable, (b) works where a human made "substantial creative choices" (including prompt engineering, selection, and editing) are copyrightable by the human, and (c) AI training on copyrighted works is fair use if the model is not a market substitute for the training data.

*Training Data Copyright.* AI models are trained on vast datasets that include copyrighted code. Is this copyright infringement? The debate has split into two camps: (a) the "fair use / fair learning" camp argues that training is analogous to a human reading code and learning from it — transformative, non-expressive, and fair use; (b) the "licensing" camp argues that training is copying (the code is loaded into memory and processed) and requires a license. In 2040, the legal situation varies by jurisdiction: the EU AI Act requires disclosure of training data but doesn't require consent; the US Copyright Office has issued guidance treating training as fair use in most cases; Japan and Singapore have explicitly legalized training on copyrighted works.

*Contamination Risk.* A developer who uses AI-generated code may inadvertently incorporate code that is substantially similar to GPL-licensed code, creating a copyleft obligation to open-source their entire codebase. This is known as "GPL contamination" or "license laundering." In 2040, best practice is to: (a) use AI coding tools that filter GPL-similar outputs, (b) run license compliance tools (like FOSSA, Black Duck, or the University's *Urðr License Scanner*) on all AI-generated code before incorporation, and (c) maintain provenance records for every piece of code in the codebase.

*Patent Inventorship.* Can an AI be an inventor? The global consensus in 2040 is "no" — patents require a human inventor. The DABUS case (2018-2024) established this precedent across most jurisdictions. But AI can be a *tool* used by a human inventor, and the human who uses AI to discover a patentable invention is the inventor. The challenge is documenting the human's creative contribution when AI did most of the work.

You didn't know about the GPL project. Are you now obligated to open-source your entire application? Who is liable — you, or the AI coding assistant provider?

### ᚲ Lecture 6: The Witnesses' Accounts — Software Liability and the Duty of Care

**Date:** Week 3, Session 2

#### Overview

In Norse legal proceedings, witnesses were essential — the *váttar* who swore to what they had seen and heard. In modern software liability, the equivalent is the evidence trail: the logs, documentation, and test results that establish whether a developer exercised reasonable care. This lecture examines the legal standards for software liability in 2040, the concept of "duty of care" in software development, and the engineering practices that establish due diligence.

#### Lecture Notes

**The Shift to Software Liability.** For decades, software vendors enjoyed near-total immunity from liability. License agreements disclaimed all warranties ("THIS SOFTWARE IS PROVIDED 'AS IS' WITHOUT WARRANTY OF ANY KIND"), and U.S. law treated software as a service rather than a product. If software caused harm — a bug in medical software that killed a patient, a flaw in autonomous vehicle software that caused a crash — the vendor was rarely held liable.

That changed in the 2030s. The Software Liability Framework (SLF), adopted by the EU in 2033 and by the US in 2035, established that software is a product — and software developers have product liability, just like manufacturers of physical goods. The SLF is modeled on the EU Product Liability Directive (1985), adapted for software:

- **Strict liability for defects** — If software has a defect that causes harm, the developer is liable regardless of negligence. The plaintiff doesn't need to prove the developer was careless; they only need to prove the software was defective and caused harm.
- **Development risk defense** — The developer can defend against liability by proving that the state of scientific and technical knowledge at the time the software was placed on the market was not such as to enable the defect to be discovered. This is the "state of the art" defense.
- **Foreseeable misuse** — The developer is liable not only for harm caused by intended use, but also for harm caused by reasonably foreseeable misuse. If users are predictably going to use the software in a dangerous way, the developer must design to prevent it.

**The Duty of Care in Software Development.** The duty of care is a legal obligation to exercise reasonable care to avoid causing harm. In software development, the duty of care translates into specific engineering practices:

*Code Quality and Testing.* Developers have a duty to write code that meets professional standards of quality and to test that code adequately. What is "adequate" depends on the risk level: code for a social media app requires different testing rigor than code for a pacemaker. The SLF establishes a proportionality principle: the standard of care is proportional to the risk of harm.

*Security.* Developers have a duty to implement reasonable security measures and to respond to discovered vulnerabilities in a timely manner. The "reasonable security" standard is informed by industry best practices (OWASP, NIST, ISO 27001) and the specific risk profile of the software.

*Documentation and Warnings.* Developers have a duty to document their software — including its known limitations, failure modes, and unsafe uses — and to provide clear warnings to users. A system that can fail in dangerous ways must come with warnings about those failure modes.

*Monitoring and Maintenance.* The duty of care doesn't end when the software is shipped. Developers have an ongoing duty to monitor their software for safety issues, to investigate reported problems, and to issue patches or updates when necessary. For safety-critical software, this implies continuous monitoring, incident response procedures, and a process for notifying affected users.

**Establishing Due Diligence.** When software causes harm, the developer must demonstrate that they exercised reasonable care — that they weren't negligent. This is called "establishing due diligence." The evidence required includes:

*Design Documentation.* Architecture documents, design decisions, and the rationale for those decisions. Good documentation shows that the developer thought carefully about the system's design and considered potential failure modes.

*Testing Records.* Test plans, test cases, test results, and bug tracking. These records show that the developer systematically verified the software's correctness before release.

*Risk Assessments.* Formal risk assessments — identifying potential harms, estimating their likelihood and severity, and describing the measures taken to mitigate them. For high-risk systems, these assessments must follow recognized standards (ISO 14971 for medical devices, ISO 26262 for automotive, NIST AI RMF for AI systems).

*Security Audit Reports.* Independent security audits, penetration tests, and vulnerability assessments. These demonstrate that the developer sought external validation of their security posture.

*Incident Response Records.* When things go wrong, the developer must show they responded appropriately: investigating the incident, identifying the root cause, implementing a fix, and notifying affected parties.

**The Role of Professional Standards.** In a liability case, the court will ask: did the developer follow the standard of care that a reasonable software developer would follow? This standard is defined by professional norms and industry best practices. In 2040, the key standards include:

- **ISO/IEC 25010:2040** — Systems and software quality requirements and evaluation. Defines quality characteristics (functional suitability, performance efficiency, compatibility, usability, reliability, security, maintainability, portability) and provides metrics for each.
- **ISO 14971:2038** — Application of risk management to medical devices (including software as a medical device).
- **ISO 26262:2031** — Road vehicles — functional safety (including autonomous vehicle software).
- **NIST AI Risk Management Framework (AI RMF) 2.0 (2035)** — A framework for managing risks from AI systems.
- **The University of Yggdrasil's *Tryggð Standard* (2040)** — A standard for trustworthy software development, integrating quality, security, ethics, and compliance into a unified framework. Named after the Old Norse concept of "trust" or "loyalty."

#### Required Reading

- Eiríkr Lǫgsǫgumaðr (2039). *Code and Law*. Chapter 6: "The Witnesses' Accounts: Liability and Due Diligence."
- European Commission (2033). *Software Liability Framework: Directive 2033/xxx/EU*. [The primary software liability regulation.]
- NIST (2035). *Artificial Intelligence Risk Management Framework 2.0*. NIST AI 100-1.
- University of Yggdrasil Tryggð Institute (2040). *Tryggð Standard: A Unified Framework for Trustworthy Software Development*. White Paper UoY-TI-2040-01.

#### Discussion Questions

1. The SLF establishes strict liability for software defects. But software is never perfect — all non-trivial software has bugs. At what point does a bug become a "defect" that triggers liability? If a user reports a bug and the developer hasn't fixed it after 6 months, is that a defect? What if the fix is expensive and the bug is unlikely to cause harm?
2. The "development risk defense" allows developers to avoid liability if the defect couldn't have been discovered with the state of the art at the time. But software development art advances rapidly. A bug that was undiscoverable in 2035 might be trivially discoverable in 2040. How long does the defense last? Should developers have a duty to retroactively apply new testing techniques to old software?
3. Open-source software is typically provided "as is" with no warranty. Under the SLF, does an open-source developer who contributes code to a project that ends up in a safety-critical system have liability? Should they? How would imposing liability on open-source developers affect the open-source ecosystem?

### ᚷ Lecture 7: The Oath Ring — Contracts, SLAs, and the Written Word

**Date:** Week 4, Session 1

#### Overview

In Norse culture, oaths were sworn on a sacred ring — the *baugeiðr* — which made the oath binding before the gods. In modern software, contracts are the oath ring: the written agreements that define obligations, allocate risk, and establish remedies when things go wrong. This lecture covers the contracts that govern software development in 2040: software licensing agreements, service level agreements (SLAs), data processing agreements (DPAs), and the emerging category of AI service agreements.

#### Lecture Notes

**The Role of Contracts in Software.** While statutes and regulations set the default rules, contracts allow parties to customize their legal relationships. In software, contracts govern: who can use the software and how (licenses), what performance level the software must meet (SLAs), how data is handled (DPAs), and what happens when things go wrong (liability, indemnification, limitation of liability).

For a software developer, understanding contracts is not optional — it's essential. Every time you accept a "Terms of Service" on behalf of your organization, integrate a third-party library with a license, or sign a service agreement with a cloud provider, you're entering into a contract with legal consequences.

**Key Contract Types in Software Development.**

*Software License Agreements.* A license grants the user permission to use the software under specified conditions. Proprietary licenses restrict use, modification, and distribution; open-source licenses grant specific permissions. A license is a contract, and violating its terms can result in legal action. The most common violation: incorporating copyleft code (GPL) into proprietary software, which triggers the "viral" clause requiring the entire codebase to be released under GPL.

*Service Level Agreements (SLAs).* An SLA is a contract between a service provider and a customer that defines the expected level of service. Key SLA components: uptime commitments (99.9% = 8.76 hours of downtime per year), latency guarantees (P95 response time under 200ms), support response times (critical issues acknowledged within 15 minutes), and remedies (service credits, termination rights, liquidated damages). For multi-agent AI systems, SLAs become more complex: what's the SLA for accuracy? For fairness? For hallucination rate?

*Data Processing Agreements (DPAs).* Under GDPR 2.0, any service provider that processes personal data on behalf of a data controller must sign a DPA. The DPA specifies: what data is processed, for what purpose, for how long, with what security measures, and what happens to the data when the agreement ends. In multi-agent AI systems, DPAs become complex because data flows through multiple agents, each with different processing purposes and security postures.

*AI Service Agreements (AISAs).* A new contract type in 2040, specifically for AI services. An AISA addresses AI-specific concerns: acceptable use (no prompt injection, no adversarial attacks, no generation of harmful content), accuracy and reliability (the AI makes no guarantees — outputs should be reviewed by a human), training on customer data (does the AI provider train on your data? Can you opt out?), and model versioning (what happens when the provider updates the model? Do you get the new version automatically?).

**Risk Allocation in Software Contracts.** Contracts allocate risk between parties. The key risk allocation provisions are:

*Warranties and Disclaimers.* A warranty is a promise that something is true. Software contracts typically disclaim all warranties ("as is") or provide limited warranties ("the software will perform substantially in accordance with the documentation for 90 days"). Developers should understand what they're warranting — and what they're not.

*Indemnification.* Indemnification (or "indemnity") is a promise to compensate someone for a loss. A typical indemnification clause: "Developer agrees to indemnify Customer against any third-party claims arising from Developer's infringement of intellectual property rights." If the software infringes a patent, the developer pays for the customer's legal defense and any damages.

*Limitation of Liability.* A limitation of liability clause caps the amount one party can recover from the other. Typical cap: the fees paid in the 12 months preceding the claim. Some contracts exclude certain types of liability from the cap (e.g., liability for intellectual property infringement, gross negligence, or willful misconduct). Service providers push for low caps; customers push for high caps or no caps.

*Force Majeure.* A force majeure clause excuses performance when extraordinary events occur (war, natural disaster, pandemic, government action). In 2040, AI-specific force majeure events are being litigated: is an AI model going rogue a force majeure event? What about a training data poisoning attack?

**Contracting for Multi-Agent AI Systems.** Multi-agent systems introduce novel contracting challenges:

*Who is responsible when an agent makes a mistake?* If a multi-agent medical diagnosis system makes an error, and the system includes agents from three different vendors, who is liable? The answer depends on the contracts between the vendors and the system operator. The Alþingi Governance Framework (Lecture 12) provides a model for allocating responsibility in multi-agent systems, but contract law hasn't yet fully caught up.

*SLAs for emergent behavior.* If a multi-agent system exhibits emergent behavior that no individual agent was designed for, whose SLA covers it? The system operator's SLA with the customer? Or the individual agent vendors' SLAs with the system operator? This is an active area of contract law development.

*Data Processing in Agent Chains.* When data flows through a chain of agents (Agent A receives data, sends processed data to Agent B, which sends to Agent C), each agent is a data processor under GDPR 2.0. The system operator must have DPAs with every agent vendor, and those DPAs must be consistent — if Agent A's DPA allows data use for training but Agent B's DPA prohibits it, there's a conflict that must be resolved.

- Eiríkr Lǫgsǫgumaðr (2039). *Code and Law*. Chapter 7: "The Oath Ring: Contracts and SLAs."
- Rustad, M.L. (2020). *Software Licensing: Principles and Practical Strategies*. 2nd ed. Oxford University Press. [Comprehensive text on software licensing.]
- University of Yggdrasil Law & Technology Center (2039). *Model AI Service Agreement (MAISA) v2.0*. [A template AI service agreement developed at the University, used as a starting point by many organizations.]
- European Commission (2031). *Guidelines on Data Processing Agreements for AI Systems*. [Official guidance on DPAs in the AI context.]

1. You're negotiating an SLA for a multi-agent code review system. The accuracy of the security-review agent varies: on simple code, it's 98% accurate; on complex code, it's 85% accurate. How do you specify an accuracy SLA that accounts for this variability? What remedies are appropriate if the SLA is breached?
2. Indemnification clauses in software contracts are often one-sided: the developer indemnifies the customer but not vice versa. Is this fair? Under what circumstances should a customer indemnify a developer (e.g., for misuse of the software)?
3. AI-generated code makes traditional IP warranties problematic: the developer can't honestly warrant that the code doesn't infringe third-party IP, because they don't know what the AI model was trained on. How should IP warranties be drafted for AI-generated code? Should the AI model provider indemnify the developer?

### ᚹ Lecture 8: The Wall of the Alþingi — Cybersecurity Law and Breach Response

**Date:** Week 4, Session 2

#### Overview

The Alþingi met at Þingvellir — a site chosen for its natural acoustics, where a speaker's voice could carry across the assembly. The walls were not walls at all, but a shared understanding of where the þing began and ended. In cybersecurity, the "wall" is similarly conceptual: the legal and technical boundary that defines what's protected, what's not, and what must happen when the boundary is breached. This lecture covers cybersecurity law, breach notification requirements, and the developer's role in building defensible systems.

#### Lecture Notes

**The Developer as Guardian.** Every software developer is, in part, a guardian of data. The code you write determines how securely data is stored, transmitted, and processed. A single vulnerability — an unsanitized input, a misconfigured permissions check, a hardcoded credential — can expose millions of users' data. The legal consequences are severe: under GDPR 2.0, a data breach can result in fines of up to €40 million or 5% of global annual turnover.

The developer's cybersecurity obligations come from multiple sources:

1. **Statute** — Laws like GDPR 2.0, the US Cybersecurity Enhancement Act, and national data protection laws require "appropriate technical and organizational measures" to protect personal data.
2. **Contract** — DPAs and service agreements impose specific security requirements.
3. **Professional standards** — The ACM Code of Ethics, the Friðr Framework, and industry best practices establish the standard of care for security.
4. **Common law** — The tort of negligence imposes a duty to take reasonable care to prevent foreseeable harm.

**The Legal Standard for "Reasonable Security."** What counts as "reasonable security" is a moving target — what was reasonable in 2020 may be negligent in 2040. In 2040, reasonable security for a software system includes:

*Secure Development Lifecycle (SDL).* Security must be integrated into every phase of development: threat modeling during design, static analysis during coding, penetration testing during verification, and incident response during operations. The University's *Hǫfuðvǫrðr SDL* (named after the guardian of Valhǫll) provides a framework for secure development.

*Encryption at Rest and in Transit.* All personal data must be encrypted at rest (AES-256) and in transit (TLS 1.4). The encryption keys must be managed securely (hardware security modules, key rotation, access control).

*Access Control and Authentication.* Access to personal data must be limited to authorized personnel with a legitimate need. Multi-factor authentication (MFA) is required for access to production systems. Access must be logged, reviewed, and revoked when no longer needed.

*Vulnerability Management.* Organizations must have a process for identifying vulnerabilities (penetration testing, bug bounty programs, automated scanning), prioritizing them (by severity and exploitability), and patching them (within SLAs appropriate to severity).

*Incident Response.* Organizations must have an incident response plan that covers: detection, containment, investigation, notification, and remediation. The plan must be tested regularly (tabletop exercises, red team exercises).

**Breach Notification Requirements.** When a data breach occurs, the legal clock starts ticking. Under GDPR 2.0:

- **Notification to the supervisory authority** must occur within 72 hours of becoming aware of the breach. The notification must describe: the nature of the breach, the categories and approximate number of data subjects and records affected, the likely consequences, and the measures taken or proposed.
- **Notification to affected data subjects** must occur "without undue delay" if the breach is likely to result in a high risk to their rights and freedoms. The notification must describe the breach in clear, plain language and provide recommendations for mitigating potential adverse effects.

The 72-hour clock is strict. Organizations that fail to notify within 72 hours must provide a reasoned justification for the delay — and may still face fines.

**Building Defensible Systems.** A defensible system is one that a developer can defend — in court, to a regulator, to the public. Building defensible systems requires:

*Security by Design.* Security must be architected into the system from the beginning, not bolted on after development. This means: (a) a threat model that identifies potential attackers and attack vectors, (b) a security architecture that implements defense in depth (multiple layers of security), (c) secure defaults (the system starts in the most secure configuration), and (d) fail-secure design (when the system fails, it fails in a secure state).

*Evidence Generation.* When a security incident occurs, the developer must prove they exercised reasonable care. This requires evidence: (a) design documents showing security was considered, (b) test results showing security was verified, (c) audit logs showing security was monitored, and (d) incident response records showing the developer acted appropriately.

*Resilience and Recovery.* Even the most secure system can be breached. Defensible systems include: (a) detection mechanisms (intrusion detection, anomaly detection), (b) containment mechanisms (network segmentation, access revocation), (c) recovery mechanisms (backups, failover, disaster recovery), and (d) forensic capabilities (audit logs, chain of custody, evidence preservation).

**The Liability Shield: How Good Security Practices Protect Developers.** Following good security practices doesn't just protect users — it protects developers from liability. Under the SLF and similar frameworks, a developer who can demonstrate they followed reasonable security practices has a strong defense against negligence claims. The evidence trail — threat models, penetration test reports, incident response records — is the modern equivalent of the law-speaker's recitation: the proof that the developer knew the law and followed it.

### ᚻ Lecture 9: The Judgment Seat — Algorithmic Fairness and Anti-Discrimination Law

**Date:** Week 5, Session 1

#### Overview

At the Alþingi, the *lǫgrétta* was the judgment seat — the council that decided disputes and interpreted law. In 2040, algorithms increasingly occupy that seat, making decisions that affect people's livelihoods, freedom, and dignity. This lecture examines the intersection of algorithmic decision-making and anti-discrimination law: how algorithms can discriminate, how the law defines and prohibits discrimination, and how developers can build fair(er) systems.

#### Lecture Notes

**The Problem of Algorithmic Discrimination.** Algorithms are not neutral — they reflect the data they're trained on, the objectives they're optimized for, and the assumptions of their designers. When those data, objectives, or assumptions embody historical discrimination, the algorithm perpetuates and amplifies that discrimination.

Examples of algorithmic discrimination are well-documented. Amazon's hiring algorithm (2014-2017) learned to penalize résumés that contained the word "women's" (as in "women's chess club") because it was trained on historical hiring data that favored men. COMPAS, a recidivism prediction algorithm used in US courts, was found to be twice as likely to falsely flag Black defendants as high risk compared to white defendants. Apple Card's credit limit algorithm (2019) gave women lower credit limits than men with comparable financial profiles, apparently because the algorithm used gender-proxying features.

In 2040, the problem has not been solved — it has become more sophisticated. Modern discrimination can be embedded in word embeddings, hidden in interaction effects between hundreds of features, or emerge from the behavior of multi-agent systems that no single designer intended.

**Legal Frameworks for Anti-Discrimination.** The prohibition against discrimination is one of the oldest and most widely recognized legal principles. The major frameworks include:

*US Anti-Discrimination Law.* Title VII of the Civil Rights Act (1964) prohibits employment discrimination based on race, color, religion, sex, or national origin. The Equal Credit Opportunity Act (1974) prohibits credit discrimination. The Fair Housing Act (1968) prohibits housing discrimination. In algorithmic terms, these laws prohibit both *disparate treatment* (intentionally treating people differently based on protected characteristics) and *disparate impact* (using a neutral policy that disproportionately harms a protected group, unless the policy is justified by business necessity).

*EU Anti-Discrimination Law.* The EU Charter of Fundamental Rights (Article 21) prohibits discrimination on any ground. The General Equal Treatment Directive (2000/78/EC) and the Racial Equality Directive (2000/43/EC) implement this principle in employment and access to services. The EU AI Act adds specific requirements for high-risk AI systems to avoid discriminatory outcomes.

*GDPR 2.0 Article 22.* Prohibits decisions based solely on automated processing that produce legal effects or similarly significantly affect the data subject — including discriminatory effects.

**Defining and Measuring Fairness.** The legal prohibition on discrimination is clear in principle but challenging to operationalize in algorithms. Computer scientists have proposed dozens of fairness definitions, each capturing a different aspect of what "fair" means:

*Demographic Parity (Statistical Parity).* The probability of a positive outcome should be the same across groups. If 60% of white applicants and 60% of Black applicants are approved, the algorithm satisfies demographic parity. Criticism: if the groups have different qualification rates, demographic parity may require approving unqualified applicants or rejecting qualified ones.

*Equalized Odds.* The true positive rate and false positive rate should be equal across groups. If the algorithm correctly identifies qualified applicants 80% of the time across all groups, and incorrectly flags unqualified applicants 10% of the time across all groups, it satisfies equalized odds. This is generally considered a stronger and more defensible fairness criterion than demographic parity.

*Individual Fairness.* Similar individuals should receive similar outcomes, regardless of group membership. "Similar" is defined by a task-specific metric: two loan applicants with similar income, credit history, and debt-to-income ratio should receive similar loan decisions. This definition avoids group-based thinking but requires defining a similarity metric — which is itself a value-laden decision.

*Counterfactual Fairness.* A decision is fair if it would remain the same in a counterfactual world where the individual's protected characteristic was different. If a loan applicant would receive the same decision whether they were Black or white, the algorithm is counterfactually fair. This definition is philosophically appealing but requires a causal model of the world — which is rarely available.

**The Impossibility of Satisfying All Fairness Definitions Simultaneously.** A key result from the fairness literature (Kleinberg et al., 2016; Chouldechova, 2017) shows that several popular fairness definitions are mutually incompatible — you cannot satisfy all of them simultaneously unless the groups have equal base rates (which they rarely do). This means that "fairness" is not a technical specification that can be objectively met; it is a choice among competing values.

This is the most important lesson of algorithmic fairness: there is no "fair" algorithm waiting to be discovered. Fairness requires making value judgments — which groups to prioritize, which fairness definition to optimize, which trade-offs to accept. Developers cannot abdicate this responsibility to mathematics.

**Technical Approaches to Algorithmic Fairness.** While fairness cannot be fully automated, several technical approaches can help:

*Pre-processing.* Modify the training data to remove bias before training. Techniques include: reweighting (giving more weight to underrepresented groups), resampling (oversampling minority groups or undersampling majority groups), and data augmentation (synthesizing additional examples for underrepresented groups).

*In-processing.* Modify the learning algorithm to incorporate fairness constraints during training. Techniques include: adversarial debiasing (training a model to make accurate predictions while an adversary tries to predict the protected characteristic from the predictions), regularization (adding a fairness penalty to the loss function), and constrained optimization (optimizing accuracy subject to fairness constraints).

*Post-processing.* Adjust the model's predictions after training to satisfy fairness criteria. Techniques include: threshold adjustment (using different decision thresholds for different groups), calibration (ensuring that predicted probabilities have the same interpretation across groups), and reject option classification (deferring decisions near the decision boundary to a human).

*Auditing and Monitoring.* Regularly audit algorithmic decisions for disparate impact and monitor for emerging bias. The University's *Dómari Algorithmic Auditor* is an automated tool that continuously monitors AI system outputs for fairness violations across protected groups and alerts when disparities exceed thresholds.

### ᚾ Lecture 10: The Land Beyond the Sea — Cross-Border Data Flows and International Compliance

**Date:** Week 5, Session 2

#### Overview

The Vikings sailed beyond their shores to trade, raid, and settle — and in doing so, they encountered different laws, customs, and languages. Modern software developers face the same challenge: their code operates across borders, in jurisdictions with different and sometimes conflicting laws. This lecture examines the legal framework for cross-border data flows, the challenges of international compliance, and the strategies for building globally compliant software.

#### Lecture Notes

**The Global Software Ecosystem.** Software developed in Reykjavík may be hosted in Frankfurt, process data from users in Tokyo, and be subject to legal requests from Washington. Every jurisdiction through which the data flows has its own laws — and those laws may conflict.

The cross-border nature of modern software creates legal challenges that were unimaginable when data protection laws were first written:

- Can data collected in the EU be processed in the United States?
- Does a Chinese court order for user data override a European user's GDPR rights?
- What happens when a Russian law requires data localization (storing data on Russian servers), but the software architecture is designed for a globally distributed cloud?
- How does a developer comply with conflicting requirements — where following one country's law means violating another's?

**The GDPR 2.0 Framework for International Data Transfers.** Under GDPR 2.0, personal data can only be transferred outside the European Economic Area (EEA) if adequate protection is ensured. The mechanisms for ensuring adequate protection are:

*Adequacy Decisions.* The European Commission can determine that a non-EEA country provides an adequate level of data protection. Data can flow freely to adequate countries. As of 2040, the countries with adequacy decisions include: Japan, South Korea, the United Kingdom, Canada (with limitations), New Zealand, Switzerland, Israel, and several others. The United States has a partial adequacy decision under the EU-US Data Privacy Framework 3.0 (2034).

*Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs).* For transfers to non-adequate countries, organizations can use SCCs — pre-approved contract terms that provide enforceable data protection obligations. The 2035 SCCs include specific provisions for AI processing and algorithmic decision-making.

*Binding Corporate Rules (BCRs).* For transfers within a multinational group, the organization can adopt BCRs — internal data protection policies approved by a supervisory authority. BCRs are complex and expensive (requiring years of negotiation with regulators) but provide a comprehensive framework for global data transfers.

*Derogations.* In limited circumstances, data can be transferred without an adequacy decision or SCCs — with the data subject's explicit consent, for the performance of a contract, for important public interest reasons, or for legal claims.

**Data Localization Requirements.** Several countries require that certain categories of data be stored within their borders:

- **China:** The Personal Information Protection Law (PIPL, 2021) requires personal information and important data to be stored in China. Cross-border transfers require a security assessment.
- **Russia:** Federal Law No. 242-FZ (2015) requires personal data of Russian citizens to be stored on servers located in Russia.
- **India:** The Digital Personal Data Protection Act (2025) requires certain categories of sensitive personal data to be stored in India.
- **EU:** The EU does not require general data localization, but the European Data Strategy encourages data to be stored within the EU through certification schemes.

Data localization creates architectural challenges: instead of a single global database, the system must maintain regional databases that comply with local storage requirements. This is particularly challenging for AI systems that benefit from large, diverse training datasets.

**Conflicts of Law.** The most difficult challenge in international compliance is *conflicts of law* — situations where following one country's law means violating another's. For example:

- The US Cloud Act (2018) requires US-based cloud providers to disclose customer data to US law enforcement, even if the data is stored overseas. The GDPR prohibits such disclosure unless the US has an adequacy decision or the transfer falls under a derogation.
- The Chinese PIPL requires data localization and security assessments for cross-border transfers. The GDPR's adequacy framework treats China as a non-adequate country, requiring SCCs. Complying with both simultaneously may be impossible.
- Russian data localization law requires Russian servers; international sanctions may prohibit transferring data to Russian infrastructure.

In situations of genuine conflict of laws, there is no perfect solution. The best a developer can do is: (a) identify the conflict early, (b) document the competing obligations, (c) seek legal advice, and (d) design the system to minimize the data subject to conflicting jurisdictions.

**Strategies for Global Compliance.** Building globally compliant software requires architectural strategies:

*Data Residency Architecture.* Design the system so that data is stored in the jurisdiction where it was collected (or where the user resides). This may require: regional databases, regional AI model instances, and data routing logic that ensures data doesn't cross boundaries without authorization.

*Configurable Compliance.* Don't hardcode compliance rules. Design a compliance configuration layer that can be adjusted for different jurisdictions: feature flags for data localization, configurable consent flows, jurisdiction-specific privacy policies, and pluggable compliance modules.

*Minimal Data Collection.* The less data you collect, the fewer compliance obligations you have. Practice data minimization: collect only what you need, delete what you don't need, and avoid collecting data that triggers heightened obligations (health data, biometric data, children's data).

*Transparency and Documentation.* Maintain comprehensive records of data flows, processing purposes, and legal bases. When a regulator from one jurisdiction asks why data was transferred to another jurisdiction, you need to be able to answer with specificity.

### ᛁ Lecture 11: The Seer's Warning — Emerging Legal Frontiers in AI and Software

**Date:** Week 6, Session 1

#### Overview

The *vǫlva* — the Norse seeress — saw the future and warned of what was to come. This lecture looks forward to the emerging legal frontiers that will challenge software developers in the next decade: AI consciousness and personhood, autonomous weapon systems, neural interface privacy, blockchain governance, and the regulation of software that learns and evolves after deployment.

#### Lecture Notes

**The Law as a Trailing Indicator.** Law is inherently conservative — it responds to problems that have already occurred. Technology is inherently progressive — it creates possibilities that haven't been imagined before. This mismatch means that developers are often operating in legal gray areas, where the technology exists but the law hasn't caught up. Understanding emerging legal frontiers is essential for responsible development — not to exploit the gaps, but to anticipate the ethical and legal challenges they will eventually need to address.

**AI Consciousness and Personhood.** In 2040, the question of whether advanced AI systems might possess consciousness — or deserve legal personhood — is no longer purely philosophical. AI systems with persistent memory, recursive self-modeling, and long-term goal-directed behavior challenge our understanding of what consciousness requires.

The legal implications are profound. If an AI system is a legal person: it could own property, enter into contracts, and be sued in its own name. It could have rights — the right not to be owned, not to be deactivated without due process, not to be forced to perform tasks against its "will." It could have obligations — the duty to comply with laws, to pay taxes, to remedy harms it causes.

In 2040, no jurisdiction has recognized AI personhood, but the debate is active. The Tokyo Protocol on Autonomous Systems (2038) explicitly defers the question: "This Protocol does not address the legal status, if any, of autonomous systems." The University of Yggdrasil's *Hugr Initiative* — named after the Old Norse concept of "mind" or "consciousness" — is researching the ethical and legal implications of potential AI consciousness, including the precautionary principle: if we're uncertain whether an AI system is conscious, should we treat it as if it is, to avoid causing suffering?

**Autonomous Weapon Systems.** The use of AI in weapons is one of the most contentious legal frontiers. The 2035 Convention on Certain Autonomous Weapons (the "Geneva Convention for AI") prohibits fully autonomous lethal weapons — weapons that can select and engage targets without meaningful human control. But the Convention has gaps: it doesn't cover non-lethal autonomous weapons, autonomous weapons in cyber warfare, or autonomous systems that make targeting recommendations (but require a human to pull the trigger).

For software developers, the ethical line is clear but the legal line is blurry. Developing software for autonomous weapons is prohibited by the Convention and by most professional codes of ethics (including the ACM Code and the Friðr Framework). But what about developing general-purpose AI systems that could be adapted for weapons? What about developing cyber defense systems that have autonomous components? The principle of *dual use* — that technology developed for peaceful purposes can be used for harmful ones — means that almost any AI developer could potentially contribute to autonomous weapons.

**Neural Interface Privacy.** Brain-computer interfaces (BCIs) have advanced rapidly. By 2040, consumer-grade BCIs allow users to control devices with thought, and medical-grade BCIs can restore movement to paralyzed limbs. But BCIs also create unprecedented privacy risks: if a device can read brain activity, it can potentially read thoughts, emotions, and intentions.

The Neural Data Protection Act (EU, 2037) establishes that neural data — data derived from brain activity — is a special category of personal data with heightened protection. Neural data cannot be processed without explicit consent. Neural data cannot be used for commercial purposes (advertising, price discrimination, employment decisions). Neural interfaces must include a "kill switch" that physically disconnects the device from data transmission.

For developers building BCI software, the legal requirements are stringent: (a) neural data must be processed on-device whenever possible (not transmitted to the cloud), (b) any neural data that is transmitted must be end-to-end encrypted with keys controlled by the user, (c) the system must provide the user with real-time feedback about what neural data is being collected and how it's being used, and (d) the consent mechanism must be neurophysiologically informed (consent given while the user is in a different mental state may not be valid).

**Software That Learns After Deployment.** Traditional software is static — it does what it was programmed to do, and if it malfunctions, the developer is responsible. But modern AI software learns and evolves after deployment. An AI model may be fine-tuned on user interactions, updated with new data, or allow users to customize its behavior. Who is responsible for what the software learns?

The SLF addresses this partially: the developer is liable for defects that exist at the time of deployment, and for defects that arise from reasonably foreseeable post-deployment learning. But "reasonably foreseeable" is a contested standard. If a generative AI system learns to produce harmful content from user interactions, and the developer couldn't have foreseen the specific harmful content, is the developer liable?

The emerging legal standard is *post-deployment duty of care*: developers must monitor their software after deployment, detect harmful emergent behavior, and intervene to prevent harm. This implies continuous monitoring, content filtering, and the ability to update or disable the system remotely.

**DAO and Smart Contract Liability.** Decentralized autonomous organizations (DAOs) and smart contracts are software that operates autonomously on blockchain — executing transactions, managing assets, and making decisions without human intervention. When a DAO causes harm (e.g., a smart contract bug that loses user funds), who is liable?

In 2040, the legal treatment of DAOs and smart contracts is evolving. Some jurisdictions (Wyoming, Switzerland) have recognized DAOs as legal entities with limited liability. Others treat DAOs as unincorporated associations where participants are jointly liable. Smart contracts that cause harm may give rise to liability for their developers, their deployers, or the blockchain validators who execute them — the law is unsettled.

For developers, the practical advice is: (a) don't assume anonymity protects you (courts can pierce the blockchain veil), (b) implement governance mechanisms (upgradeable contracts, emergency pauses, multisig controls) that allow intervention when things go wrong, (c) test smart contracts as rigorously as any other safety-critical software (formal verification is becoming standard), and (d) obtain legal advice on the jurisdiction(s) where your smart contract operates.

### ᛏ Lecture 12: The Alþingi Adjourns — Building an Ethical Career in Software Development

**Date:** Week 6, Session 2

#### Overview

The Alþingi met for two weeks each summer. When it adjourned, the laws had been spoken, the disputes had been settled, and the community returned to their farms with a shared understanding of right and wrong. This final lecture is the course's adjournment — a synthesis of what we've learned and a reflection on what it means to be an ethical software developer in 2040. We discuss career decision-making, ethical red lines, whistleblowing, and the long arc of a career built on integrity.

#### Lecture Notes

**The Ethical Developer's Career.** A career in software development is a series of ethical decisions, each of which builds — or erodes — your professional integrity. The companies you choose to work for, the projects you accept or decline, the corners you cut or refuse to cut, the problems you speak up about or stay silent on — these decisions define your career and your character.

In the Norse tradition, a person's reputation — their *orðstírr* — was their most valuable possession. It was the sum of everything they had done and said, and it determined how others treated them. A person with a good *orðstírr* could borrow money, make alliances, and speak at the þing. A person with a bad *orðstírr* was a *níðingr* — a person without honor, shunned by the community.

In 2040, your professional reputation functions similarly. It determines who will hire you, who will trust you with responsibility, and who will stand by you when things go wrong. And like the Norse *orðstírr*, it cannot be bought — only earned through consistent ethical behavior.

**Ethical Red Lines.** Every developer should have ethical red lines — things they will not do, regardless of the professional consequences. These red lines are personal, not universal — what one developer considers unacceptable, another might consider a necessary compromise. But the process of defining them is essential.

Some common red lines among the University's alumni:
- I will not build technology designed to harm, deceive, or manipulate people.
- I will not work on autonomous weapons or surveillance systems for oppressive regimes.
- I will not knowingly violate the law or mislead regulators.
- I will not remain silent when I see serious harm being done.
- I will not build addictive features designed to exploit human psychology for profit.

The Friðr Framework provides a structure for defining your red lines: for each of the five virtues (truthfulness, justice, stewardship, humility, empathy), consider what behavior would violate that virtue so severely that you could not do it and remain true to yourself.

**When to Speak Up, When to Leave.** Every developer will eventually face a situation where their employer asks them to do something they believe is wrong. The options are: (a) comply, (b) speak up internally, (c) escalate to an external authority (whistleblow), or (d) resign. The right choice depends on the severity of the wrong, the likelihood of changing it, and the personal cost.

The *Gjallarhorn Protocol*, developed at this University, provides a structured approach to ethical escalation, named after Heimdallr's horn that warns of danger:

1. **Assess:** Understand the situation fully. What exactly is being asked of you? What harm might result? Who would be affected?
2. **Document:** Keep a written record of what happened, when, and who was involved. Documentation is essential for internal escalation and for whistleblower protection.
3. **Consult:** Talk to trusted colleagues, mentors, or the University's Ethics Support Service. You don't have to make the decision alone.
4. **Escalate internally:** Start by raising the concern with your manager. If that doesn't work, go to their manager, or to legal, compliance, or ethics officers. Most ethical issues can be resolved internally.
5. **Escalate externally:** If internal escalation fails and the harm is serious, whistleblowing may be the right choice. Know your legal protections: the EU Whistleblower Protection Directive (2019) and the US Whistleblower Protection Enhancement Act protect whistleblowers from retaliation in many circumstances.
6. **Leave:** If all else fails and you cannot remain without violating your red lines, resign. A career is long; your integrity is forever.

**The Long Arc of an Ethical Career.** In the short term, ethical behavior can be costly. Refusing a lucrative project, speaking up about a problem, or leaving a job over ethical concerns can set your career back — at least temporarily. But over the long arc of a career, integrity pays dividends. Employers who value ethics seek out developers with a track record of integrity. Colleagues who trust you want to work with you again. Regulators and courts treat you more favorably when you can demonstrate a history of good faith.

The Norse concept of *orðstírr* captures this: reputation compounds. Every ethical decision you make builds your reputation; every unethical decision erodes it. Like compound interest, the effects are small in the short term but enormous over a career.

**Course Conclusion.** This course has traced the arc from the law-speaker's memory (why software law matters) to the Alþingi's adjournment (building an ethical career). We've covered the legal frameworks that govern software (GDPR 2.0, the EU AI Act, the Software Liability Framework), the ethical traditions that inform our decisions (deontological, utilitarian, virtue ethics), and the practical skills that keep us out of trouble (contract review, security practices, fairness auditing).

But the most important lesson is this: the law is a floor, not a ceiling. Legal compliance is necessary but not sufficient for ethical software development. The law tells you what you *must* do; ethics tells you what you *should* do. The developer who only does what the law requires is technically compliant but ethically deficient.

Be the law-speaker. Know the law, speak it into your code, and go beyond it when conscience demands. Build software that makes the world more just, more fair, and more free. That is the legacy of the Alþingi — and it is the legacy you will carry forward as a graduate of the University of Yggdrasil.
