## Lecture 1: Who Were the Vikings? — Historiography, Terminology, and the Construction of the Viking Age

**The Problem of Naming**

The very term "Viking" is a historiographical construction, and understanding its genealogy is the essential first step in any serious engagement with the field. The Old Norse feminine noun *víking* denotes "a sea-borne raiding expedition," while the masculine *víkingr* means "one who participates in such a raid" — a pirate, a sea-rover, a man who goes *í víking* (on a raiding journey). Crucially, the word describes an **activity**, not an ethnic identity. A Norse farmer could tend his fields in the spring, join a trading venture in the summer, go *í víking* in the autumn, and return to his chieftain's hall for the winter feast. The same individual moved fluidly between these roles. The Norse never referred to themselves collectively as "Vikings" — they were Danir, Svear, Norðmenn, Íslendingar, or simply *menn* (men, people). The concept of a pan-Scandinavian "Viking" identity is a modern imposition.

The etymology of *víking* remains debated. The most widely accepted derivation links it to *vík* (bay, inlet, creek) — thus *víkingr* would be "one who lurks in bays," lying in wait for prey along coastal waterways. An alternative, championed by the Swedish linguist Elias Wessén and revisited by Eldar Heide in a 2005 article in *Namm och Bygd*, connects it to the feminine *víkja* (to turn aside, to deviate from a path) — implying one who turns away from the settled, lawful path to become an outlaw raider. A third proposal, advanced by the Norwegian scholar Finn Hødnebø, suggests a connection to *vika* (a sea-mile, the distance rowed between rest-shifts), making the *víkingr* a professional oarsman. The 2040 University of Yggdrasil position favours the *vík* (bay) derivation on phonological grounds, but acknowledges that the semantic convergence of all three etymologies — coastal ambush, deviant path, sea-journey — captures the multidimensional reality of Viking activity.

**The Invention of the "Viking Age"**

The period designation "Viking Age" (*vikingatiden* in Swedish) was coined by Scandinavian antiquarians of the early nineteenth century as part of the Romantic nationalist project. The Swedish poet and historian Erik Gustaf Geijer is typically credited with the first systematic use in his 1811 poem *Vikingen* and his subsequent historical writings, though the Danish antiquarian Rasmus Nyerup had employed similar periodising language as early as 1806. The term gained academic currency through the work of the Swedish archaeologist Oscar Montelius, who in the 1870s formalised the Three-Age system for Scandinavia and defined the Viking Age as a distinct archaeological period characterised by particular artefact types and burial customs.

This periodisation was never ideologically neutral. The Romantic construction of the Viking Age served the nation-building projects of Denmark, Sweden, and Norway — each country claiming the Viking heritage as a golden age of national vitality. Swedish Romantics celebrated the Varangian founders of the Russian state; Norwegian patriots invoked the martial exploits of Haraldr harðráði and the North Atlantic colonisation; Danes pointed to the Danelaw and the North Sea Empire of Cnut. The Viking was transformed from a historical phenomenon into a national symbol, and this symbolic load has only intensified in subsequent centuries. The 2040 student must therefore approach the "Viking Age" not as a natural category but as a constructed one — useful as a heuristic, but always to be interrogated.

**Dating the Viking Age: Conventions and Critiques**

The conventional dates are **793–1066 CE**:

- **793 CE** — The sack of Lindisfarne monastery on the Northumbrian coast, traditionally marked as the beginning. The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle records the event with apocalyptic language: *"Her wearð hreðe hergewin on Norðhymbra lande· ða norðmen of here on Lindisfarena igle striking"* — and the contemporary letter of Alcuin of York to King Æthelred expresses the Christian world's horror: "Never before has such terror appeared in Britain as we have now suffered from a pagan race. Behold, the church of St Cuthbert spattered with the blood of the priests of God, stripped of all its ornaments; given as a prey to pagan peoples." Yet Lindisfarne was not the first Viking raid on Britain — the Chronicle records a raid on Portland in 789, and Irish annals mention Scandinavian raiders from the 790s. Lindisfarne's prominence rests on its symbolic value: the desecration of a sacred Christian site by heathen strangers.

- **1066 CE** — The death of Haraldr harðráði Sigurðarson at the Battle of Stamford Bridge, traditionally marked as the end. Haraldr, the last great Viking king, fell in a vain attempt to conquer England; his death is supposed to close the Viking chapter. Yet the same year witnessed the Norman conquest — and the Normans were themselves descendants of Norse settlers (Northmen = Norsemen). Viking activity continued well after 1066: the Norwegian crusade king Sigurðr Jórsalafari (Sigurd the Crusader) led a fleet to the Mediterranean as late as 1107–1111, and Scandinavian raiding persisted in the Baltic for generations.

The 2040 University of Yggdrasil dating convention: **c. 750–1100 CE**, acknowledging the fuzziness of both boundaries and the regional variation in the timing of Viking activity. The earliest Scandinavian settlements in the British Isles may predate 793 by decades; the latest Norse cultural enclaves in the Atlantic persisted into the fifteenth century.

**Who Were the Vikings? A 2040 Synthesis**

The people conventionally called "Vikings" were, in aggregate, Scandinavian, agrarian, polytheistic, politically decentralised, maritime, and multilingual. They were **not** a unified nation, **not** exclusively warriors, **not** exclusively male, and **not** ethnically homogeneous. The landmark 2020 *Nature* paper by Margaryan et al., analysing 442 ancient genomes from Viking Age Scandinavia, demonstrated significant genetic heterogeneity: individuals of Sami, Finnish, Slavic, British-Irish, and Mediterranean ancestry were all present in Scandinavian burials. The study further revealed that "Viking" genetic signatures varied by region — Danish, Swedish, and Norwegian populations had distinct genetic profiles reflecting different contact networks and migration patterns. The 2040 UoY position, informed by this genomic revolution, defines the Viking phenomenon thus:

**The "Vikings" were a culturally, linguistically, and religiously connected network of Scandinavian maritime communities whose expansion — both peaceful and violent — transformed the political, economic, and cultural landscape of the northern world between c. 750–1100 CE. This expansion was not directed by a central authority but emerged from the interplay of local ambitions, environmental pressures, technological capacities, and the lure of wealth in foreign lands.**

**Christian Bias and the Source Problem**

Every textual source for Viking Age history was written by Christians, and most were written by the victims of Viking attacks. The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle, the Irish annals, the Frankish Royal Annals, the writings of Rimbert and Adam of Bremen — all are products of Christian literate culture, deeply hostile to the pagan raiders. The Norse sagas, though composed in Iceland and Norway, were written down 200–300 years after the events they describe, by Christian authors with their own agenda. As the historian Judith Jesch has observed, "we see the Vikings through Christian eyes, and what we see is refracted by the lens of a hostile worldview." The archaeological record, immune to textual bias, provides an essential corrective — but it speaks in fragments, not narratives. The 2040 student must learn to read both textual and material sources with constant awareness of this fundamental asymmetry.

## Lecture 2: Scandinavia Before the Vikings — The Pre-Viking World and the Roots of Expansion

**The Iron Age Foundation: Roman Influence and the Formation of Elite Culture**

Scandinavia before the Viking Age was not a primitive backwater awaiting the civilising touch of the wider world. From the first century CE, the Roman Empire's northern frontier generated a massive demand for amber, fur, and slaves — commodities that Scandinavia could supply. Roman artefacts in Scandinavian graves (bronze vessels, glassware, coins, weapons) attest to a thriving exchange network that brought not only goods but ideas: Roman military organisation, craft specialisation, and social hierarchy all left traces in Scandinavian material culture. The Hoby hoard (Denmark, c. 1st century CE), containing a magnificent Roman silver cup depicting Priam and Achilles, demonstrates the reach of Mediterranean luxury goods into the far north.

The Roman Iron Age (c. 1–400 CE) and the Germanic Iron Age (c. 400–800 CE) saw the emergence of the institutions that would characterise the Viking Age: the *þing* (assembly), the *goði* (cultic-political leader), the retinue or *hirð* (warband), and the hall-based chieftaincy. The Gudme-Lundeborg complex on Funen (Denmark), active from the 3rd–6th centuries, reveals a settlement cluster with craft production, trade, and cult activity — a proto-urban centre that anticipates the Viking Age trading towns of Ribe, Hedeby, and Birka. At its peak, Gudme covered over 10 hectares with at least six large hall buildings, making it the largest settlement in Iron Age Scandinavia.

**The Migration Period (c. 375–550 CE): Crisis and Reorganisation**

The collapse of Rome's western half (476 CE) disrupted Scandinavian trade networks and forced communities toward greater self-sufficiency. Local chieftains who could organise protection, extract tribute, and marshal military force gained prominence at the expense of more diffuse social arrangements. The 5th and 6th centuries saw the rise of the "central place" — a fortified or elevated site combining political, religious, and economic functions. Sites like Uppåkra (Scania), Sorte Muld (Bornholm), and Helgö (Lake Mälaren) served as nodes of elite power and cult practice, often continuously occupied for centuries.

The **536 CE volcanic eruptions** — now identified through tree-ring data and ice cores as originating from the Icelandic volcano Eldgjá or a North American caldera — produced several years of cold summers, crop failure, and famine across the northern hemisphere. The Byzantine historian Procopius recorded that "the sun gave forth its light without brightness, like the moon, during the whole year." Scandinavian archaeology shows settlement abandonment and social disruption in the decades after 536. It is plausible that this catastrophe survives in Norse memory as the **Fimbulvetr** (Fimbulwinter) — the three-year winter that precedes Ragnarǫk in the Eddic poem *Vǫluspá*. Whether or not the connection is direct, the environmental shock of the mid-6th century forced a reorganisation of Scandinavian society that, in the long term, produced the conditions for Viking Age expansion.

**The Vendel Period (c. 550–800 CE): The Immediate Precursors**

The Vendel Period — named after the boat-burial cemetery at Vendel in Uppland, Sweden — is the direct antecedent of the Viking Age. Its defining features: warrior aristocracies buried with richly decorated helmets, shields, and weapons in boat graves; the expansion of the *hirð* institution; elaborate animal-style art (Vendel Style, Style III); and the consolidation of the petty kingdoms that would later be unified into the medieval Scandinavian nations.

The boat graves at Vendel and Valsgärde (also in Uppland) are miniature precursors of the great Viking ship burials: the dead are laid in small boats (7–10m) with weapons, horses, cooking gear, and gaming pieces, accompanied by slaughtered animals. The helmets — with their magnificent crest-mounts depicting warriors and beasts — are the immediate ancestors of the one complete Viking Age helmet found at Gjermundbu. These burials demonstrate that the warrior ethos, the ship as vehicle to the afterlife, and the elite retinue culture were already fully formed centuries before the traditional start of the Viking Age.

The Nydam Boat (c. 310–320 CE, Schleswig, Germany) — a 23m clinker-built rowing vessel found in a sacrificial bog — demonstrates the advanced hull construction that would become the hallmark of the Viking ship. Clinker-built (lapstrake): keel laid first, overlapping strakes riveted with iron nails, internal ribs inserted after the hull shape is determined by the planking. This method produces a flexible, lightweight, shallow-draft vessel — a technological foundation without which the Viking expansion would have been impossible.

**Why Did the Viking Age Begin? The Multiple-Triggers Model**

No single factor explains the Viking expansion. The 2040 consensus, building on Peter Sawyer's classic 1962 formulation and refined by decades of subsequent research, identifies a convergence of:

1. **Demographic pressure** — Population growth in late 8th-century Scandinavia, outstripping the carrying capacity of marginal agricultural land. This is supported by settlement expansion data from Denmark and the Mälaren valley, though recent work by Søren Sindbæk questions whether demographic pressure alone was sufficient.

2. **Technological advantage** — The perfection of the sail-equipped longship (sail technology adopted c. 600–750 CE, possibly from Frisian or Anglo-Saxon contact) gave Scandinavians a decisive maritime edge. The combination of sail and oar allowed long-range open-ocean navigation and shallow-water river penetration — a dual capability unmatched by any contemporary vessel.

3. **Political centralisation** — The consolidation of the Danish, Norwegian, and Swedish kingdoms displaced local chieftains and their retinues. Warriors and elites who lost status in the new order could seek wealth and reputation abroad. The unification of Norway under Haraldr hárfagri (Harald Fairhair, traditionally c. 872 CE) is the paradigmatic example, though the process was more gradual than the sagas suggest.

4. **Economic opportunity** — The wealthy, undefended monasteries of the British Isles and the prosperous trading towns of the Carolingian empire presented irresistible targets. The Frisian trade network that had dominated North Sea commerce was disrupted by Frankish civil wars, creating a vacuum that Scandinavian traders and raiders could fill.

5. **Climate improvement** — The Medieval Warm Period (c. 900–1300 CE) improved agricultural yields and extended the sailing season, though its onset predates the Viking expansion by several centuries and cannot be the sole trigger.

6. **Cultural worldview** — The Norse concept of *drengskapr* (courage, martial virtue), the ideology of the retinue (*hirð*), and the religious framework that valued bold action and heroic death created a cultural context in which overseas adventure was not merely opportunistic but morally compelling.

None of these factors alone is sufficient; their convergence in the late 8th century produced the conditions for expansion. The 2040 UoY model emphasises **agency**: individual Norse communities and leaders made choices within a matrix of constraints and opportunities, not as passive respondents to ecological or demographic forces.

## Lecture 3: The Viking Ship — Technology, Construction, and the Maritime Revolution

**The Ship as Civilisational Technology**

The Viking ship is not merely a vehicle — it is the material precondition for everything we call "Viking." Without the longship, there is no Viking expansion, no North Atlantic colonisation, no Varangian Guard, no Danelaw. The ship was the Norse world's most complex technological artefact, requiring the coordinated labour of dozens of specialists — shipwrights, blacksmiths, sail-makers, rope-makers, tar-distillers — and the resources of an entire community. Building a 30-metre warship consumed approximately 600 oak trees, 8,000 iron rivets, 120 square metres of woolen sailcloth, and 800 metres of rope. The ship was, in the anthropologist Tim Ingold's terms, a "taskscape" made material — the entire community's seasonal round of forestry, agriculture, pastoralism, and craft production concentrated into a single artefact.

**The Ship Family: Typology and Function**

The Viking ship is not one design but a family of related hull-forms adapted to different purposes:

- **Drekkar** (dragon ship, *dreki*): The iconic warship, 25–36 metres, 60–100 oars, with a carved animal head on the prow. The name refers to the prow ornament, not the hull type. Carved heads could be removed or covered when approaching friendly territory — a ritual practice recorded in the sagas. The Roskilde 6 ship (c. 1025–1035 CE, 36m, the longest Viking ship yet found) represents the apex of this type.

- **Knarr** (cargo vessel, *knǫrr*): The workhorse of Viking Age trade and colonisation, 15–20 metres, with a broad beam, deep hull, and single square sail. The knarr carried 20–30 tons of cargo and could make open-ocean passages (Iceland, Greenland, Vinland) that the oar-dependent longship could not. The Skuldelev 1 ship (Roskilde Fjord, c. 1030 CE) is the best-preserved example — a robust ocean-going trader of 16.3m.

- **Karve** (small coastal vessel): A versatile multi-purpose boat, 6–12 metres, used for coastal transport, fishing, and local communication. The Gokstad ship (c. 890 CE) is sometimes classified as a large karve or a small longship, illustrating the fluidity of these categories.

- **Snekkja** (narrow fast warship, *snekkja*): A lighter, narrower warship of approximately 40 oars, optimised for speed and river navigation. The snekkja was the standard vessel of the Norwegian *leiðangr* (naval levy).

**Construction: The Clinker-Built Revolution**

The clinker-built (lapstrake) method distinguishes Scandinavian shipbuilding from the Mediterranean carvel tradition. In carvel construction, the frame is built first and the planking attached flush to it; in clinker construction, the keel is laid first and the strakes (planks) are added upward, each overlapping the one below and riveted with iron nails through the overlap. The internal ribs are inserted **after** the hull shape is determined by the planking — the hull dictates the frame, not the reverse. This produces a flexible, lightweight structure that can flex with the waves rather than fighting them — a crucial advantage in the heavy seas of the North Atlantic.

Key technical features:
- **Keel** (*kjǫlr*): A massive oak timber, T-shaped in cross-section, providing the structural backbone and hydrodynamic lateral resistance for sailing close to the wind.
- **Strakes** (*þverhúfur*): Overlapping oak planks, each approximately 25–30cm wide and 2.5cm thick, tapered and fitted with remarkable precision. The overlapping joints are sealed with wool or animal hair impregnated with pine tar (*þǫrr*).
- **Rivets** (*naglar*): Iron nails with roves (washers), approximately 8–10cm long, driven through the overlap and clenched over the rove on the interior. A 30-metre ship might contain 8,000–10,000 rivets.
- **Ribs** (*hríðir*): Naturally curved timber ("grown knees") inserted into cleats carved directly into the strakes, providing internal reinforcement without penetrating the hull planking.
- **Mast partner** (*hin sveiga*): A heavy timber across the ship at the point where the mast passes through the deck, distributing the enormous loads of the sail.
- **Steering oar** (*stýri*): A quarter-rudder mounted on the starboard (steer-board) side — the origin of the term "starboard." The port side was the *bakborði* (back-board, where the helmsman's back was turned).

**Sail Technology**

The adoption of the sail — probably in the 7th–8th centuries from contact with Frisian and Anglo-Saxon sailors — was the single most transformative technological change in Norse maritime history. A sail-equipped vessel could travel three to four times faster than a rowed boat over long distances, and it freed dozens of men from the oars to serve as warriors. The sail itself was a major investment: a 90-square-metre sail required the wool of approximately 200 sheep, spun and woven by women over the course of several months. The surviving sail fragments from the Skuldelev 1 ship show a complex woven structure with reinforced bolt-ropes and a distinctive diamond-pattern twill that combines flexibility with strength.

**Key Archaeological Finds**

The Oseberg Ship (c. 820 CE, Vestfold, Norway): A 21.5m karve buried with two high-status women, decorated with exquisite carving in the Oseberg Style. The ship was seaworthy but not designed for open-ocean voyages — its low freeboard and relatively light construction suggest coastal use. The burial contained the richest Viking Age artefact assemblage ever found: a ceremonial cart, four sledges, 13 horses, two dogs, an ox, textiles, kitchen equipment, and the famous "Buddha bucket" — a wooden pail with a figure in a cross-legged meditative pose, likely of Central Asian inspiration reached via the Volga trade route.

The Gokstad Ship (c. 890 CE, Vestfold, Norway): A 23.3m vessel, sturdily built with higher freeboard than Oseberg, suitable for open-sea voyages. Buried with a tall, powerfully built man in his 40s (the "Gokstad chieftain"), along with 64 shields, weapons, gaming pieces, and 12 horses. The ship shows evidence of repair and long service before burial.

The Roskilde 6 Ship (c. 1025–1035 CE, Roskilde Fjord, Denmark): At 36 metres, the longest Viking ship discovered, with an estimated crew of 100–120 oarsmen. This vessel, probably built for King Cnut's North Sea fleet, represents the culmination of Viking warship design — a floating fortress capable of projecting royal power across the Viking world.

## Lecture 4: The Western Expansion — Raiding, Settlement, and the Danelaw

**Lindisfarne (793 CE): Symbol and Reality**

The sack of Lindisfarne on 8 June 793 CE is the conventional starting point of the Viking Age, but its significance lies less in the event itself than in its interpretation. Lindisfarne was one of the holiest sites in Anglo-Saxon England — the burial place of St Cuthbert, the home of the Lindisfarne Gospels, a centre of learning and piety. Its desecration by pagan raiders was experienced not merely as a military catastrophe but as a cosmic rupture, a sign of divine displeasure. Alcuin of York's famous letter to King Æthelred interprets the raid as God's punishment for English sins: "Consider the dress, the way of wearing the hair, the luxurious habits of the princes and people. Look at your trimming of beard and hair, in which you have wished to resemble the pagans." The Viking raid becomes, in Alcuin's reading, a mirror in which Christian society sees its own failings.

Archaeological evidence suggests that Scandinavian contact with the British Isles predated 793 by decades. The 2023 LiDAR survey of the Shetland coastline identified several potential Norse landing sites with structures predating the traditional Viking Age, and the Cleadon hoard (South Shields, c. 780s) contains Scandinavian-type hack-silver that may indicate trading contacts before the era of raiding. The Portland raid of 789, recorded in the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle, describes the killing of the king's reeve who "wanted to find out what they were" — suggesting that Scandinavian ships were still unfamiliar enough on the south coast to provoke official curiosity rather than immediate alarm.

**The Age of Raiding (c. 793–865 CE)**

The first phase of the Viking presence in the British Isles was characterised by hit-and-run raids on coastal monasteries and settlements. The monasteries were attractive targets not because of religious antagonism per se but because they concentrated portable wealth — liturgical vessels, reliquaries, books with jewelled covers — in poorly defended locations. The Irish annals record dozens of raids from the 790s onward: Iona (795, 802, 806), Inishmurray (798), Skellig Michael (812), Bangor (824). The pattern is consistent: small fleets of 3–7 ships arrive in the summer, sack the monastery, and depart with captives and treasure.

The captive trade was central to the early Viking economy. Monastic slaves were sold in Dublin, York, and the Scandinavian markets — the Arabic geographer Ibn Fadlan describes Rus traders offering "slaves and concubines" alongside furs and amber at Bulgar on the Volga. The economic logic of slave-raiding drove the escalation from small coastal raids to larger, more ambitious expeditions.

**The Great Heathen Army (865–878 CE)**

The arrival of the *mycel heathen here* (Great Heathen Army) in East Anglia in 865 CE marked a transformation in Viking activity: from seasonal raiding to permanent conquest and settlement. The army, described in the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle as composed of "the men of the Danes," was a coalition of warbands led — according to the sagas — by the sons of Ragnarr Loðbrók (Hairy-Breeches): Ívarr inn beinlausi (Ivar the Boneless), Halfdan Ragnarsson, and Ubba. The historicity of Ragnarr himself is doubtful; he may be a saga conflation of several historical figures. But the sons' existence is attested in multiple independent sources.

The army's campaign: Northumbria conquered 866–867 (York captured, King Ælla killed by the blood-eagle, according to tradition, though the ritual's historicity is fiercely debated); East Anglia subdued 869–870 (King Edmund martyred, later canonised as St Edmund); Mercia invaded 873–874 (Repton — the army's winter camp — excavated by Martin Biddle and Birthe Kjølbye-Biddle, revealing a massive D-shaped fortification using the existing church and a Viking burial incorporating a pre-existing Mercian mausoleum); Wessex attacked 871–878 (initially successful, then checked by Alfred).

**Alfred and the Treaty of Wedmore (878 CE)**

Alfred's victory at the Battle of Edington (878 CE) and the subsequent Treaty of Wedmore established a frontier between English and Danish territory. The Danish leader Guthrum accepted baptism (taking the Christian name Æthelstan) and withdrew to East Anglia. The treaty, as recorded in the *Treaty of Alfred and Guthrum*, defined the boundary along the Thames, the Lea, the Bedfordshire Ouse, and the Watling Street — a line that approximately defined the territory later known as the **Danelaw**.

**The Danelaw (c. 878–954 CE)**

The Danelaw (*Dena lagu* in Old English, "the law of the Danes") was the territory under Danish governance and Norse legal custom. It comprised approximately the eastern third of England — from the Thames to the Tees, and from the coast to the Watling Street. Its characteristics:

- **Danish governance** — The territory was divided into boroughs (the "Five Boroughs" of Derby, Leicester, Lincoln, Nottingham, and Stamford), each administered by a Danish jarl or garrison.
- **Norse place-names** — Over 1,500 Scandinavian-derived place-names survive in the Danelaw: *-by* (village, from ON *býr*), *-thorp* (secondary settlement, from ON *þorp*), *-thwaite* (clearing, from ON *þveit*), *-ness* (headland, from ON *nes*), *-kirk* (church, from ON *kirkja*). Grimsby, Whitby, Scunthorpe, Althorpe, Braithwaite, Mallerstang — the landscape itself records the Norse presence.
- **Archaeological fusion** — The Coppergate excavation in York (1976–1981, directed by Richard Hall) revealed a thriving Norse city: timber buildings with wattle walls, workshops producing amber jewellery, ironwork, and silk caps, and international trade goods from Byzantium, the Islamic world, and the Baltic. The term "Jórvík" (the Norse name for York) entered English archaeological vocabulary through these excavations.
- **Anglo-Scandinavian cultural hybrid** — The Gosforth Cross (Cumbria, c. 930–950 CE) combines Christian iconography (the crucifixion) with Norse mythological scenes (Víðarr tearing the wolf's jaws, the bound Loki, Heimdallr with his horn) in a single monument — a visual programme that fuses two religious systems rather than simply replacing one with the other.

The last Norse king of York, Eiríkr blóðøx (Eric Bloodaxe), was expelled in 954 CE, but Norse cultural influence persisted for generations. Cnut's North Sea Empire (1016–1035) briefly reunited England under Danish rule, and Scandinavian legal concepts — particularly the jury system and the concept of communal surety — survived in English common law.

## Lecture 5: The Eastern Expansion — The Rus, the River Routes, and the Byzantine Connection

**The Varangians and the Rus: Naming and Identity**

Norse traders, raiders, and mercenaries who operated along the eastern river routes were known by different names to different peoples. The Slavs called them **Varangians** (*væringjar* in Old Norse, "sworn companions," "men under oath" — from *vár*, "pledge, oath"). The Arabs and Greeks called them **Rūs** — a term of uncertain etymology, most probably derived from the Old Norse *roðsmenn* or *róþsmenn* ("men who row"), referring to the distinctive Scandinavian method of propelling river craft by oar. The Finnish word for Sweden, *Ruotsi*, preserves the same root — a linguistic fossil confirming the northern Scandinavian origin of the Rūs.

The relationship between these Norse easterners and the Slavic and Finnic populations among whom they operated was complex: they were simultaneously traders, raiders, mercenaries, colonists, and eventually rulers. The 2040 UoY position, informed by the archaeological and numismatic evidence, rejects the older "Normanist"–"Anti-Normanist" dichotomy that framed the debate as a zero-sum contest between Scandinavian and Slavic agency. Instead, the eastern expansion is understood as a **process of ethnogenesis** — the gradual emergence of a new, hybrid "Rūs" identity from the interaction of Norse, Slavic, Finnic, and Turkic elements.

**The Primary Chronicle and the "Invitation to the Varangians"**

The *Povest' vremennykh let* (Tale of Bygone Years, commonly known as the Primary Chronicle), compiled in Kyiv c. 1113 CE by the monk Nestor, records the founding myth of the Russian state: in 862 CE, the warring Slavic and Finnic tribes of Novgorod "drove the Varangians back beyond the sea and, giving them no tribute, set out to govern themselves." But "they had no law among themselves, but rose tribe against tribe, and there was strife between them." So they sent across the sea to the Varangians, to the Rūs: "Our land is great and rich, but there is no order in it. Come and rule over us." Three brothers — Rurik, Sineus, and Truvor — answered the call, and Rurik established himself in Novgorod.

This account is plainly mythological — the three brothers' names may derive from a misunderstanding of Old Norse phrases (Rurik = Hrœrekr, Sineus = *sine hus* "with his household," Truvor = *thru voring* "with his trusted men") — but it preserves a historical kernel: that Norse political authority was invited or imposed upon Slavic communities in the 9th century, and that this process created the state structures that evolved into the medieval Russian principalities.

**The Two Great River Routes**

The eastern Viking expansion followed two principal waterways, each connecting the Baltic to a different commercial world:

1. **The Volga Route** — Baltic → Neva River → Lake Ladoga → Staraya Ladoga (Aldeigjuborg) → Volkhov River → Novgorod (Hólmgarðr) → Lake Ilmen → Volga River → Caspian Sea → Baghdad. This route carried furs, slaves, honey, wax, and amber southward and returned with silver dirhams, silk, spices, and luxury goods. The Volga route served the Abbasid caliphate's enormous demand for northern commodities. Over 80,000 dirham coins have been found in Scandinavian hoards — a staggering testament to the volume of this trade. The Spillings hoard (Gotland, 1999) alone contained 14,295 dirhams, the largest Viking Age silver hoard ever found.

2. **The Dnieper Route** — Baltic → Novgorod → Lovat River → portage → Dnieper River → Kyiv (Kænugarðr) → Black Sea → Constantinople (Miklagarðr, "the Great City"). This route led to the Byzantine Empire and the Varangian Guard. The Dnieper was the more dangerous route: it required seven portages (overland carries) around rapids, each of which was an ambush point for Pecheneg nomads. The *De Administrando Imperio* of Constantine VII Porphyrogenitus (c. 950 CE) describes the Rūs voyage down the Dnieper in vivid detail, including the names of the rapids in both Slavic and Norse — a priceless linguistic document.

**Ibn Fadlan and the Rūs**

The most famous Arabic description of the Rūs is the account of Ahmad ibn Fadlan, a secretary of the Abbasid caliph who encountered a group of Rūs traders on the Volga in 922 CE. His description of their physical appearance, customs, and — most famously — their ship funeral ritual, is one of the most vivid ethnographic documents of the Viking Age:

"I saw the Rūsiyyah — I have never seen more perfect physiques, tall as palm trees, blonde and ruddy... They are the filthiest of God's creatures. They do not clean themselves after urinating or defecating, nor after sexual intercourse, and they do not wash after eating. They are like stray donkeys."

Yet Ibn Fadlan also records their elaborate funeral rites for a dead chieftain: a ceremonial ship-procession, the sacrifice of animals and a slave woman, and the burning of the ship — a description that parallels the archaeological evidence from the Oseberg and Gokstad burials and provides our most detailed account of a Viking Age cremation ritual. The tension between Ibn Fadlan's disgust and his meticulous observation is itself informative: it reveals the cultural distance between the Islamic and Norse worlds while simultaneously demonstrating that cultural alterity does not preclude careful ethnographic attention.

**The Varangian Guard**

The Varangian Guard (*tagma ton Varangon*) was the Byzantine Empire's elite imperial bodyguard, established c. 988 CE under Emperor Basil II. Its members were initially predominantly Norse and Rus, later supplemented by Anglo-Saxon exiles after the Norman Conquest of 1066. The Guard's functions: personal protection of the emperor, service as a crack assault troops in major campaigns, and garrison duty in Constantinople. Their distinctive weapon was the *rhomphaia* — a heavy two-handed axe descended from the Viking dane-axe, which earned them the Greek nickname *pelekyphoroi* ("axe-bearers").

The most famous Varangian was **Haraldr Sigurðarson** (Harald Hardrada, 1015–1066), who served in the Guard c. 1034–1042 before returning to Norway to claim the throne. The Byzantine chronicler Michael Psellos describes him as "a man of enormous size and fierce spirit" who "excelled all others in the vigour of his body and the boldness of his spirit." Haraldr's Varangian service illustrates the mobility of the Norse world: a Norwegian prince, fighting for the Byzantine emperor in the Mediterranean, accumulating the wealth that would fund his bid for the Norwegian crown.

## Lecture 6: The North Atlantic Expansion — Iceland, Greenland, and Vinland

**Iceland: Landnám and the Free State**

Iceland was settled entirely by Norse colonists in the period c. 870–930 CE — the only major territory in the Viking world with no indigenous population to displace or assimilate. The *Landnámabók* (Book of Settlements), compiled in the 12th century but drawing on older oral traditions, records the names, origins, and land-claims of over 400 settlers, their routes from Norway (often via the British Isles, where they acquired Celtic slaves and spouses), and the distribution of land across the island.

The first permanent settler was traditionally **Ingólfr Arnarson**, who landed at Reykjavík c. 874 CE — though archaeological evidence from the Suðurgata excavation (1971–1973) suggests Norse occupation from c. 871 CE, consistent with tephrochronological dating using the 871 CE volcanic ash layer (the "Landnám tephra"). The settlement pattern was rapid: by 930 CE, all habitable land was claimed, and the population may have reached 20,000–30,000.

The **Alþingi** (Althing), founded at Þingvellir (Thing-Fields) in 930 CE, is one of the oldest parliamentary institutions in the world. The Icelandic Free State (930–1262) was a society with laws but no state — a commonwealth governed by the *goðar* (chieftain-priests) through the assembly system, with enforcement delegated to private individuals. There was no executive authority, no police force, no standing army. Legal disputes were resolved through arbitration, compensation, or — if these failed — feud and outlawry. The system was remarkably stable for over three centuries, a testament to the power of law as a social technology even in the absence of coercive enforcement.

The **Grágás** (Grey Goose) lawcode, surviving in two medieval manuscripts (Konungsbók and Staðarhólsbók), provides the most detailed picture of Norse legal thought anywhere in the Viking world. It covers property law, torts, criminal law, family law, inheritance, and religious practice with extraordinary specificity — including provisions on the exact compensation for each body part injured (a finger was worth one-quarter mark; a thumb, one-half mark; an ear, one-quarter mark), the legal status of milk and dairy products, and the precise calendar of Christian observances after the conversion.

**Greenland: The Western and Eastern Settlements**

**Eiríkr rauði** (Erik the Red) was exiled from Iceland for manslaughter c. 982 CE and sailed west to a land he had heard reported by earlier Norse voyagers. He named it **Grœnland** (Greenland) — "for he said that people would be much more tempted to go there if it had an attractive name" (Eiríks saga rauða). The name was not pure deception: the southern fjords of Greenland, in the Medieval Warm Period, could support pasture and hay-production, though the interior ice sheet made the habitable zone a narrow coastal strip.

Two settlement clusters were established: the **Eastern Settlement** (Eystribyggð, near modern Qaqortoos) and the **Western Settlement** (Vestribyggð, near modern Nuuk). At their peak, the settlements may have supported 2,500–5,000 inhabitants, with 16 churches, a bishopric at Garðar (established 1126 CE), and regular trade with Iceland and Norway. The Greenland Norse exported walrus ivory, narwhal horn (sold as "unicorn horn" in European markets), seal skins, and wool — commodities that maintained their economic viability even as the climate deteriorated.

The disappearance of the Norse Greenland colonies c. 1450 CE has generated extensive debate. The 2040 consensus identifies multiple contributing factors:
- **Climate change** — The Little Ice Age (c. 1300–1850) shortened the growing season and reduced pasture productivity. The Greenland ice core record shows a dramatic temperature decline from the 14th century onward.
- **Trade disruption** — As African elephant ivory replaced walrus ivory in European markets, the economic rationale for the Greenland colony weakened. The Black Death (1347–1351) devastated Norway, disrupting the trade routes that sustained the colony.
- **Inuit (Thule) expansion** — The Thule Inuit migrated into Greenland from the north c. 1200 CE, competing with the Norse for marine mammal resources. The Norse and Inuit appear to have had limited direct contact; Norse archaeology shows no Inuit artefacts in Norse contexts and vice versa, suggesting mutual avoidance rather than conflict or exchange.
- **Cultural inflexibility** — The Norse maintained a European pastoral economy (cattle, sheep, hay) in an increasingly marginal environment, while the Inuit adapted with sophisticated marine hunting technologies (kayak, umiak, harpoon). The Norse refusal or inability to adopt Inuit techniques — Thomas McGovern's "subsistence paradox" — may have been the critical failure.

**Vinland: The Norse Reach the Americas**

**Leifr Eiríksson**, son of Erik the Red, sailed west from Greenland c. 1000 CE and encountered three lands: **Helluland** (Baffin Island, "Slab-Land"), **Markland** (Labrador, "Forest-Land"), and **Vinland** ("Wine-Land," probably Newfoundland or points south). The *Grænlendinga saga* and *Eiríks saga rauða* give somewhat differing accounts of the Vinland voyages, but both agree that the Norse found a land rich in grapes (or berries), salmon, and game — a Mediterranean abundance that must have seemed miraculous to men from subarctic Greenland.

**Þorfinnr Karlsefni** attempted a permanent settlement c. 1010 CE with 60 men and 5 women, cattle, and supplies. The settlement was abandoned after three years of conflict with the Indigenous peoples the sagas call **Skrælingjar** — a Norse term of uncertain meaning (possibly "skin-wearers," from *skrá*, "dried skin"). The sagas describe two types of Skrælingjar: small, dark people in skin boats (likely Inuit or Dorset culture), and larger people with poles and screams (likely Beothuk or Mi'kmaq).

**L'Anse aux Meadows** (Newfoundland, excavated by Helge Ingstad and Anne Stine Ingstad 1961–1968) is the only confirmed pre-Columbian Norse site in the Americas. The site consists of eight turf-and-timber buildings (three dwellings, a forge, a workshop, and storage structures), a Norse-style forge with a stone anvil and slag, and a carpentry shop with a cracked whetstone. Crucially, butternuts (*Juglans cinerea*) were found at the site — a species that does not grow north of New Brunswick, suggesting that the Norse explored south of Newfoundland. L'Anse aux Meadows was likely a base camp for further exploration, not a permanent colony — a staging point for the Vinland that the sagas describe.

The 2040 University of Yggdrasil's Virtual Reconstruction Lab has produced a VR simulation of the L'Anse aux Meadows site as it may have appeared c. 1010 CE, integrating the archaeological evidence with environmental data (climate models, pollen analysis, sea-level reconstructions) and saga narrative. Students can walk through the reconstructed buildings, examine the forge and workshop, and experience the landscape that the Norse encountered — a pedagogical tool that bridges the gap between textual description and spatial understanding.

## Lecture 7: Viking Age Society — Law, Gender, and Social Structure

**The Thing: Assembly Government and the Rule of Law**

Viking Age governance was exercised through the **þing** (thing, assembly), a periodic gathering of free men that performed legislative, judicial, and executive functions. The thing system is one of the Norse world's most enduring contributions to European political culture: the Icelandic Alþingi (930 CE), the Manx Tynwald (possibly pre-Viking but certainly Norse-influenced), and the Dano-Norwegian Frostuþing and Gulaþing laws are all descendants of this tradition. The English word "thing" itself derives from the Old Norse *þing* — originally "a meeting, an assembly," before semantic drift reduced it to its modern meaning of "an object."

The thing's functions were comprehensive:
- **Legislative** — New laws were proposed and voted upon. The *lagsǫgumaðr* (lawspeaker) in Iceland was responsible for reciting the entire lawcode from memory over a three-year cycle — a living constitution preserved in oral tradition.
- **Judicial** — Disputes were heard, verdicts rendered, and penalties assessed. Panels of judges (*dómarar*) were appointed from the community of free men.
- **Executive** — Outlawry was declared, alliances were sworn, and diplomatic business was conducted.

Within the community of free men (*bœndr*, singular *bóndi*), the thing was formally egalitarian — the poorest farmer had the same legal standing as the richest chieftain. This equality was, however, constrained by the exclusions: **women, slaves (*þrælar*), and foreigners** could not attend or participate. The thing's egalitarianism was therefore an egalitarianism among propertied male householders, not a universal political equality.

**Social Stratification: Jarls, Karls, and Þrælar**

The Rígsþula (Lay of Ríg), an Eddic poem probably composed in the 11th century, describes the god Heimdallr (disguised as Ríg) visiting three households and begetting the three social classes:
- **Jarls** (nobles) — Red-cheeked, fair-haired, skilled in weapons and governance. Ríg teaches the first Jarl the runes, the exercise of power, and the management of estates.
- **Karls** (free farmers) — Ruddy, capable, skilled in agriculture and craft. The Karl's hall is busy with the sounds of labour — the thud of the loom, the scrape of the shuttle, the clatter of the flour-mill.
- **Þrælar** (slaves) — Dark, stooped, coarse-featured, assigned the hardest physical labour — thrashing grain, hauling dung, digging peat.

The Rígsþula is a mythological justification for social hierarchy, not a neutral description. But the tripartite structure it describes — aristocracy, free farmers, slaves — corresponds broadly to the archaeological and legal evidence. Viking Age slavery was extensive and economically central: slaves performed the hardest agricultural labour, served as domestic workers, and were traded as commodities. The Arabic sources describe Viking slave-raiding and slave-trading in detail; the Irish annals record the taking of thousands of captives; and the Frankish sources describe the trade in Christian slaves to Muslim Spain. The Viking Age economy was, in significant part, a slave economy — a fact that 2040 scholarship is increasingly foregrounding after decades of comparative neglect.

**Gender in Viking Age Society: Agency and Constraint**

Viking Age women's legal position was, by the standards of contemporary European societies, remarkably favourable. Norse law granted women the right to **own property**, to **initiate divorce** (on grounds including the husband's failure to provide conjugal relations or wearing effeminate clothing), to **manage households** during their husbands' absences, and to **act as religious specialists** (vǫlur, gyðjur). Women's legal constraints were equally real: they could **not attend the thing**, could **not serve as goðar**, were subject to **bride price** (*mundr*) and **arranged marriage**, and had limited inheritance rights compared to sons.

The **Oseberg ship burial** (c. 834 CE) remains the most spectacular evidence for high-status women in the Viking world. The burial contained two women — one approximately 80 years old, the other approximately 50 — interred with a richly decorated ship, a ceremonial cart, four sledges, 13 horses, two dogs, an ox, kitchen equipment, textiles, and personal ornaments. The older woman suffered from Morgagni-Stewart-Morel syndrome, which would have produced a distinctive physical appearance. DNA analysis showed the two women were not closely related. Whether they were queen and servant, priestess and acolyte, or two independently powerful women remains debated. The burial proves beyond question that Viking Age society had **powerful, wealthy, publicly visible women** whose status was displayed in monumental funerary ritual.

The **Birka warrior grave Bj 581** (excavated 1878, Birka, Sweden) — long assumed to be a male warrior on the basis of the weapons and gaming pieces interred with the skeleton — was re-analysed by Hedenstierna-Jonson et al. in 2017 using ancient DNA, which revealed that the skeleton was chromosomally female (XX). The grave contained a sword, axe, spear, armour-piercing arrows, a battle knife, two shields, and two horses — a complete warrior's kit. The publication triggered fierce debate: some scholars argued that the sex determination must be wrong (osteological sex assessment had previously been ambiguous), while others accepted the finding and questioned whether a woman buried with weapons necessarily "was" a warrior. The 2040 UoY position acknowledges the uncertainty but notes that the burden of proof now lies with those who would deny the possibility of female warriors, not with those who would assert it.

## Lecture 8: Viking Age Religion — Gods, Sacrifice, and the Sacred Landscape

*(This lecture complements NP101 and NP102 with archaeological and cultural context for Viking Age practice. Students who have completed NP101 will find some familiar material reframed here through the lens of material culture and ritual archaeology.)*

**Blót — The Central Ritual of Viking Age Religion**

The **blót** (sacrifice) was the central ritual act of Viking Age Norse religion, the mechanism by which humans established and maintained reciprocal relationships with the gods. The word *blót* is cognate with Old English *blēt* and Gothic *bloþ* — the root meaning is "blood" or "strengthening by blood," and the ritual's core symbolism was the exchange of life-force between the human and divine communities.

The major seasonal blóts, as recorded in Hákonar saga góða (Heimskringla) and other sources:
- **Vetrarblót / Vetrarnætr** (Winter Nights, mid-October): Sacrifice to the *dísir* (female protective spirits) and Freyr for prosperity and good harvest.
- **Jólablót / Jól** (Yule, mid-January): Sacrifice to Óðinn and Freyr for the return of the sun and the success of the coming year. The Christian Christmas absorbed and transformed this festival.
- **Sigrblót / Sumarblót** (Summer sacrifice, mid-April): Sacrifice to Óðinn for victory in the coming season of raiding and warfare.

The ritual pattern reconstructed from Hákonar saga góða: **Preparation** (the sacred animal is led to the altar) → **Sacrifice** (the animal is killed — by spear if dedicated to Óðinn, by hammer-stroke if dedicated to Þórr) → **Blood sprinkling** (*hlaut*, the sacrificial blood, is collected in a bowl and sprinkled on participants, walls, and god-figures) → **Cooking and communal feast** (the meat is boiled in cooking pits and shared among all participants) → **Toasting** (the first toast to Óðinn, the second to Njǫrðr and Freyr for peace and good seasons, the third the *bragarfull* — the "cup of the chieftain," drunk with an oath) → **Oath-taking** (solemn vows sworn over the cup — vows of revenge, vows of marriage, vows of great deeds).

**Sacred Spaces: Hov, Vé, and Hörgr**

Viking Age religious practice operated in multiple spatial contexts:

- **Hof** (temple, indoor cult building): The term *hof* denotes a dedicated cult building — a structure designed specifically for ritual use. The existence of purpose-built temples has been debated, but the 2016–2023 excavations at Tissø (Denmark) by Lars Jørgensen revealed a large feasting hall (over 40m long) with a smaller adjacent building interpreted as a cult house, consistently rebuilt over 500 years — a clear case of a dedicated ritual structure within a chiefly complex.

- **Vé** (sacred enclosure, outdoor grove): The *vé* was an outdoor sacred space — a grove, a clearing, a natural feature set apart for divine contact. The *völva* (seeress) in *Eiríks saga rauða* performs her ritual outdoors on a raised platform, surrounded by women singing the *varðlokur* (guardian-songs). The concept of *vé* survives in Scandinavian place-names: Venable, Viby, Västra Vingåker.

- **Hörgr** (stone cairn altar): A smaller outdoor altar of piled stones, mentioned in the Eddic poem *Hyndluljóð* where the speaker boasts that "a hörgr I have made for Þórr" (*hörgr nam ek Þórr at görva*). The hörgr represents the simplest form of sacred space — a cairn where offerings could be placed without the apparatus of formal ritual.

**The Temple at Uppsala**

The temple at Gamla Uppsala (Old Uppsala), described by Adam of Bremen c. 1070 CE, is the most famous — and most controversial — Viking Age cult site. Adam describes a temple housing three god-statues: "Þórr, the mightiest, dwells in the sky and rules over thunder and lightning, winds and showers, fair weather and crops; Óðinn, the god of war, bestows courage upon men in battle; Freyr bestows peace and pleasure upon mortals." Every nine years, a great blót was held at which nine males of every living creature were sacrificed — a total of 72 victims (9 × 9 = 81, or 9 × 8 species), their bodies hung in the sacred grove beside the temple.

Archaeological investigation of Gamla Uppsala has revealed a major cult centre: a royal burial mound complex (the three great barrows, traditionally identified as the graves of the Yngling kings Aun, Egil, and Adils), a feasting hall beneath the present church, and extensive settlement remains. However, **no temple building has been identified**. The 2040 position, following the work of Neil Price and Olof Sundqvist, is that Adam's account likely conflates the open-air cult site at Gamla Uppsala with his knowledge of Continental Christian churches — he may have projected an indoor temple onto a ritual landscape that was primarily open-air. The "temple" may never have existed as a building; the sacred grove and the cult feast-hall together constituted the Uppsala cult complex.

## Lecture 9: Viking Warfare — Weapons, Tactics, and the Warrior Ethos

**The Material Culture of Viking Warfare**

Viking Age warfare was fought with a characteristic kit of weapons and equipment, each item carrying both practical and symbolic significance:

- **Sword** (*sverð*): The most prestigious weapon, often a Frankish import — the Ulfberht and Ingelrii inscriptions, found on over 170 blades, attest to a massive trade in high-quality Frankish steel into Scandinavia (despite Charlemagne's capillary prohibitions on selling weapons to pagans). Pattern-welded blades (multiple rods of different carbon-content steel twisted and forge-welded) produced the distinctive "serpent" pattern visible on etched blades. A good sword was worth approximately 16 milk-cows — a significant fraction of a farmer's annual wealth. Swords were named: *Leggjǫr* (Leg-biter), *Gunnlogi* (Battle-flame), *Nyðr* (Viper), *Hersteitra* (Army-thunderer) — the name conferring identity and luck upon the weapon.

- **Axe** (*øx*): The Viking axe came in two main forms: the **bearded axe** (*skeggøx*, with an extended lower edge for hooking shields and pulling opponents off-balance) and the **dane-axe** (*breiðøx*, the two-handed broad-axe carried by the housecarls of Cnut's army). The dane-axe, with its 1–1.5m haft and 20–30cm crescent blade, could cleave through shield and mail in a single stroke — the Bayeux Tapestry shows housecarls wielding dane-axes at Hastings.

- **Spear** (*spjót*): The most universal weapon — cheap, versatile, and effective in both the thrown and thrust roles. Two main types: the **light throwing spear** (*snœris-pjót*) with a narrow socketed head, and the **heavy thrusting spear** (*atgeirr*) with a broad blade. The spear was the weapon of the ordinary warrior — every free man could afford one.

- **Shield** (*skjǫldr*): The standard Viking shield was approximately 1 metre in diameter, constructed of thin wooden planks (typically 7–8mm thick lime or fir) over a leather backing, with a central iron boss (*náf*). Thin, light, and disposable — designed to absorb a few blows before being discarded. The Gokstad ship burial contained 64 shields, painted yellow and black, mounted along the gunwales.

- **Bow** (*bogi*): Longbows with self-nocks, used for both hunting and warfare. Arrowheads included bodkin types (armour-piercing, narrow square-section) and broadheads (for unarmoured targets). The sagas describe archers as specialist troops — the bow was not the universal weapon it became in later medieval warfare.

- **Armour**: **Chain mail** (*brynja*) was expensive and probably restricted to the wealthier warriors and the retinues of kings and jarls. A complete mail shirt required 20,000–30,000 riveted iron rings and took a skilled armorer months to produce. **Leather or padded gambesons** were probably more common but leave minimal archaeological traces. The **Gjermundbu helmet** (c. 900 CE, Norway) is the only complete Viking Age helmet found — a rounded iron cap with a nose guard and spectacle-like ocular protection. **Horned helmets are a 19th-century invention** — popularised by Wagner's costume designer Carl Emil Doepler for the 1876 Bayreuth production of *Der Ring des Nibelungen*. No Viking Age helmet with horns has ever been found.

**Shield Wall and Battle Tactics**

The fundamental Viking battle formation was the **skjaldborg** (shield wall) — a line of overlapping shields with spears projecting through the gaps. The shield wall was a test of endurance and collective discipline: two lines pushing, stabbing, and cursing until one broke. The sagas describe the shield wall as a terrible place — noise, sweat, blood, the press of bodies, the inability to see more than the man opposite you.

Breaking the shield wall required specialised tactics:
- **Svynfylking** (swine array, boar's head): A wedge formation designed to punch through the enemy line by concentrating force at a single point. attributed to Óðinn himself in sagas — the "boar's snout" is the god's gift to warriors.
- **Flanking manoeuvres**: Using the mobility provided by ships to land forces behind the enemy position.
- **Feigned retreat** (*þverbrǫð*): Simulating flight to draw the enemy out of formation, then turning to attack the disordered pursuers — a tactic used with devastating effect at the Battle of Maldon (991 CE).

Viking warfare emphasised **speed, surprise, and mobility** — the ship delivered warriors who fought on land, and the ability to strike suddenly and withdraw before a response could be organised was the strategic cornerstone of the raiding economy.

**The Warrior Ethos: Drengskapr and the Economy of Honour**

The Norse warrior ethic was codified in a constellation of interconnected virtues:
- **Drengskapr** (courage, magnanimity, the quality of being a *dreng* — a brave, honourable man)
- **Tryggð** (loyalty, fidelity to one's lord and comrades)
- **Sœmð** (honour, reputation, the social standing conferred by public recognition of worth)
- **Íþróttir** (skill, prowess, the physical and martial accomplishments that demonstrated personal quality)
- **Gjafar mildi** (generosity, the lord's obligation to reward his followers with gifts — the fundamental mechanism of the retinue system)

These virtues were not merely aspirational — they constituted a **moral economy** in which honour was the primary currency. A warrior's *sœmð* was his most valuable possession, more important than life itself: "Cattle die, kinsmen die, the self must also die; I know one thing that never dies: the reputation of each dead man" (*Hávamál* 77). The lord's *gjafar mildi* was not charity but investment: gifts of weapons, armour, silver, and land bound the recipient to the giver in a network of reciprocal obligation. The retinue (*hirð*) was maintained by this cycle of generosity and loyalty — a cycle that could be broken only at the cost of honour.

## Lecture 10: Viking Art — Styles, Motifs, and Cosmological Meaning

**The Six Sequential Styles**

Viking art is conventionally divided into six sequential styles, each named after a type-site or characteristic artefact. These styles overlap in time and are not rigidly bounded — a single artefact may combine elements of two or more styles. The sequence:

1. **Oseberg Style** (c. 780–870 CE): Named after the Oseberg ship burial, this style features the **"gripping beast"** — a dynamic, interlaced animal figure that grips its own body, adjacent beasts, or the surrounding frame with tiny, lively paws. The Oseberg animal post is the iconic example: a sinuous, almost serpentine creature carved with extraordinary energy and fluidity. The style is exuberant, almost frenzied — the beasts seem to wriggle and strain against the boundaries of their frames. The 2040 UoY art historian Ingrid Yngvildsdóttir interprets the gripping beast as a visualisation of the cosmos holding itself together — the interlocked bodies of the beasts enacting the tension between *órðr* (order) and *ginnungagap* (chaos).

2. **Borre Style** (c. 840–970 CE): Named after the Borre ship burial mounds in Vestfold, Norway. Characterised by **geometric ring-chain interlace** — a regular, repeating pattern of interlocking rings and chains that creates a dense, textured surface. The Borre style also features a distinctive "mask" motif — a frontal animal face with round, bulging eyes and a lopped nose, often surrounded by ribbon-like tendrils. The style is more rigid and formal than Oseberg — the wild energy of the gripping beast has been disciplined into regular, almost mechanical patterns.

3. **Jellinge Style** (c. 900–970 CE): Named after the Jelling stones (Denmark), erected by King Gorm the Old and his son Harald Bluetooth. The Jellinge style features **simplified ribbon-animals in S-shapes** — streamlined, elegant, almost calligraphic creatures rendered in a single continuous line. The Jelling stone lion (probably inspired by the Evangelist symbol from a Bible manuscript) is the type-example. The style shows the influence of Insular (British-Irish) and Carolingian manuscript decoration — a sign of increasing cultural contact with the Christian world.

4. **Mammen Style** (c. 950–1025 CE): Named after the Mammen axe (Denmark), an iron axe with silver-inlay decoration depicting a foliate creature — part animal, part plant. The Mammen style is more **naturalistic** than its predecessors: animals have recognisable bodies with legs, heads, and tails, and they are often combined with **tendril scrolls** — plant-like spiralling forms that evoke growing vegetation. The Mammen axe's beast is simultaneously animal and botanical — a hybrid form that blurs the boundary between the organic and the vegetal.

5. **Ringerike Style** (c. 980–1090 CE): Named after the Ringerike district of Norway, where many runestones display the style. The Ringerike style develops the Mammen plant-scroll into **elaborate foliage interlaced with animals**, with clear influence from Anglo-Saxon and Ottonian manuscript art. The animals are larger, more assertive, and more dynamically posed — often shown in active movement, with extended limbs and open jaws. The Vang stone (Norway) and the London rune-stone (St Paul's churchyard) are characteristic examples.

6. **Urnes Style** (c. 1040–1130 CE): Named after the Urnes stave church (Norway), whose north portal preserves the finest example. The Urnes style features **thin, elegant, fighting animals** — elongated, serpentine creatures locked in combat, rendered with extraordinary delicacy and refinement. The lines are thin, fluid, and almost calligraphic; the animals' bodies are stretched to the breaking point. The Urnes style is the last purely Norse art style; it transitions into the Romanesque, which absorbs and transforms its vocabulary into a pan-European visual language.

**Meaning in Viking Art: Decoration as Cosmology**

Viking art was not "merely" decorative — if such a category can be said to exist in a culture that did not distinguish between the aesthetic and the symbolic. The 2040 UoY position, building on the work of Lise Gjedde Nørgaard and Ingrid Yngvildsdóttir, argues that Viking art is **narrative and cosmological** — the decorative and the meaningful are inseparable.

The gripping beast of the Oseberg style may represent the cosmos holding itself together — the interlocked bodies enacting the tension between order and chaos that structures Norse cosmology. The fighting animals of the Urnes style may encode the struggle between the forces of destruction and renewal that culminates in Ragnarǫk. The interlacing patterns that pervade all six styles may represent the Norns weaving fate — the tangled lines of *ørlog* (primal law, destiny) rendered in visual form. The plant scrolls of the Mammen and Ringerike styles may evoke Yggdrasil's foliage — the world-tree whose branches shelter the nine worlds. These readings are not certain — Viking art does not come with labels — but they are plausible within the framework of Norse cosmological thought, and they give the art a depth that pure formalism cannot access.

The 2040 UoY Digital Art History Project has employed AI-assisted pattern recognition to analyse over 12,000 Viking Age artefact images, identifying recurring compositional structures and motif-distributions that were previously invisible to manual analysis. The project has revealed, for example, that the gripping beast motif appears disproportionately on objects associated with elite women (Oseberg, sets of jewellery) while the Urnes fighting animal appears disproportionately on church architecture and Christian memorials — a pattern that may reflect gendered and religious shifts in the symbolic vocabulary of the late Viking Age.

## Lecture 11: Christianity and Conversion — The End of the Viking Age

**The Process of Conversion by Region**

The conversion of Scandinavia to Christianity was not a single event but a protracted, regionally variable process spanning approximately 150 years (c. 950–1100 CE). Each region followed a different trajectory:

**Denmark:** Haraldr blátǫnn Gormsson (Harald Bluetooth) "made the Danes Christian" according to the larger Jelling stone (c. 965 CE), which bears the runic inscription: "King Harald ordered this monument made in memory of Gorm his father and Þyrvé his mother — that Harald who won for himself all Denmark and Norway and made the Danes Christian." The conversion was pragmatic — Christianity offered literate clergy (essential for administration), continental alliances (marriage into the Ottonian imperial family), and centralised religious authority (a single church organisation answerable to the king was more useful than a dispersed network of local cults). Harald's conversion did not immediately eliminate pagan practice — Adam of Bremen records that many Danes continued to sacrifice at sacred groves well into the 11th century — but it established Christianity as the official religion of the Danish state.

**Norway:** The Norwegian conversion was driven by two king-saints: **Óláfr Tryggvason** (c. 995–1000 CE), who used force and intimidation to impose baptism on coastal communities, and **Óláfr Haraldsson** (St Olav, 1015–1028 CE), who systematised church-building, imported English clergy, and enforced Christian law with increasing rigour. Óláfr Haraldsson's martyrdom at the Battle of Stiklestad (1030 CE) — killed by a coalition of pagan and Christian Norwegian chieftains who resented his centralising ambition — transformed him into Norway's patron saint and ensured the ultimate triumph of Christianity, if not of royal authority. The 2040 University of Yggdrasil's VR reconstruction of Stiklestad, based on the topographic analysis of the battlefield terrain and the saga descriptions, allows students to experience the landscape of this pivotal event — a landscape that is now a Norwegian national heritage site.

**Iceland:** The conversion of Iceland, decided by law at the Alþingi in **999/1000 CE**, is the most philosophically interesting case. The lawspeaker Þorgeirr Þorkelsson, a pagan, was given the authority to decide the island's religious future after violent disputes between Christian and pagan factions threatened civil war. After a day and a night of meditation under a cloak (the *þvengr*), Þorgeirr announced his decision: Iceland would adopt Christianity. But he secured three concessions:
1. **Private pagan worship** could continue — the law would not investigate what happened inside people's homes.
2. **Horse meat** could still be consumed — a practice forbidden by Christian dietary law.
3. **Infant exposure** (*útburðr*) could still be practised — a form of post-natal population control incompatible with Christian doctrine.

All three concessions were phased out within a century — the first by moral pressure, the second by gradual dietary change, the third by ecclesiastical enforcement. But the decision itself illustrates the Norse approach to religious change: **pragmatic, negotiated, and collective** — a political decision made by a legal assembly, not a spiritual revelation imposed by a prophet.

**Sweden:** The slowest region to convert. The temple (or cult complex) at Gamla Uppsala was still active in the 1070s, according to Adam of Bremen. The **Sigtuna runestones** (c. 1000–1050 CE) show a gradual transition from pagan to Christian memorial formulae, but pagan burial practices persisted in rural areas into the 12th century. Full Christianisation of Sweden is conventionally dated to c. 1100 CE, but the process was incomplete even then — the **Sami** population of northern Scandinavia retained their shamanic religious practices into the 17th century (and, in modified form, to the present day).

**Consequences of Conversion**

The adoption of Christianity transformed every aspect of Scandinavian society:

- **Writing and literacy** — The Latin alphabet replaced runes as the primary writing system. Runes persisted for informal and monumental use, but the church introduced parchment, ink, and the full apparatus of literate administration. The oldest surviving Scandinavian manuscript in Latin script is the **Liber daticus Lundensis** (Lund, c. 1120 CE).
- **Political centralisation** — The church provided kings with a centralised, literate bureaucracy, a universal moral framework that justified royal authority, and an international network of alliances. The conversion accelerated the consolidation of the three Scandinavian kingdoms.
- **Cultural transformation** — Church art, architecture, and music introduced new aesthetic categories. The stave churches of Norway (Borgund, Urnes, Heddal) are hybrid structures — Norse building techniques (the stave-post construction) applied to Christian spatial requirements — that physically embody the cultural fusion of the conversion period.
- **Social change** — Christian doctrine imposed monogamy (eliminating the practice of concubinage), prohibited slave-raiding against Christians (restricting the Viking economy), and forbade infanticide (altering demographic patterns). These changes were neither immediate nor complete, but their cumulative effect was transformative.
- **End of the Viking Age** — Christian kings could no longer raid Christian monasteries with religious justification. The Viking economy of raiding and slave-trading was increasingly incompatible with Christian Europe's political norms. The conversion did not end Norse maritime activity — the Norwegian crusade of Sigurðr Jórsalafari (1107–1111) shows that martial adventure persisted — but it reoriented Norse expansion from raiding to crusading, from predation to participation in the Christian European state system.

## Lecture 12: The Viking Legacy — From the Middle Ages to the 2040s

**The Long Shadow of the North**

The Viking Age ended c. 1100 CE, but the Viking legacy has never stopped being produced, contested, and reimagined. This final lecture surveys the afterlife of the Viking past — from medieval chronicles to 2040 digital humanities — and examines the political and ethical stakes of how we remember the Vikings.

**Medieval and Early Modern Reception**

The medieval Scandinavians remembered the Viking Age through the sagas — Icelandic prose narratives composed primarily in the 13th century, looking back to the 9th–11th centuries with a mixture of nostalgia, moral instruction, and national self-fashioning. The **kings' sagas** (Heimskringla, Morkinskinna, Fagrskinna) narrated the deeds of Norwegian monarchs; the **Íslendingasögur** (sagas of Icelanders) dramatised the feuds and adventures of the settlement generation; the **fornaldarsögur** (legendary sagas) reached further back into the mythic past. These texts are not transparent windows onto the Viking Age — they are literary compositions, shaped by the concerns of their 13th-century authors — but they transmitted the Viking past into the Scandinavian literary tradition with extraordinary vividness and narrative power.

The early modern period saw the first antiquarian interest in Viking Age material culture. The Swedish scholars of the 17th century — Olof Rudbeck the Elder, Johannes Schefferus — constructed elaborate historical narratives linking the Goths and the Vikings to a supposed "Gothic" civilisation that predated and surpassed the classical world. Rudbeck's *Atland eller Manheim* (1679–1702) identified Sweden with Atlantis and the Swedish language with the original tongue of humanity — a preposterous but influential fantasy that shaped Scandinavian antiquarianism for a century.

**The 19th Century: Romantic Nationalism and the Viking Revival**

The 19th century transformed the Viking from a historical curiosity into a national symbol. The Romantic movement's valorisation of the primitive, the authentic, and the heroic found a ready vessel in the Viking past. Across Scandinavia:

- **Sweden** celebrated the Varangian founders of the Russian state and the Gothic ancestors of European civilisation. Götiska Förbundet (the Geatish Society, founded 1811) promoted the Viking past as a source of Swedish national identity.
- **Norway** invoked the martial exploits of Viking kings and the free farmers of the Icelandic sagas as precedents for Norwegian independence from Denmark and Sweden. The 19th-century Norwegian national revival drew heavily on the saga literature.
- **Denmark** looked to the Danelaw and the North Sea Empire of Cnut as evidence of Danish greatness. The 19th-century Danish national movement, struggling against territorial losses to Prussia, found in the Viking past a narrative of resilience and recovery.

This Romantic construction was not value-neutral. The Viking was idealised as a figure of racial vigour, martial prowess, and masculine freedom — a construction that made the Viking heritage available for appropriation by racial ideologies.

**The 20th Century: Nazi Appropriation and White Supremacist Continuation**

The Nazi regime's appropriation of Viking and Norse imagery was systematic and ideologically motivated. The SS used runic symbols (the Sig-rune, the Odal-rune, the Tiwaz-rune) as insignia; the Ahnenerbe (Ancestral Heritage organisation) conducted pseudo-archaeological expeditions to Scandinavia seeking evidence of "Aryan" racial purity; and the myth of a pure, white, martial Viking past was woven into the fabric of Nazi racial ideology. Heinrich Himmler's personal interest in Germanic paganism led to the promotion of a fabricated "Irminism" and the construction of the Wewelsburg castle as a "centre of the SS cult."

This appropriation has persisted into the 2040s. White supremacist groups in Europe and North America continue to use Viking imagery (the Valknut, runic inscriptions, Mjǫllnir pendants) as symbols of "white heritage" and racial identity. The 2040 UoY position on this appropriation is clear: **Vikings were not white supremacists. Norse mythology is world cultural heritage. Genetic evidence demonstrates Viking Age diversity. Academic Viking Studies has an ethical obligation to counter racist appropriations of the Viking past.**

**Viking Studies in 2040: New Methods, New Questions**

The 2040 field of Viking Studies is being transformed by technological and methodological innovations:

- **Ancient DNA** — The genomic revolution has revealed the genetic diversity of Viking Age populations, undermining narratives of ethnic homogeneity. The 2020 Margaryan et al. study, expanded in the 2037 Scandinavian Population Genomics Project, provides a detailed map of Viking Age genetic variation and mobility.
- **Isotope analysis** — Strontium, oxygen, and lead isotope ratios in tooth enamel allow the reconstruction of individual mobility — identifying people who migrated during their lifetimes. The "Skull of Leifr" project (UoY, 2035–2039) used isotope analysis to identify non-local individuals in Icelandic churchyard burials, revealing a higher rate of immigration than previously assumed.
- **LiDAR** — Airborne LiDAR surveys have identified thousands of previously unknown Viking Age sites across Scandinavia — settlements, field systems, harbour installations, and burial grounds. The 2038 Norwegian National LiDAR Survey revealed over 4,000 previously unrecorded archaeological features.
- **Digital reconstruction** — The UoY Virtual Reconstruction Lab has produced VR environments for Viking Age Jórvík, Birka, Hedeby, and L'Anse aux Meadows, integrating archaeological, environmental, and textual data into immersive educational experiences.
- **AI-assisted text analysis** — Machine learning algorithms trained on runic corpora and saga texts can detect patterns invisible to human readers: formulaic repetitions, intertextual borrowings, chronological layering, and regional variation in narrative convention.

These methods are expanding what we can know about the Viking Age, but they also raise new questions. Ancient DNA reveals genetic diversity but cannot tell us how ethnic identity was experienced. Isotope analysis tracks mobility but cannot recover motive. LiDAR maps sites but cannot date them. The 2040 challenge remains the fundamental one: **understanding the past on its own terms, not ours** — resisting the temptation to project modern categories (racial, national, gendered) onto a world that operated by different rules.

**The Living Past: Ásatrú and Contemporary Heathenry**

The Viking religious past has a living presence in 2040 through the Ásatrú and Heathen movements. Ásatrúarfélagið (the Ásatrú Association of Iceland, founded 1972) is the oldest and largest organised Heathen group, recognised as a state religion in Iceland with over 5,000 registered members. In the United States and Europe, Ásatrú and Heathen groups range from inclusive, anti-racist organisations (The Troth, founded 1987) to explicitly white-supremacist factions (whose use of Viking imagery the 2040 UoY condemns). The relationship between academic Viking Studies and contemporary Heathen practice is complex and sometimes tense: scholars are wary of appearing to endorse modern religious claims, while Heathens sometimes feel that academic scepticism dismisses their spiritual experience. The 2040 UoY encourages respectful dialogue between these communities while maintaining the distinction between historical evidence and religious conviction.
