## Lecture 1: Who Were the Vikings? — Defining the Viking Age

**The Word "Viking"**

The Old Norse word *víkingr* (masculine) means "pirate, raider, sea-rover" — a person who goes on a *vík* (sea-journey, raiding expedition). The word *víking* (feminine) means "a raiding expedition by sea." Crucially, "Viking" was not an ethnic identity — it was an **activity**. The same person could be a farmer in the spring, a trader in the summer, a raider in the autumn, and a chieftain in the winter. The Norse never called themselves "Vikings" as a collective identity.

The term "Viking Age" was coined by Scandinavian antiquarians in the 19th century (first documented use: Erik Gustaf Geijer, 1811) as part of the Romantic nationalist project of constructing a shared Scandinavian heritage. It is a **period designation**, not a description of a people.

**Dating the Viking Age**

The conventional dates are **793-1066 CE**:
- **793 CE** — The sack of Lindisfarne monastery, traditionally marked as the beginning. The Anglo-Saxon Chronicle records: "In this year dire fore-warnings came over the land of Northumbria, terrified the people most woefully: there were excessive whirlwinds, and lightnings; and fiery dragons were seen flying in the air. A great famine soon followed these tokens; and a little after that, in the same year, the harrying of the heathen men woefully devastated God's church in Lindisfarne."
- **1066 CE** — The death of Haraldr harðráði (Hardrada) at Stamford Bridge, traditionally marked as the end. The same year, the Norman (Northman = Viking descendant) conquest of England began.

These dates are convenient but arbitrary. Viking activity began decades before 793 and continued well after 1066. The 2040 UoY dating: **c. 750-1100 CE**, with regional variation.

**Who Were the Vikings?**

The people we call "Vikings" were Scandinavian, agrarian, polytheistic, politically decentralized, maritime, and multilingual. They were NOT a unified nation, NOT exclusively warriors, NOT exclusively male, and NOT exclusively white. DNA analysis of Viking Age burials shows significant genetic diversity, including individuals of Sami, Finnish, Slavic, and Mediterranean ancestry.

The 2040 UoY position: **The "Vikings" were a culturally, linguistically, and religiously connected network of Scandinavian maritime communities whose expansion (both peaceful and violent) transformed the political, economic, and cultural landscape of the northern world between c. 750-1100 CE.**

## Lecture 2: Scandinavia Before the Vikings — The Pre-Viking World

**The Iron Age Background**

Scandinavia before the Viking Age was not a primitive backwater. It had well-established trade networks (Roman artifacts in Scandinavian graves), complex social hierarchies (the thicknes/chieftain class), advanced metalworking (high-quality weapons and jewelry from the Bronze Age onward), and runes (the Elder Futhark, 24 runes, in use from c. 150-700 CE).

**The Migration Period (c. 375-550 CE)**

The fall of Rome's western half (476 CE) disrupted Scandinavian trade routes and forced communities to become more self-sufficient. Local chieftains who could organize protection and raiding became more powerful. The 536 CE volcanic eruptions (identified by tree-ring data and ice cores) caused several years of cold summers — possibly remembered as the Fimbulwinter in Norse saga. The Nydam Boat (c. 310-320 CE) shows the advanced clinker-built construction that would become the hallmark of the Viking ship.

**Why Did the Viking Age Begin?**

The "multiple triggers" model identifies: population pressure, technological advantage (the longship), political centralization displacing local chieftains, economic opportunity (wealthy monasteries and trading towns), climate improvement (Medieval Warm Period), and cultural worldview (the honor code of drengskapr). None alone explains the expansion; it was their convergence.

## Lecture 3: The Viking Ship — Technology, Construction, and Naval Warfare

**The Longship — Engineering Marvel**

The Viking ship family includes: the drekkar (dragon ship, 25-36m, 60-100 oars, for raiding), the knarr (cargo vessel, 15-20m, for trade and colonization), the karve (small coastal vessel), and the snekkja (narrow fast warship, ~40 oars).

**Construction:** Clinker-built (lapstrake) method: keel laid first, strakes added upward with overlaps riveted with iron nails and caulked with wool/animal hair, internal ribs inserted after hull is shaped (hull dictates frame, not reverse). This produces a flexible, lightweight (~8-10 tons for a 30m ship), shallow-draft, repairable vessel.

Key finds: Oseberg Ship (c. 820 CE), Gokstad Ship (c. 890 CE), Roskilde 6 (c. 1025-1035 CE, 36m — longest Viking ship found).

**Naval Warfare**: Viking naval warfare was primarily transport-based — the ship delivered warriors who fought on land. Tactics included beach assaults, river raids (Seine to Paris, Volga to Novgorod), portage overland between rivers, and fortified ship camps (the Great Heathen Army's circular defensive perimeters).

## Lecture 4: The Western Expansion — Raiding, Settlement, and the Danelaw

**Lindisfarne (793 CE)** — The traditional start of the Viking Age in Britain. The attack shocked the Christian world — Alcuin of York wrote: "Never before has such terror appeared in Britain as we have now suffered from a pagan race."

**The Great Heathen Army (865-878 CE)** — Led by the sons of Ragnarr Loðbrók, this army conquered Northumbria (866-867), subdued East Anglia (869-870), conquered Mercia (873-874), and attacked Wessex. After Alfred's victory at Edington (878), the Treaty of Wedmore established the Danelaw.

**The Danelaw (c. 878-954 CE):** Danish-controlled territory governed by Norse law (Dena lagu). Features: Danish governance, Norse place-names (>1,500 by/thorp/thwaite/ness names), archaeological fusion (Coppergate/York excavation revealed thriving Norse city with woodworking, metalworking, and international trade), and Anglo-Scandinavian cultural hybrid (Gosforth cross combining Christian and Norse imagery). The last Norse king of York, Eric Bloodaxe, was expelled in 954 CE, but Norse influence continued through Cnut's dynasty (1016-1035).

## Lecture 5: The Eastern Expansion — The Rus and the River Routes

Norse traders/raiders — called **Varangians** (*væringjar*, "sworn companions") by Slavs and **Rus** (*rods*, "men who row") by Arabs/Greeks — established the states that would become Russia, Belarus, and Ukraine.

The Primary Chronicle records the "invitation to the Varangians" (862 CE) — the founding myth of the Russian state. Archaeological evidence confirms Norse presence along Russian river routes from the early 9th century.

**Two Key Routes:**
1. **Volga Route** — Baltic → Neva → Ladoga → Novgorod (Hólmgarðr) → Volga → Caspian → Baghdad. Traded furs, slaves, honey, amber for silver dirhams and luxury goods.
2. **Dnieper Route** — Baltic → Novgorod → Dnieper → Kyiv (Kænugarðr) → Black Sea → Constantinople (Miklagarðr). Route of the Varangian Guard.

**The Varangian Guard** — Byzantine imperial elite unit (established 988 CE), primarily Norse and later Anglo-Saxon. Most famous member: Haraldr Sigurðarson (Harald Hardrada), future King of Norway, served c. 1034-1042.

Arabic sources (Ibn Fadlan) describe Rus traders as "the filthiest of God's creatures" while documenting their elaborate funeral rituals. Over 80,000 dirham coins found in Scandinavian hoards attest to the trade volume. Scandinavia was the terminus of a global network connecting the Arctic to the Mediterranean.

## Lecture 6: The North Atlantic Expansion — Iceland, Greenland, and Vinland

**Iceland (c. 870-930 CE):** Settled entirely by Norse colonists (no indigenous population). The Alþingi (founded 930 CE) is one of the oldest parliamentary institutions in the world. The Icelandic Free State (930-1262) was a society with laws but no state — enforcement was private.

**Greenland (c. 985 CE):** Eiríkr rauði (Erik the Red), exiled from Iceland for manslaughter, named it "Greenland" to attract settlers. The Eastern and Western Settlements peaked at ~2,500-5,000 inhabitants with 16 churches and a bishopric at Garðar. The colonies survived ~500 years before disappearing c. 1450. Causes: climate change (Little Ice Age), trade disruption, Inuit conflict, cultural inflexibility (refused to adopt Inuit hunting technologies).

**Vinland (c. 1000 CE):** Leifr Eiríksson landed in North America c. 1000 CE, naming Helluland (Baffin Island), Markland (Labrador), and Vinland (Newfoundland). Þorfinnr Karlsefni attempted a permanent settlement c. 1010 CE but abandoned it after conflicts with Indigenous peoples (Skrælingjar). L'Anse aux Meadows (Newfoundland) is the only confirmed pre-Columbian Norse site in the Americas — a base camp with Norse-style buildings, forge, and butternuts (which don't grow north of New Brunswick, suggesting southern exploration).

## Lecture 7: Viking Age Society — Law, Gender, and Social Structure

**The Thing — Assembly Government**

Viking Age governance was exercised through the **thing** (*þing*), a periodic assembly of free men performing legislative, judicial, and executive functions. Key assemblies: Alþingi (Iceland, 930 CE), Gulaþing (W. Norway), Frostuþing (N. Norway). Within the community of free men, the thing was egalitarian — the poorest farmer had the same legal standing as the richest chieftain. Excluded: women, slaves (*þrælar*), foreigners.

**Gender in Viking Age Society**

Women's rights: own property, initiate divorce, manage households, act as religious specialists (vǫlur, gyðjur). Women's constraints: could not attend thing, could not serve as goðar, subject to bride price and arranged marriage.

The Oseberg ship burial (c. 834 CE) contained two women (one ~80, one ~50) with a decorated cart, sledges, textiles, kitchen equipment, 13 horses. DNA showed they were not closely related. The older woman had Morgagni-Stewart-Morel syndrome. Regardless of specific identity, the burial proves Viking Age society had **powerful, wealthy, publicly visible women**.

The 2040 position: Viking Age women had more legal rights than their Christian European contemporaries but fewer than modern sensibilities would consider adequate. Women were active agents in trade, religion, and even warfare (birka warrior grave Bj 581).

## Lecture 8: Viking Age Religion — Gods, Sacrifice, and the Sacred

*(Complements NP101 and NP102 with archaeological and cultural context for Viking Age practice.)*

**Blót — The Central Ritual**

Major blóts: Winter Nights (mid-October, sacrifice to dísir and Freyr), Yule/Jól (mid-January, sacrifice to Óðinn and Freyr), Summer blót/Sigrblót (mid-April, sacrifice to Óðinn for victory).

Pattern (from Hákonar saga góða): Preparation → sacrifice with spear or hammer → blood sprinkling (hlaut on participants, walls, god figures) → cooking and communal feast → toasting (to Óðinn, Njǫrðr/Freyr, bragarfull) → oath-taking.

**Sacred Spaces:** hov (indoor cult building), vé (outdoor sacred enclosure/grove), hörgr (stone cairn altar). The temple at Uppsala described by Adam of Bremen (c. 1070 CE) is the most famous — with three god statues (Þórr, Óðinn, Freyr) and a sacred grove with human and animal sacrifices every nine years. Archaeological evidence supports a major cult center but Adam's account may be exaggerated.

## Lecture 9: Viking Warfare — Weapons, Tactics, and the Warrior Ethos

**Weapons:** sword (prestigious, pattern-welded, often Frankish imports — ULFBERHT, INGELRII inscriptions), axe (bearded axe for hooking shields, dane-axe for housecarls), spear (most universal, cheap and versatile), shield (1m round, thin planks over leather, disposable), bow (longbows with self-nocks). Armor: chain mail (*brynja*, expensive), leather/padded gambesons (common). The Gjermundbu helmet (c. 900 CE) is the only complete Viking Age helmet found — a rounded iron cap with nose guard. **Horned helmets are a 19th-century invention** (Wagner's costume designer).

**Shield Wall** (*skjaldborg*): overlapping shields with spears projecting through gaps. Could form svynfylking (swine array/wedge) for offensive breakthroughs. Viking warfare emphasized speed, surprise, mobility, and personal combat within the shield wall.

**Warrior Ethos:** drengskapr (courage), tryggð (loyalty), sömmu (honor), ípróttir (skill), gjafar mildi (generosity). The dying song of Ragnar Lothbrook: "I die laughing, with full heart."

## Lecture 10: Viking Art — Styles, Motifs, and Meaning

**Six Sequential Styles:**

1. **Oseberg** (c. 780-870) — Dynamic gripping beasts, exuberant
2. **Borre** (c. 840-970) — Geometric ring-chain interlace
3. **Jellinge** (c. 900-970) — Simplified ribbon animals in S-shapes
4. **Mammen** (c. 950-1025) — More naturalistic, tendril scrolls with animals
5. **Ringerike** (c. 980-1090) — Elaborate plant foliage interlaced with animals, Anglo-Saxon/Ottonian influence
6. **Urnes** (c. 1040-1130) — Thin elegant fighting animals, transition to Romanesque

**Meaning:** Viking art was not purely decorative. The gripping beast may represent the cosmos holding itself together; fighting animals encode the struggle between order and chaos; interlacing patterns represent the Norns weaving fate; plant scrolls evoke Yggdrasil's foliage. Viking art is **narrative and cosmological** — the decorative and meaningful are inseparable.

## Lecture 11: Christianity and Conversion — The End of the Viking Age

**Process by Region:**
- **Denmark:** Harald Bluetooth "made the Danes Christian" (Jelling stones, c. 965 CE). Pragmatic — Christianity offered literate clergy, continental alliances, and centralized religious authority.
- **Norway:** Olav Tryggvason (c. 995 CE, forced conversion) and Olav Haraldsson/St. Olav (d. 1030, systematic church-building).
- **Iceland:** Decided by law at Alþingi in 999/1000 CE. Lawspeaker Þorgeirr, a pagan, decided for Christianity with three concessions: private pagan worship still allowed, horse meat permitted, infant exposure legal. Concessions phased out within a century.
- **Sweden:** Slowest to convert. Temple at Uppsala still active in 1070s. Full Christianization c. 1100 CE.

**Impact:** Writing (Latin alphabet), church organization, political centralization, cultural transformation (church art/architecture/music), social change (monogamy, prohibition of slave-raiding and infanticide), end of the Viking Age (Christian kings couldn't raid Christian monasteries with religious justification).

## Lecture 12: The Viking Legacy — From the Middle Ages to the 2040s

**Norse Heritage:** Language evolution (Icelandic is the most conservative), law (thing system → modern parliaments), place-names (thousands across the Viking world), genetics (detectable Viking signature), identity (drawn from the Viking past, for better and worse).

**The Problem of Appropriation:** 19th-century Romantic nationalism (Vikings as golden age), Victorian medievalism (Danelaw as freedom), Nazi appropriation (Viking imagery for "Aryan" supremacy), white supremacist continuation (2040s). The 2040 UoY position: Vikings were not white supremacists; Norse mythology is world cultural heritage; genetic evidence shows Viking Age diversity; academic Viking Studies has an ethical obligation to counter racist appropriations.

**Viking Studies in 2040:** Ancient DNA (genomic diversity revealed), isotope analysis (individual mobility traced), LiDAR (thousands of new sites discovered), digital reconstruction (VR Jórvík/Birka/Hedeby), AI-assisted text analysis (pattern detection in runic inscriptions and sagas). The challenge remains: understanding the past on its own terms, not ours.
