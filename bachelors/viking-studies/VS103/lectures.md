## Lecture 1: The Archaeology of the Viking Age — Methods and Paradigms

**What Is Viking Age Archaeology?**

Viking Age archaeology is the study of the material remains of Scandinavian and Scandinavian-influenced communities from approximately 750-1100 CE. It encompasses excavations in Scandinavia, the British Isles, Ireland, Iceland, Greenland, Russia, the Baltic, France, Spain, and Newfoundland — anywhere the Norse expanded, traded, raided, or settled.

The field has undergone three major paradigm shifts:

1. **Antiquarian Phase (1600-1900)** — Collecting artifacts as curiosities. Rune stones were noted, burial mounds were opened, and objects were catalogued without systematic methodology. The Oseberg ship (1904) was excavated with relative care but many earlier finds were lost.

2. **Culture-Historical Phase (1900-1970)** — Classifying artifacts into chronological sequences and cultural groups. Oscar Montelius's typological method (seriation) and Birger Nerman's typologies of Viking Age brooches established the basic chronological framework still used today. The culture-historical approach assumed that material culture = ethnic identity (a problematic assumption we'll address in Lecture 8).

3. **Processual and Post-Processual Phase (1970-present, extending to 2040)** — Processual archaeology (New Archaeology) introduced scientific methods, statistical analysis, and hypothesis testing. Post-processual archaeology emphasized meaning, symbolism, and the active role of material culture in social relations. In 2040, the field integrates both approaches with new technologies: ancient DNA, isotope analysis, LiDAR, and AI-assisted pattern recognition.

**Core Methods in Viking Age Archaeology**

- **Excavation** — Systematic removal of layers and recording of contexts. Modern excavation uses single-context recording, 3D photogrammetry, and real-time GIS mapping.
- **Typology and Seriation** — Classifying artifacts by form and dating them by their position in a sequence. Still the backbone of chronology, supplemented by dendrochronology and radiocarbon dating.
- **Dendrochronology** — Tree-ring dating. Viking Age timbers (especially ships and buildings) can be dated to the exact year they were felled. The Dendrochronological Master Sequence for northern Europe extends back to c. 5000 BCE.
- **Radiocarbon Dating (C-14)** — Dating organic materials by measuring the decay of carbon-14. Bayesian statistical methods (BayCal, OxCal) have revolutionized C-14 dating, allowing narrowing of date ranges to decades.
- **Isotope Analysis** — Strontium, oxygen, and lead isotopes in teeth and bone reveal where an individual grew up and how far they traveled. This has been transformative for understanding Viking Age mobility (see Lecture 7).
- **Ancient DNA (aDNA)** — Genome-wide analysis of bone and teeth has revolutionized our understanding of Viking Age population structure, ancestry, and identity (see Lecture 8).
- **LiDAR** — Airborne laser scanning that penetrates vegetation to reveal topographic features. Has identified thousands of previously unknown Viking Age sites (see Lecture 9).
- **Metal Detecting** — Controlled metal detecting in collaboration with archaeologists has produced enormous quantities of small finds. The British Portable Antiquities Scheme records >1.5 million objects.

## Lecture 2: Burials — Ship Burials, Chamber Graves, and Cremations

**Viking Age Burial Practices**

Viking Age burial practices varied significantly by region, period, and social status. The main types:

**Inhumation Burials:**
- **Ship burials** — The deceased placed in a ship, often with grave goods, animals, and sometimes human sacrifices. Examples: Oseberg (c. 834 CE), Gokstad (c. 890 CE), Tune (c. 900 CE). The ship itself is the coffin.
- **Chamber graves** — The deceased placed in a constructed wooden chamber underground. Examples: Birka chamber graves, some Vendel and Valsgärde burials.
- **Coffin burials** — The deceased in a wooden coffin, sometimes within a ship or chamber, sometimes alone.
- **Flat graves** — Simple pit burials without elaborate construction.

**Cremation Burials:**
- **Urn cremations** — The body cremated on a pyre; the burnt bones collected in an urn (often ceramic or metal) and buried.
- **Pit cremations** — The cremation remains placed in a pit, sometimes with unburnt grave goods.
- **Mound cremations** — The cremation conducted on site, covered with a burial mound.

**Key Burial Sites:**

**Oseberg (c. 834 CE, Vestfold, Norway)** — The richest Viking Age burial ever found. Two women buried in a 22-meter ship with a richly decorated cart, four sledges, textiles (tapestry, silk, wool), kitchen equipment, agricultural tools, 13 horses, 2 dogs, 1 ox. The older woman (c. 80 years old) had Morgagni-Stewart-Morel syndrome. The burial was disturbed in antiquity (the chamber was broken into, possibly during the burial era), and many precious metals were stolen.

**Gokstad (c. 890 CE, Vestfold, Norway)** — A man buried in a 23-meter ship with weapons, gaming pieces, horses, dogs, and a peacock (imported from the Byzantine world). The man died of battle wounds (three skull fractures). He was approximately 40-50 years old, 181 cm tall, and powerfully built.

**Birka (c. 750-970 CE, Björkö, Sweden)** — Over 3,000 graves excavated, including both inhumation and cremation. The most famous is Bj 581 (see Lecture 8), a chamber grave containing a high-status warrior with weapons, horses, and gaming pieces — DNA analysis (2017) showed the warrior was biologically female.

**Ladby (c. 925 CE, Denmark)** — A ship burial where the ship was placed in the ground and covered by a mound. The ship was badly preserved (only rivets remained in the sandy soil), but the grave goods included riding equipment, jewelry, and 11 horses and 3-4 dogs. A reconstructed ship is now at the Ladby Ship Museum.

## Lecture 3: Settlements — Towns, Farms, and Longhouses

**Urban Settlements**

Viking Age towns were rare but economically crucial:

**Hedeby (Haithabu, c. 770-1066 CE)** — Located on the Schlei fjord in modern-day Germany (then Denmark). The largest Viking Age town, with a population of approximately 1,000-1,500 at its peak. Row-organized houses along paved streets, a semi-circular fortification wall, a harbor with jetties and a ship-blocking barrier in the water. Excavations have revealed workshops for bronze casting, iron working, glass beads, leatherworking, and comb-making. Trade goods from the Frankish world, the Baltic, the Islamic Caliphate, and the British Isles confirm Hedeby's role as an international trading hub.

**Birka (c. 750-970 CE)** — On the island of Björkö in Lake Mälaren, Sweden. A major trading center with a defended hillfort, a garrison, and an extensive cemetery. Birka's strategic position (accessible from the Baltic via a navigable channel) made it a key node in the eastern trade network. The 2019-2035 Birka excavations using LiDAR and ground-penetrating radar have revealed an extensive submerged harbor structure.

**Kaupang (Skiringssal, c. 780-860 CE)** — Norway's first town, located in Vestfold. Excavations (2000-2006, continued 2025-2033) have revealed seasonal trading activity preceding the establishment of permanent houses. Kaupang represents the earliest phase of urbanization in Norway.

**Ribe (c. 704-850 CE)** — The earliest Viking Age trading settlement, located in Jutland, Denmark. Dendrochronological dating has pushed the foundation of Ribe back to the early 8th century, making it the first urban settlement in Scandinavia. The Ribe excavations have produced enormous quantities of crafts (bead-making, comb-making, metalworking) and imported goods (Frankish glass, Rhenish pottery).

**Rural Settlements — The Longhouse**

The typical Viking Age rural settlement consisted of a **longhouse** (*langhús*) — a rectangular building up to 50-80 meters long, housing both humans and animals under one roof. The longhouse had:

- **A central hearth** — The main fire for cooking, heating, and light
- **A raised platform along the walls** — Used for sleeping, sitting, and storage
- **A byre (animal section)** — At one end, housing cattle, sheep, and goats through the winter
- **A living section** — At the other end, with the high seat (*öndvegi*) for the householder
- **No chimney** — Smoke escaped through a hole in the roof or through gaps in the walls

Excavated longhouse sites include: Höfn (Iceland), Vorbasse (Denmark), Tissø (Denmark — a cult site with a large feasting hall), and L'Anse aux Meadows (Newfoundland — small longhouses, not full farms).

The 2040 LiDAR surveys of Iceland and the Faroe Islands have identified over 3,000 previously unknown longhouse sites, dramatically expanding our understanding of Viking Age settlement density.

## Lecture 4: Viking Trade — Networks, Goods, and Silver

**The Viking Trade Network**

The Viking trade network was a **world system** connecting the Arctic to the Mediterranean, the Atlantic to the Caspian. Key trade goods and their directions:

**From the North (Scandinavia):**
- Furs (fox, marten, beaver, squirrel)
- Amber (from the Baltic coast)
- Iron (from Swedish and Norwegian mines)
- Walrus ivory (from the Arctic)
- Falcon (gyrfalcons, prized in the Islamic world)
- Slaves (captured in raids and warfare)

**From the East (Byzantium, Islamic Caliphate):**
- Silver dirhams (the most common Islamic coins in Viking hoards)
- Silk (fragments found in Oseberg, Birka, and elsewhere)
- Spices and glass beads
- Coinage (dinars, miliaresia)

**From the West (British Isles, Frankia):**
- Wool cloth (English and Flemish broadcloth)
- Wine (from Frankia)
- Pottery (Rhenish stoneware)
- Salt
- Coinage (pengies, deniers)

**From the South (Mediterranean):**
- Glass (raw glass and finished vessels)
- Precious metals (gold jewelry, ecclesiastical vessels)
- Ceramics (amphorae, glazed wares)

**Silver and the Dirham Economy**

The Viking Age economy was fundamentally a **silver economy**. Silver was the primary medium of exchange, but it was not coined — it was weighed and cut. Hack silver (silver broken into pieces of appropriate weight) and silver jewelry (melted down and cut) were used alongside coins.

The most significant source of silver was the **Islamic dirham**. Over 80,000 dirhams have been found in Scandinavian hoards, ranging from single coins to hoards of thousands. The dirhams date from the 8th-11th centuries and originate from mints across the Islamic world — Baghdad, Samarkand, Damascus, Cairo, and many others. The flow of dirhams into Scandinavia peaks in the 950s-960s and then declines sharply, possibly due to the disruption of trade routes caused by the collapse of the Samanid dynasty and the rise of the Rus principalities.

The 2040 GIS-mapping of dirham hoards (following the work of the UoY Digital Numismatics Lab) reveals a clear pattern: dirhams enter Scandinavia via the eastern river routes and are distributed westward through trading networks. The concentration of hoards in Gotland (over 800 hoards) suggests that the island was a major hub for silver storage and redistribution.

## Lecture 5: Ships and Seafaring — Maritime Archaeology

**The Viking Ship in Context**

The Viking ship is not just a vehicle — it is a **cultural symbol**, a **technological achievement**, and an **economic engine**. The development of the keeled, sail-powered longship is the single most important technological innovation of the Viking Age.

**Ship Types and Their Archaeology:**

**Warships (longships/drekkar):** 25-36m long, 4-5m wide, 60-100 oars. Characterized by narrow hulls, low freeboard, and elaborate prow decorations (the dragon head, *drekahöfuð*). The Gokstad ship and the Roskilde 6 ship are the best-preserved examples.

**Cargo ships (knarr/knörr):** 15-20m long, 5-6m wide, deeper hull, broader beam. The Skuldelev 1 ship (c. 1030 CE, found in Roskilde Fjord) is the best-preserved cargo vessel — 16.5m long, 4.5m wide, with a cargo capacity of approximately 20 tons.

**Fishing and coastal vessels (ferja, karve):** Small, versatile boats for local transport. The Skuldelev 3 ship is a small coastal trader, approximately 14m long.

**The Skuldelev Fleet (c. 1000-1050 CE)** — Five ships deliberately sunk in Roskilde Fjord to create a defensive barrier, excavated in 1962. They provide a cross-section of Viking Age maritime technology: one longship (Skuldelev 2), two cargo vessels (Skuldelev 1 and 5), one coastal trader (Skuldelev 3), and one small fishing vessel (Skuldelev 6). A sixth ship (Skuldelev 7, now reclassified as Roskilde 6) was discovered in 1997.

**Ship Construction Details:**

The Viking ship was built using the clinker (lapstrake) method, with iron nails, wool caulking, and tar-sealed seams. The mast was stepped into a massive keelson (internal timber distributing mast forces throughout the hull). The sail was square, made of woven wool (vatneldr — "water-fired" sail cloth, treated with animal fat and ochre for waterproofing and wind-catching).

Navigation: The Norse had no compass. They navigated by:
- **Sun compass** — A wooden disc with a gnomon, found at the Uunarteq settlement in Greenland. Uses the sun's shadow to determine direction.
- **Sunstone** — A calcite crystal that can determine the sun's position even through clouds. Mentioned in sagas and experimentally verified as functional.
- **Stars** — The North Star (Leiðarstjarna) and constellations
- **Birds and sea life** — The flight patterns of seabirds, the color and temperature of water, the presence of whales and dolphins
- **Landmarks** — Known mountains, headlands, and sea marks

The 2040 UoY Maritime Lab has reconstructed and sailed two Viking ship replicas (the Sea Stallion from Glendalough, a reconstruction of Skuldelev 2, and the Ottar, a reconstruction of a small knarr), providing experimental data on speed, cargo capacity, seaworthiness, and crew requirements.

## Lecture 6: Runes and Runestones — Epigraphy and Commemoration

**The Runic Alphabets**

Three runic alphabets were used during the Viking Age:

1. **Elder Futhark** (c. 150-700 CE) — 24 runes. Used across the Germanic world. Primarily found on small objects (jewelry, weapons, tools) and a few memorial stones. The Elder Futhark is the most difficult to read because the rune names and sound values are not fully agreed upon.

2. **Younger Futhark** (c. 700-1100 CE) — 16 runes. The alphabet of the Viking Age. Created by reducing the Elder Futhark from 24 to 16 characters. The Younger Futhark has two main variants:
   - **Long-branch (Danish)** runes — Used in Denmark, Norway, and the Danelaw
   - **Short-twig (Swedish-Norwegian)** runes — Used in Sweden and Norway

3. **Medieval Futhork** (c. 1100-1400 CE) — Expanded back to 24+ runes with dotted variants. Used in medieval Scandinavia for practical purposes (accounting, personal notes, church inscriptions).

**Runestones as Sources**

Over 3,000 runestones survive from the Viking Age, the vast majority in Sweden (over 2,500), with significant numbers in Denmark (c. 200) and Norway (c. 50), and scattered examples in the British Isles, the Baltic, and Russia.

Runestones are primarily **commemorative monuments** — they memorialize the dead, record family relationships, claim inheritance, and commemorate specific events (journeys, deaths, constructions). They are not religious texts in the narrow sense, though many invoke Þórr, Óðinn, or CHRISTIAN imagery.

**Key Runestone Groups:**

**The Jelling Stones (c. 965 CE, Denmark):** The larger stone raised by Harald Bluetooth commemorates his parents (Gorm and Thyra) and proclaims that Harald "won for himself all of Denmark and Norway and made the Danes Christian." The smaller stone, raised by Gorm for Thyra, is the oldest runestone in Denmark to mention a woman. Together, the Jelling stones are often called "Denmark's birth certificate."

**The Runestones of the Varangian Guard (c. 1000-1100 CE):** Approximately 30 runestones in Sweden commemorate men who died in the east (Austríki), specifically in the service of the Byzantine emperor (Griklandi) or in the guard of the king of England. The most famous is the Ed runestone (U 112), which commemorates a man who "met his end among the Greeks when the host of the valiant warrior fell."

**The Ingvar Runestones (c. 1040 CE):** Approximately 26 runestones in the Swedish province of Södermanland and Uppland commemorate men who died on Ingvar the Far-Travelled's expedition to the east (probably a raid on the Caspian region that ended in disaster).

**The 2040 AI-Assisted Runology Project**

The UoY Runic Studies Lab uses AI-assisted paleographic analysis to:
- Identify and classify rune forms
- Date inscriptions based on linguistic and orthographic features
- Detect later forgeries (runological "deep fakes" created in the 19th and 20th centuries)
- Reconstruct damaged inscriptions from fragmentary evidence
- Map the distribution of runestone formulas across the Viking world

## Lecture 7: Isotope Analysis and aDNA — New Evidence, New Questions

**Strontium Isotope Analysis**

Strontium isotopes (^87Sr/^86Sr) vary geologically depending on the underlying bedrock. As humans consume food and water, strontium from the local environment is incorporated into their teeth and bones. Tooth enamel forms in childhood and does not change, so strontium isotopes in tooth enamel reveal **where a person grew up**.

Key findings from strontium isotope analysis:

- **Birka warriors** — Isotope analysis of the Birka population shows significant non-local individuals, confirming that Birka was a cosmopolitan trading center with people from across the Viking world
- **The Easteby woman** — A woman buried in a Viking Age cemetery in Denmark with expensive jewelry was shown by strontium analysis to have grown up in Norway, suggesting she migrated to Denmark
- **Greenland Norse** — Isotope analysis shows some Greenland Norse individuals grew up in Iceland or Norway, confirming migration from Scandinavia
- **The Griffin Warrior** — A high-status burial in Scandinavia contained a person who grew up in the British Isles (confirmed by oxygen isotopes and aDNA)

**Ancient DNA (aDNA)**

Ancient DNA analysis has revolutionized Viking Studies. The 2020 large-scale study (Margaryan et al., published in Nature) sequenced 442 Viking Age genomes from across Europe. Key findings:

1. **Genetic diversity** — Vikings were not a genetically homogeneous group. Norwegian, Danish, and Swedish Vikings had distinct genetic profiles, and all three groups showed admixture with local populations in the areas they settled.
2. **Sex-biased admixture** — In the British Isles, the Viking genetic signature shows predominantly Norwegian male ancestry and British (Celtic) female ancestry, consistent with Norse male settlers taking local wives.
3. **The Birka warrior (Bj 581)** — The 2017 study confirmed that the individual in this high-status warrior grave was biologically female. This was one of the most significant aDNA findings in Viking archaeology, challenging the assumption that all warrior burials were male.
4. **Southern European ancestry** — Some Viking Age burials in Scandinavia show ancestry from southern Europe, the Middle East, and East Asia, confirming long-distance migration and admixture.
5. **No "Viking gene"** — There is no single genetic marker that identifies someone as a "Viking." The genetic diversity within Scandinavia during the Viking Age was as great as the diversity between Scandinavians and other Europeans.

The 2040 UoY Ancient DNA Lab (in partnership with the University of Copenhagen) is currently analyzing a further 1,200 Viking Age genomes from previously unsequenced burials in the Baltic, Russia, and the North Atlantic.

## Lecture 8: Gender, Identity, and Ethnicity — Rethinking Viking Age Categories

**The Birka Warrior (Bj 581) Controversy**

The most controversial finding in recent Viking archaeology is the 2017 aDNA confirmation that the individual in Birka grave Bj 581 — a high-status warrior burial with weapons, two horses, and gaming pieces — was biologically female.

The Hedenstierna-Jonson et al. (2017) paper in the American Journal of Physical Anthropology sparked intense debate:
- **Supporters** argued that the DNA evidence was conclusive and that the burial must be interpreted as a female warrior
- **Skeptics** (including Judith Jesch, 2017) argued that the grave goods alone do not prove the individual was a warrior in life, and that gaming pieces might indicate a strategist or commander rather than a fighter
- **The 2040 consensus** (reflected in the UoY position) is that Bj 581 **was** a person of high status who was buried with warrior equipment and that the person was biologically female. Whether she was a "warrior" in the sense of personally fighting in battles cannot be determined from the archaeological evidence alone. What the burial DOES prove is that Viking Age society had a category for high-status females buried with martial equipment — and that category existed, whether we call it "warrior" or something else.

**Gender in Viking Archaeology**

The 2040 approach to gender in Viking archaeology follows the "intersectional bioarchaeology" model:
- **Biological sex** is determined by skeletal morphology and aDNA
- **Gender identity** is inferred from grave goods, burial position, and isotopic evidence
- **The two do not always correlate** — A significant minority of Viking Age burials (approximately 10-15%) contain grave goods associated with the opposite gender
- **Gender was fluid** in Viking Age society in ways that our binary categories do not capture

The key insight: **Viking Age gender was not a simple male/female binary.** The archaeological evidence shows multiple gender configurations, including individuals buried with both masculine and feminine grave goods, and individuals whose biological sex does not match their grave goods.

**Ethnicity in Viking Archaeology**

The culture-historical approach assumed that material culture = ethnic identity: if a burial contains "Scandinavian" grave goods, the person must be "Scandinavian." Ancient DNA has conclusively disproven this assumption.

Key findings:
- Some individuals buried with "Scandinavian" grave goods were biologically from the British Isles, the Baltic, or the Middle East
- Some individuals buried with "British" grave goods carried Scandinavian genetic ancestry
- The concept of "ethnic identity" in the Viking Age was **situational and performative** — the same individual might identify as "Norse" in one context and "Danish" or "Hiberno-Norse" in another
- Material culture was **borrowed, traded, gifted, and adapted** across ethnic boundaries — a person wearing a Scandinavian brooch might be a Norse settler, a local adopting Norse fashion, or a trader carrying goods from multiple cultures

The 2040 UoY position: **Ethnicity in the Viking Age was a social identity, not a biological category. People "became" Viking by participating in Viking culture — speaking Norse, practicing Norse religion, using Norse material culture — regardless of their genetic ancestry.**

## Lecture 9: LiDAR, Remote Sensing, and Digital Archaeology

**LiDAR — Light Detection and Ranging**

LiDAR (Light Detection and Ranging) uses airborne laser scanning to penetrate vegetation and reveal topographic features invisible from the ground. In Viking Age archaeology, LiDAR has been transformative:

**Denmark:** The 2019-2035 LiDAR survey of Denmark identified over 5,000 previously unknown archaeological features, including:
- Viking Age ring fortresses (previously unknown examples of the Trelleborg-type forts)
- Field systems and farmsteads
- Coastal fortifications

**Norway:** LiDAR surveys of western Norway have revealed:
- Over 1,000 buried longhouse sites
- Previously unknown burial mounds (many plowed flat by modern agriculture)
- Coastal landing sites and boat houses

**Iceland:** The 2028-2033 UoY Icelandic Settlement Survey used drone-mounted LiDAR to map the entire coastal settlement zone. Results: over 3,000 previously unknown longhouse sites, dramatically increasing the estimated population of the Settlement Period (870-930 CE) by approximately 30%.

**England:** The 2040 LiDAR survey of the Danelaw identified previously unknown Viking Age fortifications, field systems, and settlement sites that challenge the traditional view of the Danelaw as purely agrarian.

**Ground-Penetrating Radar (GPR)**

GPR uses radar pulses to image the subsurface without excavation. In Viking archaeology, GPR has been used to:
- Map burials without disturbing them (the Roskilde 6 ship was first detected by GPR)
- Identify settlement layouts beneath modern towns (York/Jórvík, Dublin)
- Locate buried structures in known sites (the Birka garrison, the Tissø feasting hall)

**Photogrammetry and 3D Reconstruction**

Photogrammetry (creating 3D models from 2D photographs) and 3D reconstruction have revolutionized how archaeologists document and interpret sites:
- The Oseberg and Gokstad ships have been fully 3D-scanned and can be viewed in virtual reality
- The Jórvík Viking Centre in York uses photogrammetry and 3D modeling to recreate the Viking Age city
- The UoY Digital Archaeology Lab has created VR reconstructions of Birka, Hedeby, and Tissø based on excavation data

**AI-Assisted Pattern Recognition**

The 2040 UoY AI Lab uses machine learning to:
- Identify artifact typologies from photographs
- Detect buried features in LiDAR and GPR data
- Classify runic inscriptions and detect forgeries
- Predict excavation locations based on environmental and topographic features
- Reconstruct fragmented artifacts from 3D scans of broken pieces

## Lecture 10: Viking Age Hoards — Wealth, Hiding, and Meaning

**What Is a Hoard?**

A hoard (also called a "deposit" or "cache") is a collection of valuable objects deliberately hidden or buried. Hoards are found throughout the Viking world and represent one of the most important sources of evidence for Viking Age economy, wealth, and material culture.

**Types of Hoards:**

1. **Wealth hoards** — Collections of silver, gold, and coins, often buried in times of crisis. These are the most common type.
2. **Craft hoards** — Collections of raw materials, tools, and unfinished products, buried by craftspeople for safekeeping.
3. **Weapon hoards** — Collections of weapons, sometimes deliberately broken or "killed" (ritually destroyed).
4. **Jewelry hoards** — Collections of brooches, rings, arm-rings, and other personal adornment.

**Key Viking Age Hoards:**

**The Cuerdale Hoard (1840, Lancashire, England)** — Over 8,600 items including silver coins (Anglo-Saxon, Viking, Carolingian, and Islamic), hacksilver, jewelry, and ingots. The largest Viking Age silver hoard found in England. Date: c. 905 CE.

**The Hoen Hoard (c. 875 CE, Buskerud, Norway)** — Gold jewelry, coins (including Arab dinars, Carolingian coins, and English pennies), and precious stones. The largest Viking Age gold hoard. Date: c. 875 CE.

**The Spillings Hoard (1999, Gotland, Sweden)** — Over 14,000 dirhams (the largest Viking Age coin hoard ever found), plus silver jewelry and hacksilver. Date: c. 875 CE.

**The Galloway Hoard (2014, Scotland)** — Over 100 items including silver, gold, textiles, and a unique silver vessel containing Anglo-Saxon, Irish, and Carolingian objects. The richest Viking Age hoard found in Britain. Date: c. 900 CE. The 2040 conservation and analysis of the Galloway Hoard has revealed previously unknown Anglo-Saxon textile fragments and confirmed connections to the Carolingian world.

**Why Were Hoards Buried?**

The traditional assumption is that hoards were buried for safekeeping in times of crisis (Viking raids, warfare, political instability) and never recovered because the owner died or was displaced. This is certainly true for some hoards.

But the 2040 position (following the work of the UoY Hoard Studies Group) recognizes additional motivations:
- **Ritual deposition** — Some hoards were buried as offerings to the gods, not intended for recovery. The practice of depositing valuable objects in water (bogs, lakes, rivers) is well-attested from the Bronze Age onward.
- **Social display** — A hoard could be a way of demonstrating wealth without spending it. By burying silver, a chieftain removes it from circulation, increasing its scarcity value.
- **Insurance** — A buried hoard is a form of wealth storage that is invisible to tax collectors, raiders, and creditors.
- **Accidental loss** — Some "hoards" may simply be lost purses or dropped containers.

## Lecture 11: Viking Age Religion and Ritual — The Archaeology of Belief

**Archaeological Evidence for Viking Age Religion**

Written sources tell us about Viking Age religion (see NP101, NP102, VS Lecture 8), but the archaeological evidence provides a different and complementary perspective.

**Sacred Sites:**

**Tissø (Zealand, Denmark)** — A major cult site occupied from c. 550-1000 CE. Excavations have revealed:
- A large feasting hall (over 40m long) with numerous animal bones (cattle, pig, horse, dog)
- A smaller building interpreted as a hov (temple)
- A workshop area with gold and silver objects
- A lake with ritually deposited weapons and jewelry
- Human remains (not burials — possibly sacrifices or ritual depositions)

**Uppsala (Gamla Uppsala, Sweden)** — The most famous cult site in Scandinavia, described by Adam of Bremen as containing a golden temple with statues of Þórr, Óðinn, and Freyr. Archaeological evidence includes:
- Three large burial mounds from the 6th century (pre-Viking but sacred throughout the period)
- A large building platform (the "temple" site)
- Rich grave goods (gold, weapons, imported objects)
- Sacrificial deposits in the surrounding wetlands (animals, weapons, human bones)
- The Royal Mounds (Eriksgata) — formal route for Swedish kings

**Hofstaðir (Iceland)** — A large longhouse site in northern Iceland, excavated by the UoY Iceland Expedition (2018-2039). The site shows:
- A large hall with a raised platform (possibly a cult platform)
- A pit containing horse bones (consistent with blót)
- A nearby bog with deposited objects
- No evidence of a separate temple building — the cult was practiced in the longhouse

**The 2040 Conclusion:** The archaeological evidence supports **three types of ritual practice**:
1. **Indoor rituals** in hov/hof or large halls (Tissø, Hofstaðir)
2. **Outdoor rituals** in sacred groves or at natural features (Uppsala, wetland deposits)
3. **Mobile rituals** performed at temporary camps (the ship camp model — ritual blót performed at the site of the beached ship before a raid)

**Ritual Objects**

- **Þórr's hammers** — Over 1,000 Þórr's hammer amulets have been found across the Viking world. They range from simple iron pendants to elaborate silver and gold pieces. Some are found in Christian contexts, suggesting syncretic use.
- **Valkyrie figurines** — Small silver and bronze figurines showing armed women on horses, found primarily in Denmark and Sweden. These are interpreted as representations of valkyries or dísir.
- **Gullgubber** — Tiny gold foils (0.5-1mm) stamped with images of embracing couples, found in large numbers at cult sites (Borg, Sorte Muld, Uppåkra). The meaning is debated: fertility amulets? Marriage tokens? God/goddess depictions?
- **Siege coins** — Islamic, Christian, and hack-silver coins found in ritual deposits, suggesting that wealth offering was a universal religious practice.

## Lecture 12: The Future of Viking Archaeology — 2040 and Beyond

**The 2040 State of the Field**

Viking Age archaeology in 2040 is in the middle of a methodological revolution. The convergence of ancient DNA, isotope analysis, LiDAR, and AI-assisted pattern recognition has produced more data in the last 20 years than in the previous 200.

**Key Research Questions for 2040-2050:**

1. **Population dynamics** — How many people lived in Viking Age Scandinavia? What was the rate of migration? How did population density vary by region and period?

2. **Gender and identity** — How many "non-normative" gender burials exist? How should we interpret them? What does the intersection of biological sex, grave goods, and isotopic evidence tell us about Viking Age gender?

3. **Economic networks** — How did silver flow through the Viking economy? What was the relationship between raiding, trading, and taxation?

4. **Environmental impact** — What was the ecological footprint of Viking Age settlements? How did deforestation, soil erosion, and climate change affect settlement patterns?

5. **Digital heritage** — How do we preserve and present Viking Age sites in an era of climate change, sea-level rise, and coastal erosion? The Trelleborg fortresses are threatened by rising sea levels. Birka is slowly sinking into Lake Mälaren.

6. **Ethical archaeology** — How do we handle human remains in the context of indigenous rights, descendant communities, and scientific inquiry? What is the ethical framework for excavating and analyzing Viking Age burials?

7. **AI and automation** — How do we use AI responsibly in archaeology? What are the biases in training data, and how do they affect classification, dating, and interpretation?

**The UoY Commitment**

The University of Yggdrasil's Viking Studies program is committed to:
- **Rigorous methodology** — Hypothesis-driven, evidence-based, transparent
- **Ethical practice** — Respecting descendant communities, indigenous rights, and international law
- **Anti-racist scholarship** — Actively countering white supremacist appropriations of the Viking past
- **Public engagement** — Making our research accessible to the public through VR reconstructions, museum partnerships, and open-access publications
- **Interdisciplinary collaboration** — Working with geneticists, climatologists, historians, literary scholars, and indigenous communities

The past is not neutral. How we study it, interpret it, and present it has consequences for the present. Viking archaeology in 2040 is not just about understanding the past — it is about building a future in which the study of human history is inclusive, ethical, and genuinely illuminating.

### Assignment 1: Excavation Simulation — Interpreting a Burial

**Objective:** Develop practical skills in burial interpretation using archaeological evidence.

**Task:** You are given a complete burial dataset (provided as a downloadable package) for an unnamed Viking Age burial. The dataset includes:
- Photographs of the burial in situ
- Grave goods catalog (30 items with descriptions)
- Skeleton report (sex, age, pathology)
- Isotope analysis (strontium, oxygen, carbon, nitrogen)
- aDNA results (ancestry, sex chromosomes, phenotypic traits)
- Site context (soil type, geomorphology, associated features)

Your task is to produce a complete burial interpretation that addresses:
1. **Identity** — Who was this person? (Biological sex, age, ancestry, mobility, health)
2. **Social status** — What does the grave goods assemblage tell us about their social position?
3. **Cultural affiliation** — What cultural traditions does the burial represent? Is the person "Norse," "Hiberno-Norse," "Anglo-Scandinavian," or something else?
4. **Ritual practice** — What can we infer about the funeral ritual from the arrangement of the grave goods?
5. **Controversies** — Where do the data contradict each other? What are the alternative interpretations?

**Deliverables:**
- 3500-4500 word burial report
- Grave goods catalog with typological analysis
- Isotope and aDNA interpretation (1 page each)
- Timeline of the burial's discovery and excavation
- Annotated bibliography (minimum 15 sources)

**Grading Rubric:**
- Identity interpretation (25%): Competent synthesis of biological and isotopic evidence
- Social status analysis (20%): Thoughtful analysis of grave goods and their implications
- Cultural affiliation (20%): Nuanced analysis that avoids simplistic ethnic labels
- Ritual analysis (15%): Evidence-based interpretation of the funeral
- Handling uncertainty (20%): Honest assessment of alternative interpretations and contradictions

### Assignment 2: LiDAR Lab — Identifying Viking Age Features in Landscape Data

**Objective:** Learn to identify Viking Age features in LiDAR data using the UoY Digital Archaeology Lab's visualization tools.

**Task:** You are given a LiDAR dataset covering a 10km² area in western Norway (provided as downloadable digital elevation models and hillshade maps). Your task is to:

1. **Identify features** — Locate and classify at least 20 Viking Age features in the dataset. Feature types include: burial mounds, longhouse platforms, boathouse foundations, field boundaries, trackways, landing sites, and fortifications.
2. **Map and measure** — For each feature, record: location (UTM coordinates), dimensions, orientation, associated features, and confidence level (certain/probable/possible).
3. **Interpret** — Based on the distribution and types of features, what can you infer about the Viking Age settlement pattern in this area?
4. **Compare** — Compare your results with the known Viking Age settlement record for western Norway. Are your findings consistent with existing data? Do they suggest new patterns?

**Deliverables:**
- Annotated LiDAR map (digital, minimum 20 features)
- Feature catalog (minimum 20 entries with measurements, classification, and confidence)
- 2000-2500 word settlement analysis
- Annotated bibliography (minimum 8 sources on LiDAR methodology and western Norwegian settlement)

**Grading Rubric:**
- Feature identification (25%): Correct identification and classification of features
- Measurement accuracy (15%): Reasonable dimensions and coordinates
- Settlement analysis (30%): Thoughtful interpretation of the distribution pattern
- Comparison (15%): Engagement with existing scholarship
- Presentation (15%): Clear, well-organized maps and catalog

### Assignment 3: Artifact Analysis — Typology, Dating, and Meaning

**Objective:** Analyze a Viking Age artifact using typological, chronological, and interpretive methods.

**Task:** Choose ONE of the following artifact types and analyze a specific example (provided in the course materials):

A) A Ørsnes-type brooch from Bornholm
B) A Mammen-style axe head
C) A Ringerike-style runestone (choose from a provided selection)
D) A Viking Age silver hoard (choose from Cuerdale, Spillings, or Galloway)

For your chosen artifact:
1. **Physical description** — Materials, dimensions, manufacturing technique, condition
2. **Typological analysis** — What type is it? What is its chronological range? Where does it fit in the stylistic sequence?
3. **Dating** — What dating methods have been applied? What is the probable date range? How certain is the dating?
4. **Provenance** — Where was it made? What is the evidence for its origin? How did it get to where it was found?
5. **Function** — What was it used for? How was it worn, displayed, or used?
6. **Meaning** — What symbolic or cultural significance did it have? What does it tell us about the person who made or owned it?
7. **Conservation** — What conservation issues does it present? How should it be stored, displayed, and preserved?

**Deliverables:**
- 3000-3500 word artifact analysis
- Technical drawing or 3D model (hand-drawn or digital)
- Chronological chart showing the artifact's position in the stylistic sequence
- Annotated bibliography (minimum 12 sources)

**Grading Rubric:**
- Physical description (15%): Accurate, detailed, well-organized
- Typological analysis (20%): Correct classification and chronological placement
- Dating and provenance (20%): Competent use of dating methods and origin evidence
- Interpretation (25%): Thoughtful analysis of function, meaning, and significance
- Conservation (10%): Practical, evidence-based recommendations
- Writing quality (10%): Clear, academic, properly cited

### Assignment 4: Critical Debate — The Birka Warrior Controversy

**Objective:** Develop critical thinking skills by engaging with a major ongoing debate in Viking archaeology.

**Task:** Write a position paper on the Birka Warrior (Bj 581) controversy. Your paper must:

1. **Summarize the evidence** — What are the facts? (Grave goods, skeletal morphology, aDNA results, isotopic data)
2. **Present Position A** — The individual in Bj 581 was a female warrior. What evidence supports this? What are the implications for our understanding of Viking Age gender?
3. **Present Position B** — The individual in Bj 581 was a high-status woman buried with warrior equipment, but not necessarily a warrior. What evidence supports this? What are the limitations of equating grave goods with personal identity?
4. **Present Position C** — The question is unanswerable with current evidence. What would we need to know to resolve it? What are the epistemological limits of archaeology?
5. **Argue your position** — Which position do you find most convincing, and why?

**Deliverables:**
- 3000-4000 word position paper
- Evidence table (all relevant data points, organized by category)
- Annotated bibliography (minimum 15 sources, including Hedenstierna-Jonson et al. 2017, Jesch 2017, and at least 3 post-2020 sources)

**Grading Rubric:**
- Evidence summary (20%): Accurate, complete, well-organized
- Position A presentation (15%): Fair, evidence-based, well-argued
- Position B presentation (15%): Fair, evidence-based, well-argued
- Position C presentation (15%): Fair, evidence-based, well-argued
- Your position (20%): Clear, well-reasoned, aware of limitations
- Writing quality (15%): Clear, organized, properly cited
