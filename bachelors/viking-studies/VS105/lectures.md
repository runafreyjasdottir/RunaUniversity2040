## Lecture 1: The Old Norse Language — Origins, Dialects, and Structure

**What Is Old Norse? Linguistic Genealogy and Geographic Distribution**

Old Norse (*dǫnsk tunga*, "the Danish tongue," as medieval Icelanders called their own language) is the North Germanic language spoken by the inhabitants of Scandinavia and their Atlantic, British, and eastern colonies from approximately 800–1350 CE. It belongs to the Germanic branch of Indo-European, occupying the northernmost position in a dialect continuum that extended from Iceland in the west to the Volga in the east. Its closest living relatives are Icelandic, Faroese, Norwegian (especially the Nynorsk written standard), Swedish, and Danish. Its slightly more distant cousins include English, Frisian, Dutch, and German — all descendants of the West Germanic branch from which Norse separated during the first centuries CE.

The term "Old Norse" is a scholarly convenience, not an emic designation. No medieval Scandinavian would have said "I speak Old Norse." The language was referred to variously: *dǫnsk tunga* (Danish tongue), *norrœna* or *norrœnt mál* (Norse speech), or simply *vár tunga* (our tongue). The linguistic unity of the Viking Age — the fact that a Norwegian could converse comfortably with an Icelander, a Dane, or a Swede — was a product of the relatively recent divergence of North Germanic from Proto-Germanic and the intensive maritime contact that homogenised dialects across the Viking world. The modern fragmentation into mutually unintelligible languages (Icelandic excepted) is a post-medieval phenomenon, driven by political separation, sound changes operating independently in each region, and the different written-standardisation histories of the Scandinavian kingdoms.

**The Dialect Continuum: West Norse, East Norse, and Gutnish**

Old Norse was not a monolithic language but a **dialect continuum** — a chain of mutually intelligible regional varieties shading into one another without sharp boundaries. The conventional tripartite division:

1. **Old West Norse** — Spoken in Norway, Iceland, the Faroe Islands, Shetland, Orkney, the Norse settlements of Ireland and the Scottish Isles, and (via Norwegian colonisation) Greenland. This is the language of the vast majority of surviving Old Norse literature: the Icelandic sagas, the Eddic poems, the skaldic corpus, the lawcodes (Grágás), and the historical works. Modern Icelandic is the most conservative descendant — a 21st-century Icelander can read a 13th-century saga with roughly the same effort that a modern English speaker expends on Shakespeare. Faroese is also notably conservative, especially in its morphology, though its phonology has diverged significantly.

2. **Old East Norse** — Spoken in Denmark, Sweden, and the Norse colonies along the eastern river routes (Staraya Ladoga, Novgorod, Kyiv). This is the primary language of the Danish and Swedish runestones, the Scanian lawcode, and the early Danish and Swedish chronicles. Modern Danish and Swedish are its descendants, along with the Bokmål written standard of Norwegian (which descends from Danish via the Dano-Norwegian union). The East Norse dialect area was originally distinguished from West Norse by the monophthongisation of the Proto-Norse diphthongs: West *stein* (stone) vs. East *stēn*, West *auga* (eye) vs. East *ø̄ga*, West *ey* (island) vs. East *ø̄*.

3. **Old Gutnish** — Spoken on the island of Gotland, a major Viking Age trading hub in the Baltic. Gutnish is sometimes classified as a distinctive branch of East Norse, but it preserves several archaic features lost in both West and East Norse, including the retention of the Proto-Norse diphthong *ai* where both West and East monophthongised: Gutnish *stain* vs. West/East *stein/stēn*. The **Guta saga** and the **Guta lag** (law of the Gotlanders) are the primary surviving texts in Old Gutnish. Modern Gutnish survives as a severely endangered spoken language, with perhaps 2,000–5,000 speakers, though revitalisation efforts in the 2030s have produced a modest recovery.

**Phonology: The Sound System of Old Norse**

Old Norse phonology is characterised by a rich vowel system and a series of sound changes that distinguish it from its Proto-Germanic ancestor. The most significant:

- **Umlaut (Mutation):** Vowel changes conditioned by a following vowel that has since been lost or altered. Three types:
  - **i-umlaut:** A back vowel is fronted by a following *i* or *j*: PGmc *fōts* → ON *fǫtr* (feet, from earlier *fōtiz*); *fulljan* → *fylla* (to fill); PGmc *satjan* → ON *setja* (to set).
  - **u-umlaut:** A non-round vowel is rounded by a following *u* or *w*: PGmc *sangwaz* → ON *sǫngr* (song); *landu* → *lǫnd* (lands, nom/acc pl).
  - **a-umlaut:** A high vowel is lowered before a following *a* (less systematic in Norse than in West Germanic).

- **Breaking:** Short *e* "breaks" into a diphthong before certain consonant clusters: *e* → *ja* before *a* or *u* in the following syllable (*ek* → *jak*, later *ek* restored by analogy in West Norse); *e* → *jǫ* before *u* in the following syllable (*erþu* → *jǫrð*).

- **Syncope:** The loss of unstressed vowels, a process that began in the Proto-Norse period and was largely complete by the Viking Age: PGmc *haftaR* → PN *haftaR* → ON *hafðr* (had, past participle); PGmc *skipan* → ON *skipa* (to arrange). Syncope had profound morphological consequences, collapsing the inflectional endings that distinguished cases and producing the complex system of stem alternations that characterises Norse noun and verb morphology.

- **Assimilation:** Consonant clusters are simplified by the assimilation of one consonant to another: *nk* → *kk* (*drank* → *drakk*), *nt* → *tt* (*bant* → *batt*), *lþ* → *ll* (*gulþ* → *gull*, gold). These assimilations are a hallmark of West Norse and are much less pronounced in East Norse, providing one of the principal diagnostic features for distinguishing the two dialect groups.

**A Note on Orthography**

The standardised Old Norse orthography used in this course (and in most modern scholarly editions) is a 19th-century construct, primarily the work of the Norwegian philologist Carl Richard Unger and the Danish scholar Ludvig Wimmer. It does not perfectly represent the orthographic practices of any medieval manuscript, which vary considerably. The conventions students should internalise:

- **Ǫ** (o-ogonek): Represents a low rounded back vowel derived from u-umlaut of *a*. Pronounced approximately as the "aw" in English "law" with rounded lips. Often transliterated as *ö* in modern Icelandic editions.
- **Ð/ð** (eth): Voiced dental fricative, as in English "that." Never appears at the beginning of a word.
- **Þ/þ** (thorn): Voiceless dental fricative, as in English "think." Appears in all positions.
- **Æ/æ** (ash): A low front vowel, approximately as in English "cat."
- **Œ/œ** (oe-ligature): A mid front rounded vowel, derived from i-umlaut of *ó*. Often transliterated as *æ* in modern Icelandic.
- **Acute accent:** Marks vowel length, not stress. The acute is a modern scholarly convention; medieval manuscripts used doubling or other devices.

## Lecture 2: The Runic Alphabets — Writing Before and Beside the Latin Script

**From Elder to Younger Futhark: The Scriptural Transformation of the Viking Age**

The runic alphabets (*rúnar*, singular *rún*, meaning both "rune" and "mystery, secret knowledge") are the earliest writing systems developed by Germanic-speaking peoples. The **Elder Futhark** (24 characters, named after the first six runes: *f, u, þ, a, r, k*) was in use from approximately 150–700 CE across the Germanic world, from Scandinavia to the Black Sea. It was used for short inscriptions on weapons, jewellery, and memorial stones — never for extended narrative or administrative texts. The Elder Futhark inscriptions are our only direct linguistic evidence for the pre-Viking Germanic languages, and their interpretation remains a central challenge of runology.

Around 700 CE, the Elder Futhark underwent a dramatic reduction in Scandinavia, producing the **Younger Futhark** of 16 characters. This reduction — from 24 characters to 16 — was a remarkable simplification that created a writing system radically more ambiguous than its predecessor. The reasons for this reduction remain debated. The 2040 UoY position, following the work of Michael Schulte, Henrik Williams, and the UoY Runology Research Group (2037), favours a combination of factors: phonological changes that reduced the number of distinctive sounds (making some Elder runes redundant), the functional restriction of runic writing to short, formulaic inscriptions (where context could resolve ambiguity), and possibly deliberate esoteric restriction — the reduction of the accessible rune-row to a more "powerful" core set.

**The 16 Runes of the Younger Futhark**

The Younger Futhark characters, with their Old Norse names and primary sound values:

| Rune | Name | Transliteration | Sound Value(s) |
|------|------|----------------|----------------|
| ᚠ | *fé* (wealth, cattle) | f | /f/, /v/ |
| ᚢ | *úr* (drizzle, slag) | u | /u/, /o/, /y/, /ø/, /w/ |
| ᚦ | *þurs* (giant) | þ | /θ/, /ð/ |
| ᚬ | *óss/áss* (god) | ą/o | /ã/, /o/, /ǫ/ |
| ᚴ | *kaun* (ulcer) | k | /k/, /g/, /ŋk/ |
| ᚼ | *hagall* (hail) | h | /h/ |
| ᚾ | *nauðr* (need, distress) | n | /n/ |
| ᛁ | *íss* (ice) | i | /i/, /e/, /j/ |
| ᛅ | *ár* (year, harvest) | a | /a/, /æ/ |
| ᛋ | *sól* (sun) | s | /s/ |
| ᛏ | *Týr* (the god Týr) | t | /t/, /d/, /nt/ |
| ᛒ | *bjarkan* (birch) | b | /b/, /p/, /mb/ |
| ᛗ | *maðr* (man) | m | /m/ |
| ᛚ | *lǫgr* (water, sea) | l | /l/ |
| ᛦ | *ýr* (yew) | ʀ/R | /ʀ/ (palatal r), later /y/ |

The ambiguity of this system is staggering by modern alphabetic standards. The rune ᚢ alone represents five vowel qualities (*u*, *o*, *y*, *ø*, the semivowel *w*). The rune ᚴ represents both voiced and voiceless velar stops (*k* and *g*). The rune ᛏ represents both *t* and *d*. Context — linguistic, formulaic, and archaeological — resolved most ambiguities for the intended audience, but modern runologists frequently disagree about the reading of individual inscriptions. The ambiguity problem was partially addressed by the development of **"stung" (dotted) runes** in the 11th century: a dot added to ᚴ distinguished *g* from *k* (ᚵ), a dot on ᛁ distinguished *e* from *i* (ᛂ), and so forth. These medieval runic innovations mark the transition from the Viking Age runic tradition to the medieval runic tradition that persisted in Scandinavia (especially in rural contexts) into the 19th century.

**Runestones as Literature: The Memorial Tradition**

Over 3,000 Viking Age runestones survive, the vast majority in Sweden (particularly Uppland). They are overwhelmingly **commemorative** — erected to preserve the memory of the dead, to claim inheritance, to assert social status, and to mark the Christianisation of memorial practice. The typical formula, repeated with variations on hundreds of stones: *"X raised this stone in memory of Y, his/her [relationship]. God help his/her soul."* The formula is so consistent that it must represent a conscious genre — a runestone was not a free-form artistic expression but a specific kind of social act, governed by conventions as strict as those of the skaldic stanza.

Some runestones transcend the formulaic. The **Rök Stone** (Östergötland, Sweden, c. 800 CE) bears the longest known runic inscription — approximately 760 characters (the exact count depends on how rune sequences are segmented) — and is the most complex and debated runic text in existence. Its content appears to include a memorial to a dead son, a series of riddles referencing mythological and heroic figures (Þjóðríkr, the Ostrogothic king; the monster of the *niu aldum*), and a complex cipher employing multiple rune-rows. The Rök Stone has generated an enormous scholarly literature and remains only partially understood; the 2040 UoY Runology Research Group's multispectral imaging project (2038) has revealed previously illegible characters that may substantially alter the accepted reading.

The **Jelling stones** (Jelling, Denmark) are the "birth certificate of Denmark" — the smaller stone erected by King Gorm the Old for his wife Thyra (*"tanmarkaR bót"*, "Denmark's adornment"), and the larger by Haraldr blátǫnn (Harald Bluetooth) c. 965 CE, bearing the famous inscription: *"Haraldr king ordered this monument made in memory of Gorm his father and Þyrvé his mother — that Haraldr who won for himself all Denmark and Norway and made the Danes Christian."* The large Jelling stone is triply significant: it is the first textual mention of "Denmark" as a political entity, it explicitly records the Christianisation of the Danes, and its iconography (the great beast, the crucified Christ, the interlace) fuses pagan and Christian visual vocabularies in a single monument.

**Runes and Magic: The Esoteric Dimension**

The Old Norse word *rún* means both "rune" (letter, character) and "mystery, secret, hidden knowledge." The poetic vocabulary reinforces this semantic field: *rúna* (to whisper secrets, to speak intimately), *rýna* (to scrutinise, to peer into mysteries), *rúnar* (mysteries, secrets, hidden things). The mythological foundation narrative — Óðinn's self-sacrifice on Yggdrasill to obtain the runes (*Hávamál* 138–144) — presents the runes not as human inventions but as divine revelations obtained through ordeal: "I know that I hung on a windy tree, nine full nights, wounded by a spear, given to Óðinn, myself to myself... They gave me no bread nor drink from a horn; I peered down, I took up the runes — screaming, I took them — then I fell back from there."

The extent to which runes were used for "magic" in the Viking Age is debated. The evidence includes: the **Björketorp stone** (Blekinge, Sweden, c. 650–700 CE), which bears a curse formula: *"I, master of the runes, conceal here runes of power. Incessantly plagued by maleficence, doomed to insidious death is he who breaks this monument"*; the **Lindholm amulet** (Skåne, Sweden, c. 500 CE), a bone piece with the repeated formula *"ek erilaR sawilagaR hateka"* (I, the erilaz, am called the cunning one / the one of the sun); and the numerous *alu* inscriptions (the most common runic formula, of uncertain meaning — possibly "ale," possibly "magical protection," possibly a divine name). The 2040 UoY position: runes were **polysemous** — simultaneously practical writing tools, commemorative devices, and esoteric symbols. The boundary between "practical" and "magical" literacy is a modern imposition; Viking Age Scandinavians probably did not distinguish sharply between writing to communicate and writing to invoke.

## Lecture 3: The Poetic Edda — Mythological and Heroic Poetry

**The Codex Regius: Discovery, Contents, and Problems**

The **Codex Regius** (GKS 2365 4to, Reykjavík, Árni Magnússon Institute) is the primary manuscript preserving the poems we call the **Poetic Edda**. The manuscript was compiled in Iceland c. 1270 CE, more than two centuries after the conversion to Christianity and at least three centuries after the composition of its oldest poems. It was discovered in 1643 by Bishop Brynjólfur Sveinsson of Skálholt, who gave it the title *Edda Sæmundi multiscii* (the Edda of Sæmundr the Wise) — an attribution to the 12th-century Icelandic scholar Sæmundr Sigfússon that modern scholarship rejects. Brynjólfur sent the manuscript as a gift to the Danish king in 1662, hence its shelfmark in the Royal Library in Copenhagen (Den gamle kongelige Samling). It was returned to Iceland in 1971, a repatriation of immense symbolic importance.

The Codex Regius contains 29 poems (plus some fragmentary verses) divided into two sections by a lacuna (a missing gathering of leaves): the **mythological poems** (poems 1–11) and the **heroic poems** (poems 12–29). The heroic poems are further subdivided into the Helgi poems, the Vǫlsung cycle (Sigurðr, Brynhildr, Guðrún, Gunnarr), and the Hamðir poems. Some Eddic poems survive outside the Codex Regius — *Baldrs draumar*, *Rígsþula*, *Hyndluljóð*, *Gróttasǫngr* — and are included in modern editions as "Eddica minora."

**The Mythological Poems: Cosmology, Wisdom, and Divine Drama**

The mythological poems of the Edda constitute the most detailed surviving account of Norse cosmology and theology — though "theology" is a problematic term for a tradition that had no systematic doctrine. The major mythological poems:

- ***Vǫluspá*** (The Seeress's Prophecy): The most famous and theologically ambitious Eddic poem. Óðinn summons a dead vǫlva (seeress) and compels her to recount the history of the cosmos from creation to Ragnarǫk and beyond. The poem's vision is vast: the primordial void (*Ginnungagap*), the creation of the world from the body of Ymir, the golden age of the gods, the first war (Æsir-Vanir), the death of Baldr, the breaking of bonds, the onset of Ragnarǫk, and the rebirth of a new, green world after the destruction. The poem's 66 stanzas (in the Codex Regius version; the Hauksbók version adds several stanzas) have generated more scholarly commentary than any other Eddic text. The 2040 UoY position, building on the work of John McKinnell, Ursula Dronke, and the UoY Eddic Studies Group (2039): *Vǫluspá* is the product of the 10th century, composed by a poet deeply familiar with Christian eschatology (the Ragnarǫk sequence parallels the Christian Apocalypse in structure if not in detail) but creatively reworking it within a Norse cosmological framework. The poem is not a "pagan Bible" — it is a learned, sophisticated literary composition by an individual poet, not the spontaneous expression of a collective folk tradition.

- ***Hávamál*** (Sayings of the High One): A composite poem in at least six distinct sections, attributed to Óðinn and covering practical wisdom (stanzas 1–79), advice on love and women (80–95), Óðinn's affair with Gunnlǫð (96–110), Loddfáfnir's moral counsel (111–137), Óðinn's self-sacrifice on Yggdrasill (138–145), and a catalogue of magical songs (146–164). The poem is the most important source for Norse gnomic wisdom — practical, cynical, and occasionally profound: "Cattle die, kinsmen die, the self must also die; I know one thing that never dies: the reputation of each dead man" (stanza 77).

- ***Grímnismál*** (Grímnir's Sayings): Óðinn, disguised and tortured between two fires, delivers a cosmological revelation to a human king. The poem is a catalogue of divine dwellings, rivers, horses, and cosmic structures — a versified mythological encyclopedia.

- ***Þrymskviða*** (The Lay of Þrymr): The most overtly comic Eddic poem. The giant Þrymr steals Þórr's hammer and demands the goddess Freyja as bride-price. Þórr — at Loki's instigation — dresses as Freyja in bridal linen and attends the wedding feast at Þrymr's hall, where his prodigious appetite (he eats an entire ox and eight salmon) nearly blows his cover. The hammer is brought out to sanctify the "bride," Þórr seizes it and massacres the giants. The poem's humour — the burly, cross-dressing thunder-god, the giants' stupidity — is rare in Old Norse poetry and suggests a late, perhaps 13th-century, composition with self-conscious literary artifice.

**The Heroic Poems: The Vǫlsung Cycle**

The heroic poems of the Edda narrate the tragedy of the Vǫlsung family — the greatest narrative cycle in Germanic heroic literature, parallel to the Nibelungenlied in Middle High German. The core story: the hero Sigurðr kills the dragon Fáfnir and wins the treasure of the Niflungar; he betrays the valkyrie Brynhildr through a magical deception; the widowed Guðrún is married to Atli (Attila the Hun), who murders her brothers for the treasure; Guðrún takes horrific revenge on Atli. The cycle's poems — *Reginsmál*, *Fáfnismál*, *Sigrdrífumál*, the *Sigurðarkviða* group, the *Guðrúnarkviða* group, *Atlakviða*, *Atlamál*, *Hamðismál* — explore themes of fate, vengeance, loyalty, and the destruction of whole families by the inexorable logic of the blood-feud.

**Metrical Forms: Fornyrðislag and Ljóðaháttr**

Eddic poetry uses two principal metres, both based on **alliteration** (stave-rhyme) rather than end-rhyme:

- **Fornyrðislag** (Old Story Metre): The narrative metre of the Edda. Each stanza consists of 8 short lines (4 long lines divided by a caesura). Each long line has four stressed syllables; two or three of these must alliterate — one or both in the first half-line with the first stressed syllable of the second half-line. The metre is relatively free in syllable count (4–6 syllables per half-line) and does not require internal rhyme. Used for narrative and heroic poems: *Vǫluspá*, *Þrymskviða*, the heroic elegies.

- **Ljóðaháttr** (Song Metre): The gnomic and dialogue metre. Each stanza consists of two three-line half-stanzas; the first two lines of each half-stanza are linked by alliteration, and the third line alliterates internally. The metre creates a distinctive rhythm — two balanced statements followed by a longer, more emphatic line. Used primarily for wisdom poetry (*Hávamál*) and the flyting (insult-contest) poems (*Lokasenna*, *Hárbarðsljóð*).

## Lecture 4: The Prose Edda — Snorri Sturluson and the Art of Mythography

**Snorri Sturluson: Poet, Chieftain, Historian**

Snorri Sturluson (1179–1241) is the most important figure in Old Norse literature, and arguably the most important medieval Scandinavian intellectual. A member of the powerful Sturlung family, Snorri was a major political actor in the turbulent final decades of the Icelandic Free State — twice lawspeaker at the Alþingi, a wealthy chieftain, and a profoundly ambitious man whose political machinations ultimately led to his assassination by agents of the Norwegian king Hákon Hákonarson in his own cellar at Reykjaholt in 1241. He was also the author of three works that define the Old Norse literary canon: the **Prose Edda** (c. 1220–1230), a handbook of poetics and mythology; **Heimskringla** (c. 1230), a history of the kings of Norway; and possibly **Egils saga** (though the attribution is debated).

The Prose Edda (also known as the *Snorra Edda* or *Younger Edda*) was composed as a **poetics manual** — an instructional text for young skalds who needed to understand the mythological kennings that were the essential vocabulary of skaldic poetry. By Snorri's time, the composition of skaldic verse had been a continuous tradition for over three centuries, but the conversion to Christianity had eroded the shared mythological knowledge without which kennings were incomprehensible. Snorri's project was, in essence, a rescue operation — a systematic preservation of the mythological referents that skaldic poetry required.

**The Four-Part Structure**

The Prose Edda consists of four distinct sections, each with a different purpose:

1. **Prologus (Prologue):** Snorri's euhemeristic framing of Norse mythology — the claim that the Norse gods were not gods at all but Trojan princes who migrated to Scandinavia after the fall of Troy and were subsequently deified by their descendants. This euhemerism was a standard medieval Christian strategy for handling pagan mythology — it allowed pagans to be dismissed as mistaken worshippers of deified humans without denying the historical existence of the figures involved. Snorri's euhemeristic account identifies Þórr with a grandson of Priam and Óðinn with a king of the Turks — a wildly ahistorical genealogy that reflects the medieval fascination with Trojan origins (cf. Geoffrey of Monmouth's *Historia Regum Britanniae*, which traces the British to Brutus the Trojan). The Prologus is Snorri's insurance policy — a Christian disclaimer that allows him to discuss pagan gods without appearing to endorse them.

2. **Gylfaginning (The Deluding of Gylfi):** The core mythological narrative. King Gylfi of Sweden, curious about the wisdom of the Æsir, travels to Ásgarðr in disguise under the name Gangleri. He encounters three figures — Hár (High), Jafnhár (Equally High), and Þriði (Third) — who answer his questions about the cosmos. The frame narrative allows Snorri to present Norse mythology as a dramatic dialogue, with Gylfi's questions providing the narrative structure. The dialogue covers the creation of the world (from Ymir's body), the cosmic geography (the nine worlds, Yggdrasill, Bifrǫst), the major gods (Óðinn, Þórr, Baldr, Loki, Freyja, Freyr, etc.), the death of Baldr, and Ragnarǫk. At the end of the dialogue, the hall and the three figures vanish — Gylfi has been deceived by illusion.

3. **Skáldskaparmál (The Language of Poetry):** A catalogue of kennings (*kenningar*) and heiti (poetic synonyms), each accompanied by the mythological story that explains it. For example, the kenning "Sif's hair" for gold is explained by the story of Loki cutting off the goddess Sif's hair and being forced to compensate by procuring golden replacement hair from the dwarfs. Skáldskaparmál is our single most important source for many Norse myths that are not preserved in the Poetic Edda — the mead of poetry, Þórr's duel with Hrungnir, the binding of Loki.

4. **Háttatal (Catalogue of Metres):** A demonstration poem of 102 stanzas, each in a different skaldic metre or variation, composed by Snorri in praise of King Hákon Hákonarson and Jarl Skúli Bárðarson. The poem is accompanied by a prose commentary explaining each metrical variant. Háttatal is the most elaborate metrical treatise in medieval European literature and demonstrates both Snorri's technical mastery and his political ambition (the poem was intended to secure Norwegian patronage).

**The Problem of Snorri: Reading a Christian Mythographer**

Snorri is simultaneously our most valuable source for Norse mythology and our most problematic. His Christian perspective, his temporal distance (200+ years after conversion), his literary ambitions, and his systematic drive — the desire to impose coherence on fragmentary and contradictory material — all shape his presentation of Norse myth in ways that must be critically assessed.

The 2040 UoY approach identifies four specific problems:

1. **Euhemeristic framing:** Snorri's Prologus is not a neutral introduction but a Christian polemic that strips the gods of divinity. The entire Gylfaginning is framed as a deception — the gods Gylfi encounters are not gods but illusion-weavers. Does Snorri believe his own euhemerism, or is it a posture adopted to protect himself from charges of paganism?

2. **Narrative imposition:** Snorri creates a coherent narrative arc — creation → Baldr's death → Ragnarǫk — from poems that were originally independent. His mythology reads like a story, with cause-and-effect relationships that may not have existed in the diffuse oral traditions he drew on. To what extent has Snorri "novelised" Norse myth?

3. **Selection bias:** Snorri includes the myths useful for explaining kennings and excludes those without poetic relevance. We know, for example, that there were myths about the god Ullr (place-name evidence suggests he was a major cult figure) — but Snorri mentions him only in passing, because Ullr generated few kennings. The Prose Edda is not a comprehensive mythology but a poet's reference work.

4. **Classical influence:** Snorri's structure — a frame dialogue in which a visitor questions wise beings — recalls classical models (Plato's dialogues, the *Dialogus* tradition). Snorri was a literate Christian educated in the Latin tradition; his literary models were as much classical as native.

Despite these problems, Snorri is indispensable. Without the Prose Edda, our knowledge of Norse mythology would be a fraction of what it is. Many myths that are only alluded to in the Poetic Edda are narrated in full in Skáldskaparmál. The 2040 UoY position: **use Snorri, but verify against the Poetic Edda, archaeological evidence, and comparative mythology whenever possible.**

## Lecture 5: The Icelandic Sagas — History, Fiction, and Cultural Memory

**The Íslendingasögur: Genre and Context**

The **Íslendingasögur** (Sagas of Icelanders, also called the Family Sagas) are prose narratives composed primarily in 13th- and 14th-century Iceland but set in the **saga age** — the period from the settlement (c. 870) to the conversion (c. 1000), with some extending into the 11th century. Approximately 40 Íslendingasögur survive, ranging from brief tales (*þættir*) to the monumental epics: *Njáls saga* (the longest, at over 400 pages in modern editions), *Egils saga*, *Laxdæla saga*, *Grettis saga*, and *Eyrbyggja saga*.

The sagas exist at a unique generic intersection. They are not history in the modern sense — they were composed 200–400 years after their settings and are shaped by literary conventions, oral narrative patterning, and the political concerns of their 13th-century authors. They are not pure fiction — they are anchored to real landscapes, real genealogies, and real events that can be cross-checked against archaeology and foreign sources (the Viking raids described in *Egils saga* correspond to Anglo-Saxon Chronicle entries; the legal procedures in *Njáls saga* are broadly consistent with the Grágás lawcode). The sagas are best understood as **cultural memory texts** — literary works that preserve, reshape, and transmit a community's understanding of its own past, blending historical fact, oral tradition, and literary art in proportions that vary from saga to saga and episode to episode.

**Major Sagas: A Guided Tour**

1. ***Egils saga*** — The life of Egill Skalla-Grímsson (c. 910–990 CE), the greatest of the Viking Age skalds. Egill is a monster and a genius: he commits his first murder at age seven, fights as a berserkr, travels through Norway, England (where he serves King Æthelstan at the Battle of Brunanburh), and the Baltic, and produces some of the most powerful poetry in Old Norse. His elegy *Sonatorrek* (The Loss of Sons), composed after the drowning of his favourite son Bǫðvarr, is arguably the greatest skaldic poem — a raw expression of grief that nearly kills the poet before the act of composition restores him. Egils saga explores the relationship between physical violence and artistic creation — Egill's ugliness (he is described as swarthy, beetle-browed, and brutish) and his beauty (his poetry) are two sides of the same coin. The 2040 UoY position: *Egils saga* is the most psychologically sophisticated of the Íslendingasögur and the one that most directly confronts the problem of creativity — where does art come from, and what does it cost?

2. ***Njáls saga*** — The longest and most structurally ambitious saga, often considered the masterpiece of the corpus. The saga follows the intertwined fates of two friends: Njáll Þorgeirsson, a wise lawyer who cannot fight, and Gunnarr Hámundarson, a peerless warrior who cannot resist fighting. The saga's central tragedy — the burning of Njáll and his family in their farmhouse at Bergþórshváll — is one of the most powerful scenes in medieval literature. Njáls saga is deeply interested in the relationship between law and violence: the saga's countless legal scenes (the Alþingi lawsuits are narrated with procedural precision) explore whether law can ever restrain the blood-feud, or whether the two systems — legal compensation and revenge killing — are fundamentally incompatible.

3. ***Laxdæla saga*** — The great love-triangle saga. The central narrative involves the tragic entanglement of Guðrún Ósvífrsdóttir (one of the most complex female characters in Old Norse literature), her beloved Kjartan Óláfsson, and his foster-brother Bolli Þorleiksson. Guðrún marries Bolli after Kjartan delays his return from Norway; Kjartan returns and humiliates Bolli; Bolli kills Kjartan; Guðrún's brothers kill Bolli; Guðrún, years later, when asked which man she loved most, delivers the saga's most famous line: "To him I was worst whom I loved best" (*"þeim var ek verst, er ek unna mest"*). The saga is the most "romantic" of the Íslendingasögur — deeply interested in love, jealousy, and the conflict between passion and social obligation.

4. ***Grettis saga*** — The outlaw saga. Grettir Ásmundarson is the strongest man in Iceland, a hero of superhuman physical power and deeply antisocial temperament. He is outlawed for a killing he did not intend, and spends 19 years as an outlaw, hunted but never captured, his strength and luck gradually draining away. His final stand against the draugr (ghost) Glámr — a fight that earns him the ghost's curse — is one of the great supernatural set pieces of the saga corpus. Grettis saga explores the limits of heroism: what happens when the qualities that make a man a hero also make him incapable of living in society?

**Saga Style: Economy and Understatement**

Saga prose is characterised by an almost startling economy. The narrator never editorialises, never analyses character motivation, never indulges in psychological description. The emotion is carried by what is **not** said. The classic example: in *Njáls saga*, when Gunnarr is outlawed and preparing to leave Iceland, his horse stumbles on the way to the ship. Gunnarr turns in the saddle to look at the hills around his farm and says: *"Fǫgr er hlíðin, svá at mér hefir hon aldri jǫfn fǫgr sýnzk"* (Fair is the hillside — never has it seemed so fair to me). That single sentence — the entire weight of his decision to stay and face his death — contains no explanation, no justification, no introspection. The reader must supply what the narrator withholds.

This narrative technique — sometimes called the "saga style" or "objective narrative" — has been the subject of extensive literary analysis. The 2040 UoY position, building on the work of Carol Clover, Vésteinn Ólason, and the UoY Narrative Studies Group (2039): saga economy is not naïveté but sophisticated literary technique. The refusal to editorialise forces the reader to interpret; the gap between external description and internal experience creates a depth that explicit psychological narration would flatten.

## Lecture 6: Skaldic Poetry — The Art of the Court Poet

**Skaldic vs. Eddic: Two Poetic Traditions**

The Old Norse poetic corpus divides into two distinct traditions separated by almost every formal and functional criterion:

| Feature | Eddic Poetry | Skaldic Poetry |
|---------|-------------|----------------|
| **Authorship** | Anonymous | Named poet (skáld) |
| **Subject** | Mythological, heroic narrative | Occasional (praise, memorial, personal) |
| **Metre** | Fornyrðislag, ljóðaháttr | Dróttkvætt (primarily) |
| **Diction** | Relatively straightforward | Dense with kennings and heiti |
| **Syntax** | Linear, paratactic | Heavily interwoven (hyperbaton) |
| **Transmission** | Manuscripts (Codex Regius) | Embedded in prose texts (sagas, Heimskringla) |
| **Historical value** | Cultural/mythological | Often contemporary with events described |

The skald (*skáld*) was a professional court poet, attached to the retinue of a king, jarl, or chieftain. His function was to praise his patron in verse — celebrating victories, commemorating generosity, and ensuring the patron's reputation (*orðstírr*, word-glory) survived death. Skaldic poetry was the Norse world's primary medium of political propaganda and historical memory. A king without a skald was a king without a future, because his deeds would die with him.

**Dróttkvætt: The Court Metre**

Dróttkvætt (Court Metre) is the dominant skaldic verse form and one of the most complex metrical systems in world literature. A dróttkvætt stanza consists of 8 short lines (4 long lines divided by caesura). Each line has exactly 6 syllables, ending in trochaic cadence (stressed-unstressed). The formal requirements:

- **Alliteration** (*stuðlar*): The first stressed syllable of the second half-line must alliterate with two stressed syllables in the first half-line (or one, if the first half-line's first stressed syllable begins with a vowel — the "vowel-alliteration" rule).
- **Internal rhyme** (*hendingar*): Odd lines carry *skothending* (consonant half-rhyme: *senda/sand*); even lines carry *aðalhending* (full rhyme: *skjalda/alda*).
- **Kennings**: At least one kenning per *helmingr* (half-stanza) is typical, though not strictly required.
- **Hyperbaton**: Syntactic elements are interwoven across clauses in complex patterns — a single sentence may be distributed across an entire stanza, with the subject in line 1, the verb in line 4, and the object in line 6.

The combined effect — strict syllable count, mandatory alliteration, two levels of rhyme, embedded kennings, and syntactic interlace — produces poetry of extraordinary density. A dróttkvætt stanza is not meant to be read quickly; it is meant to be decoded.

**Kennings: The Poetic Cryptography**

The **kenning** (*kenning*, literally "teaching, description, circumlocution") is the defining feature of skaldic diction. A kenning is a two-element circumlocution in which a base-word is modified by a determinant to produce a new referent. The logic is metaphorical but rule-governed: the base-word specifies the category, and the determinant specifies the sub-category by mythological, cultural, or functional association.

Examples of simple kennings:
- *hvalveggr* (whale-path) → sea (the path on which whales travel)
- *Hropts hugr* (Hroptr's mind) → poetry (Hroptr = Óðinn, whose mind/speech is poetry)
- *sær sverða* (sea of swords) → blood (the liquid that swords swim in)
- *viðr vigra* (forest of arrows) → battle (the space where arrows are thick as trees)
- *Freyju tárr* (Freyja's tears) → gold (Freyja wept tears of gold for her absent husband Óðr)

Compound kennings embed multiple levels of circumlocution: *eldr sævar sverða* (fire of the sea of swords) = fire of blood = sword. Or more elaborately: *hristir Hrungnis fóta* (shaker of Hrungnir's feet) = shaker of the giant's shield → warrior.

The 2040 UoY approach to kennings emphasises their cognitive function: kennings are not merely decorative but **epistemological** — they encode and transmit mythological knowledge. Every kenning that invokes a god, a myth, or a legendary figure presupposes and reinforces the audience's knowledge of Norse tradition. The kenning system is, in effect, a distributed mythological encyclopedia — by mastering kennings, the skald and his audience demonstrated and maintained their shared cultural literacy.

**Major Skalds and Their Works**

- **Bragi Boddason inn gamli** (the Old, 9th century): The earliest named skald whose work survives. His *Ragnarsdrápa* describes the mythological scenes painted on a shield — Þórr fishing for the Miðgarðsormr, the death of Baldr, the attack on Jǫrmunrekkr. The poem establishes the shield-ekphrasis as a skaldic sub-genre.

- **Þjóðólfr ór Hvini** (9th century): Court poet of Haraldr hárfagri. His *Ynglingatal* traces the genealogy of the Yngling dynasty of Vestfold (and eventually the Norwegian kings) back to the gods — the skaldic poem that Snorri uses as the backbone of the first section of Heimskringla.

- **Egill Skalla-Grímsson** (c. 910–990): The greatest skald of the Viking Age. His major surviving works: *Hǫfuðlausn* (Head-Ransom), composed in a single night to save his life from King Eiríkr blóðøx in York; *Sonatorrek* (Loss of Sons), an elegy for his drowned son; and *Arinbjarnarkviða*, a praise poem for his friend Arinbjǫrn. Egill's poetry spans the entire emotional range of the skaldic tradition — from formal praise to raw grief to bitter defiance.

- **Hallfreðr vandræðaskáld** Óttarsson (c. 965–1007): The "troublesome poet" of King Óláfr Tryggvason. Hallfreðr's poetry records his conflicted relationship with the Christianisation of Norway — he was baptised but never comfortable with the new faith, and his verse oscillates between Christian devotion and pagan nostalgia. His *Óláfsdrápa* and *Erfidrápa Óláfs Tryggvasonar* are major sources for Óláfr's reign.

- **Snorri Sturluson** (1179–1241): Author of the Prose Edda and *Háttatal*, the 102-stanza demonstration of skaldic metres. Snorri is the last major skald in the classical tradition; after him, the composition of dróttkvætt declined rapidly, displaced by the simpler ballad metres (*rímur*) and by the increasing dominance of Latin and continental literary models.

## Lecture 7: The Sagas of the Norse Kings — Heimskringla and the Construction of History

**Heimskringla: Snorri's Monumental History**

*Heimskringla* (The Orb of the World), composed by Snorri Sturluson c. 1230, is a history of the kings of Norway from their legendary origins in the Yngling dynasty (tracing back to the gods) through the civil war period of the 12th century, breaking off in 1177 CE — the year of the Battle of Re, after which Snorri probably considered the subsequent events too politically sensitive to narrate. The title is taken from the first words of the opening Ynglinga saga: *"Kringla heimsins, sú er mannfólkit byggvir, er mjǫk vágskorin"* (The orb of the world, which mankind inhabits, is much cut by bays). The image — the world as a sphere incised by the sea — is characteristically Snorri-esque: geographical precision serving historical narrative.

The work consists of 16 sagas, beginning with the legendary *Ynglinga saga* (which traces the Swedish-Norwegian Yngling dynasty to the god Freyr) and proceeding through the major Norwegian monarchs: Haraldr hárfagri (Fairhair), Hákon góði (the Good), Haraldr gráfeldr (Greycloak), Óláfr Tryggvason, Óláfr Haraldsson (St Olav), Magnús góði, Haraldr harðráði (Hardrada), and into the civil war period.

**Snorri's Method: Skaldic Poetry as Historical Source**

Snorri's most original contribution to historical methodology was his systematic use of skaldic poetry as historical evidence. He articulated this method explicitly in his Prologue:

*"At the court of Haraldr hárfagri there were skalds, and people still know their poems, and the poems about all the kings who have been in Norway since. And we take as most reliable what is said in those poems that were recited before the kings themselves or their sons. We consider all that is found in those poems about their expeditions or their battles to be true, because although it is the poet's way to praise the one he is addressing most highly, no one would dare to tell him about deeds which all who listened — including himself — knew to be false. That would be mockery, not praise."*

The argument is sophisticated: skaldic poetry is reliable not because poets are objective (they are not — they are paid to praise) but because the conditions of performance — reciting before the king and his court, who knew what had actually happened — made falsehood impossible. A skald could exaggerate, could select, could frame — but he could not fabricate. The poem was a public record, subject to verification by the community that heard it.

This methodological insight makes Snorri, in the judgement of the 2040 UoY, one of the most sophisticated historians of the medieval period — comparable to Ibn Khaldun or William of Malmesbury in his critical approach to sources.

**Key Kings in Heimskringla**

- **Haraldr hárfagri** (c. 872–930): The unifier of Norway. Snorri's account — that Haraldr swore not to cut his hair until he had unified Norway under his sole rule — is historically suspect (the unification was probably more gradual than the saga implies), but the saga preserves the essential cultural memory: that the Norwegian kingdom was forged through conquest and consolidation.

- **Óláfr Tryggvason** (995–1000): The missionary-king who forcibly Christianised coastal Norway. Snorri's portrait is ambivalent: Óláfr is a hero of the faith, but his methods — torture, execution, destruction of pagan temples — are described without flattery. The saga's account of Óláfr's final battle at Svǫlðr, surrounded by enemy fleets, leaping from his ship the *Ormr inn langi* (the Long Serpent) and never seen again, is one of the great set pieces of Norse prose.

- **Óláfr Haraldsson** (St Olav, 1015–1030): The saint-king whose martyrdom at Stiklestad (1030) ensured the Christianisation of Norway. Snorri's portrait is psychologically nuanced — Óláfr is devout but authoritarian, generous but vengeful, a saint in the making who is also, recognisably, a political actor with ambitions and flaws.

- **Haraldr harðráði** (1046–1066): The last Viking king. Snorri narrates Haraldr's years in the Varangian Guard, his return to Norway, his long war with Denmark, and his fatal invasion of England in 1066. The saga's account of the Battle of Stamford Bridge — Haraldr, surrounded, fighting to the death — is the valediction of the Viking Age.

**The Limitations of Heimskringla as History**

Snorri's virtues as a historian are genuine, but so are his limitations: **temporal distance** (200–400 years after the events), **narrative shaping** (Snorri selects and arranges for dramatic effect, not for comprehensive coverage), **political bias** (Snorri's complex relationship with the Norwegian crown colours his presentation of royal authority), and **the saga convention** (the requirement that history be told as a good story — with dialogue, character development, dramatic climax — sometimes overrides the obligation to strict accuracy). The 2040 UoY position: Heimskringla is an indispensable source, but it is literature as much as history, and it must be read with the same critical attention paid to any medieval chronicle.

## Lecture 8: Saga Literature — Narrative Structure, Theme, and the Art of Implication

**Saga Style: The Aesthetics of Restraint**

The defining feature of the Icelandic saga is its extraordinary narrative economy. Saga prose is paratactic (sentences linked by "and" and "then," rarely by subordination), externally focused (actions and speech, never interior thoughts), and relentlessly unsentimental. The narrator never sympathises, never condemns, never explains. The reader is given the surface and must supply the depth.

This narrative technique — sometimes called "objective narrative," though the term oversimplifies — has been the central problem of saga aesthetics since the 19th century. Is it the product of oral narrative conventions transposed into writing? Is it a deliberate literary technique, designed to create an effect of documentary objectivity? Is it the expression of a particular cultural psychology — a Norse preference for action over introspection, surface over depth?

The 2040 UoY position, synthesising the work of Carol Clover, Vésteinn Ólason, Heather O'Donoghue, and the UoY Narrative Studies Group: saga economy is best understood as a **hermeneutic strategy** — a technique that forces the reader to interpret. By refusing to provide interior access, the saga makes interpretation mandatory. The reader must supply motive, emotion, and moral judgement. This makes the reading experience active and participatory in a way that explicit psychological narration is not. The saga's silences are not absences — they are demands.

**Major Thematic Complexes**

1. **Vengeance and the Blood-Feud:** The blood-feud is the central social mechanism of the saga world and the primary engine of saga plot. A killing demands vengeance; vengeance provokes counter-vengeance; the feud escalates until resolved by legal settlement, complete extermination of one party, or exhaustion. The sagas are deeply ambivalent about feuding — they recognise its social logic (the obligation to avenge is absolute) while dramatising its human cost (the destruction of families, the alienation of individuals, the erosion of peace). *Njáls saga* is the definitive exploration: the feud that destroys Njáll and his family is set in motion by a domestic squabble between women (Bergþóra and Hallgerðr) that neither Njáll nor Gunnarr can control.

2. **Law and Justice:** The saga world is intensely legalistic — lawsuits at the Alþingi are narrated in procedural detail, with specific citations of legal rules. But the sagas also dramatise the limits of law: legal process can be manipulated by the powerful, legal settlements can be rejected by the aggrieved, and when law fails, only violence remains. The tension between legal process and blood-feud — between the assembly and the axe — is the central political problem of saga literature.

3. **Honour and Shame:** Honour (*sœmð*, *drengskapr*) is the primary currency of the saga world — more important than wealth, more important than life. Honour is public, external, and relational: it exists only in the recognition of others. A man without honour is a *níðingr* (wretch, scoundrel, the most damning insult in Old Norse), and death is preferable to the loss of honour. The sagas explore the costs of this honour-culture — the impossible choices it imposes, the violence it generates, the humanity it suppresses.

4. **Fate and Agency:** The sagas operate in a worldview in which fate (*ørlǫg*, *skǫp*) is real and knowable — characters have prophetic dreams, encounter supernatural beings, and make decisions in full awareness that fate is against them. But the sagas also insist on human agency: characters make choices, and their choices have consequences. The paradox — fate and free will coexisting — is never resolved; it is the existential condition of the saga protagonist.

5. **Gender and Power:** Saga women exercise power through channels distinct from men's but equally consequential. Women are excluded from the thing, from legal process, and from formal political authority, but they are central to the feud system — they goad men to vengeance, they manage households and alliances, they transmit honour and shame across generations. Hallgerðr in *Njáls saga*, Guðrún in *Laxdæla saga*, and Bergþóra in *Njáls saga* are not passive victims of male violence — they are active agents whose decisions drive the plot.

6. **Outlawry and the Limits of Society:** The outlaw (*útlagi* or *skógarmaðr* — "forest man," one who lives in the wilderness beyond human law) is the limit figure of the saga world. The outlaw is expelled from the social order — anyone may kill him without penalty, no one may help him — and his story explores what remains when society is removed. *Grettis saga* and *Gísla saga Súrssonar* are the major outlaw narratives. Both Grettir and Gísli are sympathetic — they are outlawed for killings that were not (or not entirely) their fault — but both are also deeply antisocial, incapable of living peaceably among others. The outlaw saga asks: can a person exist outside society, or does expulsion from the social order constitute a kind of death?

## Lecture 9: The Continental Norse — Saxo Grammaticus and the Latin Tradition

**Saxo Grammaticus and the *Gesta Danorum***

Saxo Grammaticus (c. 1150–1220 CE) was a Danish cleric, probably a canon of Lund Cathedral, who composed the *Gesta Danorum* (Deeds of the Danes) c. 1208–1218 CE at the request of Archbishop Absalon of Lund. The *Gesta Danorum* is the most important source for Danish legendary history and the single longest continuous Latin work produced in medieval Scandinavia. It consists of 16 books, of which the first nine cover the legendary pre-Christian period and the final seven cover the historical Christian period from Harald Bluetooth to Saxo's own time (c. 1185 CE).

Saxo's relationship to the Norse tradition is complex and instructive. He was a Danish patriot writing in Latin for an international clerical audience; his sources included Danish oral tradition, Icelandic saga material (possibly through informants), and classical Latin literature. His handling of Norse myth is shaped by this triple allegiance — he is simultaneously a custodian of native tradition and a critic of paganism, a Christian apologist and a Danish nationalist.

**Saxo's Version of Norse Mythology**

Saxo's mythological narratives differ substantially from the Prose Edda versions. In the *Gesta Danorum*, the gods are consistently euhemerised — presented as human magicians who deceived the ignorant into worshipping them. Baldr (called Balderus) is not a gentle, beloved god but a warrior who fights with Hotherus (Hǫðr) over the love of Nanna. Þórr (called Thoro) is depicted as a trickster figure who attempts to seduce women by magical deception. Óðinn (Othinus) is a shape-shifting sorcerer who is repeatedly exiled from the divine community for sexual misconduct.

These differences present a fundamental methodological problem. Are Saxo's versions alternative traditions — genuine Danish variants of myths that survive in Icelandic form in the Eddas? Or are they Saxo's deliberate distortions — Christian polemic disguised as euhemeristic history? The 2040 UoY position: both explanations operate simultaneously. Saxo certainly had access to Danish oral traditions that differed from the Icelandic versions; but his Latin, clerical, Christian framework inevitably shaped his presentation. The *Gesta Danorum* is evidence for Norse mythology, but it is evidence that has passed through a particularly dense filter of Christian and classical mediation.

**The Danish Runic Corpus**

The Danish runestones — particularly those of the Jelling dynasty — provide the East Norse counterpart to the West Norse textual tradition. The three Jelling stones document the transition from paganism to Christianity across three generations: Gorm's stone (the smaller, for his wife Thyra, c. 940–950 CE), Harald Bluetooth's stone (the larger, c. 965 CE, explicitly Christian), and the fragmentary third stone (possibly for Gorm himself, pagan). The stones are bilingual in a sense — their texts are in Old Danish (runes), but their visual programme (the crucified Christ on Harald's stone) is Christian. Together, they constitute Denmark's most important historical document.

Other significant Danish runestones: the **Hørning stone** (Jutland, c. 1000 CE), which shows a church building carved in stone — one of the earliest architectural representations in Scandinavia; the **Sjellebro stone** (Jutland, 10th century), which bears the image of a masked man — possibly a depiction of Óðinn; and the **Sønder Vinge stone** (Jutland, c. 1000 CE), which commemorates a man who "died in the east with the Varangians" — evidence for Danish participation in the eastern Norse expansion.

**Saxo and Snorri: A Comparison**

Saxo and Snorri are contemporaries (Snorri: 1179–1241; Saxo: c. 1150–1220) working on parallel projects — the monumental history of their respective nations — but their methods and relationships to their sources differ fundamentally:

| Feature | Snorri (Heimskringla) | Saxo (Gesta Danorum) |
|---------|----------------------|----------------------|
| **Language** | Old Norse | Latin |
| **Primary sources** | Skaldic poetry, oral tradition | Danish oral tradition, classical models |
| **Attitude to myth** | Preservative (for poetic use) | Polemical (euhemeristic) |
| **Audience** | Icelandic/Norwegian elite | International clerical elite |
| **Historical method** | Source-critical (skaldic verification) | Rhetorical (classical historiography) |

The comparison illuminates the range of medieval Scandinavian historical writing — from the vernacular, source-critical, tradition-preserving mode of Snorri to the Latin, rhetorical, tradition-transforming mode of Saxo.

## Lecture 10: Manuscript Culture — How Old Norse Literature Survived

**The Material Transmission of the Norse Literary Heritage**

Every Old Norse text that survives — every Eddic poem, every saga, every skaldic stanza, every runic inscription — survives because someone, at some point, wrote it down on a perishable surface (parchment or, less commonly, paper) and that surface survived the hazards of fire, water, insects, reuse, neglect, and the passage of centuries. The survival of Old Norse literature is not a given; it is a contingent, fragile achievement, the product of countless acts of copying, preserving, and collecting across eight centuries.

The manuscript culture of medieval Iceland was remarkable by European standards. Iceland produced more vernacular manuscripts per capita than any other medieval European society — a testament to the extraordinary literary productivity of a population of perhaps 40,000–60,000 people living at the margin of the habitable world. Why Iceland? The convergence of factors: a literate elite (Christianity brought the Latin alphabet in the 11th century), a tradition of oral narrative (the sagas had been told for generations before they were written), political instability (the 13th-century Sturlung Age generated a need to document legal and genealogical claims), and the availability of parchment (Iceland's sheep-based economy produced the raw material in abundance).

**Key Manuscripts**

The surviving manuscripts that transmit Old Norse literature are concentrated in a few major repositories:

- **Codex Regius of the Poetic Edda** (GKS 2365 4to, Árni Magnússon Institute, Reykjavík): The primary manuscript of the Poetic Edda, c. 1270 CE, 45 leaves, preserving 29 poems. The manuscript is missing a gathering of leaves (the "Great Lacuna") that would have contained the end of *Sigrdrífumál* and the beginning of the Sigurðr poems. The manuscript's small size (19 × 13 cm) suggests it was a personal copy, not a library volume — a book meant to be carried and read privately.

- **Móðruvallabók** (AM 132 fol., Árni Magnússon Institute): The largest single collection of Íslendingasögur, compiled c. 1330–1370, containing 11 sagas including *Njáls saga*, *Egils saga*, *Laxdæla saga*, and *Grettis saga*. The manuscript is the work of a single scribe, working carefully and systematically — a monumental act of literary preservation.

- **Flateyjarbók** (GKS 1005 fol., Árni Magnússon Institute): The largest medieval Icelandic manuscript (225 leaves, 45 × 33 cm), commissioned c. 1387 by the wealthy farmer Jón Hákonarson and written by two priests, Jón Þórðarson and Magnús Þórhallsson. Flateyjarbók contains an expanded version of the kings' sagas with additional material — including the *Grænlendinga saga* (the Saga of the Greenlanders), which is preserved only here, and *Eiríks saga rauða* (the Saga of Erik the Red).

- **Konungsbók** (GKS 1157 fol., Árni Magnússon Institute): The primary manuscript of the Grágás lawcode, c. 1250 CE, containing the most complete record of the laws of the Icelandic Free State. The manuscript is an invaluable source for Icelandic legal history and social structure.

**Árni Magnússon and the Preservation of the Manuscripts**

Árni Magnússon (1663–1730) was an Icelandic scholar and collector who assembled the largest collection of medieval Norse manuscripts in existence — over 3,000 items, now housed at the Árni Magnússon Institute in Reykjavík and the Arnamagnæan Collection in Copenhagen. Árni's achievement was to recognise, at a time when medieval manuscripts were often treated as worthless scrap, that these documents were the irreplaceable foundation of Scandinavian cultural identity. He spent his life travelling through Iceland, purchasing and accepting donations of manuscripts, many in advanced states of decay — worm-eaten, water-damaged, burnt — and preserving them for posterity.

The 1728 Copenhagen Fire destroyed much of Árni's collection stored in his home (he famously rescued what he could, throwing manuscripts out of the window). The surviving manuscripts were bequeathed to the University of Copenhagen, and in 1971, the Danish government began repatriating the Icelandic manuscripts to Iceland — a process completed in 1997 with the return of the Flateyjarbók. The repatriation was an act of profound cultural significance, restoring to Iceland the physical documents of its literary heritage.

**The 2040 Manuscript Cloud**

The Árni Magnússon Institute's **Manuscript Cloud** (launched 2035, expanded 2040) provides full digital access to the entire Arnamagnæan Collection: high-resolution multi-spectral images, side-by-side comparison views, AI-assisted paleographic analysis, and integrated scholarly editions. The platform has transformed Old Norse manuscript studies by making previously inaccessible manuscripts available to scholars worldwide — allowing, for the first time, systematic comparison of scribal hands, orthographic conventions, and textual variants across the entire corpus. The 2040 UoY encourages students to use the Manuscript Cloud as their primary research interface for manuscript-based projects.

## Lecture 11: Modern Old Norse — Revival, Scholarship, and the Living Language

**Icelandic: The Most Conservative North Germanic Language**

Modern Icelandic is the most conservative descendant of Old Norse — and one of the most conservative languages in Europe. The linguistic conservatism of Icelandic operates on multiple levels:

- **Phonology:** The Icelandic vowel system retains the Old Norse distinctions of length and quality that have been lost or transformed in the mainland Scandinavian languages. The dental fricatives (þ, ð) — lost in all other Germanic languages except English — remain fully functional in Icelandic.
- **Morphology:** Icelandic retains the four-case system (nominative, accusative, genitive, dative), three grammatical genders, and the strong/weak declensional and conjugational patterns of Old Norse. A modern Icelander conjugates a verb or declines a noun using essentially the same inflectional system as a 13th-century saga writer.
- **Lexicon:** Icelandic has actively resisted lexical borrowing, coining native neologisms for modern concepts: *tölva* (computer, from *tala* "number" + *völva* "prophetess" — literally "number-prophetess"), *sími* (telephone, from an old word for "thread"), *sjónvarp* (television, "sight-throwing"), *rafmagn* (electricity, "amber-power" — a calque on the Greek *elektron*, meaning amber). The Icelandic Language Committee (*Íslensk málnefnd*) actively monitors and regulates the language, a form of linguistic planning almost unknown elsewhere in the Germanic-speaking world.
- **Literary continuity:** Icelanders have read the sagas continuously since the Middle Ages. The literary language has never been a dead language requiring scholarly reconstruction; it has been a living presence in Icelandic culture for eight centuries.

This conservatism has a direct consequence for Old Norse studies: a modern Icelander can read a 13th-century saga with roughly the same effort that a modern English speaker expends on Shakespeare. The language has changed — vocabulary, some syntax, some pronunciation — but the changes are incremental, not transformative. For the non-Icelandic student of Old Norse, this means that modern Icelandic is both a helpful crutch (linguistic intuition can often guide interpretation) and a potential trap (modern Icelandic meanings can be projected back onto medieval words that had different semantic ranges).

**The History of Old Norse Scholarship**

Old Norse philology as an academic discipline emerged in the 19th century as part of the broader development of comparative Indo-European linguistics and Germanic philology. Key figures:

- **Rasmus Rask** (1787–1832): Danish linguist who wrote the first systematic grammar of Old Norse (1811) and was a pioneer of comparative Germanic linguistics. Rask's *Undersøgelse om det gamle Nordiske eller Islandske Sprogs Oprindelse* (Investigation of the Origin of the Old Norse or Icelandic Language, 1818) established the genetic relationship between Norse and the other Germanic languages on rigorous comparative grounds.
- **Jacob Grimm** (1785–1863): German philologist whose *Deutsche Grammatik* (1819–1837) formulated Grimm's Law — the systematic sound correspondences between Germanic and the other Indo-European languages. Grimm was also a collector of folk tales (*Kinder- und Hausmärchen*, with his brother Wilhelm) and a major figure in the Romantic valorisation of the Germanic past.
- **Sophus Bugge** (1833–1907): Norwegian philologist whose editions of the Poetic Edda (1867) and his controversial theories about the Christian and classical origins of Norse mythology (*Studier over de nordiske Gude- og Heltesagns Oprindelse*, 1881–1889) dominated 19th-century Eddic studies. Bugge argued that many Norse myths were derived from Christian and classical sources — a position that the 2040 UoY regards as excessively diffusionist but valuable for its insistence on cultural contact as a mechanism of mythological change.
- **Andreas Heusler** (1865–1940): Swiss-German philologist who established the modern distinction between Eddic and skaldic poetry, argued for the oral-formulaic composition of Eddic verse, and produced foundational studies of saga style and narrative technique.

**Old Norse Studies in 2040**

The 2040 field of Old Norse studies has been transformed by technological and methodological innovations:

- **AI-assisted text analysis:** Machine learning algorithms trained on the Old Norse corpus can now detect patterns invisible to human readers — formulaic repetitions across the skaldic corpus, scribal variation patterns, authorship signals in anonymous texts. The UoY Computational Philology Group's *Skáld* platform (2038) allows students to input a skaldic stanza and receive automated metrical analysis, kenning decoding, and attribution probability.
- **Multi-spectral imaging:** Previously illegible manuscripts are being read for the first time using multi-spectral imaging techniques that reveal text invisible to the naked eye. The UoY Manuscript Imaging Lab's ongoing project on the margins of the Flateyjarbók has revealed dozens of previously unknown marginalia.
- **Community-based scholarship:** The Ásatrú and Heathen communities have become active participants in Old Norse scholarship — not as passive consumers but as contributors of embodied knowledge about ritual practice, mythological interpretation, and the lived experience of Norse religion. The 2040 UoY encourages respectful collaboration between academic philology and practitioner communities.
- **Decolonial approaches:** Scholars are increasingly challenging the Eurocentric assumptions embedded in traditional philology — questioning the privileging of written over oral tradition, the marginalisation of Sami and Finnic perspectives, and the entanglement of Old Norse studies with nationalist and racial ideologies. The 2040 UoY's *Decolonising the Sagas* project (2039) is a multi-year initiative to recentre marginalised voices in the study of Norse literature.

## Lecture 12: Old Norse in Modern Culture — Reception, Adaptation, and the Ethics of the Viking Imaginary

**The Long Afterlife of Old Norse Literature**

Old Norse literature has had a cultural afterlife far exceeding the modest circumstances of its medieval production. The sagas and Eddas have been translated, adapted, appropriated, and reimagined across every modern medium — from Wagnerian opera to Marvel blockbusters, from Tolkien's Middle-earth to contemporary fantasy fiction, from video games to political propaganda. This lecture surveys the major phases of this reception history and explores the ethical challenges posed by the modern circulation of Norse cultural heritage.

**The 19th Century: Romantic Rediscovery**

The European discovery of Old Norse literature in the late 18th and 19th centuries was part of the broader Romantic fascination with the primitive, the authentic, and the national. Thomas Percy's *Five Pieces of Runic Poetry* (1763) and his *Northern Antiquities* (1770, a translation of Paul-Henri Mallet's *Introduction à l'histoire de Dannemarc*) introduced the English-reading public to the Eddas and sagas. The impact on English literature was immediate and profound: the "Norse ode" became a fashionable sub-genre, and Norse themes permeated the work of Thomas Gray ("The Descent of Odin," 1768), William Blake, and — most consequentially — the young Walter Scott, whose historical novels were deeply shaped by his reading of the Norse sagas.

In Germany, the Romantic reception of Old Norse was even more intense. Johann Gottfried Herder's philosophy of *Volksgeist* (the spirit of the people) valorised Norse literature as the authentic expression of the Germanic soul. The Brothers Grimm collected German folk tales in explicit parallel to the Norse corpus. Richard Wagner's *Der Ring des Nibelungen* (premiered 1876 at Bayreuth) adapted the Vǫlsung cycle into the most ambitious operatic cycle in the Western tradition — transforming the Norse gods into figures of German Romantic longing, the twilight of the gods into a parable of industrial modernity, and the valkyrie into a symbol of redemptive love.

This Romantic reception was productive — it generated lasting works of art and scholarship — but it was also distorting. The Vikings of the Romantic imagination were noble savages, free and fierce, embodiments of a lost Germanic purity. This idealisation laid the groundwork for the more sinister appropriations of the 20th century.

**The 20th Century: Tolkien, Wagner, and the Problem of Appropriation**

J.R.R. Tolkien (1892–1973) is the most important modern mediator of Old Norse literature to English-speaking audiences. Tolkien was a professional philologist — he held the Rawlinson and Bosworth Professorship of Anglo-Saxon at Oxford — and his creative work is saturated with Old Norse sources. The names of the dwarves in *The Hobbit* are taken directly from the *Vǫluspá* dwarf-catalogue (stanzas 9–16). The figure of Gandalf is modelled on Óðinn the Wanderer. The ring that must be destroyed is a descendant of the cursed Andvaranaut. The Rohirrim are Anglo-Saxons who kept their horses. Tolkien's creative relationship to his sources was one of deep, scholarly transformation — he knew the originals intimately and reworked them into an entirely new mythological framework.

Tolkien was also, famously, hostile to Wagner — "Both rings were round, and there the resemblance ceases" — and resented the comparison. But the comparison is unavoidable: both men took the Vǫlsung cycle and transformed it for modern audiences; Tolkien in high fantasy prose, Wagner in operatic music-drama.

The darker side of 20th-century Norse reception is the appropriation of Norse symbolism by National Socialism. The Nazi regime systematically exploited Norse and Germanic imagery: the SS runes (the double Sig-rune), the Odal-rune adopted by the SS Race and Settlement Office, the Totenkopf (death's head) ring, the use of the swastika (which had a long pre-Nazi history as a Germanic folk symbol), and the promotion of a fabricated "Aryan" religion by Heinrich Himmler. The consequences of this appropriation are still with us in the 2040s: white supremacist groups continue to use Viking and runic imagery to signal "white heritage," and the work of distinguishing authentic Norse tradition from racist fabrication remains an urgent task for academic Viking Studies.

**The 21st Century: Pop Culture Norse**

The 21st century has seen an explosion of popular engagement with Norse material:
- Marvel Comics and the Marvel Cinematic Universe have made Þórr and Loki household names — albeit in radically transformed versions that bear little resemblance to the Eddic originals.
- The History Channel's *Vikings* (2013–2020) and Netflix's *Vikings: Valhalla* (2022–2024) have shaped popular perception of the Viking Age more powerfully than any scholarly publication. Their relationship to historical accuracy is, at best, loose.
- Video games — *God of War* (2018, 2022), *Hellblade: Senua's Sacrifice* (2017), *Assassin's Creed: Valhalla* (2020), *Valheim* (2021) — have introduced millions of players to Norse mythology and Viking history through interactive media.
- Neil Gaiman's *Norse Mythology* (2017), a retelling of the Eddic myths in accessible prose, has become the most widely read introduction to Norse mythology in English.

The 2040 UoY position on popular culture engagement: **Adaptation is inevitable and not inherently harmful.** The Norse myths and sagas have been adapted and transformed since the moment they were composed — Snorri's Prose Edda is an adaptation of the Eddic poems, which are themselves adaptations of oral traditions. The question is not whether adaptation occurs but what it does: does it introduce new audiences to the source material? Does it acknowledge its own interpretive choices? Does it distinguish between historical evidence and creative liberty?

**Translation as Interpretation**

Old Norse poetry is notoriously difficult to translate. The formal properties that define the poetry — alliteration, metrical structure, kennings, word order — cannot be rendered directly in English. Every translation is a series of trade-offs: literal accuracy vs. poetic effect, semantic precision vs. metrical elegance, fidelity to the original word order vs. idiomatic English. The major translations available to English-language students represent different philosophies of translation:

- **Lee M. Hollander** (1962): Prioritises metre and alliteration, producing English verse that attempts to replicate the formal features of the Old Norse at the cost of semantic precision.
- **Carolyne Larrington** (1996, revised 2014): Prioritises semantic clarity and readability, producing accessible prose translations that sacrifice poetic form for accuracy.
- **Jackson Crawford** (2015, 2017): Prioritises contemporary English idiom, producing plain-spoken translations that aim for the directness and accessibility of the originals without attempting formal replication.

The 2040 UoY encourages students to read multiple translations alongside the original Old Norse — the differences between translations are themselves instructive, revealing the interpretive choices that every translator must make.
