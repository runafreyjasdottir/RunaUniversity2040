## Lecture 1: The Old Norse Language — Origins, Dialects, and Structure

**What Is Old Norse?**

Old Norse is the language spoken by the inhabitants of Scandinavia and their overseas colonies from approximately 800-1300 CE. It belongs to the North Germanic branch of the Germanic language family, along with Old English, Old Frisian, Old Saxon, Old High German, and Gothic.

Old Norse is not a single language but a **dialect continuum** with three major branches:

1. **Old West Norse** — Spoken in Norway, Iceland, the Faroe Islands, and the Norse colonies. This is the language of the Icelandic sagas and most written Old Norse literature. Modern descendants: Icelandic, Faroese, Nynorsk.

2. **Old East Norse** — Spoken in Denmark, Sweden, and the Baltic colonies. This is the language of the Danish and Swedish runestones. Modern descendants: Danish, Swedish, Bokmål (through Danish influence).

3. **Old Gutnish** — Spoken on the island of Gotland. A conservative dialect with archaic features. Modern descendant: Gutnish.

**Phonology — Key Sound Changes**

The most important phonological changes distinguishing Old Norse from Proto-Germanic:

- **Umlaut** (mutation) — Vowels changed by following vowels: i-umlaut (*fōts* → *fœtr*, feet), u-umlaut (*sǫng* → *sǫngr*, song), a-umlaut (*tala*, to count)
- **Breaking** — Short vowels "broken" before certain consonant clusters: *e* → *ja/jǫ*
- **Syncope** — Loss of unstressed vowels: *haftaR* → *hafðr*
- **Assimilation** — Consonant clusters simplified: *skipid* → *skipit*

**Morphology — Grammar Overview**

Old Norse has four cases (nominative, accusative, genitive, dative), three genders (masculine, feminine, neuter), two numbers (singular, plural). Nouns decline in several patterns (strong/weak, a-stems/i-stems/ō-stems). Verbs are strong (ablaut/vowel gradation) or weak (dental suffix *-ði/-di/-ti*).

The vocabulary reflects Viking Age culture: ships and seafaring (*langskip, knǫrr, segll, rǫðull*), warfare (*sverð, øx, spjót, skjǫldr, brynja*), law and society (*lǫg, þing, goði, þræll, bóndi*), religion (*ǫss, vǫlva, blót, hof*).

## Lecture 2: The Runic Alphabets — Writing Before the Latin Script

**From Elder to Younger Futhark**

The runic alphabets are the earliest Germanic writing systems. The Viking Age uses the **Younger Futhark** (16 runes, c. 700-1100 CE), reduced from the 24-character Elder Futhark.

The 16 runes: ᚠ (fé/wealth), ᚢ (úr/rain), ᚦ (þurs/giant), ᚬ (óss/god), ᚴ (kaun/ulcer), ᚼ (hagall/hail), ᚾ (nauðr/need), ᛁ (iss/ice), ᛅ (ár/harvest), ᛋ (sól/sun), ᛏ (týr/Týr), ᛒ (bjarkan/birch), ᛗ (maðr/man), ᛚ (lǫgr/water), ᛦ (yr/yew).

**The Ambiguity Problem:** Reducing 24 characters to 16 created serious ambiguity — ᚢ represents /u/, /o/, /y/, /ø/, /w/; ᚴ represents /k/ and /g/. Context resolved most ambiguities, and later medieval runic added dots to distinguish sounds.

**Runestones as Literature:** Over 3,000 survive, primarily in Sweden. They are commemorative — preserving memory of the dead, claiming inheritance, commemorating events. Typical formula: "X raised this stone in memory of Y, his/her [relationship]." The Rök Stone (760 characters) is the longest runic inscription known.

## Lecture 3: The Poetic Edda — Mythological and Heroic Poetry

**The Codex Regius** (GKS 2365 4to, c. 1270 CE) — The primary manuscript preserving 29 poems (plus fragments) covering mythological and heroic subjects. Discovered 1643 by Bishop Brynjólfur Sveinsson.

**The Mythological Poems:** *Vǫluspá* (creation and destruction of the world), *Hávamál* (Óðinn's wisdom), *Vafþrúðnismál* (wisdom contest), *Grímnismál* (cosmological revelation), *Þrymskviða* (Þórr in drag), *Alvíssmál* (Þórr outwits a dwarf).

**The Heroic Poems:** The Vǫlsung cycle — *Helgakviða* poems, *Reginsmál*, *Fáfnismál*, *Sigrdrífumál*, *Sigurðarkviða*, *Guðrúnarkviða* I-III, *Atlakviða*, *Atlamál*, *Hamðismál*.

**Metrical Forms:**
- **Fornyrðislag** (old story meter) — 4-6 syllable lines with alliteration on 2-3 stressed syllables. Narrative meter.
- **Ljóðaháttr** (song meter) — Alternating full and half lines with heavy alliteration. Wisdom and dialogue meter.

Eddic poetry is anonymous and communal — unlike skaldic poetry, which names its poet.

## Lecture 4: The Prose Edda — Snorri Sturluson and Mythological Synthesis

**Snorri Sturluson (1179-1241)** — Icelandic chieftain, poet, historian. Wrote the Prose Edda c. 1220-1230 as a handbook for skalds who needed to understand mythological kennings.

**Structure:** Prologus (euhemeristic Christian introduction), Gylfaginning (narrative of Norse mythology from creation to Ragnarǫk, framed as King Gylfi questioning the gods), Skáldskaparmál (catalog of kennings with mythological explanations), Háttatal (demonstration of 100+ skaldic meters).

**The Problem of Snorri:** He was a Christian writing 200+ years after conversion. His euhemeristic introduction (gods as Trojan heroes), narrative imposition (creating coherence from fragmentary poems), selection bias (including myths useful for kennings, excluding others), and temporal distance all require critical reading. The 2040 UoY position: essential but suspect — compare with Poetic Edda whenever possible.

## Lecture 5: The Icelandic Sagas — History, Fiction, and Memory

The Icelandic sagas (*Íslendingasögur*) are prose narratives written in 13th-14th century Iceland, set in the 9th-11th centuries. They exist between history and fiction — literary works that preserve genuine historical memory through generations of oral tradition.

**Major Sagas (Recommended Reading Order):**
1. *Egil's Saga* — The poet-warrior Egil Skallagrimsson. Most psychologically complex.
2. *Njal's Saga* — The longest and most famous. Friendship, vengeance, the burning of Njal.
3. *Laxdaela Saga* — Passion, jealousy, revenge. Strong female protagonist.
4. *Grettir's Saga* — The outlaw hero. Strength, isolation, limits of heroism.
5. *The Vinland Sagas* — The Norse discovery of America.

**2040 UoY Approach:** Read as literature first (plot, character, theme), then identify historical elements, then assess reliability (compare with archaeology, foreign sources), then assess literary shaping (how does the saga shape historical material for narrative effect?).

## Lecture 6: Skaldic Poetry — The Art of the Court Poet

**Skaldic vs. Eddic:** Skaldic poetry is authored, individual, occasional (praising/memorializing), written in dróttkvætt. Eddic is anonymous, communal, narrative, written in fornyrðislag/ljóðaháttr.

**Dróttkvætt** (court meter): 8 short lines per stanza, alliteration, internal rhyme (skothending in odd lines, aðalhending in even lines), exactly 6 syllables per line, plus kennings. One of the most complex metrical forms in world literature.

**Kennings** — Poetic circumlocutions: *hvalveggr* (whale-path) = sea, *Hropts hugr* (Óðinn's mind) = poetry, *veðr valkastar* (wind of the valkyrie) = battle. Simple kennings (2 elements) or compound (multiple nested). A form of poetic cryptography.

**Major Skalds:** Bragi Boddason (9th c.), Þjóðólfr of Hvinir (9th c.), Egil Skallagrimsson (c. 910-990, *Hǫfuðlausn* and *Sonatorrek*), Hallfreðr vandraeðaskáld (c. 965-1007), Snorri Sturluson (1179-1241, *Háttatal*).

## Lecture 7: The Sagas of the Norse Kings — Heimskringla

Snorri's *Heimskringla* (c. 1230) covers Norwegian history from the legendary period to 1177 CE — from Óðinn (euhemerized) through Haraldr Fairhair, Olav Tryggvason, St. Olav, Haraldr Hardrada, and the civil war era.

**As Historical Sources:** Contains contemporary skaldic verse (most reliable), oral tradition (transmitted 200+ years), Snorri's biases (Christian, narrative-shaping), and narrative selection (drama over documentation). The 2040 approach treats Snorri as a historian working with available sources — his biases must be accounted for, but his skaldic sources are often reliable.

## Lecture 8: Saga Literature — Structure, Theme, and Meaning

**Saga Style:** Extraordinary economy — chronological narrative, dialogue-driven, simple direct language, no editorializing. The emotion is carried by what is NOT said. Example from Njal's Saga: Gunnar turns to look at the hills and says "Fair is the lithe" — the entire weight of his decision to stay (and die) is in that single adjective.

**Major Themes:**
1. **Vengeance** — Blood vengeance as social obligation
2. **Law and legal process** — Lawsuits at the Alþingi as narrative device
3. **Fate and foreknowledge** — Facing known destiny with courage
4. **Gender and power** — Women exercise power through persuasion, legal action, sexual pressure
5. **Honor and shame** — Reputation (drengskapr) is everything
6. **Outlawry** — Útlagi (outlaw) can be killed by anyone

## Lecture 9: The Continental Norse — Saxo and Latin Learning

**Saxo Grammaticus** (c. 1150-1220) wrote *Gesta Danorum* (Deeds of the Danes) c. 1208-1218, the most important source for Danish history. His versions of Norse myths differ from the Eddas: Baldr is a warrior, Þórr is a trickster. Must be read as Christian polemic against paganism, not neutral record.

**Danish Runestones:** Short, formulaic, commemorative, often explicitly Christian. The three Jelling stones (Gorm, Harald Bluetooth, Thyra) document the transition from paganism to Christianity in real time.

## Lecture 10: Manuscript Culture — How the Sagas Were Preserved

Key manuscripts: Codex Regius (Poetic Edda, c. 1270), Móðruvallabók (11 sagas, c. 1350), Flateyjarbók (kings' sagas, c. 1387-1394). The Árni Magnússon Collection (3,000+ manuscripts, assembled 1666-1730, returned to Iceland 1971-1997). In 2040, the Árni Magnússon Institute's **Manuscript Cloud** provides full digital access with multispectral imaging, side-by-side comparison, and AI-assisted paleographic analysis.

## Lecture 11: Modern Old Norse — Revival, Scholarship, and the Living Language

**Icelandic** is the most conservative North Germanic language — speakers can read medieval manuscripts relatively easily due to conservative phonology, morphology, vocabulary (native neologisms: *tölva* = computer, *sími* = telephone), and unbroken literary tradition.

**Scholarship History:** Rasmus Rask (comparative Germanic linguistics, 1818), Jacob Grimm (Grimm's Law, German folk tales), Sophus Bugge (mythological origins debate), Andreas Heusler (Eddic/skaldic distinction, saga style analysis). In 2040, AI-assisted analysis has revolutionized text comparison, kenning interpretation, and metrical analysis.

---

## Lecture 12: Old Norse in the 21st Century — Reception, Adaptation, and the Future

**Norse Literature in Modern Culture:** Tolkien (*The Lord of the Rings* is deeply influenced by the Vǫlsung cycle), Wagner (*Der Ring des Nibelungen*), Marvel Comics/MCU (Þórr, Loki), video games (God of War, Hellblade), and fantasy literature more broadly. The 2040 position: adaptation is inevitable and not inherently harmful, but students must distinguish between authentic sources and modern reinterpretations.

**Translation Issues:** Old Norse poetry is notoriously difficult to translate. Kennings, alliteration, and metrical constraints cannot be rendered directly in English. Every translation is an interpretation. Key translations: Lee Hollander (metrical, literal), Carolyne Larrington (accessible, literary), Jackson Crawford (contemporary, plain-spoken).

**The Future of Old Norse Studies in 2040:** AI-assisted text analysis (pattern detection in kenning usage, metrical analysis, authorship attribution), multispectral imaging (reading previously illegible manuscripts), community-based scholarship (Ásatrú practitioners contributing embodied knowledge), and decolonial approaches (challenging the field's historic Eurocentrism).
