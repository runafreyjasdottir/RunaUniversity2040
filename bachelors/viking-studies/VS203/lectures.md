## Lecture 1: What Is Viking Art? — Definition, Classification, and the Problem of Meaning

**The Problem of "Art" in the Viking Age**

The term "art" applied to Viking Age Scandinavia is an anachronism — a modern category imposed on a culture that did not distinguish between the aesthetic and the functional, the decorative and the meaningful. The objects that we classify as "Viking art" — the carved animal posts of Oseberg, the silver-inlaid axe of Mammen, the rune-stones of Jelling — were not created for disinterested aesthetic contemplation. They adorned objects that were used: worn on the body (brooches, pendants, belt-fittings), carried in battle (sword-hilts, axe-blades, shield-bosses), sailed across the sea (ship prows, weather-vanes), raised to commemorate the dead (runestones), and deposited in graves to accompany the deceased into the afterlife.

Viking art was, in the formulation of the 2040 University of Yggdrasil's Art History Research Group (2037–2040), **functional, narrative, and cosmological**. It was **functional** — it decorated objects that served practical purposes, and its decorative programmes were inseparable from those purposes. It was **narrative** — the interlaced beasts, gripping creatures, and fighting animals were not abstract ornament but encoded stories, myths, and cosmic dramas. It was **cosmological** — the visual language of Viking art expressed fundamental ideas about the structure of the universe, the relationship between order and chaos, and the place of human beings within the cosmic order.

**The Classification of Viking Art: The Six-Style Sequence**

The modern classification of Viking Age art into six sequential styles was established by the Swedish archaeologist **Bernhard Salin** (1904, *Die altgermanische Thierornamentik*), who divided Germanic animal ornament into three styles (I, II, III), and refined by **David Wilson** and **Ole Klindt-Jensen** (1966, *Viking Art*), who defined the six-style sequence that remains the standard framework:

1. **Oseberg Style** (c. 780–870 CE) — The gripping beast
2. **Borre Style** (c. 840–970 CE) — Ring-chain interlace and mask motifs
3. **Jellinge Style** (c. 900–970 CE) — Ribbon animals in S-shapes
4. **Mammen Style** (c. 950–1025 CE) — Naturalistic animals and foliate scrolls
5. **Ringerike Style** (c. 980–1090 CE) — Elaborate plant-scroll and animal interlace
6. **Urnes Style** (c. 1040–1130 CE) — Elegant, thin fighting animals

These categories are indispensable but must be used critically. The styles overlap in time; single artefacts may combine elements of two or more styles; and the boundaries between styles are the constructions of modern archaeologists, not the conscious categories of Viking Age artists. The 2040 UoY Digital Art History Project, which has analysed over 12,000 Viking Age artefact images using AI-assisted pattern recognition (2038), has confirmed the basic validity of the six-style sequence while revealing that the transitions between styles were more gradual and the regional variation more pronounced than the traditional framework acknowledges.

**The 2040 Approach: A Tripartite Methodology**

The 2040 UoY advocates a tripartite methodology for the study of Viking art:

1. **Formal analysis** — What does the object look like? Identify the art style, the motifs, the composition, the technique, and the materials. This is the foundation: without accurate formal description, interpretation is empty.

2. **Contextual analysis** — Where was the object found? In what archaeological context (grave, hoard, settlement, stray find)? What other objects were associated with it? Who owned it, and what does the context tell us about its social function?

3. **Interpretive analysis** — What did the object mean? What narratives, myths, or cosmological ideas does its decoration encode? How did it communicate social identity (gender, status, ethnicity, religious affiliation)? What was its role in the ritual, political, and economic life of Viking Age society?

This methodology requires the integration of art history with archaeology, Old Norse literature, and comparative mythology. Viking art cannot be understood in isolation from the texts that describe the myths it encodes, the archaeology that reveals its social contexts, or the comparative evidence that illuminates its meanings.

## Lecture 2: The Pre-Viking Artistic Inheritance — Migration Period and Vendel Art

**The Germanic Animal Style: Origins and Development**

Viking Age art did not emerge from a vacuum. It was the culmination of a Germanic artistic tradition that stretched back to the Roman Iron Age and had been developing for over half a millennium before the first Viking ships appeared on the North Sea. Understanding the pre-Viking artistic inheritance is essential for grasping what is distinctive about Viking art — and what is continuous with older traditions.

The **Germanic animal style** — the decorative use of stylised, interlaced, and often fragmented animal figures — first appears in the archaeological record in the 5th century CE, on objects from the Migration Period (c. 375–550 CE). The origins of the style are debated: some scholars trace it to Roman provincial metalwork (especially the chip-carved belt-fittings and brooches of the late Roman army), adapted by Germanic craftsmen to their own aesthetic preferences; others emphasise the continuity with pre-Roman Celtic art, transmitted through the La Tène tradition; still others argue for an independent Germanic development, stimulated but not determined by Roman contact. The 2040 UoY position: **all three factors contributed — Roman technique, Celtic aesthetic vocabulary, and Germanic symbolic concerns — and their interaction produced the distinctive artistic tradition that culminated in Viking art**.

**Salin's Styles I, II, and III**

Bernhard Salin's classification of Germanic animal ornament (1904) identified three styles that precede and lead into the Viking Age styles:

- **Salin Style I** (c. 450–550 CE): The earliest Germanic animal style, characterised by **chip-carved** ornament on gilded silver and bronze. The animals are highly stylised, often fragmented (heads, legs, and bodies are separated by geometric panels), and arranged in complex, non-representational compositions. The Nydam Style (named after the bog finds from Nydam Mose) is the Scandinavian variant. The animals are recognisable — they have heads, eyes, legs — but they are not naturalistic; they are decorative elements, arranged for visual effect rather than narrative content.

- **Salin Style II** (c. 550–700 CE): A more integrated and dynamic animal style. The animals are larger, more coherent, and more vigorously posed — they interlace, bite each other, and seem to be in active motion. The style is associated with the **Vendel Period** in Scandinavia (named after the boat-burial cemetery at Vendel, Uppland, Sweden), and its finest expressions are the gilt-bronze helmet plates from Vendel and Valsgärde, which depict warriors, gods, and mythical scenes in elaborate narrative compositions. Style II shows the influence of **Insular art** (the manuscript illumination and metalwork of Ireland and Anglo-Saxon England), transmitted through the intensive contacts of the Migration Period.

- **Salin Style III** (c. 700–800 CE): The transition from Vendel to Viking art. Style III animals are highly stylised, ribbon-like, and increasingly interlaced. The style represents a simplification and abstraction of the Style II animal — the body becomes a continuous ribbon, the head is reduced to a few essential features, and the composition is dominated by interlace patterns. Style III is the immediate precursor of the Oseberg gripping beast.

**Key Pre-Viking Objects**

The pre-Viking artistic tradition produced some of the most extraordinary objects of the Germanic Iron Age:

- **The Vendel and Valsgärde Helmets** (Uppland, Sweden, 6th–7th centuries): Gilt-bronze helmet plates depicting warriors on horseback, foot soldiers in combat, and mythological scenes (the one-eyed figure with a spear — possibly Óðinn — appears on several plates). The helmets combine the Germanic animal style with narrative content — they tell stories, not merely decorate.
- **The Torslunda Plates** (Öland, Sweden, 6th–7th centuries): Bronze dies used for stamping helmet plates, depicting a dancing warrior with a horned helmet (possibly a berserkr or a cult dancer), a warrior fighting a bear, and a bound figure. The Torslunda plates are among the most important iconographic documents of pre-Viking Germanic religion.
- **The Sutton Hoo Helmet** (Suffolk, England, c. 620 CE): The most famous single object of the Germanic Iron Age, combining Anglo-Saxon, Scandinavian, and possibly Frankish artistic influences. The helmet's face-mask, with its dragon-headed eyebrows and garnet-inlaid eye-sockets, is a masterpiece of early medieval metalwork.

## Lecture 3: The Six Viking Art Styles — Formal Analysis

**The Oseberg Style (c. 780–870 CE)**

Named after the Oseberg ship burial (Vestfold, Norway, c. 834 CE), this style is characterised by the **gripping beast** — a dynamic, interlaced animal figure that grips its own body, adjacent beasts, or the surrounding frame with tiny, lively paws. The Oseberg animal is sinuous, energetic, and almost frenzied — its body twists and strains against the boundaries of its frame, and its tiny gripping paws create a continuous interlace that pulls the entire composition together.

The finest examples are the carved wooden objects from the Oseberg ship itself: the **animal-head post** (a snarling beast's head with elaborate interlace ornament on the neck), the **sledge poles** (with running gripping beasts), and the **ceremonial cart**. The Oseberg style is also found on metalwork, especially on oval brooches from Norway and Sweden. The style is exuberant, confident, and technically virtuosic — the carvers of Oseberg were masters of their medium.

The 2040 UoY interpretation of the gripping beast motif, developed by Ingrid Yngvildsdóttir (2037): **the gripping beast visually enacts the Norse cosmology of tension between order (*órðr*) and chaos (*ginnungagap*)**. The beast's body strains against the frame — but it is the beast's own grip that holds the frame together. Order is not imposed from without but generated from within, by the interlocking bodies of the creatures that constitute the cosmos. The gripping beast is a visualisation of a universe held together by the tension of its own constituent forces.

**The Borre Style (c. 840–970 CE)**

Named after the Borre ship-burial mounds (Vestfold, Norway), the Borre style is characterised by **geometric ring-chain interlace** — a regular, repeating pattern of interlocking rings and chains that creates a dense, textured surface — and the distinctive **Borre mask**: a frontal animal face with round, bulging eyes, prominent ears, and a lopped-off nose, often surrounded by ribbon-like tendrils.

The Borre style is more disciplined than Oseberg. The wild energy of the gripping beast has been tamed into regular, almost mechanical patterns. The ring-chain can be repeated indefinitely, covering large surfaces with a uniform texture. The Borre mask — staring, symmetrical, frontal — is confrontational: it looks at the viewer, and the viewer looks back. Its function may have been apotropaic (warding off evil) or protective, though this interpretation is speculative.

Borre-style objects are found across the entire Viking world, from Scandinavia to the Danelaw, from Ireland to Russia. The style's wide distribution reflects the integration of the Viking world in the 9th–10th centuries — the Borre style was the first pan-Viking artistic idiom, recognisable from York to Kyiv.

**The Jellinge Style (c. 900–970 CE)**

Named after the Jelling stones (Jutland, Denmark), the Jellinge style features **simplified ribbon-animals in S-shapes** — streamlined, elegant, almost calligraphic creatures rendered in a single continuous line. The Jelling stone's "great beast" (on Harald Bluetooth's stone, c. 965 CE) is the type-example: a sinuous animal with a ribbon-like body, a long curled tail, and a simplified head, executed with extraordinary economy of line.

The Jellinge style shows the influence of **Insular manuscript illumination** (the interlace initials of Anglo-Saxon and Irish gospel-books) and **Carolingian art** (the animal ornament of Frankish metalwork). This influence reflects the cultural contacts of the 10th century — the conversion, the importation of Christian manuscripts, and the increasing integration of Scandinavia into the European cultural sphere.

**The Mammen Style (c. 950–1025 CE)**

Named after the **Mammen axe** (Jutland, Denmark, c. 970 CE) — an iron axe with silver-inlaid decoration depicting a foliate creature: part animal, part plant. The Mammen style is more naturalistic than its predecessors. The animals have recognisable bodies with legs, heads, tails, and manes; they are often combined with **tendril scrolls** — plant-like spiralling forms that evoke growing vegetation.

The Mammen axe's beast is simultaneously animal and botanical — a hybrid form that blurs the boundary between fauna and flora. This hybridity is characteristic of the Mammen style and may reflect the infusion of Christian iconographic conventions (the vine-scroll, a common motif in Christian art) into the Norse decorative vocabulary. The **Cammin Casket** (c. 1000 CE, Schleswig, lost in World War II, known from photographs) is the most elaborate Mammen-style object — a wooden casket decorated with extensive Mammen-style ornament and possibly used as a reliquary, demonstrating the adaptation of Norse art to Christian liturgical functions.

**The Ringerike Style (c. 980–1090 CE)**

Named after the Ringerike district of Norway (where many runestones display the style), the Ringerike style develops the Mammen plant-scroll into **elaborate foliage interlaced with animals**, with clear influence from Anglo-Saxon and Ottonian manuscript art. The animals are larger, more assertive, and more dynamically posed — often shown in active movement, with extended limbs and open jaws. The plant scroll becomes the dominant element, sometimes subordinating the animal to the point where the animal is barely recognisable amid the foliage.

The **Vang stone** (Valdres, Norway) and the **London rune-stone** (St Paul's churchyard, London, c. 1040 CE — a monument raised by Norse residents of 11th-century London) are characteristic Ringerike monuments. The London stone combines the Ringerike animal with a runic inscription in Old Norse — the visual and the textual integrated on a single monument.

**The Urnes Style (c. 1040–1130 CE)**

Named after the **Urnes stave church** (Sogn, Norway), whose north portal preserves the finest example, the Urnes style features **thin, elegant, fighting animals** — elongated, serpentine creatures locked in combat, rendered with extraordinary delicacy and refinement. The lines are fluid, almost calligraphic; the animals' bodies are stretched to the breaking point; and the composition is dominated by sweeping figure-eight loops that create a continuous, flowing movement.

The Urnes style is the last purely Norse art style. It is contemporary with the earliest Romanesque art in Scandinavia (the stone churches of the 12th century), and the transition from Urnes to Romanesque marks the end of the Norse artistic tradition as an autonomous development. The Urnes style's fighting animals — locked in eternal combat — have been interpreted as a visual encoding of the cosmic struggle that pervades Norse mythology: the conflict between gods and giants, order and chaos, that culminates in Ragnarǫk.

## Lecture 4: Iconography and Meaning — Decoding the Visual Language of the Vikings

**The Problem of Interpretation: Can We Read Viking Art?**

The interpretation of Viking Age iconography is one of the most challenging and controversial areas of Viking Studies. The images that cover Viking Age artefacts — the gripping beasts, the fighting animals, the masks, the interlaced serpents, the armed figures — were produced in a culture that left no explicit commentary on its own visual art. Unlike Christian art, which can be read against biblical texts and patristic commentaries, Norse pagan art has no surviving interpretive key. We must read the images with the aid of texts (the Eddic poems, Snorri, the sagas) that were composed later, by Christians, and for different purposes — a hermeneutic circle that is productive but perilous.

The 2040 UoY position: **the interpretation of Viking iconography is possible but must be probabilistic, not dogmatic**. We can identify patterns — recurring motifs, compositional structures, contextual associations — that suggest meaning, but we can rarely be certain. Every interpretation should be presented with explicit acknowledgement of its uncertainty and with attention to alternative readings.

**The Major Iconographic Motifs**

1. **The Gripping Beast** (Oseberg Style): The most distinctive and enigmatic Viking Age motif. The beast grips itself, its neighbours, and its frame. Interpretations: (a) **cosmological** — the beast enacts the tension between order and chaos, the cosmos holding itself together; (b) **apotropaic** — the grip is protective, warding off evil; (c) **social** — the grip represents the bonds of loyalty (*tryggð*) that hold society together.

2. **The Great Beast** (Jellinge Style): The sinuous, ribbon-bodied animal that dominates the Jelling stone. Interpretations: (a) **the lion of Judah** — Christian iconography (the Evangelist symbol) absorbed into Norse visual language; (b) **the Miðgarðsormr** (Midgard Serpent) — the cosmic serpent that encircles the world, depicted at the moment of its confrontation with Þórr; (c) **a royal emblem** — the beast as the Harald Bluetooth's personal or dynastic symbol, in which case its Christian or pagan significance is subordinate to its political function.

3. **The Mask** (Borre Style): The staring, frontal face with round eyes and a lopped nose. Interpretations: (a) **Óðinn** — the one-eyed god whose face confronts the viewer with its asymmetrical, unsettling gaze; (b) **a protective spirit** — the mask as apotropaic device; (c) **a Berserkr** — the mask as depiction of the ecstatic warrior in his trance-state.

4. **The Armed Woman / Valkyrie** (Various styles): Small silver and bronze figurines depicting armed women with shields, swords, and sometimes drinking-horns. Interpretations: (a) **valkyries** — the choosers of the slain, depicted in their function of welcoming the dead to Valhǫll; (b) **vǫlur** — female ritual specialists, whose association with Óðinn and the dead gave them a martial dimension; (c) **goddesses** — Freyja in her martial aspect.

5. **The Rider / Horse** (Vendel–Viking transition): Depictions of armed riders on horseback, found on helmet plates, runestones, and tapestries. Interpretations: (a) **the god Óðinn** on his eight-legged horse Sleipnir; (b) **the dead warrior** on his journey to the afterlife; (c) **the chieftain** displaying his martial status.

6. **The Interlaced Serpent** (Ringerike and Urnes): The ubiquitous serpent that winds through the vegetal scrolls of the late Viking Age. Interpretations: (a) **Jǫrmungandr**, the Miðgarðsormr — the cosmic serpent that encircles the world; (b) **Níðhǫggr**, the serpent that gnaws the root of Yggdrasill; (c) **the Norns' thread** — the serpent as visualisation of fate (*ørlǫg*), the thread that the Norns weave and cut.

**The 2040 UoY Digital Iconography Project**

The 2040 UoY Digital Iconography Project (2038–2040) has employed AI-assisted image recognition to catalogue the iconographic content of over 12,000 Viking Age artefacts. The project's key findings include:
- **Gendered motif distribution:** Gripping beasts appear disproportionately on objects associated with elite women (oval brooches, the Oseberg assemblage); fighting animals appear disproportionately on objects associated with men (weapons, sword-hilts, belt-fittings). This gendered distribution may reflect the social contexts in which these objects were used and viewed.
- **Religious shift:** The Urnes-style fighting animal appears disproportionately on Christian monuments (church portals, baptismal fonts, Christian runestones), suggesting that the motif was reinterpreted within a Christian framework even as its formal vocabulary remained Norse.
- **Regional variation:** The Borre mask appears predominantly in Denmark and the Danelaw, while the gripping beast is concentrated in Norway and the Norse Atlantic colonies — evidence for regional variation in the symbolic vocabulary of Viking art.

## Lecture 5: Craft Techniques — Metalwork, Wood-Carving, and Textiles

**The Craftsman in Viking Society**

The Viking Age craftsman — smith, jeweller, wood-carver, textile-worker, shipwright — occupied an ambiguous social position. The mythological framework that structured Norse society did not assign a clear place to the craftsman. The *Rígsþula* (Lay of Ríg) describes the three social orders — Jarls, Karls, and Þrælar — but the craftsman fits none of them. He was not an aristocrat (though he might serve one), not a free farmer (though he might own land), and not a slave (though his skill was economically valuable). The smith — the *smiðr* — was a liminal figure, associated with the dwarfs (*dvergar*), the master craftsmen who lived beneath the earth and forged the treasures of the gods.

The greatest concentration of craft evidence comes from the trading towns — Hedeby, Birka, Kaupang, York, Dublin — where specialised craftsmen operated permanent workshops. The archaeology of these workshops reveals: hearths and forges for metalworking, moulds for casting jewellery, raw materials (ingots, scrap metal, imported glass, amber), finished and unfinished products, and the waste products of craft production (slag, crucible fragments, casting spills). The trading-town craftsman was a full-time specialist, producing for a market, not a part-time farmer supplementing his income with craft-work.

**Metalwork: The Dominant Craft**

Metalwork is the most visible and archaeologically abundant Viking Age craft. The techniques employed:

- **Casting:** The most common method for producing jewellery and decorative fittings. The lost-wax process was used for unique, high-quality objects; the piece-mould process for mass-produced items. The thousands of identical oval brooches found across the Viking world (and especially in female graves) were mass-produced using reusable moulds — one of the earliest examples of serial production in northern Europe.

- **Forging:** Used primarily for weapons (swords, axes, spears) and tools. Pattern-welding — the technique of twisting, folding, and forge-welding rods of different carbon-content steel to produce a distinctive "serpent" pattern on the finished blade — was the most sophisticated forging technique of the Viking Age. Pattern-welded sword blades (the Ulfberht and Ingelrii workshops of the Frankish Rhineland) were the highest-prestige weapons in the Viking world.

- **Filigree and Granulation:** Imported techniques, probably via the eastern trade routes, used for high-status jewellery. Filigree (delicate gold or silver wire soldered to a metal background in decorative patterns) and granulation (tiny gold or silver spheres soldered in patterns) reached Scandinavia in the 6th–7th centuries, probably from Byzantium via the Avars and the Slavs.

- **Niello and Inlay:** Niello (a black silver-sulphide compound inlaid into engraved metal surfaces) was used to create contrast on silver jewellery. The Mammen axe's silver inlay — hammered into engraved channels in the iron blade — is the finest surviving example of inlay technique.

**Wood-Carving: The Lost Masterpieces**

Wood was the most important artistic medium of the Viking Age — but it is also the least archaeologically preserved. The Oseberg ship burial is our primary source for Viking wood-carving, and without it, our understanding of the art form would be fragmentary. The Oseberg carvings — the animal-head posts, the sledge poles, the ceremonial cart, the bed-posts — demonstrate a mastery of relief carving, openwork, and three-dimensional sculptural form that must represent a tradition of enormous depth, now almost entirely lost.

The tools of the wood-carver — axe, adze, chisel, gouge, knife, scraper — were essentially the same as those of the carpenter. The distinction between "craft" and "art" is modern; the Viking wood-carver moved fluidly between functional carpentry (building ships, houses, furniture) and decorative carving, and his tools and techniques were the same for both.

**Textiles: The Women's Craft**

Textile production was the quintessential female craft of the Viking Age. The warp-weighted loom was the standard weaving technology; the spindle and distaff were the universal tools of spinning. The production of a single sail for a Viking ship required the wool of approximately 200 sheep, spun and woven by women over the course of several months — a major economic investment, and one controlled entirely by women.

The surviving textile fragments from the Oseberg burial (the richest textile assemblage of the Viking Age), the Birka graves, and the Coppergate excavation in York demonstrate sophisticated weaving techniques: tabby (plain) weave, twill (diagonal) weave, and the complex diamond-twill pattern characteristic of high-status Norse textiles. Wool, linen (flax), and — very rarely — silk (imported from Byzantium and Central Asia) were the principal fibres. Dyes included woad (blue), madder (red), weld (yellow), and a range of lichen and bark dyes producing browns, greens, and greys.

## Lecture 6: Jewellery and Personal Adornment — The Body as Canvas

**The Jewellery Typology**

Viking Age jewellery was the most visible and socially significant category of personal adornment. Jewellery communicated gender, status, ethnicity, and religious affiliation; it was worn at all social levels, from the humblest bronze finger-ring to the most elaborate gold neck-ring; and it was deposited in graves as grave goods, making it the most archaeologically abundant category of Viking material culture.

The **brooch** was the essential item of Viking female dress. Two types dominated:

- **Oval Brooches** (*skálarspǫng*): Paired brooches worn on the upper chest to fasten the straps of the apron-dress (*hangerock*), the standard female garment of the Viking Age. Oval brooches are the most common artefact in Viking female graves, and their typological development — from the simple, unadorned brooches of the 8th century to the elaborate, gilded, Borre-style-decorated brooches of the 10th century — provides a chronological framework for the entire Viking Age.

- **Trefoil, Equal-Armed, and Round Brooches**: Variations on the basic brooch form, reflecting regional styles and changing fashions. The trefoil brooch is characteristic of Denmark and the Danelaw; the equal-armed brooch is more common in Norway; the round brooch appears across the Viking world.

Men's dress required fewer brooches — typically a single **penannular brooch** (ring-brooch with a pin) at the shoulder to fasten the cloak. Men's jewellery was dominated by **arm-rings** and **neck-rings** — thick, twisted silver or gold torcs that served simultaneously as ornament, portable wealth (hack-silver), and symbols of the lord's generosity (*gjafar mildi*). The giving of a ring by a lord to his follower was the fundamental act of the Viking gift-economy.

**Amulets and Religious Jewellery**

The most archaeologically abundant category of Viking religious jewellery is the **Þórr's hammer** (*Þórshamarr*) — over 1,500 examples found, concentrated in the 10th century. The hammer amulets are small, typically 2–5 cm in length, made of silver, bronze, iron, or (rarely) gold, and worn on a chain or thong around the neck. Their distribution — concentrated in female graves and settlement contexts — suggests that they were worn by both women and men, and their peak production in the period of intensifying Christian contact (late 10th century) suggests that they functioned as a conscious pagan counter-symbol to the Christian cross.

Other religious amulets include: **miniature sickles** (associated with agricultural fertility and possibly with the goddess Sif), **miniature chairs** (possibly representing the vǫlva's *seiðhjallr*), **Thor's hammer rings** (finger-rings with hammer-shaped bezels), and the enigmatic **"valkyrie" figurines** — small silver pendants depicting armed women, possibly representing valkyries, goddesses, or vǫlur.

**Gullgubber: The Golden Couples**

The **gullgubber** (singular *gullgubbe* — "little gold old man") are tiny gold-foil figures, typically 1–2 cm in height, stamped with images of embracing couples. Over 3,000 gullgubber have been found across Scandinavia, concentrated at cult sites: especially **Sorte Muld** (Bornholm, Denmark), **Borg** (Lofoten, Norway), **Uppåkra** (Scania, Sweden), and **Helgö** (Mälaren, Sweden). The intimate embrace of the figures — one arm around the partner's shoulder, the other at the waist — has been interpreted as the **sacred marriage** (*hieros gamos*) of Freyr and Gerðr, enacted at the cult-site as part of a fertility ritual. The gullgubber were deposited in post-holes, wall-trenches, and thresholds — liminal points where the human and divine worlds intersected.

The 2040 UoY Gullgubbe Project (2039) has applied high-resolution 3D scanning to the entire corpus, revealing that multiple stamps were used at multiple sites — the same stamp produced gullgubber found at Sorte Muld and at Uppåkra, 200 km apart. This suggests that gullgubber were not merely local cult ex-votos but participated in a regional ritual network.

## Lecture 7: Weapons as Art — The Decorated Instruments of War

**The Aesthetics of Violence**

Viking Age weapons were simultaneously instruments of violence and vehicles of artistic expression. The sword, in particular, was the most prestigious object a Viking warrior could own — worth approximately 16 milk-cows, a significant fraction of a free farmer's wealth — and the finest swords were decorated with elaborate ornament on the hilt, the guard, and the pommel. The decoration served multiple functions: it **displayed the owner's wealth and status**, it **identified the weapon as an heirloom** (swords were named and passed down through generations), and it may have been believed to **imbue the weapon with supernatural power** — the runes, animals, and interlaced patterns on the blade were not merely decorative but talismanic.

**Swords: The Decorated Hilt**

The Viking sword hilt consisted of three main components — the **pommel** (at the top of the grip), the **upper guard**, and the **lower guard** (protecting the hand from the blade). All three were typically made of iron, overlaid or inlaid with silver, copper, gold, and niello. The decorative vocabulary drew on the contemporary art styles:

- The **Borre style** appears on sword-hilts as ring-chain interlace and mask motifs, cast in silver and gilded.
- The **Jellinge style** appears as sinuous ribbon-animals inlaid in silver on the guard.
- The **Mammen and Ringerike styles** appear as foliate scrolls and interlaced animals, inlaid in silver and copper.

The most famous and valuable swords of the Viking Age were **Frankish imports** — the blades of the Ulfberht and Ingelrii workshops in the Rhineland, which were pattern-welded from high-carbon crucible steel of a quality not produced in Scandinavia. These blades were traded to Scandinavia (despite Charlemagne's prohibitions on selling weapons to pagans) and fitted with locally made hilts decorated in the Norse style — a perfect example of the combination of imported technology and local artistic tradition.

**Axes: The Mammen Axe and the Dane-Axe**

The **Mammen axe** (c. 970 CE, Jutland, Denmark) is the finest surviving example of a decorated Viking Age axe. The iron blade is inlaid with silver wire forming a foliate creature — part animal, part plant — in the Mammen style. The axe was a ceremonial or prestige object, too delicate for combat; it was probably the possession of a Danish chieftain, displayed at feasts and deposited in a grave.

The two-handed **dane-axe** (*breiðøx*), wielded by the housecarls of Cnut's army, was occasionally decorated with silver inlay on the blade — though the decoration was typically simpler than on swords, reflecting the axe's more utilitarian character.

**Shields and Helmets**

The standard Viking shield — approximately 1 metre in diameter, constructed of thin wooden planks over a leather backing, with a central iron boss — was painted, not carved. The Gokstad ship burial contained 64 shields, painted yellow and black, mounted along the gunwales. The colour scheme may have identified the ship's crew or the chieftain to whom they belonged. Only one complete Viking Age helmet survives — the **Gjermundbu helmet** (c. 900 CE, Norway), a simple rounded iron cap with a nose guard and spectacle-like ocular protection. **Horned helmets are a 19th-century invention**, popularised by Wagner's costume designer for the 1876 Bayreuth production of *Der Ring des Nibelungen*.

## Lecture 8: Runestones — Text, Image, and Monumental Memory

**The Runestone as Gesamtkunstwerk**

The Viking Age runestone is the most characteristic monumental art form of the Norse world — a **Gesamtkunstwerk** (total work of art) that combines text, image, and stone in a single artefact. Over 3,000 Viking Age runestones survive, the vast majority in Sweden (especially Uppland). They are memorial monuments — erected to commemorate the dead, to claim inheritance, to assert social status, and (increasingly, after the conversion) to mark Christian identity.

The typical runestone consists of three integrated elements:
- **The runic inscription** — commemorative, formulaic, and usually brief, though some stones (the Rök Stone, the Jelling stones) contain extended texts.
- **The sculptural decoration** — animal ornament, interlace, and figural scenes, executed in shallow relief and often painted (traces of pigment survive on many stones).
- **The stone itself** — a natural boulder or quarried slab, selected for its size, shape, and colour, and erected at a prominent location: a road, a bridge, a thing-site, a churchyard.

**The Swedish Rune-Stone Tradition**

Sweden produced the overwhelming majority of Viking Age runestones — over 2,500 of the approximately 3,000 surviving stones. The concentration is particularly dense in **Uppland**, the region around Uppsala, where the 11th-century runestone tradition reached its apogee. The Uppland stones are characterised by the **Ringerike style** ornament: elaborate plant-scrolls and interlaced serpents that frame and enclose the runic inscription.

The carver **Öpir** (active c. 1070–1100 CE) is the most famous named rune-carver (though the signatures *"Œpir risti"* — "Öpir carved" — may represent a workshop rather than an individual). Öpir's stones are distinguished by elaborate Urnes-style serpents, fine calligraphic runes, and a distinctive compositional formula: the serpent's body frames the text, and the serpent's head and tail terminate in a decorative knot.

**The Jelling Stones: Denmark's Birth Certificate**

The two surviving Jelling stones (Jutland, Denmark) are Denmark's most important historical documents:

- The **smaller Jelling stone** (c. 940–950 CE), erected by King Gorm the Old for his wife Thyra, bears the inscription: *"Gorm king made this memorial for Þyrvé his wife, Denmark's adornment"* (*tanmarkaR bót*). This is the earliest textual mention of "Denmark" as a political entity.

- The **larger Jelling stone** (c. 965 CE), erected by Gorm's son Haraldr blátǫnn (Harald Bluetooth), bears the most famous runic inscription of the Viking Age: *"Haraldr king ordered this monument made in memory of Gorm his father and Þyrvé his mother — that Haraldr who won for himself all Denmark and Norway and made the Danes Christian."* The stone's three faces depict: (a) a **great beast** (the Jellinge style animal) and a serpent, (b) a **crucifixion scene** (the earliest depiction of Christ in Scandinavia), and (c) the **runic inscription**. The stone is simultaneously a memorial, a political proclamation, and a conversion monument — a single artefact that marks Denmark's emergence as a Christian kingdom.

**The Rök Stone: The Longest Runic Inscription**

The **Rök Stone** (Östergötland, Sweden, c. 800 CE) bears the longest known runic inscription — approximately 760 characters — and is the most complex and debated runic text in existence. The text is carved on all five faces of a granite monolith, using multiple rune-rows including cipher runes, and its content is partially encoded in riddles and allusions that remain only partially understood. The stone was erected by a man named Varinn in memory of his dead son Væmóðr. The text appears to reference the Ostrogothic king Þjóðríkr, a monster of the *niu aldum* (nine ages), and a series of riddles — possibly an encoded memorial, possibly a ritual invocation.

The Rök Stone has generated an enormous scholarly literature, and the 2040 UoY Runology Research Group's multispectral imaging project (2038) has revealed previously illegible characters that may substantially alter the accepted reading.

## Lecture 9: Imported Goods and Cultural Contact — The Material Evidence for the Viking World-System

**The Material Signature of Long-Distance Trade**

The Viking Age was a period of unprecedented long-distance trade, and the material signature of this trade is imprinted on the archaeological record. The presence of foreign goods in Scandinavian contexts — and of Scandinavian goods in foreign contexts — provides the most direct evidence for the extent and intensity of Viking Age cultural contact. The 2040 UoY Material Provenance Project (2038–2040) has compiled a database of over 50,000 provenanced artefacts, enabling the first comprehensive mapping of commodity flows in the Viking world.

**Imports to Scandinavia**

The goods that reached Scandinavia from abroad spanned the known world:

- **Islamic silver dirhams** — Over 80,000 found in hoards; the largest single source of precious metal in the Viking world economy. Mint identified by Arabic inscriptions: Samarkand, Bukhara, Baghdad, Damascus, Cairo.
- **Frankish sword blades** — Ulfberht and Ingelrii workshop blades, traded to Scandinavia despite Frankish prohibitions. Identified by the inlaid iron inscriptions on the blade.
- **Byzantine and Central Asian silk** — Silk fragments found in high-status graves, especially at Birka. Identified by weave analysis (samite, a weft-faced compound twill characteristic of Byzantine and Central Asian looms).
- **Islamic glass beads** — Thousands found in graves and settlements. Chemical analysis (soda-lime-silica glass with elevated magnesium and potassium) identifies Middle Eastern manufacture.
- **Rhenish pottery and wine** — Amphorae and pitchers from the Rhineland, found at Hedeby and Birka. The wine trade was substantial — Frankish wine was the prestige beverage of the Norse elite.
- **Anglo-Saxon and Irish metalwork** — Ecclesiastical metalwork looted from monasteries and reused as jewellery or hack-silver. The Gokstad burial contained a large Anglo-Saxon hanging-bowl, probably looted from a British monastery.

**Exports from Scandinavia**

Scandinavian goods and materials reached every corner of the known world:

- **Walrus ivory** — From Greenland and the Arctic, carved into crucifixes, reliquaries, and game pieces, traded throughout Europe. The Lewis Chessmen (12th century, found in Scotland, probably made in Trondheim, Norway) are the most famous ivory objects of the Norse world.
- **Furs** — Sable, ermine, fox, and squirrel pelts from the Russian forests, traded south to the Islamic Caliphate and Byzantium.
- **Falcons** — Gyrfalcons from Iceland and Scandinavia, exported to the Islamic courts, where they were valued as hunting birds.
- **Amber** — Baltic amber, collected on the shores of Jutland and the eastern Baltic, traded throughout Europe and the Mediterranean since the Neolithic.
- **Soapstone** (*kleber*) — Carved into cooking pots, lamps, and spindle whorls, exported throughout the Norse Atlantic and the British Isles.
- **Slaves** — The most significant Norse "export," captured in raids on the British Isles and the Baltic, traded through Dublin, Hedeby, and the Volga route to the Islamic world.

The 2040 UoY interpretation: **the Viking Age Scandinavian economy was a transit economy** — Scandinavia's geographic position between the North Atlantic resource zone (walrus, furs, falcons) and the European/Islamic consumption centres made it a natural intermediary. The Norse did not produce most of the goods they traded; they moved them.

## Lecture 10: Material Culture and Social Identity — Gender, Status, and Ethnicity

**Dress and Gender Identity**

The most archaeologically visible dimension of Viking Age social identity is gender, and gender was communicated primarily through **dress**. The standard female ensemble — the **apron-dress** (hangerock) fastened with a pair of oval brooches, with strings of beads suspended between the brooches — is so consistent across the Viking world that it functions as an ethnic marker: a woman buried in this dress was recognised as Norse, regardless of her genetic ancestry.

The standard male ensemble is more variable but equally communicative: tunic and trousers, fastened with a belt, sometimes a cloak secured by a penannular brooch, and weapons appropriate to status. The presence of weapons in a grave (sword, axe, spear, shield) communicated male martial identity even when the deceased was too old or too young to have actually fought. Weapons were symbols of manhood, not just instruments of violence.

**The Birka Warrior Grave Bj 581: Gender, Sex, and Interpretation**

The **Birka warrior grave Bj 581** (excavated 1878, re-analysed 2017) has become the most controversial artefact in Viking gender studies. The grave contained a full warrior's kit — sword, axe, spear, armour-piercing arrows, two shields, two horses — and was long assumed to be a male warrior burial. In 2017, Hedenstierna-Jonson et al. published ancient DNA analysis demonstrating that the skeleton was chromosomally female (XX). The finding triggered intense debate: some scholars argued that the DNA must have been contaminated; others accepted the finding but questioned whether a woman buried with weapons necessarily "was" a warrior; others accepted both the sex determination and the warrior interpretation and argued for the existence of female warriors in the Viking Age.

The 2040 UoY position: **the Bj 581 burial demonstrates that sex (biological) cannot be assumed from grave goods (cultural)**. The relationship between the body in the grave and the objects around it is complex, mediated by social convention, ritual practice, and the specific circumstances of death and burial. The Bj 581 burial challenges the automatic equation of "weapons" with "male" and "male" with "warrior" — and, in doing so, opens up the possibility that Viking Age gender categories were more fluid than the traditional model assumes.

**Status Display: The Grammar of Hierarchy**

Social status in the Viking Age was communicated through a **grammar of display** — a system of sumptuary conventions in which specific objects, materials, and combinations signalled specific social positions:

- **Gold** was restricted to the highest elite — kings, jarls, and their immediate families. Gold arm-rings, neck-rings, and brooches were the insignia of royal and aristocratic rank.
- **Silver** was the metal of the broad free population — the *bœndr* (free farmers) who constituted the majority of Viking Age society. Silver arm-rings, hammer amulets, and hack-silver were widely distributed.
- **Copper-alloy** (bronze, brass) was the metal of the lower-status population — the *leysingjar* (freedmen) and poorer farmers. Copper-alloy brooches and ornaments are abundant but simpler in decoration.
- **Imported goods** — silk, glass, fine weapons, wine — signalled access to long-distance trade networks and the wealth and political connections necessary to participate in them.

The grammar was not rigid — individuals could (and did) manipulate the system, signalling higher status than they possessed, or deliberately understating their status — but it provided a shared visual language that made social hierarchy legible to all members of the community.

## Lecture 11: Production and Exchange — From Craft Workshop to World Market

**The Organisation of Craft Production**

Viking Age craft production operated at multiple scales, from the household to the specialised urban workshop:

- **Household production:** The basic unit of textile production was the household — every farmstead had a warp-weighted loom, and the women of the household produced the textiles needed for clothing, bedding, and sails. Spinning and weaving were universal female skills.

- **Itinerant specialists:** Some crafts — especially metalworking — may have been practised by itinerant smiths who travelled between settlements, carrying their tools and producing goods to order. The literary sources (sagas, Eddic poems) describe smiths as outsiders — dangerous, powerful, and mobile.

- **Urban workshops:** The trading towns — Hedeby, Birka, Kaupang, York, Dublin — contained permanent workshops with fixed hearths and forges, producing for a market. The concentration of craft debris (slag, crucibles, mould fragments, casting waste) in specific areas of these towns indicates zoning — crafts were not randomly distributed but concentrated in "industrial" quarters.

**The Social Organisation of Trade**

Viking Age trade was conducted at multiple levels:

- **Individual traders:** The sagas describe farmers who travelled to trading towns to sell surplus produce and buy luxury goods. The trading voyage was a seasonal activity, integrated into the agricultural cycle.

- **Professional merchants:** The Arabic sources describe professional Rūs merchants who operated as companies, pooling capital and sharing risk. The Gotlandic picture-stones may depict merchant ships with cargo.

- **Royal and aristocratic control:** The largest trading towns — Hedeby, Birka, Kaupang — were founded by kings and maintained under royal protection. The king provided security (suppressing piracy, guaranteeing safe passage) and in return received tolls, taxes, and access to prestige goods.

- **The assembly as market:** The thing (assembly) functioned as a periodic market — farmers and traders gathered at the thing-site not only for legal business but for buying, selling, and exchanging goods. The association of assembly and market is ancient and widespread.

**The 2040 Model: Network Analysis of Viking Trade**

The 2040 UoY Trade Networks Project (2038–2040) has applied social network analysis to the Viking trade data, modelling the flow of goods between sites and identifying the most central nodes in the network. The key findings:

- **Hedeby** was the most central node in the Viking trade network, connected by multiple routes to the North Sea, the Baltic, and the Frankish Empire.
- **Birka** was the primary eastern node, connecting the Baltic to the Russian river routes.
- **Kaupang** and **York** were the primary western nodes, connecting Scandinavia to the British Isles and the Atlantic.
- The network was **resilient** — the loss of any single node (due to political disruption or economic decline) did not collapse the system, because alternative routes and destinations existed.

## Lecture 12: The Legacy of Viking Art — From the Middle Ages to the 2040s

**The Medieval Afterlife: Urnes to Romanesque**

The Urnes style — the last purely Norse art style — was contemporary with the earliest Romanesque art in Scandinavia (the stone cathedrals of the 12th century). The transition from Urnes to Romanesque was not a rupture but a transformation: the Norse animal style was absorbed into the Romanesque vocabulary, its characteristic forms — the interlaced serpents, the fighting animals, the foliate scrolls — adapted to Christian architectural programmes. The carved portals of the Norwegian stave churches (Urnes, Borgund, Heddal) are the finest surviving expressions of this transitional moment — structures that are simultaneously Norse wooden architecture (the stave-post construction) and Christian ecclesiastical architecture, decorated with Urnes-style fighting animals that have been reinterpreted as the struggle between good and evil.

**The 19th-Century Revival: Romantic Nationalism and the Viking Style**

The 19th-century "Viking Revival" in art and architecture drew on the newly discovered and published Viking Age material. The publication of the Oseberg ship finds (excavated 1904, published 1917–1928) and the Mammen axe, the systematisation of the six styles by Salin and Wilson, and the Romantic nationalist enthusiasm for the Viking past all fed into a revival of Norse decorative motifs. The "Dragon Style" (*dragestil*) in Norwegian architecture (wooden villas and hotels with carved dragon-heads and interlace ornament, c. 1880–1910) was the most visible expression, but Norse motifs appeared on everything from jewellery to furniture to book illustrations.

**The 20th and 21st Centuries: Appropriation and Popular Culture**

The 20th century saw the Viking art heritage appropriated by the Nazi regime (SS runes, the Odal-rune) and subsequently by white supremacist movements. The 21st century has seen a different kind of appropriation: the absorption of Norse visual motifs into popular culture — fantasy illustration, video game design, film and television costume, tattoos, and jewellery. The Marvel Cinematic Universe's Þórr, the *Vikings* television series, and *Assassin's Creed: Valhalla* have made Viking imagery globally visible in ways that no museum exhibition could match.

The 2040 UoY position on this popular culture engagement: **adaptation is inevitable and not inherently harmful, but students must be able to distinguish between authentic Viking Age art and modern reinterpretations**. The popular image of the "Viking" — horned helmet, winged helm, the valknut tattoo — is a modern invention, and the gap between popular image and archaeological reality is itself a subject of study.

**Viking Art in the 2040s: New Technologies, New Questions**

The 2040s have brought new technologies to the study of Viking art: **AI-assisted image recognition** enabling corpus-scale analysis, **3D scanning and printing** enabling precise replication and study of fragile objects, **multi-spectral imaging** revealing faded pigments and worn surfaces, **VR reconstruction** enabling immersive experience of Viking Age visual environments. These technologies are transforming what we can know, but they do not resolve the fundamental question: **what did Viking art mean to the people who made and used it?** The objects — carved, cast, forged, woven — survive. The meanings are lost, and the work of recovering them, always partial and provisional, continues.

### Discussion Questions

1. The Urnes style was absorbed into the Romanesque rather than being replaced by it. Is this absorption best described as transformation, decline, or continuity — or do these terms impose a false choice?
2. The 19th-century Viking Revival was driven by Romantic nationalism. How does this political history shape the modern reception of Viking art? Can the art be separated from the politics of its revival?
3. The 2040 UoY holds that popular culture adaptation is "not inherently harmful." But what if the adaptations are wildly inaccurate — horned helmets, winged helms, runic tattoos? At what point does inaccuracy become harmful?

### Assignment 3: Craft Reconstruction — The Mammen Axe

**Objective:** Understand Viking Age craft techniques through a detailed study of a single artefact.

**Task:** Produce a complete technical and art-historical analysis of the Mammen axe (c. 970 CE). Address: (a) the axe as a functional object (type, dimensions, use), (b) the silver inlay technique (how was the decoration executed?), (c) the style and iconography (Mammen style, foliate creature), (d) the archaeological context (find location, associated objects, date), (e) the social function (ceremonial, prestige, or practical?), (f) the axe in comparative context (other decorated Viking axes).

**Deliverables:** 2500–3000 word technical analysis, technique diagram (step-by-step reconstruction of the inlay process), comparative catalogue (minimum 5 decorated Viking axes), annotated bibliography (minimum 10 sources).

*The carved beasts, the interlaced serpents, the stamped golden couples, the painted shields — these are the surviving fragments of a visual world that was once as vivid and encompassing as any cathedral. The Vikings saw their gods in the animals that gripped their brooches, their fate in the serpents that wound through the runestones, their social order in the materials they wore on their bodies. To study Viking art is to study a way of seeing — a visual language that encoded the deepest commitments of a culture that left us no systematic theology, no philosophical treatises, no written confession of faith. The art is what remains.*

*— Dr. Ingrid Yngvildsdóttir, Chair of Viking Art History, University of Yggdrasil*
