## Lecture 1: Introduction to Viking Age Art — Definition, Classification, and Meaning

**What Is Viking Art?**

Viking Age art is not "art" in the modern Western sense. It was not created for galleries, not signed by individual artists (with rare exceptions), and not displayed in isolation. Viking Art was **functional, narrative, and cosmological** — it adorned objects that were used in daily life (jewelry, weapons, ships, tools, clothing, buildings), and its meaning was inseparable from its function.

The six Viking Age art styles (see Lecture 3) were defined by modern archaeologists (Bernhard Salin, 1904; David Wilson and Ole Klindt-Jensen, 1966) based on formal characteristics. These categories are useful but artificial — the styles overlap, and some objects combine elements of multiple styles.

**The 2040 Approach to Viking Art:**
1. **Formal analysis** — Describe the visual elements (motifs, compositions, techniques)
2. **Contextual analysis** — Where was it found? Who owned it? How was it used?
3. **Interpretive analysis** — What did it mean? What did it communicate?
4. **Comparative analysis** — How does it relate to earlier Germanic, contemporary European, and later Romanesque art?

## Lecture 2: Materials and Techniques — The Craft of Viking Age Objects

**Metalwork:** The highest-status Viking Age objects are made of precious metals (gold, silver, bronze). Techniques: casting (lost-wax and piece-mold), filigree (fine gold/silver wire), granulation (tiny metal beads soldered onto a surface), repoussé (hammering from the reverse), chasing (hammering from the front), niello (black sulfur compound inlaid into engraved lines), and gilding (applying gold leaf or gold amalgam).

**Woodworking:** The most common material for everyday objects (buildings, ships, tools, furniture, bowls, spoons). Techniques: carving (both low-relief and full-round), turning (pole lathe for bowls and cups), painting (pigments on wood — traces found on the Oseberg ship and stave churches).

**Textiles:** Wool (from Icelandic and Norwegian sheep) and linen (from flax) are the primary fiber materials. Techniques: tablet weaving (for decorative bands), loom weaving (warp-weighted loom for broadcloth), nalbinding (single-needle knitting for mittens and socks), embroidery (on the Oseberg tapestries).

**Glass:** Glass beads were produced in large numbers at Ribe, Kaupang, and other trading towns. Techniques: wound beads (molten glass wrapped around a metal rod), drawn beads (pulled from a molten gather), and mosaic beads (patterned with slices of multicolored glass canes). Viking glass beads are found from Iceland to Russia.

**Bone, Antler, and Ivory:** Walrus ivory, reindeer antler, and bone were carved into combs, pins, gaming pieces, knife handles, and Christian crosses. The most common Viking Age object is the **comb** — single- and double-sided combs made of reindeer antler are found in enormous quantities at every trading town.

## Lecture 3: The Six Viking Age Art Styles — Chronology and Characteristics

**1. Oseberg Style (c. 780-870 CE):**
Named after the Oseberg ship burial (c. 834 CE). Characteristics: dynamic gripping beasts, interlacing animal bodies, exuberant surface decoration, ribbon-like bands. The Oseberg animal has a rounded body, gripping paws, and a yawning head with large eyes. The style is dense, playful, and almost baroque in its exuberance.

Key objects: Oseberg ship prow (dragon head), Oseberg cart (gripping beasts), Oseberg sledges (interlacing animals).

**2. Borre Style (c. 840-970 CE):**
Named after the Borre mound cemetery in Vestfold, Norway. Characteristics: geometric ring-chain interlace, mask-like faces framed by interlacing bands, pendant triangles with mask motifs, "gripping beast" but more stylized than Oseberg. The Borre style is the most widely distributed Viking style — found from Iceland to Russia.

Key objects: Borre buckle, Oseberg tapestry fragments, numerous brooches and harness mounts.

**3. Jellinge Style (c. 900-970 CE):**
Named after the Jelling stones (c. 965 CE). Characteristics: simplified ribbon-like animals in S-shaped curves, double-contoured bodies (outline and fill line), spiral hip joints, tendril-like extensions. The Jellinge animal is a degenerated gripping beast — no longer gripping, but flowing in sinuous curves.

Key objects: Gorm's Jelling stone (smaller), Harald's Jelling stone (larger), Mammen axe.

**4. Mammen Style (c. 950-1025 CE):**
Named after the Mammen axe (c. 970-971 CE). Characteristics: more naturalistic than Jellinge, tendril scrolls with animal masks, plant motifs (acanthus leaves), double-contoured bodies with spiral hips. The Mammen style represents a reintroduction of naturalism and the first appearance of significant plant motifs — possibly under Anglo-Saxon or Carolingian influence.

Key objects: Mammen axe (silver-inlaid decoration with tendril scrolls and mask), Jelling cup (silver cup with Mammen-style decoration).

**5. Ringerike Style (c. 980-1090 CE):**
Named after the Ringerike district in Norway (where many rune stones in this style are found). Characteristics: elaborate plant foliage interlaced with animals, larger and more realistic than Mammen, symmetrical compositions, Anglo-Saxon and Ottonian influence (this is the most "international" of the Viking styles). The Ringerike animal is muscular, dynamic, and often shown in full profile with a spiral hip and flowing tendrils.

Key objects: The Vang stone (Norway), the Alstad stone (Norway), the Sutton Cloth (fragments of embroidered textile in Ringerike style from York).

**6. Urnes Style (c. 1040-1130 CE):**
Named after the Urnes stave church portal (c. 1070-1100 CE, Norway). Characteristics: thin, elegant, serpentine animals in combat — one animal eating another, with elongated bodies, interlacing limbs, and pointed heads. The Urnes style is the most refined of the Viking styles and the one that most clearly transitions into Romanesque art. It represents the last purely Scandinavian style before the European Romanesque synthesis.

Key objects: Urnes stave church portal, the Meadle stone (England), many coins of Harald Hardrada, metalwork from York and Sigtuna.

## Lecture 4: Viking Age Jewelry — Brooches, Arm-Rings, and Personal Adornment

**Brooches:** The most common Viking Age jewelry type. Oval brooches (tortoiseshell brooches) were worn by women in pairs to fasten the apron dress. Penannular brooches (ring brooches with a pin) were worn by both genders to fasten cloaks. Disc brooches were worn at the throat. The majority of brooches are bronze; precious metal brooches (silver, sometimes gilded) indicate high status.

**Arm-Rings and Neck-Rings:** Arm-rings (armbands) and neck-rings (torcs) are characteristic Scandinavian jewelry types. Arm-rings could be plain, twisted, or decorated with stamps and filigree. Neck-rings were often massive solid silver pieces weighing up to 500g. Some arm-rings and neck-rings are hack-silver — cut or folded, indicating they were used as currency.

**Þórr's Hammer Amulets:** Over 1,000 found across the Viking world. Mostly bronze or silver pendants in the shape of Þórr's hammer (a T-shaped hammer with a short handle). Some are plain; others are elaborately decorated with faces, interlace, or runic inscriptions. A few are gilded. The hammer amulet is the most distinctive Norse religious artifact — it identified the wearer as a follower of Þórr and, during the conversion period, as a non-Christian.

**Glass Beads:** Produced in large numbers at Ribe, Kaupang, and other trading towns. Viking Age glass beads range from simple monochrome to elaborate mosaic patterns. The most common are blue and green wound beads, but mosaic beads with complex layered patterns (rosette, eye, and strip designs) are found at high-status sites.

## Lecture 5: Viking Age Weapons — Swords, Axes, and the Language of Violence

**Swords:** The highest-status weapon. Pattern-welded blades (welded from multiple iron rods twisted and forged together, producing visible patterns in the steel) are the most common type. Frankish-made blades (ULFBERHT inscriptions, 9th-11th centuries) are the most prestigious — imported from the Rhineland and fitted with Scandinavian hilts. A good sword cost the equivalent of 8-12 cattle.

**Axes:** The most common weapon. The bearded axe (with an extended lower edge) was used for both woodworking and combat. The dane axe (broad, thin blade on a long shaft) was the weapon of the housecarl — the elite warrior. The Mammen axe (c. 970-971 CE, found in a chieftain's burial at Mammen, Denmark) is the most elaborately decorated Viking Age weapon.

**Spears:** The most universal weapon. Cheap to produce, effective in the shield wall, and easy to train with. Spearheads range from simple leaf-shaped forms to elaborate decorated examples with silver inlay.

**Shields:** Round, approximately 1m in diameter, made of thin wooden planks (fir, pine, or linden) over a leather facing, with a central iron boss. Extremely thin — designed to be used once and discarded in battle. Shieldmaiden/shieldwall tactics relied on overlapping shields.

**Helmets and Armor:** The Gjermundbu helmet (c. 900 CE, Norway) is the only complete Viking Age helmet found — a rounded iron cap with a nose guard. **Horned helmets are a 19th-century invention.** Chain mail (brynja) was expensive and rare. Leather and padded gambesons were more common.

## Lecture 6: Viking Ships as Art — The Decorated Vessel

**The Oseberg Ship (c. 834 CE):** The most elaborately decorated Viking ship. The prow is a carved dragon head with gripping beast decoration. The stern is a carved dragon tail. The hull is decorated with interlacing animal bands in the Oseberg style. The ship also carried a richly decorated cart, four sledges, and twelve horses — all decorated with interlacing animals.

**The Gokstad Ship (c. 890 CE):** Less elaborately decorated than Oseberg (no dragon head carving), but still with decorative features: a carved animal head post (now in the museum), decorated shields along the gunwales, and a decorated tent. The Gokstad ship represents the more practical side of Viking ship decoration — functional beauty, not ostentatious display.

**The Roskilde 6 Ship (c. 1025-1035 CE):** The longest Viking ship found (36m). Hull planking is decorated with carved interlacing in the Mammen/Ringerike style. The ship represents the peak of Viking ship design — it was probably a royal warship.

**Ship Decoration as Status:** The elaborately decorated ship was a mobile symbol of power. A chieftain arriving in a dragon ship with carved prows, decorated shields, and gilded figureheads was making a statement about wealth, status, and military capability. Ship decoration was not merely aesthetic — it was political communication.

## Lecture 7: Viking Age Textiles — From the Loom to the Grave

**Fiber Types:** Wool (most common), linen (undergarments and fine cloth), silk (imported, extremely high-status — found in the Oseberg and Birka burials), hemp (rope and coarse cloth).

**Weaving Techniques:** Warp-weighted loom (the standard Viking Age loom — vertical, with clay or stone weights holding the warp threads taut), tablet weaving (decorative bands for edges, belts, and headbands — produces complex geometric patterns), nalbinding (single-needle knitting, used for mittens, socks, and caps).

**The Oseberg Textiles:** The Oseberg ship burial preserved an extraordinary collection of textiles: tapestry fragments (depicting a procession of women, a cart with a figure, and a building), pattern-woven wool bands, silk and samite fragments (imported from Central Asia and the Byzantine world), and plain-woven woolen cloth in various qualities.

**The Birka Textiles:** The Birka graves produced a large collection of textile fragments, including: silk trim on woolen garments (evidence of silk trading), tablet-woven bands with silver and gold thread (extremely high-status), and silk embroideries with geometric patterns.

**Clothing in the Viking Age:** Women wore an underdress (serk), an overdress (hanging dress/apron dress) fastened with oval brooches, a shawl or mantle, and a head covering (cap or hood). Men wore a tunic (kyrtill), trousers (brók), a cloak (feldr) fastened with a penannular brooch, and a cap or hood. Colors: natural (white, brown, black, gray), dyed (blue from woad, red from madder, yellow from weld), and patterned (through tablet weaving and embroidery).

## Lecture 8: Runestones as Art — Form, Function, and Decoration

**The Intersection of Art and Text:** Runestones are the primary surviving Viking Age public art form. They combine runic inscription (text) with decorative carving (art) in a single monument.

**Runestone Decoration Styles:**
- **Urnes style** — The most common decorative style on late Viking Age runestones (Sweden, Uppland). Thin, serpentine animals intertwined with runic bands.
- **Ringerike style** — Plant foliage interlaced with animals, symmetrical compositions. Common on Norwegian runestones.
- **Jellinge style** — Simplified ribbon animals. Found on the Jelling stones and early Danish runestones.
- **Pr1/Pr2/Pr3 styles** — Pauline Müller's classification system for runestone decoration. Pr1 (simple), Pr2 (intermediate), Pr3 (elaborate).

**The Uppland Runestone Tradition:** The province of Uppland in Sweden has the highest density of runestones in Scandinavia (>1,300). The Uppland tradition (c. 1010-1050 CE) is characterized by: elaborate Urnes-style decoration, long runic inscriptions with detailed genealogical information, Christian crosses and invocations combined with traditional Norse commemorative formulas, and professional carvers who signed their work (Balle, Visäte, Fot, Öpir).

**The Jarlabanke Stones:** A set of 20+ runestones in Uppland raised by the chieftain Jarlabanke and his family. They constitute a single monumental program — a public declaration of power, wealth, and Christian faith. The Jarlabanke stones demonstrate that runestones were not just private memorials but **political statements** about land ownership, inheritance, and social status.

## Lecture 9: Viking Art and European Connections — Influence and Exchange

**Carolingian Influence:** The Carolingian Renaissance (c. 768-877 CE) had a profound impact on Viking art. Carolingian metalwork, manuscript illumination, and architectural decoration provided models for the Mammen and Ringerike styles. The introduction of acanthus leaf motifs, vine scrolls, and mask-like faces into Viking art can be traced to Carolingian models.

**Anglo-Saxon Influence:** Viking contact with the Anglo-Saxon kingdoms produced a rich hybrid art — Anglo-Scandinavian. The most distinctive products are the York metalwork school (elaborate decorated brooches, strap-ends, and harness mounts), the Gosforth Cross (combining Christian iconography with Norse mythological scenes), and theRemaining Dale hogback (a Anglo-Scandinavian grave marker combining Christian and Norse elements).

**Insular (Celtic) Influence:** Viking contact with Ireland and Scotland introduced Insular (Celtic) artistic motifs into the Scandinavian repertoire: interlace patterns, spiral decoration, and the "gripping beast" motif may have Insular origins. The Viking colonization of Ireland produced a distinctive hybrid art — Hiberno-Norse — combining Scandinavian and Irish elements.

**Byzantine Influence:** The Byzantine Empire provided luxury goods (silk, gold jewelry, enamelwork, ivory) that were imitated and adapted by Viking craftsmen. The most significant Byzantine influence is the cloisonné enamel technique, used in several high-status Viking Age objects (the Mortlake Drinking Horn, the Jelling cup fragment).

**Islamic Influence:** Islamic art influenced Viking craft primarily through silver dirhams. Viking craftsmen adapted Islamic coin weights, decorative patterns, and metallurgical techniques. The ring from Birka with a Kufic inscription reading "Allah" is the most dramatic example of direct Islamic influence on Viking material culture.

## Lecture 10: Stave Churches — The Architecture of Conversion

**Stave Church Construction:** Stave churches are the most distinctive Norwegian medieval building type. Built from vertical stave columns (staver) resting on a frame of horizontal sill beams (sviller). The interior is a simplified basilica plan (nave, aisle, and chancel). The exterior is elaborately decorated with dragon heads, interlacing animal bands, and carved portals.

**The Urnes Stave Church (c. 1070-1100 CE):** The oldest surviving stave church and the finest example of Viking art in wood. The north portal features the Urnes style at its most refined: a thin, sinuous animal fighting a serpent, intertwined with plant scrolls. The portal is the last great masterpiece of Viking animal art before the transition to Romanesque.

**Other Surviving Stave Churches:** Borgund (c. 1150, the best-preserved), Heddal (c. 1200, the largest), Hopperstad (c. 1140), and approximately 28 others. All are in Norway.

**Stave Churches as Conversion Architecture:** Stave churches represent the architectural transition from pagan hov to Christian church. They are built using the same post-and-beam construction as the Viking Age longhouse, but with a Christian basilica plan and Christian liturgical arrangement. The dragon heads on the roof ridges may be a continuation of the Viking ship prow tradition — Christian buildings wearing pagan decoration.

## Lecture 11: Viking Art and the Afterlife — Grave Goods and Their Meaning

**What Do Grave Goods Mean?**

Grave goods are not simply "belongings buried with the dead." They are **performative statements** — public declarations of the identity, status, and relationships of the deceased. The 2040 position (following the work of the UoY Mortuary Archaeology Lab):

1. **Identity statements** — Grave goods declare who the deceased was (warrior, seeress, chieftain, farmer)
2. **Status displays** — The quality and quantity of grave goods communicate the social rank of the deceased and their family
3. **Relationship markers** — Grave goods from distant places (Islamic dirhams, Frankish swords, Byzantine silk) communicate the family's trade connections and political alliances
4. **Belief statements** — Grave goods may communicate religious beliefs (Þórr's hammer = pagan, cross = Christian, both = syncretic)
5. **Ritual objects** — Some grave goods were specifically made for the funeral (killed swords, bent spears, ceremonial jewelry)

**The Problem of Interpretation:** A sword in a grave could mean the person was a warrior (identity), a high-status individual (status), a member of a family with Frankish trade connections (relationship), a follower of Óðinn (belief), or that the sword was ritually "killed" to release its spirit (ritual). The same object can have multiple meanings simultaneously.

## Lecture 12: Viking Art in the 21st Century — Reception, Reproduction, and Responsibility

**The Viking Art Revival:** Viking art motifs have been revived and adapted by: Art Nouveau/Jugendstil (Finnish and Scandinavian national romanticism, 1890s-1910s), modern Norse-inspired design (Scandinavian design tradition, 1950s-present), contemporary Ásatrú (reproduction jewelry, rune stones, ritual objects), and digital media (video games, films, TV shows).

**The Problem of Appropriation:** Viking art has been appropriated by: 19th-century Romantic nationalists (constructing a "pure" Scandinavian heritage), Nazi Germany (appropriating runes, swastikas, and "Aryan" imagery), contemporary white supremacist groups (using Viking symbols as identity markers), and commercial brands (using Viking imagery to sell products from beer to insurance).

**The 2040 Academic Responsibility:** The UoY position: Viking art is world cultural heritage. It should be studied, preserved, and shared with respect and intellectual honesty. We must: (1) challenge racist appropriations with evidence, (2) support inclusive and respectful engagement with Viking art, (3) distinguish between historically informed reproduction and fantasy, (4) acknowledge the damage done by Nazi and white supremacist appropriation.
