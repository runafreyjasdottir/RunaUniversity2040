## Lecture 1: Introduction to Viking Mortuary Archaeology — Why the Dead Matter

Viking Age burials are among the most spectacular archaeological discoveries in European history. From the Oseberg ship burial to the warrior graves of Birka, these elaborate interments have shaped public understanding of the Viking Age for over 150 years.

But Viking Age burial is far more diverse than ship burials and warrior graves suggest. The full spectrum includes:

- **Cremation** — The dominant burial form in early Viking Age Scandinavia. The body was burned on a pyre, the remains collected, and placed in a mound or urn.
- **Inhumation** — Burial of the unburned body, often in a coffin, chamber, or ship. Became more common in the later Viking Age, probably under Christian influence.
- **Ship burial** — The body placed in a ship, along with grave goods, and covered with a mound. The most spectacular form of Viking Age burial (Oseberg, Gokstad, Tune, Ladby).
- **Chamber grave** — A wooden chamber built in a grave pit, containing the body and grave goods. Common at trading towns (Birka, Hedeby, York).
- **Simple pit grave** — A body placed in a pit with few or no grave goods. The most common burial form, often invisible archaeologically.
- **Deviant burial** — Burials that deviate from the norm (decapitations, staked bodies, prone burials). Often interpreted as social outcasts or revenants.

**Why Burials Matter:** Viking Age burials are not passive reflections of social reality. They are **performative** — the living create a burial that communicates specific messages about the dead person's identity, status, and relationships. The burial is the last public statement the community makes about the deceased.

**The 2040 Mortuary Archaeology Approach:** Every burial is a text that must be read in context — the grave goods, the body position, the burial form, the landscape setting, and the chronological context all contribute to the meaning of the burial. No single element is determinative.

## Lecture 2: Cremation and the Pyre — Fire as Transformation

**The Dominant Form:** Cremation was the dominant burial practice in Scandinavia from the Bronze Age through the early Viking Age. The shift from cremation to inhumation is one of the most significant changes in Viking Age burial practice and is closely linked to Christianization.

**The Cremation Process:**
1. **Preparation of the body** — The body was dressed in clothing and jewelry, sometimes arranged on a bier or in a cart.
2. **The pyre** — A substantial structure of wood (oak preferred for its heat and duration). Pyres at major cremation sites reached temperatures of 800-1000°C.
3. **The burning** — The pyre was lit. The cremation took several hours. The community likely gathered around the pyre — this was a public event.
4. **Collection of remains** — After the pyre cooled, the cremated bone was collected from the ash. This was not a thorough process — small fragments and teeth were often left behind.
5. **Deposition** — The cremated remains were placed in an urn, scattered on the ground surface, or placed in a mound with other grave goods.

**Cremation as Transformation:** Cremation is not merely disposal — it is **transformation**. The body is reduced from a recognizable individual to a collection of fragments. The living gather the fragments and place them in a new context (urn, mound, pit). This transformation mirrors the journey of the dead from the world of the living to the world of the dead.

**The Uppsala Cremation Mounds:** The three Royal Mounds at Gamla Uppsala (c. 550-600 CE, Vendel Period) are the most famous Scandinavian cremation burials. Each mound contained a cremation with rich grave goods. The mounds are enormous (40-50m in diameter) and dominate the landscape.

## Lecture 3: Inhumation and the Body — Preserving the Dead

**The Shift to Inhumation:** Inhumation (unburned burial) gradually replaced cremation in Scandinavia during the Viking Age, beginning in the late 8th century in Denmark and spreading north. By the 10th century, inhumation was the dominant burial form in Christian areas.

**Types of Inhumation:**
- **Simple pit grave** — Body in a pit, often without a coffin. Most common for low-status individuals.
- **Coffin burial** — Body placed in a wooden coffin. More common for higher-status individuals.
- **Chamber grave** — A wooden chamber built in the grave pit, with the body and grave goods arranged as if in a room. The highest-status form of inhumation (next to ship burial).
- **Ship burial** — Body placed in a ship within a mound. The most spectacular form.
- **Churchyard burial** — Body buried in a Christian churchyard, oriented east-west, without grave goods. The dominant form by the 11th century.

**Body Position:** Inhumation burials vary in body position: extended supine (most common), flexed (legs bent), prone (face-down — possibly deviant), and seated (rare — possibly ritual). Orientation varies: north-south (pre-Christian), east-west (Christian), and various intermediate positions.

**Preservation Conditions:** Inhumation graves are far more informative archaeologically than cremations — the body is preserved (in favorable conditions) along with the grave goods, allowing the archaeologist to reconstruct the burial as a complete scene. The best preservation occurs in waterlogged, acidic, or frozen conditions: Oseberg (waterlogged clay), Skatteby (bog preservation), and the Greenland burials (frozen).

## Lecture 4: Ship Burials — The Most Spectacular Interments

**What Is a Ship Burial?**

A ship burial is a burial in which the body (or bodies) is placed in a ship, along with grave goods, and the entire assembly is covered with a mound. The ship may be fully functional (Oseberg, Gokstad) or symbolic (a few planks representing a ship).

**Norwegian Ship Burials:**
- **Oseberg (c. 834 CE)** — Two women (one ~80, one ~50) buried in a 22m ship with a richly decorated cart, four sledges, twelve horses, a tapestry, beds, chests, and numerous personal items. The most spectacular Viking Age burial ever discovered. The older woman may have been a vǫlva (seeress) based on the presence of cannabis seeds, a staff, and ritual objects.
- **Gokstad (c. 890 CE)** — A man (~40, tall and powerfully built) buried in a 24m ship with three boats, 12 horses, 6 dogs, a peacock, weapons, gaming pieces, and personal items. He had a healed battle wound on his leg.
- **Tune (c. 900-910 CE)** — A man buried in a 20m ship with weapons, horses, and personal items. The least well-preserved of the three.
- **Borre (c. 900 CE)** — Multiple mounds, one containing a ship burial (now destroyed by excavation). The Borre style is named after this site.

**The Ladby Ship (c. 925 CE, Denmark):** A 21m ship containing a man with 11 horses, 4 dogs, weapons, and personal items. Unique feature: the ship's anchor was placed inside the bow. The ship was covered not with a mound but with a layer of earth.

**The Sutton Hoo Ship Burial (c. 625 CE, England):** Not strictly Viking (it's Anglo-Saxon), but the closest parallel to the Scandinavian ship burials and a major influence on our understanding of elite burial in early medieval Northern Europe. The helmet, shield, and sword are masterpieces of Germanic metalwork.

**British Isles Ship Burials:**
- **Scar, Sanday, Orkney (c. 870-950 CE)** — A Viking boat burial containing a man, woman, and child with weapons, tools, and a comb.
- **Ballinaby, Islay (c. 9th century)** — A Viking boat burial with weapons and horse equipment.

## Lecture 5: Grave Goods — What the Dead Carry

**Categories of Grave Goods:**

1. **Personal adornment** — Jewelry (brooches, arm-rings, neck-rings, finger rings, beads), clothing fasteners, and combs. The most numerous category.
2. **Weapons** — Swords, axes, spears, shields, arrows, and archery equipment. Primarily male graves.
3. **Tools** — Smithing tools, woodworking tools, sewing equipment, and fishing gear. Both male and female graves.
4. **Household items** — Cooking pots, drinking vessels, tableware. Primarily female graves.
5. **Transport** — Ships, boats, horses, sledges, carts, riding equipment.
6. **Ritual objects** — Þórr's hammer amulets, staffs (possibleseiðr equipment), cult figures.
7. **Exotic/foreign goods** — Islamic dirhams, Byzantine silk, Frankish swords, Irish metalwork. Indicators of long-distance trade or raiding.
8. **Food and drink** — Animal bones (from funeral feasts), grain, and sometimes honey mead residues.

**Interpreting Grave Goods — Three Models:**
- **Identity model** — Grave goods reflect the identity of the deceased (a warrior is buried with weapons, a seeress with a staff, a trader with scales).
- **Status model** — Grave goods reflect the social status of the deceased and their family (rich graves = high status, poor graves = low status).
- **Performance model** — Grave goods are a public performance by the living — a statement about who the dead person was, who the surviving family is, and what claims they are making about social status and inheritance.

**The 2040 Position:** All three models are valid. A grave is simultaneously an identity statement, a status display, and a social performance. The challenge is to determine which model is most relevant for a given burial, and how the three models interact.

## Lecture 6: The Oseberg Burial — A Case Study in Interpretation

**The Discovery (1904):** A farmer named Oskar Rom dug into a mound on his property at Lille Oseberg farm in Vestfold, Norway, and found the prow of a ship. Professor Gabriel Gustafson of the University of Oslo was called to the site and supervised the excavation over the next four months.

**The Burial:** The Oseberg mound contained a 22m oak ship (clinker-built, with 15 oar-holes on each side). Inside the ship were two women, a richly decorated cart, four sledges, twelve horses, a tapestry, beds, chests, kitchen equipment, and numerous personal items.

**The Two Women:**
- Woman A: ~80 years old. Suffered from a variety of ailments (cancer, osteoporosis, fused vertebrae). Her fine clothing and personal items suggest high status.
- Woman B: ~50 years old. Muscular build. Her clothing is simpler. She may have been a servant or companion.

**Interpretations of the Oseberg Burial:**
1. **The Queen Theory** (conservative, early 20th century): Woman A was a queen. Her fine grave goods reflect royal status.
2. **The Vǫlva Theory** (1970s-present): Woman A was a vǫlva (seeress). The staff, the cannabis seeds, and the ritual objects support this interpretation.
3. **The Political Burial Theory** (1990s-present): The Oseberg burial is a **political statement** by the Vestfold dynasty — a claim to territorial authority expressed through spectacular mortuary display. The burial coincides with a period of political consolidation in Vestfold.
4. **The Kinship Burial Theory** (2010s-present): The two women were kin (mother and daughter?).

**The 2040 Consensus:** The Oseberg burial cannot be reduced to a single interpretation. It is simultaneously a high-status burial, a possible vǫlva burial, a political statement, and a kinship burial. The challenge is to hold all of these interpretations at once.

## Lecture 7: Gender and Burial — The Birka Warrior Controversy

**The Birka Cemetery (Björkö, Sweden):** Approximately 3,000 burials (c. 800-950 CE), containing one of the richest collections of Viking Age grave goods in Scandinavia. Birka graves include chamber graves, cremations, and inhumations, with grave goods ranging from simple beads to spectacular weapon sets.

**Bj 581:** The most famous Birka grave. A chamber grave containing a sword, an axe, a spear, a shield, a bow, 25 arrows, two horses, and gaming pieces. Long interpreted as a "great warrior" — one of the most elite military burials in the Viking world.

**The 2017 DNA Study:** Charlotte Hedenstierna-Jonson and colleagues tested the DNA of the Bj 581 individual and found that **the warrior was biologically female**. The paper, published in the American Journal of Physical Anthropology, caused an international media sensation.

**The Controversy:** Some scholars have challenged the interpretation. Criticisms include: grave goods don't necessarily reflect the identity of the buried individual (they may be gifts from mourners), gaming pieces suggest a military strategist rather than a front-line warrior, and the woman's physical build may not be consistent with a warrior lifestyle.

**The 2040 Position:** The DNA evidence is clear — Bj 581 is a biological female. The question is not whether women could be warriors (they could — shieldmaidens appear in the sagas, and female warriors are attested at other Viking Age sites), but what this specific burial tells us about the range of roles available to Viking Age women.

## Lecture 8: Hoards — What People Hide and Why

**What Is a Hoard?** A hoard is a deliberately concealed deposit of valuable objects, buried in the ground or hidden in a container. Hoards are found throughout the Viking world and contain a wide range of materials: silver (coins, hack silver, jewelry), gold (rings, armlets, pendants), weapons, tools, and personal items.

**Types of Hoards:**
1. **Economic hoards** — Concealed for safekeeping during times of danger (war, raiding, political instability). The owner intended to recover them. Most coin hoards and hack-silver hoards are economic hoards.
2. **Ritual hoards** — Deposited as offerings to gods, spirits, or ancestors. May be placed in bogs, lakes, or special features associated with cult practice. The Illerup Ådal and Nydam Mose weapon deposits are ritual hoards.
3. **Founder's hoards** — Concealed by a craftsperson (smith, jeweler) for professional reasons. Contains raw materials, tools, and unfinished products.
4. **Lost hoards** — Objects lost accidentally (dropped in water, buried under building collapse). These are not hoards in the strict sense but function similarly archaeologically.

**The Cuerdale Hoard (c. 905 CE, Lancashire, England):** Over 8,500 items including silver coins, hack silver, jewelry, and ingots. The largest Viking Age hoard found in England. Probably deposited during the chaos of the Viking withdrawal from Northumbria.

**The Hoen Hoard (c. 855-875 CE, Norway):** Gold jewelry, coins, and other items — the largest gold hoard from the Viking Age. Probably concealed by a wealthy woman during a period of political instability.

**The Spilling Hoard (c. 950-970 CE, Gotland, Sweden):** Over 14,000 Islamic dirhams — the largest Viking Age coin hoard ever found. Evidence of the massive east-west silver trade.

## Lecture 9: Bog Bodies and Water Deposits — Sacrifice, Offering, and the Sacred

**The Illerup Ådal Deposits (c. 200-500 CE, Denmark):** Over 15,000 weapons deposited in a lake in Jutland. The weapons are Roman, Germanic, and Nordic — from defeated enemies. They were deliberately broken, bent, and deposited in the lake as war booty offerings to the gods. The Illerup deposits are not Viking Age (they're Roman Iron Age), but they establish the tradition of weapon deposition that continues into the Viking Age.

**Nydam Mose (c. 200-400 CE, Denmark):** A bog deposit containing two ships, weapons, and personal items. The Nydam ship (c. 310-320 CE) is the oldest known Nordic clinker-built ship — a direct ancestor of the Viking ship. The deposit is a war booty offering like Illerup.

**Viking Age Bog and Water Deposits:**
- **Weapon deposits** — Swords, spears, shields, and arrows deposited in lakes and bogs. Probably war booty offerings.
- **Jewelry deposits** — Arm-rings, neck-rings, and brooches deposited in bogs and wetlands. Probably votive offerings.
- **Animal deposits** — Horses, dogs, and other animals deposited in bogs and lakes. Probably sacrificial offerings.
- **Human deposits** — Bog bodies (tollundmanden, grauballemanden — though these are pre-Viking). In the Viking Age, human sacrifice is attested textually (Ibn Fadlan's account of the Rus ship funeral) but not archaeologically confirmed.

**The Ritual Landscape Model:** Bog and water deposits are part of a broader pattern of sacred landscape use. Bogs, lakes, springs, and rivers were liminal spaces — places where the world of the living met the world of the gods. Depositing offerings in these spaces was a way of communicating with the divine.

## Lecture 10: Christianization and Changing Burial Practices

**The Archaeological Signature of Conversion:**
1. **Burial orientation shifts** — Pre-Christian graves are oriented north-south or in various directions. Christian graves are oriented east-west (facing Jerusalem).
2. **Grave goods decline** — Christian burials have fewer or no grave goods. The body is buried in a shroud or simple clothing.
3. **Churchyard burial** — Burials cluster around churches instead of in separate cemeteries or mounds.
4. **Cross amulets replace Þórr's hammer amulets** — The transition from hammer to cross is the clearest archaeological marker of conversion.
5. **Coffins become more common** — Plain wooden coffins, sometimes with cross motifs.
6. **Sex segregation decreases** — Pre-Christian cemeteries often segregate male and female graves (weapons in one area, jewelry in another). Christian churchyards mix genders.

**The Transitional Period (c. 950-1050 CE):** The most interesting burials archaeologically are from the transitional period, when pre-Christian and Christian practices coexisted. These include:
- Graves with both Þórr's hammer and cross amulets
- Mound burials near churches
- East-west oriented graves with grave goods
- Christian symbols on pre-Christian-style jewelry

**The Jelling Transition:** The Jelling stones (Denmark) illustrate the transition perfectly. Gorm's stone (the smaller one) is pagan — it commemorates Gorm's wife Thyra with a traditional inscription. Harald's stone (the larger one) is Christian — it commemorates Harald's parents and celebrates his conversion of Denmark. The two stones stand side by side, marking the pivot point of Danish religious history.

## Lecture 11: Social Stratification and Burial — Reading Inequality in the Grave

**The Viking Age Social Hierarchy (from grave goods):**

1. **Elite burials** — Ship burials, chamber graves with imported luxury goods (Byzantine silk, Frankish swords, Islamic dirhams). Found in fewer than 1% of graves.
2. **Prosperous farmer burials** — Weapon sets, jewelry, good-quality tools. Found in 10-15% of graves.
3. **Ordinary farmer burials** — Simple grave goods (a knife, a few beads, perhaps a simple brooch). Found in 50-60% of graves.
4. **Poor burials** — No grave goods, or a single item. Found in 25-30% of graves.
5. **Absent burials** — Many people (slaves, servants, the destitute) may have received no formal burial at all. Their graves are invisible archaeologically.

**What Grave Goods Can't Tell Us:** Grave goods reflect the wealth and status of the **surviving family**, not necessarily the deceased. A rich burial means the surviving family had resources to spend on the funeral — it doesn't mean the deceased was personally wealthy. A poor burial might reflect poverty, or it might reflect a funerary tradition that didn't include grave goods.

**The 2040 Social Archaeology Position:** Viking Age society was **unequal** — the archaeological record shows a clear hierarchy of wealth and status visible in burial practice. But the hierarchy is not simple. There are regional variations (Denmark has more rich burials than Norway), chronological variations (the proportion of elite burials increases over time), and gender variations (men's graves tend to contain more weapons, women's graves more jewelry).

## Lecture 12: Ethics in Mortuary Archaeology — Whose Dead? Whose Past?

**The Repatriation Question:** Viking Age burials contain human remains. Whose remains are they? Who has the right to decide what happens to them? The 2040 position: human remains should be treated with respect, and descendant communities should be consulted about their handling and display.

**The Display Question:** Should Viking Age burials be displayed in museums? The Oseberg ship is the most visited archaeological exhibit in Scandinavia — but the two women buried in it are still in the mound (their bones were removed to the University of Oslo for study). Is it ethical to display grave goods without acknowledging the people they were buried with?

**The DNA Question:** Ancient DNA analysis has revolutionized Viking Age archaeology (the Bj 581 warrior is only the most famous example). But DNA analysis requires destructive sampling of human remains. Who gives consent for the destruction of ancestral remains in the name of science?

**The Digital Afterlife:** The 2040 solution: 3D scanning, photogrammetry, and digital reconstruction. The Oseberg ship has been fully scanned in 3D. The Birka graves have been digitized. The digital archive allows scholars to study the burials without accessing the physical remains — reducing the need for destructive analysis.
