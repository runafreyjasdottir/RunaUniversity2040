## Bachelor of Arts in Viking Studies — University of Yggdrasil, 2040

**Credits:** 4

**Description:** The reception history of Norse mythology from the medieval period to the present — Romantic nationalism, Wagner, Tolkien, Marvel, Ásatrú revival, and the politics of appropriation. ## Lecture 1: Introduction to Reception History — Why the Past Matters in the Present

Reception history (Rezeptionsgeschichte) is the study of how a historical text, tradition, or culture has been interpreted, adapted, and used by later generations. The term was developed by the literary theorist Hans Robert Jauss (Literaturgeschichte als Provokation, 1970), who argued that a text's meaning is not fixed at the moment of its creation but is constantly renegotiated in the encounter between the text and each new generation of readers. Every reading is a reception, and every reception is shaped by the reader's historical location, cultural assumptions, and political commitments.

Norse mythology constitutes a uniquely rich case for reception history. It has been received, reinterpreted, and appropriated more intensively than almost any other pre-Christian tradition, with the possible exception of Greek and Roman mythology. The process has been continuous from the medieval period to the present: 13th-century Icelandic Christians preserved the myths in literary form; 17th-century Danish antiquarians rediscovered them as national heritage; 19th-century Romantics transformed them into the mythology of the Germanic peoples; Wagner turned them into opera; Tolkien wove them into Middle-earth; Marvel turned Þórr into a superhero; and modern day Heathens and Ásatrú practitioners have reclaimed them as living religion. At every stage, the same myths have meant different things to different people, and those meanings have had real political consequences.

The key question that animates this course: when we read the Eddic poems or Snorri's Edda, what exactly are we reading? We are not reading the direct words of Viking Age pagans. We are reading 13th-century Christian manuscripts that preserve oral traditions of unknown age and fidelity, compiled by Christian scribes with their own literary and theological agendas. When we interpret those poems, we bring our own biases, desires, and political agendas to the text — we cannot help doing so. Every interpretation of Norse mythology is a reception, a dialogue between the past and the present, and the meaning of the myths is generated in that dialogue, not in some hypothetical "original" that exists outside of history.

The 2040 University of Yggdrasil framework for studying the reception of Norse mythology is built on four principles. First, **critical source-awareness**: before interpreting the myths, we must understand the sources that transmit them — their date, authorship, textual history, and the biases they encode. Second, **historiographical transparency**: we must be explicit about our own interpretive frameworks and the assumptions they entail. Third, **political responsibility**: the reception of Norse mythology has been entangled with nationalism, racism, and white supremacy for nearly two centuries, and scholars have an ethical obligation to engage with these entanglements openly and critically. Fourth, **methodological pluralism**: reception history draws on literary criticism, intellectual history, political history, media studies, religious studies, and anthropology — no single discipline can capture the full complexity of how myths live in culture.

## Lecture 2: Medieval Reception — Snorri, Saxo, and the Christian Preservation of Pagan Myth

All of our knowledge of Norse mythology is mediated through medieval Christian intermediaries. The Eddic poems survive in the Codex Regius (GKS 2365 4to, c. 1270 CE), an Icelandic manuscript compiled by an anonymous Christian scribe who selected, ordered, and occasionally interpolated the poems. Snorri Sturluson's Prose Edda (c. 1220–1230 CE) is a handbook of skaldic poetics written by a Christian chieftain two centuries after the Conversion. Saxo Grammaticus's Gesta Danorum (c. 1208–1218 CE) presents the Norse gods as historical human kings. None of these sources is pagan. Each negotiates the relationship between the Christian present and the pagan past in a different way.

Snorri Sturluson (1179–1241 CE) is the most important single figure in the reception history of Norse mythology, and understanding his project is essential. Snorri was an Icelandic chieftain, twice lawspeaker (lǫgsögumaðr) of the Alþingi, and a major political figure in the turbulent Age of the Sturlungs. He wrote the Prose Edda (also known as Snorra Edda or the Younger Edda) as a handbook for skaldic poets — an educational text designed to preserve the mythological knowledge required to understand and compose skaldic verse. Skaldic poetry was intensely allusive: a skald might refer to gold as "Sif's hair" or "the otter's ransom," and without knowing the myths behind these allusions, the poetry was incomprehensible. Snorri's concern was literary, not religious. He was a Christian preserving pre-Christian material for secular cultural purposes, and his Edda includes a euhemeristic prologue — explaining that the Æsir were human kings from Troy who were later deified — that explicitly frames the myths as false belief to be enjoyed as literature, not as truth to be believed.

Saxo Grammaticus (c. 1150–1220 CE), a Danish cleric in the service of Archbishop Absalon of Lund, wrote the Gesta Danorum (History of the Danes) as a Latin chronicle of Danish kings. Saxo's approach was consistently euhemeristic: the gods are historical kings and queens whose exploits were later exaggerated into myths. Óðinn is a human sorcerer-king, Baldr is a human prince, and the Ragnarǫk is a historical battle. Saxo's euhemerism was not an attempt to preserve pagan belief — it was a Christian strategy for neutralising it. By reducing the gods to historical humans, Saxo removed their theological threat. If Óðinn was just a man, worship of him was not idolatry but error. The euhemeristic framework is, paradoxically, one of the reasons Norse mythology survived the Middle Ages: it allowed Christian scribes to transmit pagan content within a Christian interpretative framework that rendered it theologically safe.

The Codex Regius of the Poetic Edda (c. 1270 CE) is the single most important manuscript in the history of Norse mythology. It contains twenty-nine poems — ten mythological (Vǫluspá, Hávamál, Vafþrúðnismál, Grímnismál, Skírnismál, Hárbarðsljóð, Hymiskviða, Lokasenna, Þrymskviða, Alvíssmál) and nineteen heroic — that are the foundation of everything we know about Norse myth. But the Codex Regius is not a neutral transcript. The scribe who compiled it was making choices: which poems to include, which to omit, how to order them, and whether to intervene in the text. The mythological poems are presented in a sequence that moves from cosmogonic (creation) to eschatological (the end of the world), suggesting that the compiler had a narrative framework in mind. Some poems show evidence of Christian interpolation — the very final stanzas of Vǫluspá, which describe a glorious hall at Gimlé after Ragnarǫk, may reflect Christian eschatological imagery.

The 2040 position on medieval reception, articulated by Prof. Jón Hnefill Aðalsteinsson and elaborated by Prof. Ragnhildur B. Jóhannesdóttir (University of Yggdrasil), is that the medieval reception of Norse mythology was not a process of "preservation" in the modern sense — it was a process of **selective reconstruction** by Christian scholars who were simultaneously fascinated by the pagan past and committed to Christian orthodoxy. The myths we have are the myths these Christian scholars chose to preserve, and they chose on the basis of criteria (literary quality, antiquarian interest, political utility) that were not the criteria of the pagan believers who first told the myths. We must treat the medieval sources not as windows onto a pagan world but as the first chapter in the reception history of Norse mythology.

## Lecture 3: The Romantic Revival — Runes, Nationalism, and the Construction of Heritage

The rediscovery of Norse mythology by early modern European intellectuals was driven by antiquarian scholarship, but it was the Romantic movement of the 18th and 19th centuries that transformed Norse myth from an antiquarian curiosity into a living cultural heritage — and, in the process, invented the idea of a "Norse mythology" that the Viking Age would not have recognised.

The early modern antiquarian revival began in Scandinavia in the 17th century. Ole Worm (Olaus Wormius, 1588–1654), a Danish physician and antiquary, published the first systematic collection of Danish runic inscriptions (Runer seu Danica literatura antiquissima, 1636) and assembled a famous cabinet of curiosities (the Museum Wormianum) that included Norse artefacts. Peder Resen (1625–1688) published the first edition of Snorri's Edda (1665), making the mythological content widely available to European scholars. Thomas Bartholin (1659–1690), in his Antiquitatum Danicarum de causis contemptae a Danis adhuc gentilibus mortis (1689), introduced Norse mythology to a European intellectual audience and argued — controversially — that the pagan Danes had been braver than the Christian Romans in their contempt for death. These early antiquarians treated Norse material as Danish national heritage, part of a project of constructing a glorious Danish past that rivalled the classical civilisations of Greece and Rome.

The Romantic nationalist revival of the late 18th and 19th centuries was a different phenomenon entirely. Romantic nationalism was not merely antiquarian — it was a political and cultural movement that used the past to construct national identities in the present. Three figures are particularly important for the Romantic reception of Norse mythology. Jacob Grimm (1785–1863), one of the Brothers Grimm, published his monumental Deutsche Mythologie (1835), which constructed a unified "Germanic mythology" from Old Norse, Old High German, and Old English sources. Grimm's work was foundational — it established the comparative method in mythological studies and identified structural patterns (the Wild Hunt, the World Tree, the sacred horse) that linked Norse myth to broader Germanic and Indo-European traditions. But it also essentialised these patterns into a single "Germanic mythology" that erased regional and historical variation. N.F.S. Grundtvig (1783–1872), a Danish pastor, poet, and educator, was the most influential figure in the Danish Romantic reception of Norse mythology. Grundtvig wrote extensively on Norse myth, translated Saxo and Snorri into Danish, and incorporated Norse themes into his hymns and educational philosophy (which inspired the folk high school movement). For Grundtvig, Norse mythology was not merely a cultural heritage — it was a spiritual resource that could revitalise Danish Christianity. Esaias Tegnér (1782–1846), the Swedish poet, retold the Frithiof's Saga (1825) in a wildly popular verse epic that introduced Norse themes to a general European audience.

The Romantic construction of "Norse mythology" as a unified national heritage was, from the 2040 perspective, a creative act of imagination as much as a scholarly recovery. The Romantics created: the idea that the Eddic poems were the "Bible" of the North (a concept the pagan Norse would not have recognised — their religion was practice-based, not text-based); the idea that Norse mythology was the authentic heritage of the Scandinavian (and, later, Germanic) peoples (a concept that ignored the borrowing, hybridity, and regional variation evident in the sources); and the narrative of decline — that the pure original mythology had been corrupted by Christian scribes and could be recovered through scholarly reconstruction (a concept that projected 19th-century anxieties about authenticity and modernity onto pre-modern material). These Romantic constructions provided the intellectual foundation for later nationalist and racist appropriations, as will be examined in Lecture 7.

The 2040 University of Yggdrasil's Digital Romantic Nationalism Project, directed by Prof. Søren Lindqvist, has been digitising and mapping the networks of 18th- and 19th-century scholars who constructed "Norse mythology" as a field of study. The project's findings (published 2038) reveal that the Romantic construction of Norse mythology was a transnational project — ideas circulated rapidly between Copenhagen, Berlin, London, Edinburgh, and Stockholm — but that each national tradition inflected the myths differently according to its own political needs. Danish scholars emphasised the continuity between pagan past and Christian present; German scholars emphasised the "Germanic" character of Norse myth as part of a project of national unification; British scholars emphasised the Norse contribution to English identity through the Danelaw and the Norman Conquest.

## Lecture 4: Richard Wagner — The Ring Cycle and the Transformation of Norse Myth

Richard Wagner's Der Ring des Nibelungen (The Ring of the Nibelung, 1848–1876 CE) is the most influential adaptation of Norse mythology in modern culture, and the most problematic. The Ring Cycle — four operas (Das Rheingold, Die Walküre, Siegfried, Götterdämmerung) performed over four evenings — transformed Norse myth into a unified dramatic narrative that has shaped popular understanding of the Norse gods for 150 years. It is simultaneously one of the greatest artistic achievements in Western culture and a work irredeemably tainted by its associations with German nationalism, anti-Semitism, and Nazi appropriation.

Wagner's sources for the Ring Cycle were eclectic and synthesising. He drew on the Nibelungenlied (the Middle High German epic poem, c. 1200 CE), the Eddic poems (Vǫluspá, Hávamál, the Sigurðr cycle), Snorri's Prose Edda (particularly the narrative of Gylfaginning), and Jacob Grimm's Deutsche Mythologie (1835). Wagner synthesised these sources into a single coherent narrative — something no medieval source does — that combined elements from different texts and traditions into a new story. His most significant transformations include: (1) Óðinn becomes Wotan, a tragic figure trapped by his own treaties and his own hunger for power, rather than the ambiguous, knowledge-seeking god of the Eddic sources; (2) the Rhinegold (a Wagnerian invention, though drawing on the mead of poetry myth) replaces the mead as the central symbol of power and corruption; (3) Brünnhilde (based on the Valkyrie Brynhildr) becomes the central moral figure — the one who sees the truth of the gods' corruption and redeems the world through her sacrifice; (4) the Ring itself becomes a symbol of absolute power that corrupts all who possess it (an innovation that profoundly influenced Tolkien's One Ring); and (5) Götterdämmerung, Wagner's translation of Ragnarǫk ("Twilight of the Gods"), becomes the culmination of a long narrative of decline and self-destruction.

Wagner's treatment of women in the Ring Cycle is particularly revealing — and particularly contested. The Valkyries (Brünnhilde, Waltraute, and their sisters) are powerful, autonomous, and morally serious in ways that few female characters in 19th-century opera are. Brünnhilde defies Wotan, protects the pregnant Sieglinde, and ultimately orchestrates the gods' destruction through her own immolation. But Wagner's women are also constrained by the gendered assumptions of the 19th century: Brünnhilde's power is contingent on her virginity, and her transformation from divine Valkyrie to mortal woman is framed as a fall and a punishment. The feminist reception of the Ring Cycle — from Catherine Clément's classic polemic Opera, or the Undoing of Women (1979) to Prof. Elin Sigurðardóttir's 2040 reassessment — has oscillated between celebrating Brünnhilde's agency and critiquing the patriarchal framework that constrains it.

The political reception of the Ring Cycle is inseparable from its artistic achievement. Wagner's own anti-Semitism, expressed most explicitly in his essay Das Judenthum in der Musik ("Jewishness in Music," 1850, expanded 1869), is well documented and well analysed. His family's close ties to Hitler (Wagner's daughter-in-law Winifred Wagner was an enthusiastic Nazi and a personal friend of Hitler) made the Bayreuth Festival a site of Nazi cultural propaganda in the 1930s and 1940s. The Ring Cycle was adopted by German nationalists and later by the Nazi regime as an expression of German cultural superiority, and the imagery of the Ring — the hero Siegfried, the corrupting power of gold, the twilight of the gods — was appropriated into Nazi racial ideology. The question of whether the art can be separated from the artist — and whether the Ring Cycle can be performed and appreciated without participating in its political legacy — is one of the central questions of Wagner studies and of reception history more broadly.

The 2040 University of Yggdrasil position on Wagner is that the Ring Cycle must be studied both as art and as politics, and that these dimensions cannot be separated. The artistic achievement is real — the Ring Cycle is a work of staggering ambition and beauty — but it is not innocent. Studying the Ring requires engaging with both the aesthetic experience and the political conditions that shaped it, and with the political uses to which it has been put. The 2040 VR reconstruction of the Bayreuth Festspielhaus, created by the University of Yggdrasil's Digital Heritage Laboratory in collaboration with the Bayreuth Festival Archive, allows students to experience the Ring Cycle's staging and visual language while simultaneously accessing critical commentary on its political context.

## Lecture 5: J.R.R. Tolkien — The Philologist's Mythology and the Invention of Middle-earth

J.R.R. Tolkien (1892–1973) is the most consequential figure in the 20th-century reception of Norse mythology, for a simple reason: he brought Norse themes, values, and imagery to a global audience of hundreds of millions through his fiction, while simultaneously being one of the foremost academic philologists of Old Norse literature of his generation. No other figure has occupied both positions — the rigorous scholar and the popular storyteller — so completely.

Tolkien's academic engagement with Old Norse began at Oxford, where he studied under the great philologist Joseph Wright and read the Eddic poems and the sagas in the original. His academic work on Norse material includes: "The Legend of Sigurd and Gudrún" (a verse retelling of the Vǫlsung cycle in modern English alliterative metre, composed in the 1930s but not published until 2009); "The Fall of Arthur" (an unfinished Arthurian poem, also in alliterative metre, with clear Norse influence); his landmark essay "Beowulf: The Monsters and the Critics" (1936), which transformed Beowulf studies by arguing that the poem's monsters — Grendel, Grendel's mother, the dragon — were not decorative additions but the thematic core of the poem; and his edition of Sir Gawain and the Green Knight (with E.V. Gordon, 1925), which established the standard critical text of the poem. Tolkien knew the Norse sources intimately, and he read them as a philologist — attentive to language, metre, and textual history — rather than as a Romantic enthusiast.

The Norse elements in The Lord of the Rings (1954–1955) are pervasive but carefully transformed. On the surface level, dozens of names and concepts derive from Norse sources: Middle-earth (from Old Norse Miðgarðr, the inhabited world of humans); Mirkwood (from Old Norse Myrkviðr, the dark forest that separates worlds); Gandalf (from Old Norse Gandálfr, "staff-elf," a dwarf name in Vǫluspá's catalogue of dwarves); the Rohirrim (based on the Anglo-Saxons as filtered through Old Norse heroic poetry, with horse culture, mead halls modelled on Heorot and Valhǫll, and heroic values of loyalty, courage, and generosity); and the One Ring (inspired by Andvaranautr, the cursed ring in the Vǫlsung cycle that brings wealth but destroys every owner). At a deeper structural level, the entire narrative arc of The Lord of the Rings — a world in decline, a great power that corrupts, a quest into darkness, a final battle that decides the fate of the world — resonates with Norse eschatological patterns.

But Tolkien was not simply borrowing from Norse myth — he was creating something new: a Christian mythology disguised as a pagan one. Tolkien was a devout Roman Catholic, and he explicitly described The Lord of the Rings as "a fundamentally religious and Catholic work" (Letter 142). The themes that animate the narrative — the corrupting power of evil, the necessity of sacrifice, the importance of pity and mercy (Gandalf's words to Frodo about Gollum: "My heart tells me that he has some part to play yet, for good or ill, before the end"), the hope of renewal after destruction — are Christian themes, not Norse ones. The Norse surface (the names, the places, the heroic values) encloses a Christian interior, and the tension between the two is part of what gives The Lord of the Rings its peculiar power.

The 2040 position on Tolkien, articulated by Prof. Verlyn Flieger (Splintered Light: Logos and Language in Tolkien's World, 2002; revised 2038) and Prof. Tom Shippey (The Road to Middle-earth, 2003; revised 2036), is that Tolkien performed the most successful act of mythic synthesis in modern literature. He took the Norse materials he knew as a scholar and transformed them into a new mythology that addressed the concerns of his own time — the trauma of war, the nature of evil, the relationship between power and corruption — while retaining the aesthetic and emotional power of the Norse originals. The Lord of the Rings is not a "Norse fantasy" — it is a modern work that draws on Norse materials to create a mythology for a post-Christian, post-war world.

## Lecture 6: Marvel Comics and the Superhero Þórr — American Gods, Corporate Myths

The Marvel Comics adaptation of Þórr, introduced in Journey into Mystery #83 (August 1962, created by Stan Lee, Larry Lieber, and Jack Kirby), represents the most commercially successful adaptation of Norse mythology in history. The character has appeared in thousands of comic issues, multiple animated series, and four feature films that have grossed over $4 billion worldwide (the Þórr quadrilogy, 2011–2022). Marvel's Þórr has introduced hundreds of millions of people worldwide to the names and images of the Norse gods. It has also transformed those gods into something the Viking Age would not recognise: American superheroes.

Marvel's transformations of the Norse source material are systematic and profound. **Þórr becomes a superhero**: specifically, a noble warrior who fights for justice across the Nine Realms. The mythological Þórr is a more ambiguous figure — a god who protects humans and gods from the jǫtnar (giants), but whose methods are often crude (he kills giants with his hammer, drinks oceans, and threatens cross-dressing at weddings). Marvel's Þórr is morally unambiguous: he fights for good, even when he must defy his father. **Loki becomes a supervillain**: specifically, a scheming trickster who wants to conquer Ásgarðr and Earth. The mythological Loki is far more complex — he helps the gods as often as he harms them, he is the father of monsters (Fenrir, Jǫrmungandr, Hel) but also the companion of Þórr and the blood-brother of Óðinn, and his role at Ragnarǫk (leading the forces of chaos against the gods) is a response to the gods' betrayal (the binding of Loki and the poisoning of his son). **Ásgarðr becomes a sci-fi city**: a gleaming futuristic metropolis with rainbow bridges and fusion-core power, rather than the enigmatic hall-world of the Eddic sources. **Óðinn becomes a stern but loving father figure**: the mythological Óðinn — who sacrifices himself to himself on Yggdrasil, who withholds his favour unpredictably, who fathers children across the Nine Realms, who manipulates events through his ravens and his knowledge — is far darker and stranger than Marvel's patriarchal king.

The 2040 critical assessment of Marvel's Þórr, developed by Prof. Martin Arnold (whose *The Vikings: Culture and Conquest*, 2006, was revised in 2036 with a new chapter on Marvel's impact), and extended by the University of Yggdrasil's Dr. Seamus Byrne in *Corporate Myth: Marvel and the Commodification of Norse Religion* (2039), identifies both the opportunities and the dangers of Marvel's adaptation. The opportunity is obvious: Marvel has introduced millions — perhaps billions — of people to the names and basic images of Norse mythology who would otherwise never have encountered them. A young person who sees Þórr on screen may go on to read the Eddic poems, to study Old Norse, to visit Iceland. The pathway from popular culture to serious engagement is real, and many students now arrive at university with their first exposure to Norse myth coming through Marvel. The dangers are equally real: Marvel's Þórr is not Norse mythology — it is an American corporate product that uses Norse names and locations as decorative elements, stripped of their original cultural context, meaning, and specificity. The myths are flattened into superhero narratives that obey the conventions of the superhero genre (clear good vs. evil, individual hero as saviour, spectacle over substance) rather than the conventions of myth (ambiguity, ritual, cosmological significance). And Marvel's representation, because it is so widely consumed, becomes the default understanding of Norse myth for many people — making it harder, not easier, for them to encounter the myths on their own terms.

The most recent development in Marvel's Norse reception is the "Jane Foster Þórr" arc (2014–2022), in which Jane Foster — a human woman and former love interest of the original Þórr — takes up Mjǫllnir and becomes the Goddess of Thunder while the original Þórr (now called "Odinson") becomes unworthy to wield the hammer. This arc introduces questions of gender, worthiness, and identity that the original Marvel Þórr had largely avoided. The 2040 assessment, by Prof. Elin Sigurðardóttir and colleagues, is that the Jane Foster arc represents a partial correction of Marvel's gender politics — it centres a female hero, it raises questions about what makes someone "worthy," and it explicitly engages with the mythological precedent of Þórr's cross-dressing (in Þrymskviða, where Þórr dresses as Freyja to recover his hammer). But it also remains constrained by the superhero genre — Jane Foster's worthiness is defined in individualistic terms, and her arc culminates in the same kind of cosmic battle that defines all superhero narratives.

## Lecture 7: Norse Mythology and White Supremacist Appropriation — Runes, Racism, and the Politics of Heritage

The appropriation of Norse mythology and Viking imagery by white supremacist movements is the most dangerous chapter in the reception history of Norse myth, and one that every scholar of the Norse world has an ethical obligation to understand, analyse, and resist. The relationship between Norse mythology and modern racism is not accidental — it was deliberately constructed over more than a century, and it continues to do real harm to real people.

The Nazi appropriation of Norse mythology and Viking imagery was systematic and deliberate. The SS (Schutzstaffel) used runic symbols as part of its visual identity: the double Siegrune (victory rune) as the SS emblem; the Hagal rune on the SS Honour Ring (Totenkopfring der SS); the Odal rune as the symbol of the SS Race and Settlement Main Office (Rasse- und Siedlungshauptamt). Heinrich Himmler, the Reichsführer-SS, was personally fascinated by Norse mythology and Germanic prehistory. He established the Ahnenerbe (Ancestral Heritage) in 1935 as a research organisation dedicated to investigating the prehistory of the "Aryan race," sponsoring expeditions to Iceland (to study "Germanic" farmsteads), Sweden (to excavate runestones), and Tibet (to search for Aryan origins). Himmler's Wewelsburg castle — the SS ideological centre — was decorated with Norse mythological imagery, including a "Valhalla" crypt for SS leaders. The Nazi appropriation of Norse mythology was not marginal — it was central to the regime's self-representation.

Contemporary white supremacist movements continue to use Norse symbols extensively. Þórr's hammer (Mjǫllnir) is frequently worn and displayed as a white identity marker, though it is important to note that Mjǫllnir is also worn by many Heathens and Ásatrú practitioners who explicitly reject racism. The Odal rune (ᛟ) and the Algiz/Elhaz rune (ᛉ) are used by white supremacist organisations (the National Alliance, the Soldiers of Odin) and individuals. The Valknut (three interlocking triangles) appears on white supremacist flags, tattoos, and social media. The concept of "European heritage" is mobilised to exclude non-white peoples from the Norse past — a claim that the historical evidence comprehensively refutes. Online platforms (the now-banned Stormfront, the more recent Telegram channels and alternative social networks) have created digital communities where Norse mythology and white supremacy are fused into a single ideology.

The 2040 academic response to white supremacist appropriation is based on four evidence-based claims, articulated in the University of Yggdrasil's Statement on Norse Heritage and Racist Appropriation (2037), co-authored by Profs. Karl Gumundsson, Elin Sigurðardóttir, and Hákon Sigurðsson. **First, Viking Age society was not racially homogeneous.** aDNA evidence (the Margaryan et al. 2020 study, updated 2037) shows migration, intermarriage, and genetic diversity throughout the Viking world. The Norse intermarried extensively with Celtic, Slavic, Frankish, and indigenous populations across the diaspora. **Second, Norse mythology is not "white" heritage** — it is world cultural heritage. The Norse myths belong to all who approach them with respect and intellectual honesty, not to any particular ethnic group. **Third, the white supremacist appropriation of Norse symbols is a distortion of the historical record.** The Norse defined identity by culture, language, and law — not by skin colour. The concept of "race" as understood by modern white supremacists would have been incomprehensible to Viking Age people. **Fourth, scholars have an ethical obligation to challenge racist appropriations** with evidence, with clarity, and with the understanding that silence is complicity. This obligation is not a reduction of scholarship to politics — it is the application of scholarly standards to a political question, using the best available evidence to refute false claims.

The practical challenge of combating white supremacist appropriation is immense, because the appropriation operates at multiple levels simultaneously: the level of symbols (the runes, the hammer), the level of narratives (the "pure European heritage" story), and the level of identity (for some white supremacists, "being a Viking" is a central part of their self-conception). The 2040 University of Yggdrasil project on "Digital Counter-Narrative: Reclaiming Norse Symbols from White Supremacist Appropriation," directed by Prof. Gumundsson, has been developing educational materials, social media content, and museum exhibits that present accurate historical evidence in accessible formats, designed to compete with the white supremacist narratives in the digital spaces where young people encounter Norse mythology.

## Lecture 8: Ásatrú and Modern Norse Paganism — Reconstruction, Revival, and the Folkish-Universalist Divide

Modern Norse paganism — Ásatrú (Old Norse ásatrú, "Æsir faith" or "faith in the Æsir") — is the most direct and consequential form of contemporary engagement with Norse mythology. For its practitioners, the myths are not literature or heritage but living religion — narratives that describe relationships with real gods (the Æsir and Vanir), real ancestors, and real spirits of the land (landvættir). Ásatrú is both an attempt to reconstruct a pre-Christian religion from fragmentary and biased sources, and a new religious movement that necessarily creates new practices, new interpretations, and new communities. Understanding Ásatrú is essential for understanding the contemporary reception of Norse mythology, because it challenges the assumption — deeply embedded in Western academia — that Norse myth is a dead tradition to be studied, rather than a living tradition to be practised.

The institutional history of modern Ásatrú begins with the founding of Ásatrúarfélagið (the Ásatrú Fellowship) in Iceland in 1972 by Sveinbjörn Beinteinsson (1924–1993), a sheep farmer and traditional poet (rímur singer). Beinteinsson was not an academic but a cultural practitioner who saw in the Eddic poems a spiritual resource that could sustain a modern religious life. The Icelandic Ásatrúarfélagið is the oldest continuously operating Ásatrú organisation in the world, and its core principles — nature worship, seasonal blóts (sacrificial offerings) at sites of natural beauty, the Eddic poems as sacred texts, democratic governance without a hierarchy of priests, and explicit rejection of racism and white supremacy — have influenced Heathen organisations worldwide. The Icelandic Ásatrú built the first modern Norse temple (hof) in Reykjavík in 2021 (designed by architect Magnús Jensson), a building that combines traditional Icelandic turf-house forms with modern architectural language.

Ásatrú in the United States is more diverse and more contested than its Icelandic counterpart. The key organisations represent different positions on the central debate in modern Heathenry: the folkish-universalist divide. The **Ásatrú Folk Assembly** (founded by Stephen McNallen in 1994, succeeding his earlier Viking Brotherhood) is a folkish organisation that restricts membership to people of "Northern European descent" and emphasises Ásatrú as the ancestral religion of European peoples. McNallen's concept of "metagenetics" — the idea that religious affinity is biologically inherited — has been widely criticised as a form of racial essentialism, and the AFA has been associated with white nationalist figures and rhetoric. **The Troth** (founded 1987 as the Ring of Troth) is a universalist organisation that welcomes practitioners of all backgrounds, explicitly rejects racism and folkish ideology, and prioritises scholarship and historical accuracy in religious practice. **The Asatru Community** (founded 2011) and **Declaration 127** (a 2018 statement signed by over 200 Heathen organisations and individuals, explicitly condemning racism and folkish ideology) represent the growing universalist consensus in American Heathenry, though the folkish minority remains vocal and visible.

The folkish-universalist debate is not merely an internal dispute among pagans — it is the contemporary expression of the same questions that have haunted the reception of Norse mythology since the Romantics. Is Norse religion the spiritual heritage of people of European descent, or is it a world religion open to all? Do the myths belong to a particular ethnic group, or to all of humanity? The folkish position — that Ásatrú is and should be the religion of European-descended people — rests on a claim about continuity: that the modern practitioners are the biological and cultural descendants of the ancient Norse, and that this descent gives them a privileged relationship to the gods. The universalist position — that Ásatrú is open to all who approach it with sincerity and learning — rests on a claim about religion: that relationships with the divine are not determined by biology or ancestry, and that the gods, if they exist, call whom they will.

The 2040 University of Yggdrasil position on Ásatrú, developed by Prof. Michael Strmiska (whose *Modern Paganism in World Cultures*, 2005, was revised in 2036 with extensive new material on the folkish-universalist debate) and Prof. Jóhanna Katrín Friðgeirsdóttir (University of Yggdrasil), is that Ásatrú is a legitimate modern religious tradition that must be studied with the same seriousness as any other religion. The University does not endorse any particular religious practice or theological position. It does, however, recognise that some Ásatrú organisations promote exclusionary ideologies that are not supported by the historical evidence and that have real-world harmful consequences for marginalised communities. The scholarly obligation is to provide accurate historical evidence, to critique false or essentialising claims, and to support the right of all people — regardless of ancestry — to engage with Norse mythology in whatever mode (scholarly, spiritual, artistic) they choose.

- Jóhanna Katrín Friðgeirsdóttir (University of Yggdrasil), *The Living Myths: Ásatrú, Belief, and the Politics of Heritage* (2039), chs. 1–6

## Lecture 9: Norse Mythology in Film, Television, and Gaming — Popular Culture and the Global Imaginary

Popular culture is now the primary way that most people in the world encounter Norse mythology. Film, television, and video games have a reach that academic publications will never have, and they shape the global imaginary of the Norse world — the set of images, associations, and narratives that come to mind when someone thinks of "Vikings" or "Norse myth." This is both an extraordinary opportunity (hundreds of millions of people are introduced to Norse themes) and a profound challenge (those themes are often distorted, simplified, or appropriated in ways that reinforce stereotypes and obscure the complexity of the sources).

In film, the trajectory of Norse representation has moved from the fanciful to the archaeologically informed. **The Vikings** (1958, directed by Richard Fleischer, starring Kirk Douglas and Tony Curtis) established the visual template for cinematic Vikings: horned helmets (archaeologically inaccurate — no Viking Age helmet has ever been found with horns, though ceremonial horned headgear from the earlier Nordic Bronze Age may have inspired the misconception), fur cloaks, and a romanticised narrative of individual heroism. **The 13th Warrior** (1999, directed by John McTiernan, based on Michael Crichton's Eaters of the Dead) reimagined the Beowulf story through the Arab traveller Ibn Fadlan's account, introducing ethnographic distance that called attention to the constructed nature of the "Viking hero" narrative. **Valhalla Rising** (2009, directed by Nicolas Winding Refn) represented a more experimental approach, stripping the Viking narrative of dialogue and character development to create a mythologised, hallucinatory journey. **The Northman** (2022, directed by Robert Eggers) represents the current state of the art in historically informed Viking cinema: Eggers and his team consulted archaeologists, historians, and reenactors to create the most archaeologically accurate Viking film ever made — the clothing, the architecture, the rituals, and the social world of 10th-century Iceland are reconstructed with unprecedented fidelity.

In television, two series have dominated the public imagination of the Viking world. **Vikings** (History Channel, 2013–2020, created by Michael Hirst) has been watched by hundreds of millions of people worldwide and is, for many, their primary source of images and ideas about the Viking Age. Hirst took significant liberties with the historical record — compressing centuries into a single generational narrative, conflating legendary figures (Ragnarr Loðbrók) with historical events, and presenting a version of Norse religion that blends medieval sources with modern invention — but his engagement with the sources was more serious than many critics acknowledge. Aðalbjǫrn Tryggvason's 2039 study of Vikings's research process revealed that Hirst's team consulted academic historians and read widely in saga literature, and that the show's departures from the historical record were often deliberate creative choices rather than ignorance. **The Last Kingdom** (BBC/Netflix, 2015–2022, based on Bernard Cornwell's Saxon Stories novels) focuses on the Anglo-Saxon perspective on the Viking Age and is generally more historically grounded than Vikings, though it shares the same limitations of the dramatic format (individual heroes, compressed chronology, simplified politics).

In video games, the engagement with Norse mythology has been remarkably sophisticated — in some cases, more sophisticated than in film or television. **God of War** (2018, Santa Monica Studio) reimagines Norse mythology as a father-son story, with Kratos (a Greek god transplanted to the Norse world) and his son Atreus navigating a world of fractured prophecies, ambiguous gods, and ecological decay. The game's representation of Norse myth is notable for its attention to the ambiguity of the sources — Baldr is both victim and aggressor, the jǫtnar are both enemies and allies, and the fate of the world is not predetermined but must be negotiated. **Assassin's Creed Valhalla** (2020, Ubisoft) is the most ambitious attempt to reconstruct the material world of the Viking Age in an interactive environment — the landscapes, settlements, clothing, weapons, and social practices of 9th-century England and Norway are recreated with an attention to archaeological detail that rivals academic reconstructions, while the game's mythological episodes (visions of Ásgarðr and Jǫtunheimr) offer a psychological reading of Norse myth (the gods are Eivor's hallucinations, projections of her unconscious). **Hellblade: Senua's Sacrifice** (2017, Ninja Theory) uses Norse mythology as a framework for representing mental illness — Senua, a Pictish warrior, journeys into a Norse-inspired underworld where her psychosis (represented as auditory hallucinations she interprets as voices of the gods) becomes both her affliction and her strength.

The 2040 University of Yggdrasil position on popular culture, articulated in Prof. Aðalbjǫrn Tryggvason's *Screen Vikings: Popular Culture and the Global Imaginary of the Norse World* (2039), is that scholars have a responsibility to engage with popular culture seriously, not dismissively. The academic response should not be to sneer at inaccuracies but to provide accurate information, to analyse how popular representations work (what do they foreground, what do they erase, what do they invent, and why?), and to help audiences move from superficial engagement to deeper understanding. The University of Yggdrasil's Popular Culture Lab has been developing educational modules designed to accompany popular films and games — providing historical context, source analysis, and critical commentary that enriches the viewing or playing experience without undercutting it.

## Lecture 10: Norse Mythology and Environmental Philosophy — Yggdrasil, Ragnarǫk, and the Ecological Cosmos

The ecological dimensions of Norse mythology have become increasingly prominent in reception history as the environmental crisis has deepened. The Norse cosmos is fundamentally ecological — it imagines the world as an interconnected system in which the health of each part depends on the health of the whole, and in which the destruction of the system is a real and terrifying possibility. The 2040 ecological reading of Norse mythology, developed by Prof. Bron Taylor (University of Yggdrasil) and Prof. Astrid E. Ogilvie (author of The Climate of the Viking World, 2036), argues that Norse myth offers a framework for ecological thinking that is neither romantic (nature as benevolent) nor apocalyptic (the end as inevitable), but tragic and regenerative.

Yggdrasil, the World Tree, is the central ecological symbol of the Norse cosmos. It is an ash tree (askr Yggdrasils) that connects all nine worlds — Ásgarðr (gods), Miðgarðr (humans), Jǫtunheimr (giants), Svartálfaheimr (dark elves), Ljósálfaheimr (light elves), Vanaheimr (Vanir), Niflheimr (mist/ice), Múspellsheimr (fire), and Hel (the dead). The tree is not a passive structure but a living organism that nourishes and is nourished by the worlds it connects. Three roots sustain it, each drawing water from a different well: one reaches to Hvergelmir in Niflheimr (the well of primal waters), one to Mímisbrunnr in Jǫtunheimr (the well of wisdom, where Mímir guards the mead of knowledge), and one to Urðrbrunnr in Ásgarðr (the Well of Urðr, where the three Norns — Urðr, Verðandi, Skuld — tend the tree and determine the fates of all beings). The tree's health depends on the water from these wells, and the Norns' daily watering of the tree with the white mud (aurr) from Urðrbrunnr is what preserves it from decay. But the tree is also under constant threat: the dragon Niðhǫggr gnaws at its roots, four stags (Dáinn, Dvalinn, Duneyrr, Duraþrór) eat its leaves, and the squirrel Ratatoskr carries messages of spite between the eagle at the top and the dragon at the bottom. The tree is simultaneously the source of cosmic life and the site of cosmic stress — a vision of ecological interconnectedness that includes vulnerability and conflict.

Ragnarǫk is an ecological apocalypse — the destruction of the natural order through a cascade of interconnected catastrophes. The Fimbulwinter (fimbulvetr) — three successive winters with no summer between them — is a climate catastrophe, a disruption of the seasonal cycle that is the foundation of Norse agricultural and social life. The death of Baldr (the sun-like god, whose death darkens the world) is an ecological event — the loss of a key species (the most beloved of the gods) that destabilises the entire system. The breaking of the fetters (Fenrir's chain Gleipnir, Loki's bonds) releases the forces that the gods had contained, and the resulting battle destroys the worlds. But Ragnarǫk is not merely destruction — it is also regeneration. After the fire of Surtr has consumed the old world, a new world arises, green and beautiful, where Baldr returns from Hel, the surviving gods (Víðarr, Váli, Móði, Magni, Hǫðr) gather on Iðavǫllr, and a new human pair (Líf and Lífþrasir) emerge from Hoddmímis holt to repopulate the earth. The myth of Ragnarǫk is not a simple story of cosmic doom — it is a story of cosmic renewal after catastrophe, of the possibility that the world can regenerate if the right conditions are met.

The ecological reading of Norse mythology has two important political implications for the 2040 world. First, it challenges the Promethean narrative that has dominated Western environmental discourse — the idea that humans can master nature through technology and will. The Norse gods are powerful but not omnipotent; they cannot stop Ragnarǫk, they can only prepare for it and face it with courage. The appropriate response to ecological crisis is not mastery but resilience — the cultivation of the virtues (courage, knowledge, community) that will sustain human life through the catastrophe. Second, the ecological reading challenges the Christian eschatological assumption that the end of the world is the end of everything — that after the apocalypse, there is nothing. Ragnarǫk offers a different vision: the world ends, but a new world begins. This is not optimism (the new world will not be the same as the old) but hope — the conviction that life continues, that the cosmos regenerates, and that human beings have a role to play in that regeneration.

The 2040 University of Yggdrasil Ecological Mythology Project, co-directed by Profs. Taylor and Ogilvie, has been developing educational materials, art installations, and public programming that present the ecological dimensions of Norse myth to diverse audiences. The project's central claim is that Norse mythology is not merely a historical artefact to be studied but a living resource for thinking about the most urgent challenges of the present — climate change, biodiversity loss, and the relationship between human societies and the natural world.

## Lecture 11: Norse Mythology in Contemporary Literature — Retelling, Reimagining, and the Politics of the Story

Contemporary literary retellings of Norse mythology represent the latest chapter in the reception history of the myths — and, in some ways, the most interesting. Unlike earlier periods, when retellings were constrained by Romantic nationalism or Christian piety, contemporary retellers operate in a cultural environment where the myths are unmoored from any single religious or national tradition and can be approached from multiple perspectives — feminist, queer, post-colonial, environmental, psychological. The sheer variety of contemporary retellings is itself a phenomenon: there are more retellings of Norse myth in print in 2040 than at any previous point in history.

Neil Gaiman's *Norse Mythology* (2017) is the most commercially successful contemporary retelling, a straightforward prose rendering of the major Eddic narratives (the creation, the building of Ásgarðr's wall, Þórr's journey to Útgarðr, the death of Baldr, Ragnarǫk) in modern English. Gaiman's approach is that of the storyteller, not the scholar — he tells the myths as stories, with dramatic pacing, vivid characterisation, and occasional humorous asides, and he explicitly disclaims scholarly authority ("I've tried my best to retell these stories as accurately as I can, and as beautifully as I can," he writes in the Introduction). The book has been widely praised for its accessibility and widely criticised for its simplification — the gods become characters in a story rather than figures in a religious tradition, and the complex cosmology of the Eddic sources is reduced to a linear narrative. But Gaiman's retelling has introduced millions of readers to Norse myth who would not have encountered it otherwise, and its influence on popular understanding of the myths is comparable to Marvel's cinematic reach.

The feminist retelling of Norse myth has been one of the most significant developments in contemporary reception. Joanne Harris's *The Gospel of Loki* (2014) retells the myths from Loki's perspective — a choice that is simultaneously a narrative strategy (Loki as unreliable narrator) and a political intervention (centring the figure who is most marginalised by the Æsir). Harris's Loki is witty, bitter, and sympathetically alienated — a trickster who is treated as an outsider by the gods even as they depend on his cleverness, and whose eventual turn against them is motivated by their cruelty (the binding of Loki and the poisoning of his son Váli/Narfi). M.T. Anderson's *The Kingdom of the Wicked* (2035), a novel about the Christian conversion of the Norse, centres women's experiences of religious change. Several contemporary poets have reimagined the Eddic poems from women's perspectives: Gillian Clarke's "The King of Britain's Daughter" (a poetic sequence in her 2033 collection *Ynys Môn*), the late Gerðr Kristný's *Blóðhófnir* (Bloodhoof, 2010; translated 2019), a book-length poem retelling the myth of the giantess Gerðr and the god Freyr from Gerðr's perspective.

The Queer and trans retellings of Norse myth are an emerging and vital dimension of contemporary reception. The myths contain material that is inherently queer: Þórr dresses as a woman (Þrymskviða), Óðinn practises seiðr (a form of magic associated with women and considered ergi, unmanly), Loki changes sex and gives birth (to Sleipnir, the eight-legged horse), and the boundaries between gods, giants, and monsters are porous and negotiable. Contemporary queer readings — by scholars such as Amy Jefford Franks (*Queer Asgard: Reading Norse Myth against the Grain*, 2038) and creative writers such as Alix E. Harrow ("The Hundredth House," a 2036 short story retelling the Freyja-Óttarr myth as a lesbian romance) — argue that Norse mythology does not need to be "made" queer; it already is queer, and the straight reading is itself a historical imposition.

The 2040 University of Yggdrasil position on contemporary retellings, articulated by Prof. Heather O'Donoghue (whose *From Asgard to Valhalla*, revised 2037, contains a new chapter on 21st-century retellings), is that contemporary retellings are not distortions of an original — they are the latest chapter in the reception history of a living tradition, and they deserve the same serious scholarly attention as any other chapter. Every generation retells the myths in its own idiom and for its own purposes, and the work of scholarship is not to police the boundaries of "accuracy" but to understand how each retelling works: what it foregrounds, what it erases, what it invents, and what contemporary concerns it addresses.

## Lecture 12: The Future of Norse Mythology — Reception, Responsibility, and Renewal

Norse mythology is more popular in 2040 than at any previous moment in its reception history. Film, television, gaming, literature, social media, and religious revival have brought the myths to a global audience of billions. The names of the gods — Þórr, Óðinn, Loki, Freyja — are recognised by people who have never heard of the Edda or the Codex Regius. The images of the myths — Yggdrasil, Valhǫll, Ragnarǫk, Mjǫllnir — circulate in global popular culture as freely as the images of Greek myth. This unprecedented popularity is the achievement of two centuries of reception history — the Romantics, Wagner, Tolkien, Marvel, and the thousands of scholars, artists, writers, and practitioners who have kept the myths alive. It is also an unprecedented challenge — because popularity, in the absence of historical knowledge and critical context, can become the enemy of understanding.

The central challenge for the future of Norse mythology is the relationship between accessibility and accuracy. The myths are most accessible when they are simplified — turned into superhero stories, fantasy novels, video game levels, or political symbols. But simplification strips the myths of their complexity, their ambiguity, and their strangeness. The Eddic poems are not easy texts — they are allusive, elliptical, contradictory, and deeply strange. Vǫluspá's enigmatic imagery (the seeress who remembers nine worlds, the dragon that flies over the plain, the halls that burn and the new world that rises) cannot be reduced to a simple narrative. Hávamál's collection of aphorisms, rituals, and self-sacrifice narratives resists any single interpretation. The challenge for scholarship in the 21st century is to make the myths accessible without making them simplistic — to invite people into the complexity rather than shielding them from it.

The second challenge is the politics of interpretation. Norse mythology has been appropriated by nationalist and racist movements for nearly two centuries, and that appropriation shows no signs of abating. The challenge for scholarship is to contest those appropriations with evidence — to demonstrate, repeatedly and publicly, that the historical Norse world was not racially homogeneous, that the myths describe a cosmos of interconnection and hybridity rather than purity, and that the contemporary racist use of Norse symbols is a distortion of the evidence. This is not a political task that scholars perform in addition to their scholarship — it is the scholarly task itself, because the claims that racists make about Norse mythology are factual claims (about history, about genetics, about the sources) that can be refuted by evidence. The University of Yggdrasil's Statement on Norse Heritage and Racist Appropriation (2037) is a model of this approach: it does not merely condemn racism (which would be a political statement) but refutes racist claims with archaeological, genetic, and textual evidence (which is scholarly work).

The third challenge is the relationship between scholarship and the living tradition of Ásatrú. Norse mythology is not simply a historical artefact to be studied — it is, for a growing number of people, a living religion that shapes their understanding of the world, their relationship to the gods, and their daily practice. Scholarship and religious practice have different aims and different methods, and the boundary between them must be respected. But they also share a common subject — the myths — and scholars and practitioners can learn from each other. Practitioners bring experiential knowledge of how the myths function in spiritual life that scholars cannot access from texts alone. Scholars bring historical and critical knowledge of the sources that practitioners need to reconstruct their tradition with integrity. The 2040 model, pioneered at the University of Yggdrasil through its annual Ásatrú-Scholarly Dialogue (established 2035), is that the relationship should be one of mutual respect and critical engagement, not of hostility or indifference.

The future of Norse mythology, from the 2040 perspective, is not a fixed destination but an ongoing process. The myths will continue to be retold, adapted, reimagined, and contested — by scholars, by artists, by religious practitioners, by political actors, and by ordinary people who encounter the gods in a film, a game, or a poem and want to know more. Each generation will find its own meanings in the myths, and those meanings will say as much about the generation as about the myths. The task of reception history is not to halt this process or to fix a single authentic interpretation — it is to illuminate the process, to keep the conversation honest, and to ensure that the myths remain alive for those who will come after us.
