## Lecture 1: The Dead Viking — How the Middle Ages Buried the Norse Past

Every generation remakes the Vikings in its own image. This course is not about the Viking Age itself — that ground is covered in the preceding three years of the program. It is about what came after: the long, strange afterlife of the Norse in the European imagination. And that afterlife begins not with a revival but with a burial.

**The Medieval Erasure:** For approximately 500 years after the conventional end of the Viking Age (conventionally dated to the Norman Conquest of England in 1066 or the death of Haraldr harðráði at Stamford Bridge the same year), Scandinavia largely vanished from the European historical imagination. The unified Christian kingdoms of Denmark, Norway, and Sweden integrated into the medieval European order, and their pagan Viking past became an embarrassment — a barbaric prehistory to be forgotten, not celebrated. The Icelandic sagas were preserved as antiquarian curiosities but were not widely read outside Iceland. The runic inscriptions weathered on standing stones that no one could read. The ship burials slumbered under their mounds, undisturbed.

This erasure was not accidental but systematic. The Church actively suppressed memory of the pagan past. Runestones were incorporated into church walls — sometimes face-down, hidden, sometimes face-out as trophies of Christian triumph over paganism. The Old Norse language retreated before Latin and Low German. The great burial mounds were avoided as haunted places (haugar, "howes") associated with draugar — the restless dead — in rural folklore that survived into the 19th century. The word "Viking" itself nearly vanished from European languages; when it reappeared, it was not from continuous oral tradition but from the rediscovery of the sagas by antiquarian scholars.

**The First Antiquarians:** The earliest systematic interest in the Viking past came not from Scandinavia but from Icelandic scholars preserving their own literary heritage. The great manuscript collector Árni Magnússon (1663-1730) spent decades traveling through Iceland, gathering every medieval vellum manuscript he could find — often purchasing them from farmers who were using the pages as patterns for clothing or as stiffening in bishop's mitres. Magnússon's collection, bequeathed to the University of Copenhagen, remains the single most important repository of Old Norse literature. His work was driven by a pre-Romantic nationalism — the belief that Iceland's medieval literature proved the island's cultural distinction from Denmark and justified its claim to political autonomy, a project that would not be realized until 1944.

In Sweden, the 17th-century "Gothicist" movement (Göticism) made more extravagant claims. Scholars like Olaus Rudbeck (1630-1702) argued in his four-volume *Atlantica* that Sweden was the lost Atlantis, the original homeland of all civilization, and that Old Norse was the primordial language from which all others descended. Rudbeck's theories were spectacularly wrong — he "translated" runic inscriptions to support his claims by assigning arbitrary values to the runes — but they established a pattern that would recur through the history of Viking reception: the use of the Norse past to support nationalist claims of exceptional antiquity and cultural superiority. Rudbeck's Gothicist framework directly influenced the 19th-century Romantics who would transform the Viking from a forgotten barbarian into a cultural hero.

**What Survived in Popular Memory:** Even as the literary tradition was lost, fragments of the Viking Age survived in European culture: the days of the week (Tuesday from Týr, Wednesday from Óðinn, Thursday from Þórr, Friday from Frigg or Freyja); place-names by the thousands across England, Scotland, Ireland, and Normandy; technical vocabulary in English (law, sky, window, knife, husband, die — all from Old Norse); and folklore traditions — the Yule log, the Wild Hunt (a folkloric echo of Óðinn's host), the "Danes' blood" invoked in English harvest traditions. These survivals demonstrate that the Viking Age was never truly forgotten, merely transformed — absorbed into the fabric of northern European culture so thoroughly that its origins were no longer recognized.

In 2040, students have access to AI-powered search tools that can trace these linguistic and folkloric survivals across the entire corpus of medieval and early modern European texts. A standard exercise in this course: feed a modern English word of Old Norse origin into the "Norse Echo" language model (developed at UoY in 2037), and trace its journey from Proto-Germanic through Old Norse into Anglo-Norman, Middle English, and modern English, with every attestation linked to its source text and geolocated.

## Lecture 2: The Norse Renaissance — 18th and Early 19th Century Rediscovery

The second life of the Vikings began in the mid-18th century, driven by the confluence of two intellectual currents: the Enlightenment's taxonomic impulse to catalogue and classify all human knowledge, and the emerging Romantic reaction against Enlightenment rationalism — a hunger for the primitive, the authentic, the sublime. The Vikings satisfied both cravings simultaneously.

**The Ossian Effect:** The single most influential text in the European rediscovery of the North was not Norse at all. James Macpherson's *Fragments of Ancient Poetry Collected in the Highlands of Scotland* (1760), followed by *Fingal* (1762) and *Temora* (1763) — collectively known as the Ossian poems — purported to be translations of ancient Gaelic epics from the 3rd century CE. They were largely Macpherson's own invention, stitched together from fragments of genuine ballad tradition and generous quantities of his own poetic imagination. But their impact was immense: Napoleon carried a copy of Ossian into battle; Goethe praised it; Thomas Jefferson considered Ossian the greatest poet since Homer. Ossian created a European appetite for the "poetry of the North" — misty, heroic, melancholy, authentic.

When Scandinavian scholars looked at their own medieval literature through the lens of Ossian, they saw something similar: a heroic northern antiquity every bit as grand as the classical Mediterranean inheritance. The Icelandic scholar Grímur Jónsson Thorkelin (1752-1829) traveled to England in 1786 specifically to locate Anglo-Saxon manuscripts that might shed light on Scandinavian history, and in the process became the first modern scholar to transcribe the unique manuscript of *Beowulf* — a poem he recognized as fundamentally Scandinavian in its subject matter despite its Old English language. Thorkelin's 1815 edition of *Beowulf* (the first ever published, under the Latin title *De Danorum Rebus Gestis* — "Concerning the Deeds of the Danes") explicitly framed the poem as a monument of Danish — not English — national heritage.

**The Saga Translations:** The systematic translation of the Icelandic sagas into modern European languages began in the late 18th century and accelerated through the early 19th. Key milestones: Gerhard Schøning's Norwegian translations (partially published, 1770s-1780s); the Copenhagen "Arnamagnæan Commission" editions (from 1772); and perhaps most influentially, the Danish scholar Peter Erasmus Müller's *Sagabibliothek* (1817-1820), a comprehensive survey of saga literature that became the standard reference for European intellectuals.

But the translations that truly brought the sagas to a mass audience were not scholarly but literary. The Norwegian poet and playwright Bjørnstjerne Bjørnson translated selections from *Heimskringla* — Snorri Sturluson's great history of the Norwegian kings — into modern Norwegian in the 1860s. The English translator William Morris — better known as a designer, socialist, and founder of the Arts and Crafts movement — collaborated with the Icelander Eiríkr Magnússon on a series of saga translations (1869-1905) that rendered the spare, terse prose of the originals into a consciously archaic English that profoundly influenced the emerging genre of fantasy literature. Morris's *The Story of the Volsungs and Niblungs* (1870) was on the desk of a young J.R.R. Tolkien when he began constructing his own mythology, and it shows.

**Runic Revival:** Alongside the literary rediscovery came renewed scholarly interest in runes. The Danish scholar Ole Worm (1588-1654) had published *Runir seu Danica Literatura Antiquissima* ("Runes, or the Most Ancient Danish Literature") in 1636, but serious runology began in the 19th century with the work of Wilhelm Grimm (of Brothers Grimm fame), whose *Über deutsche Runen* (1821) attempted the first systematic classification of runic alphabets. The decipherment of the older fuþark was largely completed by the 1860s, and the runestone began its transformation from unreadable relic to treasured national monument.

The 2040 perspective: we can now trace the reception history of individual runestones with digital precision. A UoY project (the "Runestone Afterlife Database," launched 2038) tracks every published reference, every sketch, every photograph, every rubbing, every cast, and every 3D model of each of the approximately 6,000 known Viking Age runestones, from the earliest antiquarian notices through the latest academic publications. The database reveals, among other things, that runestones in border regions (Scania, Schleswig, Jämtland) were disproportionately cited in nationalist literature during periods of territorial dispute — the stones were deployed as arguments in the political geography of the 19th and 20th centuries.

## Lecture 3: Romantic Nationalism — Vikings as Founders of Nations

The 19th century was the great age of European nationalism, and every European nation that could claim — however tenuously — a Viking heritage did so with enthusiasm. The Vikings were refashioned from barbaric raiders into noble ancestors: explorers, state-builders, freedom-lovers, the fathers of national character.

**Denmark: The Golden Age:** In Denmark, the early 19th century was the "Golden Age" (Guldalder) of art, literature, and philosophy, and the Viking past was central to the national self-image. The poet Adam Oehlenschläger's *Nordens Guder* ("Gods of the North," 1819) was a verse epic of the Norse myths that became a foundational text of Danish Romantic nationalism. The sculptor Bertel Thorvaldsen — the most famous European artist of his generation, the "Phidias of the North" — created neoclassical interpretations of Norse gods that were explicitly intended to provide Denmark with a visual mythology equivalent to the Greco-Roman tradition. The composer J.P.E. Hartmann's opera *Liden Kirsten* (1846) set a medieval Danish legend to music that incorporated folk melodies and was received as an expression of national character.

The Danish relationship with the Viking past was complicated by territorial loss. Denmark had been a great power in the medieval North Sea empire of Knútr inn ríki (Cnut the Great), but by the 19th century it was a diminished nation, having lost Norway to Sweden in 1814 and facing the eventual loss of Schleswig-Holstein to Prussia in 1864. The Viking past served as a compensatory fantasy — a reminder of what Denmark had been, and a promise of what it might become again.

**Norway: Independence and the Viking Spirit:** Norway's situation was different. Having been under Danish rule from 1380 to 1814 and then under Swedish rule (in a personal union) from 1814 to 1905, Norway's 19th century was a long struggle for full independence. The Viking past was weaponized in this struggle: the Norwegian Vikings were portrayed as the last free Norse — the ones who refused to submit to Haraldr hárfagri's unification and sailed west to colonize Iceland, Greenland, and Vinland. This narrative of freedom-loving individualism resisting centralizing tyranny was tailor-made for Norwegian nationalist aspirations.

The poet Henrik Wergeland (1808-1845) was the great voice of Norwegian Romantic nationalism, and his poetry is saturated with Viking imagery. Wergeland's *Den engelske Lods* ("The English Pilot," 1841) imagines a Norwegian sailor who embodies the Viking spirit of exploration and freedom. His younger contemporary, the historian P.A. Munch (1810-1863), published *Det norske Folks Historie* ("History of the Norwegian People," 1851-1863), a monumental work that traced Norwegian national character from the Viking Age to the present and explicitly argued for Norwegian independence on historical grounds.

The most visible monument to this Viking-inflected nationalism is the University of Oslo's Aula (ceremonial hall), decorated by Edvard Munch between 1911 and 1916. Munch's monumental paintings — *The Sun*, *History*, *Alma Mater* — are not explicitly Viking in subject matter, but their scale, their cosmic ambition, and their central figure of a nude, heroic Nordic man reaching toward the sun are infused with the Romantic vision of the Viking as a figure of primal, liberating energy.

**Sweden: The Gothic Inheritance and Runic Pride:** Sweden's 19th-century Viking revival was shaped by a different historical trajectory. Sweden had been a major European power in the 17th century, and its nationalist mythology drew heavily on the Gothicist tradition (see Lecture 1) that claimed the Swedes — the *götar* (Goths) — as the original inhabitants of Scandinavia and the ancestors of the Germanic peoples. The Vikings thus represented not a lost empire to be mourned but a racial inheritance to be celebrated.

The key institution was the Swedish Antiquities Society (Svenska Fornminnesföreningen), founded in 1869, which sponsored surveys, excavations, and publications that systematically documented Sweden's archaeological heritage. The society's journal, *Svenska Fornminnesföreningens Tidskrift*, published hundreds of articles on Viking Age sites, runestones, and artifacts, establishing the empirical foundation for Sweden's claim to be the heartland of the Viking world.

The 2040 perspective on this nationalist archaeology is necessarily critical. Students analyze how excavation priorities, site interpretation, and artifact selection in 19th-century Sweden were systematically biased toward monuments that supported the nationalist narrative — ship burials over cremation sites, runic inscriptions over mundane domestic assemblages, "Swedish" artifacts over imported ones. The Vendel Period (c. 550-800 CE), which was archaeologically rich but lacked the textual sources (sagas, runestones) that made the Viking Age legible to nationalist historiography, was systematically neglected until the mid-20th century. The Scandinavian past was selectively remembered.

## Lecture 4: Wagner, Morris, and the Arts — Vikings in Music, Painting, and Craft

The most powerful engine of Viking romanticism in the 19th century was not scholarship but art. The Viking image that persists in the popular imagination — the horned helmet, the dragon ship, the shield-maiden — was created not by archaeologists but by painters, composers, and poets who were less interested in historical accuracy than in the emotional and aesthetic power of the Norse material.

**The Horned Helmet: A Case Study in Manufactured Tradition:** No single artifact better illustrates the gulf between the historical Vikings and their Romantic re-imagination than the horned helmet. The historical Vikings did not wear horned helmets. The evidence is unambiguous: no Viking Age helmet with horns has ever been found in an archaeological context. The famous helmets of the Vendel Period (pre-Viking, 6th-8th centuries) found at Vendel and Valsgärde in Sweden have crests and brow-guards but no horns. The horned helmet entered Viking iconography through a chain of artistic decisions that can be traced with precision.

The chain begins with the Danish artist Niels Christian Kierkegaard, who was commissioned to design costumes for a Copenhagen production of Adam Oehlenschläger's play *Hagbarth og Signe* in the 1820s. Kierkegaard drew on antiquarian illustrations of Bronze Age ceremonial headgear (the Viksø helmets, dated c. 1000 BCE, found in a bog in Zealand in 1842 — note: after Kierkegaard began his designs, but similar imagery was known from rock art), conflating them with the Vendel Period helmets and projecting the whole imaginary ensemble backward onto the Viking Age. The horned Viking entered the European visual vocabulary through Carl Emil Doepler's costume designs for the first complete production of Wagner's *Der Ring des Nibelungen* at Bayreuth in 1876. Doepler's Vikings wore horned helmets because they looked dramatic on stage, and the image stuck.

In 2040, the horned helmet is a fixture of student exercises in reception historiography. Students trace the image through 200 years of popular culture, from 19th-century Romantic painting through 20th-century comic books (the Marvel Comics Thor, created by Jack Kirby and Stan Lee in 1962, wears a winged helmet — a variant on the horned theme), through 21st-century tourist souvenirs, memes, and video games. The exercise teaches that "authenticity" is a moving target, and that every generation's Viking is a construction.

**Wagner and the Ring:** Richard Wagner's *Der Ring des Nibelungen* (composed 1848-1874, first performed complete at Bayreuth in 1876) is a four-opera cycle — *Das Rheingold*, *Die Walküre*, *Siegfried*, and *Götterdämmerung* — based loosely on the Norse myths (the *Vǫlsunga saga* and the *Poetic Edda*, filtered through the Middle High German *Nibelungenlied*). Wagner's relationship to his Norse sources was complex: he used the mythic material freely, altering characters, compressing timelines, and imposing a 19th-century philosophical framework (Schopenhauer's pessimism, Feuerbach's humanism, nascent German nationalism) on the medieval texts.

Wagner's impact on Viking reception cannot be overstated. For millions of Europeans and Americans in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, Wagner's *Ring* was their first and only encounter with Norse mythology. The "Ride of the Valkyries" — the opening of Act III of *Die Walküre* — became divorced from its operatic context and entered global popular culture as the soundtrack for aerial warfare (*Apocalypse Now*, 1979) and comic violence. The Valkyrie herself — Wagner's warrior-maiden who gathers the slain for Valhǫll — became the template for every fictional shield-maiden who followed.

Yet Wagner's *Ring* is not a straightforward celebration of the Norse gods. It is a tragedy about power, greed, and the inevitable destruction of a corrupt order — themes that resonate with the Norse concept of *ragna rǫk* (the doom of the powers) but that Wagner inflected through his own revolutionary politics (he was a participant in the 1849 Dresden uprising) and his later embrace of a mystical German nationalism. The complexity of Wagner's relationship to his Norse sources — simultaneously reverent and appropriative, scholarly and wildly creative — makes his work an ideal case study for the course.

**William Morris and the Arts and Crafts Viking:** In Britain, the most significant 19th-century engagement with the Viking past was that of William Morris (1834-1896). Morris's interest in Norse literature was partly aesthetic — he admired the terse, concrete language of the sagas, so different from the Latinate prolixity of Victorian prose — and partly political. Morris was a revolutionary socialist, and he saw in the "free" Icelanders of the saga age — farmer-chieftains who governed themselves through the Alþingi assembly without a king — a model of the decentralized, egalitarian society he advocated.

Morris's Icelandic journals (1871 and 1873) record two journeys through Iceland on horseback, during which he visited saga sites, sketched landscapes, and immersed himself in the terrain of the literature he had been translating. His journal entries capture the moment when the sagas ceased to be merely texts and became lived experience: "This is the very valley where Gunnar rode, and this the river where he fought — it makes the story thrice alive." Morris's experience in Iceland transformed his design aesthetic: the interlaced patterns of his wallpapers and textiles echo the zoomorphic interlace of Viking Age art, and the spare, functional beauty of his furniture reflects a saga-age sensibility.

Morris's socialism also shaped his interpretation of the Viking Age. In *The House of the Wolfings* (1889), a prose romance set among a Germanic tribe resisting Roman invasion, Morris imagined a pre-feudal, communal society in which all free people had a voice — a vision of the past that was also a critique of Victorian industrial capitalism. The novel, with its invented mythology, archaic diction, and narrative structure modeled on the sagas, is a direct ancestor of the modern fantasy genre — Tolkien acknowledged Morris's influence, and the line from *The House of the Wolfings* through Tolkien's Middle-earth to the billion-dollar fantasy industry of 2040 is direct.

## Lecture 5: The Darkest Heir — Nazi Appropriation of the Norse Past, 1920-1945

No lecture in this course is more difficult to deliver, or more essential to hear. The Nazi appropriation of Norse and Germanic symbols, mythology, and "racial science" represents the most catastrophic perversion of the Viking legacy in history — a perversion that resulted in millions of deaths and that continues to poison the reception of Norse culture in 2040.

**The völkisch Roots:** Nazi engagement with the Norse past did not emerge from nowhere. It drew on a pre-existing German *völkisch* ("folkish") movement that had been developing since the late 19th century, combining Romantic nationalism with pseudo-scientific racial theory. The *völkisch* movement produced a vast literature of speculative prehistory that traced the "Aryan" or "Germanic" race to a primordial northern homeland (often identified with Scandinavia, sometimes with Atlantis, sometimes with the mythical Hyperborea of Greek legend) from which it had spread to civilize the world.

Key *völkisch* texts that fed into Nazi ideology: Guido von List's *Das Geheimnis der Runen* ("The Secret of the Runes," 1908), which invented an 18-rune "Armanen" fuþark that had no historical basis but was enormously influential in German occult circles; Jörg Lanz von Liebenfels's *Ostara* magazine (1905-1930), which promoted a mystical racism in which blond "Aryans" were the divine master race; and Houston Stewart Chamberlain's *Die Grundlagen des neunzehnten Jahrhunderts* ("The Foundations of the Nineteenth Century," 1899), which argued that all European civilization was the product of the "Germanic race." Hitler read Chamberlain, quoted him, and attended his funeral in 1927.

**Himmler's Ahnenerbe:** The institutional vehicle for Nazi appropriation of the Norse past was the *Ahnenerbe* ("Ancestral Heritage"), a research organization founded by Heinrich Himmler in 1935 and incorporated into the SS in 1939. The Ahnenerbe sponsored archaeological excavations, runological research, folklore collecting, and "racial science" across Germany and occupied Europe, all in service of constructing a pseudo-scholarly foundation for Nazi racial ideology. Its emblem — the "Odal rune" (ᛟ), lifted from the elder fuþark — remains a hate symbol in 2040, used by neo-Nazi groups worldwide.

Ahnenerbe archaeologists excavated at sites selected for their supposed connection to Germanic prehistory: the Externsteine rock formation (claimed as a pagan cult site, though evidence for pre-Christian use is negligible); the Viking Age trading center of Haithabu (Hedeby), which was interpreted not as a multicultural trading emporium but as a purely Germanic settlement; and, most infamously, the Crimea, where SS archaeologists searched for traces of a supposed Gothic Germanic kingdom that would justify German territorial claims to Ukraine and the Caucasus. The connection between archaeological research and genocidal violence was not incidental but structural: the Ahnenerbe's excavations were the academic wing of the same SS apparatus that was operating the extermination camps.

**The SS and Runic Symbolism:** Himmler — a failed chicken farmer with a consuming obsession with Germanic mysticism — was personally responsible for the SS's adoption of runic symbolism. The SS insignia itself (the double "sig" rune, ᛋᛋ) was designed by the graphic artist Walter Heck in 1929, drawing on the Armanen runic system. The SS death's-head ring (Totenkopfring) was inscribed with runes inside the band, visible only to the wearer — a secret mark of membership in Himmler's occult inner circle. SS wedding ceremonies replaced Christian rituals with neo-pagan "Germanic" rites. The Wewelsburg castle in Westphalia — Himmler's planned ideological center of the SS — was designed around an occult interpretation of Norse mythology, with a "Hall of the Dead" in the crypt intended as a Valhǫll for fallen SS officers.

It is impossible to overstate the damage this did to the public perception of Norse symbolism. Before the Nazi era, runes were obscure scholarly objects; after 1945, they were indelibly associated with genocide. The swastika — an ancient solar symbol found in cultures from India to Scandinavia (where it appears on Viking Age picture stones and runic inscriptions) — was rendered unusable in Western culture for at least a century. The 2040 Heathen community continues to struggle with this legacy: Heathen groups that use historic runes in their religious practice must constantly distinguish themselves from the neo-Nazi groups that use the same symbols for hate.

**Resistance and Reclamation:** It is important — and the course emphasizes this — that Nazi appropriation of the Norse past was contested even at the time. The Icelandic government protested Germany's use of Norse mythology in its propaganda. Norwegian resistance fighters wore runic symbols as badges of anti-Nazi defiance (the "sol-runen" or sun-rune was used by the Norwegian resistance press). The Danish historian Vilhelm la Cour, writing in 1941, explicitly rejected the Nazi racial interpretation of the Viking Age: "The Vikings were not a race but a profession — anyone who went í víking was a víkingr, regardless of their ancestry."

The 2040 student exercise for this lecture is a "source critique" in which students analyze an Ahnenerbe archaeological report alongside a modern academic excavation report from the same site (for Haithabu, both are available in translation). Students identify: what did the Nazi archaeologists see and record correctly? what did they ignore or distort? how did their ideological framework shape their interpretation? The exercise teaches that even the most ideologically corrupted archaeology may contain recoverable data — and that the responsibility of the modern archaeologist is to separate the data from the ideology while never forgetting the context in which the data was collected.

## Lecture 6: Post-War Rehabilitation — Academic Viking Studies, 1945-2000

After 1945, Viking studies faced an existential crisis. The field had been so thoroughly contaminated by Nazi appropriation that any positive depiction of the Vikings risked being read as crypto-fascist nostalgia. The academic response was a deliberate turn toward empiricism — away from grand narratives of national character and toward the patient accumulation of archaeological data, philological analysis, and economic history.

**The Scandinavian Empiricist Turn:** The dominant figure in post-war Viking studies was not a charismatic theorist but a meticulous empiricist: the Danish archaeologist Johannes Brøndsted (1890-1965), whose three-volume *Danmarks Oldtid* ("Denmark's Prehistory," 1938-1940, revised 1957-1960) set the standard for Scandinavian archaeology for a generation. Brøndsted's *The Vikings* (1960, English translation 1965) was the first major English-language survey of the Viking Age to emerge from post-war Scandinavia, and it is notable for what it does not contain: no racial theory, no nationalist bombast, no speculation about "the Viking spirit." Instead, Brøndsted offered careful descriptions of artifacts, settlements, and economic networks — a deliberate, almost austere empiricism that was a conscious repudiation of the ideological archaeology of the 1930s and 1940s.

This empiricist tradition continues to dominate Scandinavian archaeology and shapes the training students receive in the UoY Viking Studies program. The emphasis on rigorous fieldwork methodology (see VS307), careful typological classification, and quantitative analysis is not merely a scientific preference — it is an ethical commitment to building knowledge that is resistant to ideological appropriation.

**The Annales School and Economic History:** Another significant post-war influence was the French Annales school of history, which emphasized long-term structural change (the *longue durée*) over political events and great men. Applied to the Viking Age, the Annales approach produced a re-interpretation of the Viking expansion as an economic phenomenon — a market response to population pressure, trade opportunities, and the demand for silver in the Islamic world, rather than an outburst of racial energy or national destiny.

The key text in this tradition is the Danish historian Niels Lund's work on the *lið* (the Viking ship-crew as an economic institution), which reconceptualized the Viking raids not as spontaneous acts of barbarism but as organized commercial ventures with shareholding structures, risk-pooling, and profit distribution. Similarly, the Norwegian historian Kåre Lunden's analysis of the Viking Age economy emphasized the role of the *skattr* (taxation) and trade in integrating Scandinavia into the European and Islamic economic systems. These economic interpretations remain central to the UoY curriculum; students encounter them in VS105 and VS107, and VS401 situates them historically as products of post-war empiricism.

**The Feminist Intervention:** Beginning in the 1970s and accelerating through the 1980s and 1990s, feminist archaeologists challenged the androcentric assumptions that had dominated Viking studies. The "male Viking warrior" paradigm — in which men went raiding and trading and exploring while women stayed home and wove — was systematically deconstructed. Key contributions: Elisabeth Arwill-Nordbladh's work on gender and material culture in Viking Age Scandinavia, which demonstrated that grave goods previously sexed as "male" (weapons) or "female" (jewelry) were not reliable indicators of biological sex; and Judith Jesch's *Women in the Viking Age* (1991), the first comprehensive survey of the evidence for women's roles in the Viking world.

The discovery in 2017 that the famous "Birka Warrior" (grave Bj 581) was biologically female — confirmed by genomic analysis — was both a vindication of decades of feminist critique and a demonstration that the empiricist tradition could produce transformative results. The Birka Warrior was not discovered by a revisionist reading of the evidence but by the application of a new empirical method (ancient DNA analysis) to an old question. This is the 2040 ideal of Viking studies: rigorous empiricism in service of revisionist conclusions.

**The Peter Sawyer Revolution:** The single most influential book in post-war Viking studies is Peter Sawyer's *The Age of the Vikings* (1962, revised 1971 and 1982). Sawyer, a British historian trained at Oxford, challenged nearly every assumption of the received Viking narrative: he argued that the Viking raids were smaller in scale than traditionally imagined (the "great armies" of the Anglo-Saxon Chronicle numbered in the hundreds, not the thousands); that the Vikings were motivated by profit rather than bloodlust; and that the primary sources (annals, chronicles, sagas) were so ideologically biased — written by the Vikings' Christian victims — that they could not be taken at face value. Sawyer's book was controversial at publication but is now the consensus position, and it is required reading in VS101.

## Lecture 7: Vikings on Screen — Film, Television, and Popular Culture

For most people in 2040, the Vikings are not archaeological data or saga texts. They are a visual brand — created and sustained by film, television, video games, and tourism marketing. The visual Viking is a product of the screen, and understanding how that product was manufactured is essential to understanding how the Viking past functions in the present.

**Hollywood's Vikings:** The Hollywood Viking film emerged in the 1950s as part of the broader genre of the historical epic (*The Robe*, *Quo Vadis*, *Ben-Hur*). Richard Fleischer's *The Vikings* (1958), starring Kirk Douglas and Tony Curtis, established the visual template that still dominates: the rugged, bearded warrior in leather and fur; the dragon-prowed longship cresting the waves; the mead-hall with its roaring fire and horned helmets (yes, horned — the film's historical advisor protested but was overruled by the director on aesthetic grounds). *The Vikings* was filmed on location in Norwegian fjords and at the reconstructed fortress of Fort-la-Latte in Brittany, and its use of authentic landscapes gave it a visual credibility that compensated for its historical inaccuracies.

The film's narrative — two half-brothers, one a Viking prince and one a captured English slave who does not know his true identity, competing for the same woman — is pure Hollywood. But its depiction of Viking society influenced public perception for decades: the Vikings as a warrior aristocracy, obsessed with honor and violence, living in a world of clear moral binaries. The film's most famous line, spoken by the one-eyed chieftain played by Ernest Borgnine as he dies: "Let the Valkyries carry me to Valhalla!" — a sentiment no historical Viking would have recognized in those terms, but which has become the definitive cinematic Viking death.

**The 21st-Century Television Wave:** The 2010s saw a dramatic expansion of Viking-themed television, driven by changes in the media landscape. The History Channel's *Vikings* (2013-2020), created by Michael Hirst, was the first long-form dramatic series to take the Viking Age as its primary subject. Over six seasons and 89 episodes, *Vikings* followed the legendary saga figure Ragnarr loðbrók and his descendants through raids on Lindisfarne (793) to the settlement of Iceland and the exploration of North America. The series drew heavily on the saga sources — particularly *Ragnars saga loðbrókar* and *Ragnarssona þáttr* — and its production design, supervised by Irish archaeologist Steve Fanning, was more historically informed than any previous screen depiction.

Yet *Vikings* also took substantial liberties. Ragnarr loðbrók is a composite figure, possibly legendary, whose historical existence is disputed. The series compressed events across two centuries into a single generation. The Old Norse dialogue was rendered in a mix of Old Norse, Old English, and fictionalized language, pleasing to the ear but inaccurate. And the series' treatment of religion — particularly its depiction of the Christian missionary Athelstan as a figure torn between faiths — reflected 21st-century concerns about religious pluralism and doubt more than 9th-century realities.

Netflix's *Vikings: Valhalla* (2022-2024), a sequel set a century after the original series, extended the brand into the Christianization era and the Norman Conquest. And Robert Eggers's *The Northman* (2022), a film adaptation of the *Amlethus* legend (the Scandinavian source for Shakespeare's *Hamlet*), represented the most historically rigorous screen depiction of the Viking Age to date — the film's production team consulted widely with archaeologists and historians, and the result is an immersive, violent, and myth-saturated vision of 10th-century Scandinavia that has become the reference point for 2040 screen Vikings.

**2040 Media Landscape:** By 2040, the screen Viking has proliferated across media platforms: immersive VR experiences (the UoY Media Lab's "Walk Through Birka" allows users to explore a reconstructed Viking Age town with AI-generated inhabitants who respond to questions), AI-generated "saga films" that dynamically adapt the narrative to viewer choices, and a thriving ecosystem of YouTube and social media creators who produce Viking content ranging from rigorous educational videos to sensationalist "Viking mysteries" clickbait. Students in VS401 analyze examples of each format, identifying the balance of scholarship and spectacle and tracing the lineage of creative decisions back through the century of screen Vikings that preceded them.

## Lecture 8: The Modern Heathen Revival — Reconstructing Norse Paganism

One of the most remarkable developments in the reception of the Viking Age has been the emergence of modern Norse Paganism — a religious movement that seeks to reconstruct, revive, or reimagine the pre-Christian religious traditions of Scandinavia. This movement is simultaneously a spiritual practice, a cultural project, and a complex field of identity politics that the 2040 student of Viking studies must understand.

**The Roots of Reconstruction:** Modern Norse Paganism emerged from several independent sources in the mid-20th century. In Iceland, the poet and farmer Sveinbjörn Beinteinsson founded the Ásatrúarfélagið ("Ásatrú Fellowship") in 1972, which was recognized as an official religious organization by the Icelandic government in 1973. Beinteinsson's Ásatrú was a gentle, nature-oriented spirituality built around the blót (ritual offering) to the Norse gods, the reading of the Eddic poems, and a deep connection to the Icelandic landscape. It was explicitly non-racist and inclusive — Beinteinsson himself was a small, soft-spoken man who published poetry about sheep and was as far from the Viking warrior stereotype as it is possible to imagine.

In the United States, Ásatrú developed along different lines. The Viking Brotherhood (later the Ásatrú Free Assembly), founded by Stephen McNallen in 1972, combined Norse religious reconstruction with a "folkish" ideology that restricted membership to those of Northern European descent — a position that McNallen explicitly distinguished from racism but that critics (and many Heathens) identify as functionally identical. The "universalist" versus "folkish" split remains the central fault line within modern Heathenry, and the UoY course devotes substantial attention to the theological, political, and ethical dimensions of this debate.

**Theological Reconstruction vs. Innovation:** All reconstructive religions face the same fundamental challenge: the historical record is fragmentary, and the gaps must be filled. Modern Heathens have access to the *Poetic Edda*, the *Prose Edda*, the sagas, the runic inscriptions, the archaeological record, and comparative Indo-European mythology — but these sources answer only a fraction of the questions that a living religion must address. What is the proper form of a blót? How should the gods be addressed? What is the relationship between the individual and the community? The historical sources are often silent or contradictory.

The 2040 Heathen community's response to this challenge ranges across a spectrum. At one end are strict reconstructionists who attempt to reproduce Viking Age religious practice as closely as the evidence permits — this includes experimental archaeology, in which reconstructed Viking Age tools and techniques are used to prepare ritual offerings. At the other end are eclectic practitioners who draw freely on New Age spirituality, Wicca, shamanism, and personal revelation, creating a modern syncretic practice that would be unrecognizable to a 10th-century Scandinavian but is spiritually meaningful to its adherents.

The UoY course does not take a position on the theological validity of any form of modern Heathenry — that is not the university's role. But it does analyze the movement sociologically and historically: How do Heathens use archaeological evidence to authorize their practices? How do they negotiate the gaps and contradictions in the source record? How do they relate to academic Viking studies — as allies, as critics, as consumers of scholarly research? The relationship between academic Viking studies and the modern Heathen community is complex: many Heathens are deeply knowledgeable about the historical sources and support academic research; others view academics as gatekeepers who have no right to define "authentic" practice for a living religious community.

**The 2040 Heathen Landscape:** In 2040, Heathenry is a recognized minority religion in all the Scandinavian countries, with legal protections, registration procedures, and (in some cases) state funding comparable to other religious organizations. The Icelandic Ásatrúarfélagið has approximately 8,000 members (about 2% of Iceland's population). The Swedish Samfundet Forn Sed Sverige ("The Old Custom Community of Sweden") has approximately 5,000 members. These numbers are modest in absolute terms, but they represent the first time in nearly a thousand years that people have gathered in Scandinavia to honor Óðinn, Þórr, Freyja, and the other Norse gods as living deities rather than literary characters.

The course also addresses the challenges that modern Heathenry faces in 2040: the persistent influence of folkish/racist elements (though these are a minority, they are vocal and their use of Norse symbolism for hate continually compromises the legitimacy of the broader movement); the tension between institutionalization and anti-authoritarian pagan ethos (official recognition requires bureaucracy that many Heathens find antithetical to their spirituality); and the question of cultural ownership (is Norse Paganism exclusively for people of Scandinavian descent, or is it open to all? The universalist position — dominant in 2040 — holds that the gods call whom they will, regardless of ancestry, but the folkish position persists).

## Lecture 9: The Viking in Video Games, Comics, and Digital Culture

The most popular Viking of the 21st and 22nd centuries is not a historical figure or a saga hero but a digital avatar. Video games have become the primary medium through which global audiences encounter the Viking Age, and the volume of Viking-themed entertainment produced by the gaming industry dwarfs all other media combined. Understanding how games construct the Viking past — and how players interact with those constructions — is a central concern of 2040 reception studies.

**Interactive Vikings — The Game Medium:** The video game differs from film and literature in a crucial respect: the player is not a passive spectator but an active participant. The Viking game does not merely show the player a Viking — it allows the player to be one. This interactivity creates a qualitatively different relationship to the past: the player does not learn about the Viking Age so much as inhabit a version of it. The ethical implications are significant. When a player in *Assassin's Creed: Valhalla* (Ubisoft, 2020) — still the best-selling Viking game of all time in 2040, with over 20 million copies sold — raids a monastery, burns a village, and kills civilians, the game encourages the player to identify with the Viking raider and to experience violence as entertainment. The historical trauma inflicted on the victims of Viking raids is rendered invisible.

*Assassin's Creed: Valhalla* is an interesting case because of its "Discovery Tour" mode — a non-violent, educational mode in which players can explore a meticulously reconstructed 9th-century England and Norway, with guided tours narrated by historians and archaeologists. The Discovery Tour represents an attempt to bridge the gap between entertainment and education, but it also raises uncomfortable questions: does the educational mode legitimize the violence of the main game? Can the same platform be both a classroom and a playground for simulated atrocity? The UoY course asks students to engage critically with these questions, using Valhalla as a case study in the ethics of historical video games.

**The Fantasy Viking — Norse Mythology in Gaming:** Beyond historically-themed games, Norse mythology has become a default fantasy setting for an entire genre of games. Sony Santa Monica's *God of War* (2018) and *God of War: Ragnarök* (2022) transplanted the series' protagonist, the Spartan warrior Kratos, into a world of Norse gods, giants, and monsters. The games take extreme liberties with the mythology — Óðinn is portrayed as a paranoid tyrant, Baldr as a sadistic villain, and the entire mythic cycle of ragna rǫk is compressed and rewritten — but they also demonstrate an engagement with the source material that is, in some respects, more sophisticated than many popular treatments: the games' depiction of the World Serpent, Jǫrmungandr, draws on the Eddic account of Þórr's fishing expedition; the runic magic system is loosely based on historical rune lore; and the game world is structured around Yggdrasill, the world-tree, with its nine realms accessible through the Bifrǫst.

Smaller indie games have explored the Norse material with greater historical fidelity. *Hellblade: Senua's Sacrifice* (Ninja Theory, 2017) — a game about a Pictish warrior experiencing psychosis — draws on Norse mythology for its depiction of the "Northmen" who raid Senua's village and for its exploration of trauma, death, and the afterlife. The game was developed in consultation with neuroscientists and people with lived experience of psychosis, and its representation of mental illness is widely regarded as a landmark in the medium.

**The 2040 Game Landscape:** By 2040, Viking-themed gaming has expanded into new formats: fully immersive VR experiences in which players physically swing axes and row longships; AI-driven narrative games that generate unique saga-style stories based on player choices and personality profiles; and augmented-reality experiences that overlay Viking Age reconstructions onto real world landscapes (walk through modern York and see Jórvík reconstructed around you through your AR glasses). The UoY Game Lab is developing an educational AR game, "Goði," in which players manage a Viking Age chieftaincy — balancing raiding, trading, farming, and ritual — with outcomes determined by both historical data models and player decisions.

## Lecture 10: Heritage Tourism, the Viking Brand, and Commercialization

The Viking is a commercial product. From the Viking Ship Museum in Oslo (1.2 million visitors annually in 2039) to the "Viking Experience" dinner theater in York to the plastic horned helmets sold at every Scandinavian airport gift shop, the Viking Age generates billions of euros in annual revenue. Heritage tourism is the economic engine that drives much of the public's engagement with the Viking past, and the tension between scholarly accuracy and commercial viability is one of the defining challenges of 2040 Viking heritage management.

**The Living History Museum:** The "living history" approach — in which costumed interpreters demonstrate crafts, cook food, and engage visitors in character as Viking Age people — is the dominant mode of Viking heritage presentation. The Ribe VikingeCenter in Denmark (founded 1992), the Lofotr Viking Museum in Norway (opened 1995 as the largest reconstructed Viking longhouse ever built), and the Jorvik Viking Centre in York (opened 1984, rebuilt after flooding in 2015) are the exemplars. These sites attract millions of visitors and have transformed the economies of their host communities.

The interpretive challenges are substantial. The living history interpreter must simultaneously entertain and educate, must make the past accessible without falsifying it, and must navigate visitor expectations that are shaped by film and television rather than by scholarship. The visitor who arrives expecting horned helmets and bloodthirsty berserkers must be gently guided toward a more nuanced understanding without being made to feel foolish or disappointed. The Ribe center's approach — which UoY students study in detail — is exemplary: interpreters acknowledge popular misconceptions with good humor ("Yes, the horned helmets look dramatic, don't they? Let me tell you where that image actually comes from...") and pivot to authentic material culture.

**The Viking Brand:** The word "Viking" has become a global brand signifying ruggedness, adventure, authenticity, and Nordic quality. It sells cars (the Swedish "Viking" tires), kitchen appliances, beer (dozens of brands worldwide), clothing, survival gear, and financial services. The brand operates independently of historical accuracy — the consumer buying Viking-branded outdoor equipment is not purchasing a historical experience but a set of associations (strength, freedom, masculinity, connection to nature).

The commodification of the Viking past raises ethical questions that the course addresses. Who profits from the Viking brand? The communities whose ancestors were raided by Vikings receive no royalties from Viking-themed products. The Sami, whose material culture is sometimes conflated with "Viking" in tourist marketing, have protested the appropriation of their heritage. And the modern Scandinavian welfare states that fund much Viking heritage infrastructure are among the least "Viking" societies imaginable — peaceful, egalitarian, bureaucratic, and thoroughly post-heroic. The gap between the brand and the historical reality is the subject, not the obstacle, of this course.

**Digital Tourism in 2040:** By 2040, heritage tourism has been transformed by digital technology. The UoY "Virtual Jorvik" project (2038) allows anyone with a VR headset to walk through 10th-century York in photorealistic 3D, with AI-generated inhabitants who speak reconstructed Old Norse and Old English, responsive to user queries. The project raises the same ethical questions as the video games discussed in Lecture 9 — is a VR recreation that erases the trauma of the Viking occupation of Northumbria (the "great heathen army" of 865-878 caused massive displacement and suffering) guilty of sanitizing the past? The project's developers addressed this by including "shadow layers" in the VR experience — a toggle that shifts the scene from the idealized reconstruction to a "trauma layer" showing burned buildings, displaced families, and monastic ruins. The visitor can choose to see the Viking Age as either triumph or catastrophe — or, ideally, to toggle between both perspectives and understand that it was both.

## Lecture 11: Neo-Nazi Appropriation and Anti-Racist Reclamation in the 21st Century

The lecture on Nazi appropriation (Lecture 5) covered the period 1920-1945. This lecture addresses the afterlife of that appropriation in the 21st century and the 2040 present — the ongoing struggle between neo-Nazi and white supremacist groups who continue to use Norse symbolism, and anti-racist movements (including many Heathens, academics, and heritage professionals) who work to reclaim those symbols for inclusive, non-racist purposes.

**The Persistence of Hate Symbols:** In 2040, the runic symbols appropriated by the Nazis continue to be used by neo-Nazi and white supremacist groups globally. The "odal" rune (ᛟ), the "sig" rune (ᛋ), the *Wolfsangel*, the *Schwarze Sonne* (Black Sun), and the Týr rune (ᛏ) all appear in the insignia of hate groups, on extremist online forums, and in the manifestos of mass shooters. The 2040 European Union maintains an updated registry of hate symbols, and several of these runes are classified alongside the swastika as prohibited imagery in several member states.

The effect on academic and cultural use of runes is chilling. A 2038 survey of Scandinavian museums found that 40% had removed runic imagery from their public-facing marketing materials due to concerns about being misperceived as promoting hate ideology. The UoY's own runology courses now include a mandatory module on "runes and hate" that prepares students for the experience of seeing symbols they study academically being used for political violence. The course does not shy away from this reality: students are shown examples of neo-Nazi runic imagery (with content warnings) and guided through an analysis of how these groups construct a pseudo-historical justification for their ideology.

**The Anti-Racist Heathen Movement:** Parallel to — and in active opposition to — the neo-Nazi use of Norse symbolism, a vigorous anti-racist Heathen movement has developed. Organizations like Heathens Against Hate (founded 2016), the Declaration 127 movement (named for a 13th-century Icelandic law prohibiting duels — a call for Heathens to settle disputes peacefully), and the Troth (the largest inclusive Heathen organization in North America, founded 1987) actively work to exclude racists from Heathen spaces and to promote an inclusive vision of Norse Paganism.

The "Hammer of Thor" (Mjǫllnir) as a symbol illustrates the contested nature of Norse iconography in 2040. For the neo-Nazi, Mjǫllnir is a symbol of white power and racial purity. For the anti-racist Heathen, it is a symbol of protection, community, and resistance to oppression — some anti-racist Heathens display Mjǫllnir pendants in rainbow colors or alongside anti-fascist symbols. The same object, the same historical referent, carries diametrically opposed meanings depending on the community of interpretation. This polysemy — the capacity of symbols to mean different things to different people — is a central theoretical concept of the course.

**Academic Responsibility:** The UoY faculty takes the position that academic Viking studies has a responsibility to actively oppose neo-Nazi appropriation. This is not a neutral scholarly position — it is an ethical commitment grounded in the history covered in Lecture 5. The department's public statement (posted on all course websites): "We study the Viking Age as a diverse, interconnected period of human history. We reject attempts to use Viking heritage to promote racism, nationalism, or exclusion. The Norse world extended from Greenland to Baghdad; its people traded, settled, and intermarried across ethnic and cultural boundaries."

Students are encouraged to engage critically with this statement — is it the role of a university to take political positions? Should scholarship remain neutral? These questions are debated in the course's discussion forums, and students are evaluated on the quality of their arguments, not on their agreement with the department's position. But the faculty's view is clear: the history of Nazi appropriation of Viking studies is too catastrophic to permit scholarly neutrality.

## Lecture 12: The Viking in 2040 — AI, Immersion, and the Future of the Past

The final lecture looks forward. What will the Viking Age mean in 2080, in 2120, in the centuries that follow? How are the reception dynamics of the present — the digital mediation of the past, the commercialization of heritage, the political contestation of symbols, the religious reconstruction of practice — shaping the Vikings that future generations will inherit?

**AI-Generated Heritage:** The most significant development of the 2030s has been the application of generative AI to heritage reconstruction. The UoY's "Saga Generator" (2035) is a large language model trained on the entire corpus of Old Norse literature that can generate new saga-style narratives indistinguishable from genuine medieval texts by non-specialist readers. An AI-generated "lost saga" — *Hrafnkels saga hins nýja* ("The New Saga of Hrafnkell") — composed by the Saga Generator in 2037, fooled several amateur saga enthusiasts into believing it was a newly discovered manuscript. The academic community identified it as AI-generated within hours, but the incident raised questions that the course addresses: if an AI can generate new "sagas" that are linguistically and narratively consistent with the medieval corpus, what is the status of these texts? Are they forgeries? Creative works? New contributions to the saga tradition? And what happens to the public's relationship with the past when they can no longer distinguish between historical artifact and AI generation?

The UoY position: AI-generated content must be clearly labeled as such. The Saga Generator includes an invisible watermark in every output. Any course assignment that uses AI assistance must disclose it. But the larger question — what happens to the concept of "authenticity" in a world of AI-generated heritage — remains open, and VS401 students are encouraged to articulate their own positions.

**Immersive Heritage and the End of Distance:** The trend toward immersive, interactive engagement with the past (VR, AR, AI-driven experiences, living history) narrows the distance between the present and the past. In 2040, a student can put on a VR headset and walk through a photorealistic reconstruction of 9th-century Birka, hear Old Norse spoken around them, watch a blót being performed, and (if the simulation permits) interact with the inhabitants. This is a qualitatively different relationship to the past than reading about it in a textbook or looking at artifacts in a museum case.

The benefits are obvious: immersion generates emotional engagement and understanding that passive study cannot. The risks are less obvious but equally real: immersion encourages the illusion that we can "know" the past directly, without the mediation of sources and the limitations of evidence. The VR Birka is not the historical Birka — it is a reconstruction based on the best available evidence, but that evidence is incomplete, and every gap has been filled by a designer's imagination. The immersive experience, by making the reconstruction feel real, may suppress the user's awareness of how much is unknown.

**Climate Change and the Vanishing Record:** The most urgent challenge facing Viking heritage in 2040 is climate change. Rising sea levels threaten coastal sites (Haithabu, Birka, Kaupang, the Skuldelev barrier). Thawing permafrost in Greenland is exposing — and destroying — the organic remains of the Norse Eastern and Western Settlements. Increased storm frequency damages runestones exposed to the elements. Acidification of Scandinavian soils continues to dissolve bone and metal. The archaeological record is degrading, and every year, sites that could have been excavated in 2040 will be lost.

The heritage sector's response —"rescue archaeology," prioritizing excavation of the most threatened sites — is a form of triage that demands difficult choices. Which sites are saved? Which are allowed to disappear? Who decides? These are not abstract questions for VS401 students; they are the professional reality they will face as working archaeologists and heritage managers.

**Conclusion: The Viking as Mirror:** The central argument of this course — the thesis toward which every lecture has been building — is that the Vikings are a mirror. Every generation constructs the Vikings it needs: the 19th century needed nationalist heroes to justify new nation-states; the early 20th century needed racial ancestors to justify genocide; the late 20th century needed economic actors to justify a materialist historiography; the 21st century needs content for its global entertainment industry. The Vikings themselves — the people who lived and died in Scandinavia between roughly 750 and 1100 CE — are inaccessible to us except through the fragmentary evidence they left behind and the centuries of interpretation that have accumulated on top of that evidence like strata on an archaeological site.

The ethical obligation of the Viking Studies graduate in 2040 is not to strip away those strata to reveal some "authentic" Viking underneath — that project is impossible. It is to understand the strata themselves: to see clearly how each generation's Vikings reflect that generation's needs, fears, and desires, and to be honest about which generation's Vikings we are constructing in our own work. The mirror shows us ourselves. The discipline of Viking reception studies is learning to recognize our own reflection.
