# VS405: Capstone Thesis Seminar II
## Bachelor of Arts in Viking Studies — University of Yggdrasil, 2040

**Credits:** 4
**Description:** The second capstone seminar in the Viking Studies thesis sequence. Students complete, revise, and defend a substantial original research thesis (10,000-12,000 words) on a Viking Studies topic of their choice. The seminar provides structured support for the writing process: advanced argumentation, data presentation, revision strategies, and oral defense preparation. Each student works with a faculty advisor and participates in weekly peer-review workshops. The completed thesis represents the culmination of the four-year Viking Studies program.

**Prerequisites:** VS401, VS403, all 100- and 200-level VS courses, plus at least two 300-level VS courses
**Corequisite:** None; this is the terminal course in the program

## Lecture 1: From Proposal to Thesis — Revisiting the Research Design

VS405 builds directly on the capstone research proposal developed in VS403. The first task of the thesis seminar is to revisit that proposal — not as a completed document to be executed mechanically, but as a living plan that will evolve as the research progresses. The most productive theses often depart significantly from their original proposals, because the research process inevitably reveals evidence, questions, and complications that the proposal could not have anticipated.

**Revisiting the Research Question:** The first session of the seminar is devoted to revisiting the research question. Each student presents their revised question to the seminar group, and the group (faculty and peers) interrogates it: Is it still specific enough? Is it still answerable with the available evidence? Has the literature review revealed that the question has already been answered — or that a better, more interesting question lies adjacent to the original one?

The most common failure mode at this stage is the question that is too broad. "What was the role of women in the Viking Age?" cannot be answered in a 10,000-word thesis — it would require a multi-volume monograph. The seminar's role is to force narrowing: "What can the oval brooch assemblages from three mid-10th-century farmsteads in southwestern Iceland tell us about women's participation in textile production and exchange?" That question is specific, evidence-based, and answerable within the thesis format.

**Revising the Evidence Base:** The proposal's evidence section must be revisited in light of what the student has actually found. Are the proposed sources accessible? Have new sources been discovered? Have some sources proven less informative than anticipated? The seminar provides training in evidence triage: the difficult skill of abandoning evidence that, however interesting, does not directly address the research question. A thesis is not a comprehensive survey of everything the student knows about a topic — it is a focused argument that selects evidence strategically.

**The Thesis as Argument:** The most important intellectual transition in the capstone seminar is from the thesis as a report ("here is what I found out about X") to the thesis as an argument ("here is my claim about X, and here is the evidence that supports it"). Every thesis, regardless of topic, must have a thesis statement — a single sentence (typically appearing at the end of the introduction) that articulates the argument the entire document exists to support. The thesis statement must be: (1) contestable — someone could reasonably disagree with it; (2) specific — it makes a precise claim, not a vague generalization; (3) significant — it matters for understanding the Viking Age.

**The 2040 Thesis Toolkit:** Students in VS405 have access to the full suite of UoY digital research tools (Viking Evidence Platform, SagaSearch, RuneReader AI, RuneBase, LitScan) introduced in VS403. By this point in the program, students are expected to be fluent in these tools — not merely using them but critically evaluating their outputs and integrating them with traditional scholarly methods. The seminar devotes one session to troubleshooting digital research problems: corrupted datasets, AI-generated false positives, software compatibility issues, and the perennial challenge of citation formats for born-digital sources.

## Lecture 2: Argumentation and Structure — Building the Skeleton of the Thesis

A thesis is a complex document, and its structure must be deliberate. The reader should be able to follow the argument from beginning to end without confusion, and each section should have a clear function in advancing that argument. The "skeleton" of the thesis — the outline of sections and the logical relationships between them — must be constructed before the flesh of prose is written.

**The Standard Thesis Structure:** The Viking Studies thesis typically follows a variant of the IMRAD structure (Introduction, Methods, Results, and Discussion), adapted for humanities and interdisciplinary research:

1. **Introduction (10-15% of total):** The research question, the thesis statement, the significance of the question, the structure of the argument, and a brief road map of the chapters to follow. The introduction should hook the reader — open with a specific, evocative detail (an artifact, a saga passage, an archaeological puzzle) that embodies the larger question.

2. **Literature Review / Background (15-20%):** A critical synthesis of the relevant scholarship, organized thematically rather than as an annotated bibliography. The literature review should establish what is known, what is contested, and what gap the thesis fills. It should not summarize every publication on the topic; it should select works strategically to set up the thesis argument.

3. **Methodology (10%):** An explanation of how the evidence was collected and analyzed, written at a level of detail sufficient for another researcher to replicate the study. The methodology chapter should justify the choice of methods (why these and not others?) and acknowledge limitations (what can't these methods tell us?).

4. **Evidence Presentation / Analysis (30-40%):** The core of the thesis. The evidence is presented and analyzed in a logical sequence that builds toward the argument. Depending on the discipline and topic, this may be divided into multiple chapters (one per site, one per evidence type, one per chronological phase). Each chapter should have its own mini-argument that contributes to the overall thesis.

5. **Discussion / Synthesis (15-20%):** The evidence is synthesized into an argument. The discussion chapter answers the "so what?" question: what does the evidence mean? how does it change our understanding of the Viking Age? It should explicitly return to the research question and thesis statement from the introduction, demonstrating how the evidence supports (or modifies) the argument.

6. **Conclusion (5-10%):** A brief summary of findings, a restatement of the thesis argument, an acknowledgment of limitations, and suggestions for future research. The conclusion should not introduce new evidence or arguments; it should close the circle opened by the introduction.

**Alternative Structures:** The standard structure is a guideline, not a straitjacket. Some theses — particularly those that are heavily text-based or comparative — may benefit from a thematic structure (each chapter addressing a different theme that cuts across chronological or geographical divisions) or a case-study structure (each chapter a detailed case study, with comparison across chapters in the discussion). The seminar encourages students to experiment with structure during the outlining phase, and the peer-review workshops provide feedback on whether the chosen structure serves the argument.

**The Outline as Diagnostic Tool:** Students are required to submit a detailed outline (1,000-1,500 words, with paragraph-level topics and estimated word counts for each section) by Week 3. The outline is not a contract — it will change as the writing progresses — but it is a diagnostic tool. An outline that cannot articulate the logical relationship between sections is a warning that the argument is not yet clear in the student's mind. An outline with a sagging middle (the evidence chapters lack clear argumentative direction) is a warning that the research question may need to be sharpened. The faculty advisor and peer reviewers use the outline to identify structural problems before the student has invested weeks in writing prose that will need to be restructured.

## Lecture 3: Working with Evidence — From Data to Argument

The heart of the thesis is the evidence. But evidence does not speak for itself — it must be selected, presented, analyzed, and interpreted. The transition from "here is my data" to "here is what my data means" is the most intellectually demanding move in the thesis, and the seminar devotes substantial attention to it.

**Selecting Evidence:** Not everything the student has found belongs in the thesis. The most common error in undergraduate theses is the compulsion to include everything — every artifact, every saga passage, every isotopic result — whether or not it directly supports the argument. The result is a bloated evidence chapter that buries the argument under a landslide of data.

The selection principle: each piece of evidence included in the thesis must perform a specific argumentative function. It must either: (a) directly support a claim, (b) illustrate a pattern, (c) provide a counterexample that the argument must address, or (d) establish the context necessary for the reader to understand the argument. Evidence that does not perform one of these functions — however interesting — belongs in an appendix or in a future publication, not in the thesis body.

**Presenting Evidence:** The form of evidence presentation must match the nature of the evidence and the needs of the argument.

- **Archaeological evidence:** Plans, sections, photographs, distribution maps, artifact illustrations, data tables. The standard for archaeological illustration is high — students are expected to produce publication-quality figures using the UoY digital tools (GIS, photogrammetry, Illustrator/Draw). A hand-sketched artifact drawing is acceptable only if the student can demonstrate that digital methods are inappropriate (e.g., for objects too fragile for photogrammetry).

- **Textual evidence:** Quotations in Old Norse with English translations (the standard format: normalized Old Norse text in italics, followed by the student's own translation in quotation marks, with a footnote citing the edition). Extended saga passages should be summarized with key quotations selected. The student must decide whether to use the original language or rely on published translations — the UoY standard is to use original language where the student's language skills permit, always providing a translation for the non-specialist reader.

- **Scientific evidence:** Isotope data tables, radiocarbon calibration curves, aDNA principal component analysis plots, lipid residue chromatograms. These must be presented in a format accessible to the non-specialist reader. A table of δ¹³C and δ¹⁵N values is meaningless without an explanation of what these values indicate about diet. A PCA plot of aDNA data is incomprehensible without a key explaining what the axes represent and what the clustering pattern means.

**Analyzing Evidence:** Analysis is the bridge between presentation and interpretation. It is the step in which the researcher demonstrates that the evidence supports the claim, rather than merely asserting it. For archaeological evidence, analysis includes: spatial patterning (is the distribution of finds clustered, random, or dispersed? what does the pattern indicate about activity areas?), quantitative comparison (are the frequencies of artifact types statistically different between contexts?), and stratigraphic association (which contexts are contemporary? which are earlier/later?). For textual evidence, analysis includes: close reading of specific passages, comparison of parallel accounts in different sources, source-critical evaluation of reliability, and contextual reading against the archaeological record.

**The "Negative Evidence" Problem:** What is not found is often as significant as what is found — but negative evidence must be handled carefully. The absence of evidence is not necessarily evidence of absence. A farmstead excavation that finds no weapons may indicate a peaceful settlement — or it may indicate that weapons were curated (passed down, not buried), deposited off-site, or simply not recovered due to excavation sampling. The responsible thesis acknowledges the limits of negative evidence: "No weapons were recovered from the excavated area, which covered approximately 60% of the estimated floor area of the longhouse. This absence is suggestive but does not preclude the presence of weapons in unexcavated deposits, nor can it exclude the possibility that weapons were removed from the site before abandonment."

## Lecture 4: Writing the Viking Studies Thesis — Voice, Style, and the Craft of Academic Prose

The content of the thesis is only as good as the prose that conveys it. A brilliant argument expressed in unclear, jargon-laden, or error-riddled prose fails to persuade — and a clear, elegant argument expressed in graceful prose can elevate even modest evidence. The seminar includes a sustained focus on the craft of academic writing, conducted through weekly writing workshops in which students critique each other's drafts.

**Finding Your Scholarly Voice:** Every academic writer develops a voice — a distinctive way of writing that reflects their intellectual personality. The undergraduate thesis is often where this voice first emerges. The seminar encourages students to experiment: try writing the same paragraph in different registers (formal/academic, accessible/public, analytical/detached, narrative/engaged) and compare the effects. The instructor's role is to help each student find the register that best serves their argument — not to impose a single "correct" academic style.

That said, certain conventions govern Viking Studies prose, and students are expected to observe them:

- **Old Norse orthography:** Use proper characters (ǫ, ð, þ, æ, œ, ǽ). Use nominative forms when referring to individuals (Eiríkr inn rauði, not "Erik the Red" except in the first instance with the Old Norse in parentheses). Italicize Old Norse words on first use with a gloss (or translation in parentheses), then use the Old Norse term without italics for subsequent mentions.

- **Citation style:** The Department uses the *Scandinavian Studies* citation format (Chicago-based, with footnotes for first citation and short-form for subsequent citations). All students are provided with the style guide and a Zotero template.

- **Dates:** Use "CE" (Common Era) rather than "AD," except when quoting sources that use "AD." Radiocarbon dates should be reported as "calibrated years CE" with the laboratory code, uncalibrated date, and calibrated range at 2σ (95.4% confidence).

**The Paragraph as Argumentative Unit:** Each paragraph in the thesis should make a single point. The point should be stated in a topic sentence (typically the first sentence), supported with evidence in the body of the paragraph, and connected to the larger argument in a concluding sentence or transition. A paragraph that contains multiple unrelated points should be split. A paragraph that does not advance the argument should be cut. The "reverse outline" technique — after writing a draft, extract the topic sentence from each paragraph and read them in sequence — is the most powerful diagnostic tool for structural problems: if the sequence of topic sentences does not tell a coherent story, the structure needs revision.

**Managing Old Norse Quotations:** The thesis will likely contain quotations from Old Norse sources. The standard format: the normalized Old Norse text in italics, followed by the student's own English translation in quotation marks (or, for longer passages, as a block quotation without quotation marks). If the student's Old Norse is not sufficient for independent translation, published translations may be used with full citation, and the student should acknowledge the translator in a footnote. Machine translation (AI) of Old Norse is not permitted for thesis work without explicit advisor approval — current AI translation tools for Old Norse have error rates that are unacceptable for scholarly work.

**The 2040 Writing Toolkit:** Students have access to the UoY "VikingThesis" LaTeX template (which handles all formatting, citation, and orthography automatically) and the "WriteWell" AI writing assistant. WriteWell is an AI tool that provides feedback on prose — not content generation, but style diagnostics: passive voice overuse, sentence length variation, jargon density, readability scores, and consistency of terminology. The tool is explicitly a diagnostic, not an editor; it identifies potential problems for the student to address, but it does not rewrite prose. The UoY AI ethics policy requires that WriteWell use be disclosed in the thesis acknowledgments.

## Lecture 5: Figures, Tables, and Visual Argument — Communicating Through Images

In a discipline that depends heavily on visual evidence — artifact photographs, site plans, distribution maps, runestone drawings — the quality of the thesis's figures directly affects the persuasiveness of its argument. A well-designed figure can communicate a pattern that would require paragraphs of prose to describe. A poorly designed figure can confuse the reader and undermine the credibility of the research. The seminar includes a session with the UoY Visual Resources Librarian covering the standards for thesis figures.

**Map Design Principles:** Every map in a Viking Studies thesis should include: a scale bar (not a ratio — "1:10,000" means nothing without a ruler), a north arrow, a legend that explains every symbol and color used, and a caption that describes what the map shows and its relevance to the argument. Maps should use a consistent visual language across the thesis — the same symbol for settlement sites, the same color for water features, the same scale and projection where possible.

The most common map errors in student theses: (1) the "floating site" — dots indicating site locations without base map features (coastlines, rivers, elevation) that would allow the reader to understand the spatial context; (2) the "garish palette" — overly saturated colors that obscure the data and strain the eye; (3) the "missing legend" — symbols and colors that are never explained; (4) the "GIS default" — accepting the software's default symbology and layout without customization. Every map should be interrogated: does a reader unfamiliar with the site understand what they are looking at?

**Data Visualization:** Tables are for precise values; charts are for patterns. A table listing the strontium isotope ratios for 50 individuals is appropriate; a bar chart comparing the mean strontium values across five sites is appropriate. A bar chart showing 50 individual values is inappropriate — it is neither precise enough to recover the data nor clear enough to show the pattern. The principle: choose the visualization that communicates the argument most effectively, not the one the software produces by default.

For statistical data, error bars and confidence intervals must be shown. A mean value without an indication of variability (standard deviation, standard error, confidence interval) is misleading — it implies precision that does not exist. Radiocarbon dates must be shown as probability distributions (the familiar bell-shaped curves from calibration), not as single-point "dates."

**Photographs and Photogrammetry:** Artifact photographs should be taken against a neutral background (the UoY photographic standard: 18% gray card background, scale bar, color calibration target included in the frame). 3D photogrammetric models should be presented as selected still views from the interactive model, with the URL or QR code linking to the full model in the UoY digital repository. The student must decide whether a photograph or a drawing better serves the argument — photographs capture surface detail and color; drawings can emphasize diagnostic features and omit distracting damage or encrustation.

**Captions and Integration:** Every figure must have a caption that does more than label. A good caption tells the reader what to notice and why it matters: "Figure 3. Distribution of Insular metalwork in Norwegian graves, 800-950 CE. The concentration in Vestlandet (circled) corresponds to the region of documented Viking activity in Ireland and western Scotland, suggesting that these objects reached Norway as loot or trade goods from the British Isles rather than through indirect exchange networks." The caption makes the figure's argumentative function explicit.

Figures must be integrated into the text — referenced by number in the relevant paragraph, placed as close to the reference as layout permits, and discussed in the prose. A figure that appears without textual reference ("see Figure 4" with no explanation) is a failure of integration. The figure and the text should work together; neither should be comprehensible without the other.

## Lecture 6: Revision Strategies — From First Draft to Final Submission

First drafts are always bad. This is not a sign of failure — it is the nature of the writing process. The difference between a competent thesis and an excellent thesis is not the quality of the first draft but the quality and thoroughness of the revision. The seminar teaches systematic revision strategies that transform a rough draft into a polished final product.

**The Revision Hierarchy:** Not all revisions are equal. Structural revision (reorganizing sections, cutting or adding chapters, rethinking the argument) must precede paragraph-level revision (rewriting individual paragraphs, improving transitions). Paragraph-level revision must precede sentence-level revision (tightening prose, fixing grammar, varying sentence structure). Sentence-level revision must precede copyediting (spelling, punctuation, citation format). Reversing this order — polishing individual sentences before ensuring the argument is sound — is the most common and most wasteful revision error.

The seminar imposes a revision schedule:
- **Week 7-8: Structural Revision.** Students submit a complete draft to their advisor and peer group. Feedback focuses exclusively on argument, evidence, and structure — not on prose. The student produces a revision plan identifying the major changes needed.
- **Week 9-10: Paragraph and Section Revision.** The restructured draft is revised at the paragraph level. Each section is reviewed for internal coherence, transitions, and the clarity of its argumentative contribution.
- **Week 11-12: Sentence-Level Revision and Copyediting.** The polished draft is read aloud (the most effective way to catch awkward phrasing, repetition, and rhythm problems). Citation checking, figure formatting, and final formatting are completed.
- **Week 13: Final Review.** The advisor reads the near-final draft and provides final feedback. The student makes final corrections.
- **Week 14: Submission.**

**Peer Review Protocol:** The seminar's weekly peer-review workshops are the engine of the revision process. Each week, two students distribute a section of their thesis (2,000-3,000 words) to the group. The group reads the section in advance and prepares written feedback organized around four questions: (1) What is the argument of this section? (state it in your own words; if you can't, the section is unclear); (2) What is the strongest piece of evidence, and why is it strong? (3) What is the weakest point, and what would strengthen it? (4) What one change would most improve this section?

The peer review protocol is strict: feedback must be specific ("the transition between paragraphs 3 and 4 is unclear because paragraph 3 ends with trade networks and paragraph 4 begins with burial practice without connecting the two") and constructive ("consider adding a sentence that explains how trade networks affected burial practice — e.g., 'The influx of Insular metalwork through trade networks transformed burial assemblages in Vestlandet...'"). Vague feedback ("this needs work") is not accepted. Personal criticism ("this is badly written") is not accepted. The instructor models effective peer review in the first sessions and monitors the workshops to ensure the protocol is followed.

**Common Revision Problems and Solutions:**

- **The "Literature Review Dump":** A literature review that summarizes every publication the student has read without organizing them into an argument. Solution: reorganize thematically — group sources by the positions they take on the key debates, not by author or date.

- **The "Disappearing Argument":** An introduction that states a clear thesis, followed by evidence chapters that don't return to the thesis. Solution: at the end of each evidence chapter, add a paragraph that explicitly connects the chapter's findings to the thesis argument.

- **The "Data without Analysis":** Evidence presented as raw data (tables, lists, photographs) without interpretation. Solution: for every piece of evidence, ask: "What does this mean? Why does it matter? How does it advance the argument?" If you can't answer, the evidence doesn't belong.

- **The "Conclusion That Goes Nowhere":** A conclusion that merely summarizes without offering new insight. Solution: the conclusion should extend the argument — suggest implications, identify limitations, propose future research.

## Lecture 7: The Thesis as Contribution — Situating the Argument in the Field

A thesis is not merely an academic exercise — it is a contribution to the field of Viking Studies. Even an undergraduate thesis, if well-executed, can advance knowledge on a specific question, challenge an established interpretation, or bring new evidence to bear on an old debate. The seminar's seventh session focuses on articulating the thesis's contribution and situating it within the scholarly conversation.

**Identifying the Contribution:** Every thesis should be able to answer the question: "What does my thesis contribute that did not exist before?" The contribution may be: (1) new evidence (artifact analysis, archival research, excavation results not previously published); (2) a new interpretation of existing evidence (re-reading a saga passage in light of new archaeological findings, re-analyzing a published dataset with new methods); (3) a synthesis that brings together evidence types not previously integrated; (4) a critical intervention that identifies a problem in the existing literature and proposes a solution; or (5) a methodological contribution (applying a method from another discipline to a Viking Studies problem).

The contribution should be stated explicitly in the introduction and returned to in the conclusion. It should not be left implicit — the reader should never have to guess why the thesis matters.

**Engaging the Scholarly Conversation:** The thesis is not a monologue; it is a contribution to an ongoing conversation. The student must demonstrate awareness of the conversation by engaging with the published literature — not just summarizing it but positioning the thesis in relation to it. The literature review should identify: (1) the scholars whose work the thesis builds on (explicitly acknowledging intellectual debts); (2) the scholars whose interpretations the thesis challenges (engaging respectfully — "as Jesch has argued, the Birka warrior's sex does not necessarily indicate a woman warrior, but the genomic evidence, combined with the grave goods and burial treatment, suggests a more complex picture"); (3) the gaps in the conversation that the thesis fills.

**Thesis Statements in Published Research:** An effective exercise in this session: students select a published monograph or article in Viking Studies and identify its thesis statement — the single sentence that articulates its central argument. The exercise reveals that even experienced scholars sometimes bury their thesis statement, making the reader work to extract the argument. Students then practice writing their own thesis statement in multiple versions — bold, cautious, narrow, broad — and the group discusses which version best serves the argument.

**The Humility Principle:** A thesis is a contribution, not a definitive settlement. The student should claim what the evidence supports and no more. Overclaiming — "this thesis definitively proves that..." — undermines credibility. The language of scholarly contribution is provisional: "this thesis argues that...," "the evidence suggests...," "these findings are consistent with...," "further research is needed to...." Humility is not weakness — it is intellectual honesty, and it strengthens rather than weakens the argument.

## Lecture 8: Managing the Advisor Relationship — Communication, Feedback, and Independence

The faculty advisor is the single most important resource for the thesis student — and the most underutilized. Students who communicate regularly with their advisors, who submit drafts on schedule, and who engage seriously with feedback produce stronger theses than students who disappear for weeks and resurface with a final draft. The seminar devotes a session to the practical skills of managing the advisor relationship.

**The Advisor's Role:** The advisor is not a co-author. The advisor will not write the thesis, will not fix the argument, and will not correct every citation. The advisor's role is to: (1) guide the student toward viable research questions and evidence sources, (2) provide critical feedback on drafts, (3) hold the student accountable to the thesis schedule, and (4) serve as the student's advocate in the evaluation process. The advisor is a mentor, not an editor.

**Effective Communication:** The most successful thesis students communicate with their advisors regularly — a brief weekly email update (progress, problems, next steps) keeps the advisor informed and the student accountable. The student should prepare for meetings: send materials in advance, come with specific questions (not "is this okay?" but "I'm struggling with the transition between the archaeological and textual evidence — does this paragraph bridge the two effectively?"), and take notes during the meeting.

**Receiving Feedback:** Feedback on a draft is always uncomfortable — the advisor is pointing out problems, and the natural response is defensiveness. The seminar teaches a feedback-receiving protocol: (1) read the feedback, (2) set it aside for 24 hours (the emotional reaction will subside), (3) re-read it as if it were about someone else's work, (4) identify the actionable suggestions, (5) make a revision plan. The student is not obligated to accept every suggestion, but every suggestion must be considered. If the student disagrees with the advisor's feedback, the professional response is to articulate the disagreement clearly and discuss it — not to ignore it.

**Independence and Ownership:** The thesis is the student's work, not the advisor's. The student makes the final decisions about argument, evidence, and structure. The advisor who says "I think you should argue X" is offering advice, not issuing a directive — the student may accept the advice, reject it with reasons, or modify the argument in a direction the advisor did not suggest. The evaluators will judge the thesis on its own merits, not on its conformity to the advisor's preferences. The seminar emphasizes: the student owns the thesis.

**When Things Go Wrong:** The seminar addresses the difficult situations that sometimes arise: the advisor who is unresponsive to emails, the advisor whose feedback is vague or contradictory, the student who falls behind schedule and is afraid to admit it, the student who realizes their research question cannot be answered with the available evidence. For each scenario, the seminar provides: (1) a protocol for escalation (talk to the advisor first, then the course instructor, then the department chair); (2) a strategy for recovery (renegotiating the scope, adjusting the timeline); and (3) the reassurance that problems are normal and solvable.

## Lecture 9: Presenting Research — The Oral Defense and the Academic Conference

The thesis is evaluated primarily on the written document, but the oral defense is the culminating ritual of the Viking Studies program — a 45-minute presentation to the thesis committee (advisor, second reader, and one external examiner) followed by 30 minutes of questions. The seminar provides training in oral presentation and defense preparation.

**The Defense Presentation:** The standard format: a 20-minute presentation summarizing the research question, methodology, key evidence, argument, and contribution. The remaining 25 minutes are for committee questions. The presentation should not attempt to cover everything in the thesis — it should highlight the most important points and leave the committee wanting to ask questions. The seminar's advice: (1) structure the presentation around the argument, not the chapter structure; (2) use visuals strategically — a few well-designed slides with maps, artifact images, and key data, not a screen full of bullet points; (3) practice the presentation aloud at least five times, timing each run; (4) anticipate the hardest questions and prepare answers.

**Handling Questions:** The defense questions are not designed to trap the student — they are designed to test the depth of the student's knowledge and the robustness of the argument. The most common question types: (1) clarification ("can you explain how you dated this context?"), (2) challenge ("your interpretation of the saga passage seems inconsistent with Larrington's reading — how do you respond?"), (3) extension ("if your argument is correct, what are the implications for our understanding of skaldic verse?"), and (4) the "gap question" ("you've focused on elite burials — what about non-elite burial practices?"). The seminar provides a session of mock defense questions, with faculty and peers role-playing the committee.

The most important defense skill is the pause. When asked a difficult question, the student should pause, think, and then answer — not panic and fill the silence with words. A thoughtful "That's an excellent question — let me work through my reasoning" is far better than a rushed, incoherent answer.

**The Academic Conference Presentation:** Beyond the defense, the seminar teaches the skills of academic conference presentation — an essential professional skill for students planning graduate study or heritage careers. Students are required to submit an abstract to the UoY Undergraduate Research Symposium (held in Week 13) and to present their research in a 15-minute conference-style format. The conference presentation differs from the defense: it assumes a general audience (not specialists in the student's subfield), it must be accessible and engaging, and it must fit within a strict time limit. The seminar covers abstract writing, slide design, public speaking technique, and the etiquette of the Q&A session.

**The 2040 Presentation Environment:** The UoY defense room is equipped with full telepresence capabilities, allowing committee members to participate remotely (common for external examiners). The student may use any combination of traditional slides, interactive 3D models (manipulated in real time during the presentation), and augmented reality demonstrations (e.g., projecting a 3D reconstruction of a Viking Age longhouse onto the presentation table). The technology should serve the argument, not distract from it. The seminar's advice: use one interactive element at most, and test it thoroughly before the defense. Nothing undermines credibility like a technology failure in front of the committee.

## Lecture 10: The Ethics of Authorship — Citation, Acknowledgment, and Academic Integrity

The ethics of authorship are the foundation of scholarly practice. The thesis must scrupulously distinguish between the student's own work and the work of others, must acknowledge all sources, and must represent the research process honestly. Failure to meet these standards is not merely a technical error — it is a breach of academic integrity that can result in thesis rejection and disciplinary action.

**Citation Practice:** Every claim that is not the student's original contribution must be cited. The citation must be specific — a page number, a figure number, a database entry — not a vague reference to an entire book. The principle: the reader must be able to locate the exact source for the claim within a reasonable effort. The thesis bibliography must include every work cited, and only works cited (no "padding" the bibliography with unread sources).

The 2040 citation landscape includes new source types that require specific citation formats: AI-generated content (if used with advisor approval) must be cited with the tool name, version, prompt, and date; born-digital data from the Viking Evidence Platform must be cited with the VEP accession number and retrieval date; 3D models must be cited with the UoY Digital Repository DOI.

**Plagiarism in the Age of AI:** The availability of AI text generators has made plagiarism detection more complex. The UoY academic integrity policy is clear: submitting AI-generated text as one's own work is plagiarism, regardless of whether the AI was prompted or the output was edited. The thesis is evaluated on the student's own thinking, analysis, and writing — AI can assist (grammar checking, formatting, translation of non-scholarly texts) but cannot substitute for the student's intellectual labor. All theses are submitted through the UoY integrity verification system, which scans for both traditional plagiarism (matching published sources) and AI-generated text patterns.

**Acknowledgment of Assistance:** The thesis must include an acknowledgments section that names and thanks: the advisor, the second reader, any specialists who provided advice or data, any institutions that provided access to collections or archives, any funding sources, and any individuals who provided practical assistance (proofreading, translation, GIS training, lab work). The acknowledgments should also declare: the use of AI tools (WriteWell, translation tools), the use of editorial services (if any), and the role of the advisor and others in shaping the thesis. Over-acknowledgment is better than under-acknowledgment.

**Data Integrity:** The thesis must accurately represent the evidence. Data fabrication (inventing evidence), data falsification (manipulating evidence to produce a desired result), and selective reporting (presenting only the evidence that supports the argument while concealing contradictory evidence) are all violations of academic integrity. The ethical researcher reports all relevant evidence, including evidence that complicates or contradicts the argument, and addresses the contradictions honestly.

## Lecture 11: Beyond the Thesis — Publication, Graduate Study, and Professional Pathways

The thesis is the culmination of the undergraduate program, but it is not the end of the student's engagement with the Viking Age. The penultimate seminar session addresses the pathways that follow the thesis: publication, graduate study, and professional careers in the heritage sector.

**From Thesis to Publication:** A strong undergraduate thesis can form the basis of a publishable article. The seminar covers the process of converting a thesis chapter into a journal article: selecting a target journal (for Viking Studies theses, the options include *Viking and Medieval Scandinavia*, *Saga-Book*, *Scandinavian Studies*, *Norwegian Archaeological Review*, *Journal of the North Atlantic*, or the UoY undergraduate research journal *Yggdrasill*); adapting the length and format to the journal's requirements (an article is typically 6,000-8,000 words, substantially shorter than a thesis chapter); writing an abstract; and navigating the peer-review process.

The instructor provides realistic expectations: most undergraduate theses require substantial revision before they are publishable, and the publication process (submission, review, revision, acceptance, publication) typically takes 12-18 months. Students who are interested in publication are encouraged to begin the revision process while the thesis is still fresh, ideally within six months of submission.

**Graduate Study in Viking Studies:** The seminar provides guidance on graduate programs in Viking and Medieval Scandinavian Studies, both at UoY (which offers MA and PhD programs in Viking Studies and Computational Occultism) and at other institutions (Oslo, Copenhagen, Uppsala, Reykjavík, Aberdeen, Nottingham, Cambridge, Harvard). The session covers: the application process (statement of purpose, writing sample, letters of recommendation), funding (research council grants, university fellowships, external scholarships), and the transition from undergraduate to graduate research expectations.

**Professional Careers in Heritage:** The seminar's professional development component — in collaboration with the UoY Career Services office — provides information on non-academic career pathways: cultural resource management (CRM) archaeology, museum curation and conservation, heritage interpretation and tourism, digital heritage and VR development, public archaeology and community engagement, and heritage policy and regulation. A panel of UoY alumni working in these fields visits the seminar (in person or via telepresence) to discuss their career trajectories and answer student questions.

The market reality: academic positions in Viking Studies are scarce — there are more qualified PhDs than permanent university positions. But the broader heritage sector offers a range of fulfilling careers, and the skills developed in the Viking Studies program — critical thinking, research design, data analysis, clear writing, interdisciplinary synthesis — are valued across the sector.

## Lecture 12: The Thesis as Artifact — Reflection, Metacognition, and the Scholar You Have Become

The final seminar session is devoted to reflection. The thesis is a document, but it is also an artifact of the student's intellectual development — a record of what they thought, what they learned, and who they became during the process of creating it.

**The Reflective Essay:** In addition to the thesis itself, each student submits a 1,500-word reflective essay that addresses: (1) How did your understanding of the Viking Age change during the thesis process? (2) What was the most difficult intellectual challenge you faced, and how did you address it? (3) What would you do differently if you were starting over? (4) What skills have you developed that will serve you beyond the Viking Studies program? (5) What do you now know about yourself as a scholar — your strengths, your limitations, your interests — that you did not know when you began?

The reflective essay is not graded for content (there are no "wrong" reflections), but it is required for thesis submission. It serves two purposes: for the student, it consolidates the learning of the thesis process; for the faculty, it provides feedback on the program's effectiveness in developing the scholarly skills the thesis is meant to demonstrate.

**Metacognition and Scholarly Identity:** Throughout the seminar, students have been practicing metacognition — thinking about their own thinking — in the form of research journals, peer review, and advisor meetings. The final session makes this practice explicit: the student who can articulate their own intellectual process is better equipped to tackle the next research project, the next intellectual challenge, and the next stage of their career. The thesis is not an endpoint but a foundation — the first substantial piece of original research the student has produced, and the platform from which they will launch whatever comes next.

**The Thesis as Legacy:** In 2040, all UoY Viking Studies theses are deposited in the university's digital repository and made available open-access. The thesis the student writes this semester will be read by future students, by future researchers, and potentially by members of the public who discover it through search engines or AI recommendation systems. It is a permanent contribution to the collective understanding of the Viking Age — a small contribution, perhaps, but a real one. The final lecture closes with this thought: the Vikings inscribed their names on runestones that have endured for a thousand years. The student is engaging in the same practice — leaving a mark, making a record, contributing to the long human conversation about who we are and where we come from. The medium has changed — from stone to screen — but the impulse is the same.

## Assignments

### Assignment 1: Thesis Outline and Annotated Bibliography (15%)

**Objective:** Develop a detailed thesis outline and an annotated bibliography that demonstrates the feasibility of the research question and the adequacy of the evidence base.

**Task:**

1. **Thesis Outline (1,000-1,500 words):** Prepare a paragraph-level outline of the entire thesis. For each planned section (chapter or major subsection), write a brief paragraph describing its content, its argumentative function, and its relationship to the overall thesis argument. Include estimated word counts for each section. The outline should be sufficiently detailed that your advisor — and your peers in the seminar — can evaluate whether the structure serves the argument.

2. **Thesis Statement:** State your thesis argument in a single sentence (maximum 50 words). This sentence should appear prominently in the outline document.

3. **Annotated Bibliography (minimum 20 sources):** For each source, provide: (a) full bibliographic citation in *Scandinavian Studies* format, (b) a 100-150 word annotation summarizing the source's argument and evidence, and (c) a sentence explaining how the source will be used in your thesis (background? evidence? theoretical framework? counterargument?).

4. **Evidence Inventory:** List the primary evidence you will use in your thesis — archaeological sites/collections, textual sources (specific saga passages, skaldic stanzas, runic inscriptions), scientific datasets. For each item, note its accessibility (do you have the data? do you need to request access? is a site visit required?) and any access issues you anticipate.

**Deliverable:** A single document submitted by Week 3. Reviewed by the advisor and two peers.

**Grading Rubric:**
- Outline coherence and argumentative logic (30%)
- Thesis statement clarity and contestability (15%)
- Annotated bibliography comprehensiveness and relevance (30%)
- Evidence inventory completeness and feasibility assessment (15%)
- Professional presentation and citation accuracy (10%)

---

### Assignment 2: First Draft of Core Chapter (20%)

**Objective:** Produce a complete draft of the thesis's central evidence/analysis chapter and receive structured peer and advisor feedback.

**Task:** Submit a complete draft of the chapter that forms the analytical core of your thesis — typically the longest and most evidence-intensive chapter (2,500-3,500 words). The draft must include:

1. **Chapter Introduction:** A paragraph articulating the chapter's argument and its relationship to the overall thesis argument.

2. **Evidence Presentation and Analysis:** The evidence presented in the format appropriate to its type (archaeological data with maps/tables/figures, textual passages with close reading, scientific data with appropriate visualization). Analysis of the evidence — not raw data presentation.

3. **Chapter Conclusion:** A paragraph synthesizing the chapter's findings and transitioning to the next stage of the argument.

4. **Self-Assessment Memo (500 words):** A memo to your advisor identifying: (a) what you think is working well in this draft, (b) what you are struggling with, (c) 2-3 specific questions you want your advisor and peer reviewers to address.

**Deliverable:** Draft chapter plus self-assessment memo. Submitted Week 7. Reviewed by advisor and two peers in the Week 8 workshop.

**Grading Rubric:**
- Argument clarity and evidence support (30%)
- Analysis quality and depth (25%)
- Writing quality and professional presentation (20%)
- Self-assessment honesty and specificity (15%)
- Figure and table quality (if applicable) (10%)

---

### Assignment 3: Peer Review Portfolio (10%)

**Objective:** Demonstrate the ability to provide constructive, specific, and professionally formatted feedback on scholarly work-in-progress.

**Task:** Over the course of the seminar, you will serve as a peer reviewer for two of your colleagues' work. For each review, you will:

1. Read the submitted material (outline, draft chapter, or near-final section) carefully.

2. Prepare written feedback (500-750 words per review) addressing the four standard peer-review questions: (a) What is the argument? (State it in your own words. If you cannot, the argument is unclear.) (b) What is the strongest piece of evidence, and why? (c) What is the weakest point, and what would strengthen it? (d) What one change would most improve this section?

3. Provide marginal comments (in the document, using comment/track-changes) identifying specific passages that illustrate your points.

4. Participate actively in the peer-review workshop discussion.

Your portfolio (submitted at the end of the semester) should include: the two written reviews, the documents you reviewed (with your marginal comments visible), and a 500-word reflective memo on what you learned about your own writing from reviewing others' work.

**Deliverable:** Peer review portfolio submitted Week 14. Peer review quality is also assessed through the instructor's observation of the workshops.

**Grading Rubric:**
- Feedback specificity and constructiveness (40%)
- Argument identification accuracy (20%)
- Marginal comment quality and precision (20%)
- Workshop participation and collegiality (10%)
- Reflective memo insight (10%)

---

### Assignment 4: Oral Defense and Conference Presentation (15%)

**Objective:** Demonstrate the ability to present research orally in both a defense/committee context and a general academic conference context.

**Task:**

1. **Thesis Defense (Week 13-14):** Prepare and deliver a defense presentation (20 minutes presentation + 30 minutes questions) to your thesis committee. Your presentation should: (a) articulate your research question and its significance, (b) present your key evidence, (c) make your argument, (d) acknowledge limitations, and (e) engage with committee questions thoughtfully and substantively.

2. **Conference Presentation (Week 13):** Prepare and deliver a 15-minute presentation at the UoY Undergraduate Research Symposium. Your presentation should be accessible to a general academic audience (not specialists in your subfield) and should communicate your research clearly and engagingly.

3. **Presentation Materials:** Submit your slides, speaker notes, and a 250-word abstract for the conference presentation. The abstract should follow the standard conference format: context, research question, methods, key findings, significance.

**Grading Rubric:**
- Defense presentation clarity and argumentation (30%)
- Defense Q&A performance — thoughtfulness and knowledge depth (25%)
- Conference presentation accessibility and engagement (20%)
- Slide/visual aid quality and effectiveness (15%)
- Abstract quality (10%)

---

### Assignment 5: Final Thesis (40%)

**Objective:** Submit a polished, original, and professionally presented thesis that makes a genuine contribution to Viking Studies.

**Task:** Submit the completed thesis by the end of Week 14. The thesis must meet all of the following requirements:

**Content Requirements:**
- 10,000-12,000 words (excluding bibliography, figures, and appendices)
- A clearly articulated research question and thesis statement
- A critical literature review that situates the thesis in the scholarly conversation
- A methodology section that explains and justifies the research methods
- Substantive analysis of primary evidence (archaeological, textual, scientific, or a combination)
- A discussion that synthesizes the evidence into a coherent argument
- A conclusion that articulates the thesis's contribution and acknowledges limitations

**Format Requirements:**
- UoY "VikingThesis" LaTeX template or approved alternative
- *Scandinavian Studies* citation format
- Proper Old Norse orthography (ǫ, ð, þ, æ, œ, ǽ)
- All figures professionally formatted with captions, scale bars, and legends as appropriate
- Complete bibliography of all works cited
- Acknowledgments section
- Signed academic integrity declaration

**Submission:** Digital submission via the UoY thesis portal. A bound copy is also required for the departmental library (the department provides a binding subsidy).

**Grade Breakdown:**
- Argument — clarity, originality, and significance (25%)
- Evidence — quality, selection, analysis, and integration (25%)
- Methodology — appropriateness, justification, and execution (15%)
- Literature review — comprehensiveness and critical engagement (10%)
- Writing — clarity, style, and professional polish (15%)
- Presentation — formatting, figures, citations, orthography (10%)
