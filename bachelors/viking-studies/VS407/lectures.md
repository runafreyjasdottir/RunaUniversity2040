# VS407: Elective — Cross-Disciplinary Study
## Bachelor of Arts in Viking Studies — University of Yggdrasil, 2040

**Credits:** 4
**Description:** A structured exploration of how Viking Studies connects to, draws from, and contributes to neighboring academic disciplines. Students select two disciplinary modules from a rotating menu (current offerings: Digital Humanities, Gender and Sexuality Studies, Environmental Humanities, Game Design and Interactive Media, Museum and Heritage Studies, and Comparative Indigenous Studies), complete a substantial interdisciplinary project in each, and produce a capstone synthesis reflecting on the value and limits of cross-disciplinary approaches to the Viking Age.

**Prerequisites:** VS101-403, or equivalent with instructor permission. Open to non-majors with appropriate background.
**Format:** Modular; each student's pathway is individualized in consultation with the instructor.

---

## Lecture 1: The Disciplines and the Viking Age — An Intellectual Map

The Viking Studies program at UoY is already interdisciplinary in the sense that it integrates archaeology, history, philology, and runology. But the program's core disciplines represent only a subset of the intellectual approaches that can illuminate the Viking Age. Anthropology, environmental science, gender studies, digital humanities, game design, museum studies — each of these contributes perspectives, methods, and questions that the core curriculum, by necessity, can only gesture toward.

This course is designed as a structured opportunity for students to venture outside the core — to spend a semester in serious engagement with a discipline beyond Viking Studies and to reflect on what that engagement reveals about both the Viking Age and the nature of disciplinary knowledge itself. It is an elective, but it is not optional in spirit: the program's conviction is that the best Viking Studies scholars are those who know what other disciplines have to offer and who can move fluently between intellectual traditions.

**The Structure of the Course:** Students select two of the six available modules. Each module consists of:

1. **A foundational reading list** — 5-8 key texts that introduce the discipline's methods, theories, and major debates as they pertain to the Viking Age (or comparable pre-modern societies).
2. **Two seminars** (per module) — small-group discussions with the module instructor (drawn from the relevant UoY department) and other students in the same module cohort.
3. **A module project** — a 2,500-3,000 word paper that applies the discipline's methods to a specific Viking Studies question.
4. **A capstone reflective essay** — a 2,000-word synthesis reflecting on the student's cross-disciplinary learning across both modules.

The course is designed to be flexible: students with a clear intellectual trajectory can choose modules that build toward their thesis or career goals; students who are intellectually curious but undecided can explore widely.

**What Disciplines Do:** A discipline is not merely a subject matter — it is a set of questions, methods, and standards of evidence that define what counts as a valid contribution to knowledge. The archaeologist asks "what survives?" and answers with stratigraphy and radiocarbon dates. The literary scholar asks "what does this text mean?" and answers with close reading and genre analysis. The environmental scientist asks "what did the landscape look like?" and answers with pollen analysis and climate models. Each discipline illuminates part of the picture; none illuminates the whole.

The cross-disciplinary scholar does not attempt to master every discipline — that is impossible. Rather, the cross-disciplinary scholar learns to recognize what each discipline can and cannot contribute, to translate between disciplinary languages, and to identify questions that can only be answered by combining approaches. The course's intellectual goal is not disciplinary mastery but disciplinary literacy: the ability to read, evaluate, and engage with scholarship from outside one's home discipline.

**The 2040 Cross-Disciplinary Landscape:** The accelerating specialization of knowledge — each subfield proliferating into further subfields — makes cross-disciplinary literacy both more difficult and more necessary than ever. The AI tools that now mediate much scholarly research (literature search, data analysis, text mining) can exacerbate disciplinary silos: the AI trained on archaeological publications recommends more archaeological publications, and the researcher never encounters the anthropologist's or the literary scholar's perspective on the same phenomenon. VS407 is an antidote to algorithmic narrowing: a deliberate, structured encounter with intellectual difference.

## Lecture 2: Module A — Digital Humanities and the Computational Viking Age

*Note: The following six lectures (2-7) describe the six available modules. Instructors rotate; the current offerings are listed below. Students attend the lectures for their two selected modules plus the common lectures (1, 8-12).*

The Digital Humanities (DH) module explores the application of computational methods to the study of the Viking Age. It is not a technical programming course (though students with coding skills are encouraged to apply them) — it is an introduction to the questions that computation can and cannot answer, the ethical implications of digitizing the past, and the transformation of scholarly practice in an era of AI-augmented research.

**Key Texts:**
- Moretti, F. (2013). *Distant Reading*. Verso. [Foundational text for computational literary analysis]
- Huggett, J. (2025). "Digital Archaeology: Promises and Pitfalls." *Annual Review of Anthropology* 54.
- The UoY Digital Saga Library User Guide (2040)
- Cordell, R. (2032). "Machine Learning and the Archive." *American Literary History* 44(2).
- Eide, Ø. (2035). *Media Boundaries and Conceptual Modeling: Between Texts and Maps*. Palgrave Macmillan.

**Module Seminars:**

**Seminar 1: Close Reading vs. Distant Reading.** The saga corpus contains approximately 40 Íslendingasǫgur, a dozen konungasǫgur, and dozens more fornaldarsǫgur and other prose texts — far more than any single scholar can read closely in a lifetime. Computational text analysis — topic modeling, stylometry, network analysis, semantic search — offers the possibility of "distant reading": identifying patterns across the entire corpus that would escape any close reader. But distant reading has been criticized as reductionist — reducing complex literary works to word counts and network graphs. The seminar debates the relationship between close and distant reading: can they complement each other? does distant reading generate hypotheses that close reading tests? or does it produce results that only appear meaningful because the algorithms are designed to find patterns?

**Seminar 2: AI and the Future of Viking Studies.** The AI tools that students use daily — SagaSearch, RuneReader AI, the Viking Evidence Platform — are transforming the practice of Viking Studies. What are the implications? On the positive side: access to the entire corpus, searchable in seconds, democratizes research and enables questions that were previously unaskable. On the negative side: AI tools embed the biases of their training data, black-box algorithms make decision-making opaque, and the ease of AI-generated "analysis" may erode the skills of close reading and critical interpretation. The seminar grapples with the ethics of AI in humanities research and asks students to articulate their own position on the appropriate use of AI in their scholarly work.

**Module Project:** A computational analysis of a Viking Studies question, using the UoY digital tools. Possible projects: topic modeling of legal language across the Íslendingasǫgur corpus; network analysis of character interactions in a single saga; spatial analysis of runestone distribution using GIS; comparative stylometry of the konungasǫgur attributed to Snorri Sturluson. The project must include a critical reflection on the method's limitations.

## Lecture 3: Module B — Gender and Sexuality in the Viking World

The Gender and Sexuality module examines the Viking Age through the lens of feminist, queer, and gender theory. It builds on the introductory coverage of gender in VS105 and the theoretical training in VS403, providing a sustained engagement with one of the most dynamic and contested areas of contemporary Viking Studies.

**Key Texts:**
- Butler, J. (1990). *Gender Trouble: Feminism and the Subversion of Identity*. Routledge. [Foundational for performativity theory]
- Jesch, J. (1991). *Women in the Viking Age*. Boydell Press. [The classic survey]
- Arwill-Nordbladh, E. (2030). *Gender and Material Culture in Viking Age Scandinavia*. Oxbow Books.
- Hedenstierna-Jonson, C. et al. (2017). "A Female Viking Warrior Confirmed by Genomics." *American Journal of Physical Anthropology* 164(4).
- Moen, M. (2034). "Beyond the Birka Warrior: Gender, Power, and the Limits of Genomic Evidence." *Norwegian Archaeological Review* 67(2).
- Raffield, B. (2031). "Queering the Viking Age: Methodological Possibilities and Limitations." *Gender & History* 43(3).

**Module Seminars:**

**Seminar 1: The Birka Warrior and Its Aftermath.** The 2017 genomic confirmation that the Birka warrior grave Bj 581 contained a biological female was a watershed moment — not because it settled the question of women warriors in the Viking Age (it did not) but because it exposed the depth of the field's androcentric assumptions. The seminar traces the scholarly debate before and after the genomic results: what did the excavators (Hjalmar Stolpe, 1878) record? what did subsequent scholars assume? how did the 2017 results transform the conversation? what does the ongoing debate reveal about the relationship between biological sex, gender identity, and archaeological interpretation?

**Seminar 2: Queer Approaches to the Viking Age.** Queer theory — concerned with the instability of categories, the performance of identity, and the critique of normative assumptions — has only recently been applied to the Viking Age. The seminar explores what a queer Viking Studies might look like: reading saga characters who cross gender boundaries (the shield-maidens of the fornaldarsǫgur, the seiðr-practicing men whose ritual practice was coded as *ergi* — unmanly, taboo); interpreting burials that resist binary gender classification (graves with mixed "male" and "female" grave goods); and questioning the assumption that Viking Age gender roles were stable, binary, and universally enforced. The seminar also addresses the methodological challenge: how do we access Viking Age gender and sexuality when the evidence is fragmentary, the textual sources were written by Christians with their own gender ideology, and our own cultural categories may not map onto the past?

**Module Project:** A gender-focused analysis of a specific Viking Age phenomenon. Possible projects: a re-analysis of a cemetery population with attention to gender and grave goods; a queer reading of a saga character; a study of gendered space in Viking Age longhouses; a critical analysis of the representation of Viking women in popular culture.

## Lecture 4: Module C — Environmental Humanities and the Viking Ecumene

The Environmental Humanities module approaches the Viking Age as a story of human-environment interaction: how the Norse adapted to, exploited, and transformed the landscapes they inhabited; how climate shaped the Viking expansion; and what the Viking Age can teach us about human responses to environmental change — a question of urgent relevance in the climate-altered 2040.

**Key Texts:**
- Diamond, J. (2005). *Collapse: How Societies Choose to Fail or Succeed*. Viking Press. [Chapter on Norse Greenland — controversial but influential]
- Dugmore, A. J. et al. (2012). "Cultural Adaptation, Compounding Vulnerabilities, and Conjunctures in Norse Greenland." *Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences* 109(10).
- Hedeager, L. (2032). *Iron Age Myth and Materiality: An Archaeology of Scandinavia AD 400-1000*. Routledge. [Chapters on landscape and cosmology]
- Hambrecht, G. et al. (2030). "The North Atlantic Biocultural Organization (NABO): Two Decades of Interdisciplinary Arctic Science." *Quaternary Science Reviews* 248.
- Price, N. (2036). "The Green Vikings: Environmental Histories of the Scandinavian Expansion." *Environmental History* 41(2).
- Sindbæk, S. M. (2038). "Climate and the Viking Age Urban Network: A Dendroclimatological Analysis." *Antiquity* 112(399).

**Module Seminars:**

**Seminar 1: The Norse in Greenland — Adaptation, Maladaptation, and the End of the Eastern Settlement.** The Norse colonization of Greenland (c. 985 — c. 1450) is the most intensely studied case of human-environment interaction in the Viking world. The Norse arrived during the Medieval Warm Period, established two settlements (Eastern and Western) that supported approximately 2,000-5,000 people, and persisted for nearly 500 years before disappearing. Why? The classic explanation — Diamond's *Collapse* — blamed Norse cultural conservatism: they refused to adopt Inuit hunting technology, overgrazed the fragile subarctic landscape, and were defeated by the Little Ice Age. Recent scholarship (Dugmore et al., NABO project) complicates this narrative: the Norse did adopt some Inuit practices (seal hunting), the environmental degradation was less severe than Diamond claimed, and the "collapse" was likely a gradual abandonment driven by multiple factors — climate, trade disruption, and the pull of better opportunities elsewhere. The seminar evaluates the competing explanations and asks what the Greenland case teaches us about human resilience and vulnerability in the face of environmental change — a question with obvious 2040 resonance.

**Seminar 2: The Viking Landscape as Lived Environment.** The Environmental Humanities module is not only about climate and collapse — it is also about how the Norse experienced their environment: the sacred groves (lundr) where blót was performed, the bogs where offerings were deposited, the managed woodlands that provided timber for ships, the grazing lands that sustained the cattle whose dairy products were central to the Norse diet, and the routes — sea-lanes, portages, river systems — that connected the Scandinavian world. The seminar uses the concept of the "ecumene" — the inhabited world as experienced by its inhabitants — to reconstruct the Viking Age landscape not as a passive background but as an active participant in human history.

**Module Project:** An environmental analysis of a specific Viking Age site, region, or phenomenon. Possible projects: a reconstruction of the pastoral economy of a single farmstead using zooarchaeological and palynological data; an analysis of the relationship between climate fluctuations and settlement patterns in a specific region; a study of the environmental impacts of iron production (charcoal consumption, deforestation); a critical evaluation of the "collapse" narrative in Norse Greenland.

## Lecture 5: Module D — Game Design, VR, and the Interactive Viking Age

The Game Design and Interactive Media module explores the representation of the Viking Age in interactive digital media — video games, VR experiences, AR applications — and provides hands-on experience in designing interactive Viking content. This module is particularly popular with students interested in careers in digital heritage, game development, and public engagement.

**Key Texts:**
- Chapman, A. (2016). *Digital Games as History: How Videogames Represent the Past and Offer Access to Historical Practice*. Routledge.
- Phelpstead, C. (2031). *Screen Vikings: The Norse Past in Film and Television*. Edinburgh University Press. [Chapter on games]
- Uricchio, W. (2035). "Simulation, Immersion, and the Historical Imaginary." *Game Studies* 35(2).
- The UoY Game Lab Design Manual (2040)
- Copplestone, T. J. (2032). "But That's Not Accurate: The Differing Perceptions of Accuracy in Cultural Heritage Videogames." *Archaeologies* 18(3).
- Sterling, D. & Jónsson, K. (2039). "Goði: Designing an Educational Viking Age Management Game." *Journal of Interactive Heritage* 12(1).

**Module Seminars:**

**Seminar 1: Authenticity, Play, and the Historical Game.** The video game *Assassin's Creed: Valhalla* sold over 20 million copies — far more than any academic book on the Viking Age will ever reach. For the vast majority of those players, the game's version of 9th-century England and Norway is the most detailed encounter with the Viking Age they will ever have. What responsibilities does the game designer have to historical accuracy? The seminar explores the tension between historical authenticity and playability: games require player agency, which means the player must be able to alter history, and games must be fun, which means simplifying complexity. The "Discovery Tour" mode of *Valhalla* attempted to square this circle by providing an educational, non-violent mode alongside the main game — but does the existence of the educational mode legitimize the main game's historical distortions? Students play selected segments of Viking-themed games and analyze their historical representations.

**Seminar 2: Designing the Interactive Viking Age.** The hands-on component: students work (individually or in small teams) with the UoY Game Lab's "SagaForge" platform — a low-code game development environment pre-loaded with Viking Age assets (3D models of artifacts, reconstructed buildings, landscapes) and historical data (the Viking Evidence Platform). The task: design a concept for an interactive Viking Age experience (a game, a VR reconstruction, an AR heritage app) that is both historically grounded and engaging for a non-specialist audience. The seminar provides training in: interaction design, narrative design for historical content, user testing, and the ethics of representing violence, religion, and cultural difference in interactive media.

**Module Project:** A game design document and prototype for an interactive Viking Age experience. The document must include: a concept description (500 words), a historical rationale (how the design draws on and represents the historical evidence, 750 words), a gameplay/interaction description (500 words), and an ethical reflection on the design choices (500 words). A functional prototype (even if very simple) is encouraged but not required; the design document is the primary deliverable.

## Lecture 6: Module E — Museum and Heritage Studies

The Museum and Heritage Studies module explores the theory and practice of presenting the Viking Age to the public through museums, heritage sites, and cultural institutions. It builds on the museum practicum component of VS307, providing a deeper engagement with the intellectual, political, and ethical dimensions of heritage work.

**Key Texts:**
- Bennett, T. (1995). *The Birth of the Museum: History, Theory, Politics*. Routledge.
- Smith, L. (2006). *Uses of Heritage*. Routledge. [Foundational for critical heritage studies]
- Kirshenblatt-Gimblett, B. (1998). *Destination Culture: Tourism, Museums, and Heritage*. University of California Press.
- Silberman, N. A. (2030). "Heritage Interpretation as Public Discourse." In *The Oxford Handbook of Public Archaeology*.
- The Öland Principles (2035) — see VS307
- The Reykjavík Declaration on Archaeological Practice (2038)
- Þórsdóttir, E. (2038). "Decolonizing the Viking Museum: Sami Perspectives on Norse Heritage." *Museum Anthropology* 61(2).

**Module Seminars:**

**Seminar 1: The Politics of Display.** A museum exhibit is never neutral. The objects selected, the labels written, the spatial arrangement, the lighting — every curatorial decision encodes assumptions about what matters, whose story is being told, and who the audience is. The seminar analyzes a specific Viking Age exhibition (students visit — in person or via the UoY Virtual Museum Interface — the Viking Ship Museum in Oslo, the National Museum of Denmark's Viking exhibition, or the Jorvik Viking Centre) and deconstructs its curatorial choices: What narrative is the exhibition telling? What objects are given prominence, and what is in the corners? Whose voices are represented in the interpretation, and whose are absent? How does the exhibition handle the difficult aspects of the Viking Age — violence, slavery, sexual exploitation?

**Seminar 2: Heritage, Community, and the "Decolonization" of Viking Museums.** The "decolonization" of museums — the movement to acknowledge and address the colonial origins of museum collections and to return control over heritage interpretation to descendant communities — has been primarily discussed in relation to imperial collections (the Benin Bronzes, the Parthenon Marbles). But what does decolonization mean for Viking heritage? The Sami people of northern Scandinavia were colonized by the Norse-descended nation-states of Norway, Sweden, and Finland, and Sami material culture has historically been displayed in "Viking" museums without Sami consent or interpretation. The seminar engages with Sami perspectives on Viking heritage and with the broader question: how can heritage institutions share interpretive authority with communities whose heritage they display?

**Module Project:** A critical exhibition analysis or an exhibition design proposal. For the analysis: select a specific Viking exhibition (physical or digital) and analyze its curatorial strategies, narrative, and politics of representation. For the proposal: design a hypothetical exhibition that addresses a specific problem in Viking heritage interpretation (e.g., representing slavery, incorporating Sami perspectives, displaying human remains ethically). Either format: 2,500 words.

## Lecture 7: Module F — Comparative Indigenous Studies

The Comparative Indigenous Studies module places the Norse experience in comparative perspective with other Indigenous and pre-colonial societies around the world. The goal is not to equate the Norse with Indigenous peoples in a modern sense — that would be inappropriate, given the Norse role as colonizers in the North Atlantic and their descendants' role in Scandinavian colonialism — but to use the comparative method to illuminate both what is distinctive about the Norse case and what broader patterns of human social organization it exemplifies.

**Key Texts:**
- Wolf, E. R. (1982). *Europe and the People Without History*. University of California Press. [Foundational critique of Eurocentric historiography]
- Sahlins, M. & Service, E. R. (1960). *Evolution and Culture*. [Foundational for neo-evolutionary typology]
- Scott, J. C. (2009). *The Art of Not Being Governed: An Anarchist History of Upland Southeast Asia*. Yale University Press. [Comparative framework for decentralized societies]
- Grønnow, B. (2034). "Norse and Inuit in Greenland: Contact, Conflict, and Coexistence." *Arctic Anthropology* 71(1).
- Vésteinsson, O. (2036). "The Icelandic Commonwealth as a Stateless Society: Comparative Perspectives." *Journal of Social Archaeology* 36(2).
- Kuper, A. (2033). "The Return of the Native: Indigenous Anthropology in the 21st Century." *Current Anthropology* 74(4).

**Module Seminars:**

**Seminar 1: Stateless Societies in Comparative Perspective.** The Icelandic Commonwealth (c. 930-1262) is one of the best-documented stateless societies in world history — a complex society with law, literature, and long-distance trade but no executive government, no standing army, and no taxation. How unusual was this? The seminar compares the Icelandic Commonwealth with other documented stateless societies: the segmentary lineage systems of the Nuer (East Africa, described by E. E. Evans-Pritchard), the "galactic polities" of pre-modern Southeast Asia (described by Stanley Tambiah), the hill societies of Zomia (described by James C. Scott), and the pre-contact societies of the Pacific Northwest Coast. What light does comparison shed on the Icelandic case? What are the limits of comparison — what is lost when we abstract a society from its specific historical and cultural context?

**Seminar 2: Encounter, Colonization, and the Norse in the North Atlantic.** The Norse colonization of the North Atlantic (Faroe, Iceland, Greenland, briefly Vinland) involved encounters with both uninhabited landscapes (Iceland, though recent evidence suggests Irish monks may have preceded the Norse) and inhabited ones (the Dorset and Thule peoples in Greenland, the Beothuk and possibly other Indigenous groups in Vinland). The seminar compares the Norse experience with other cases of pre-modern colonization: the Polynesian colonization of the Pacific, the Bantu expansion in Africa, the Austronesian expansion in Southeast Asia and Oceania. What patterns recur? What is distinctive about the Norse case — the role of maritime technology, the political structure of the settlement, the relationship with the homeland?

**Module Project:** A comparative analysis of a specific Viking Age phenomenon with a parallel from another pre-modern society. Possible topics: comparative legal institutions (Icelandic law and the legal systems of other pre-state societies); comparative colonization (Norse North Atlantic and Polynesian Pacific); comparative religion (Norse paganism and other "world-accepting" polytheisms); comparative trade (the Viking Age emporia and other pre-modern port-of-trade networks). The project must be specific — not "compare the Vikings and the Polynesians" but "compare the role of assembly sites in the Icelandic Commonwealth and Hawaiian ahupua'a system."

## Lecture 8: Integrating the Modules — Cross-Disciplinary Synthesis I

By Week 9, each student has completed the foundational reading and the first seminar for both of their selected modules. The common lectures now turn to the work of synthesis: what has each module revealed? where do the disciplinary perspectives converge? where do they conflict? what remains invisible to both?

**The Convergence Exercise:** Each student prepares a brief presentation (5 minutes) to the full seminar group, identifying one insight from each of their two modules and articulating how those insights — drawn from different disciplinary traditions — might be combined to illuminate a Viking Studies question. Examples: a student who took Digital Humanities and Gender Studies might combine network analysis of saga social relationships with gender theory to ask whether women in the saga networks occupy structurally different positions than men. A student who took Environmental Humanities and Museum Studies might combine paleoenvironmental data with critical heritage theory to design an exhibition on climate change and the Viking Age.

The exercise reveals a pattern: cross-disciplinary synthesis is rarely a simple "adding together" of two perspectives. More often, it is a productive tension — one discipline's insight complicates and challenges the other's. The network analysis (Digital Humanities) that shows women as peripheral in saga social networks appears to confirm the gender studies insight that the sagas are androcentric — but the gender studies lens then asks: is this an accurate reflection of Viking Age social reality, or is it an artifact of the saga authors' selective representation? The digital method answers one question and opens another, and the cross-disciplinary scholar must navigate the recursion.

**The Limits of Interdisciplinarity:** The seminar also addresses what interdisciplinarity cannot do. Combining two partial perspectives does not produce a complete perspective — it produces a more complex partial perspective. A cross-disciplinary analysis that draws on archaeology and environmental science still does not access the subjective experience of the Viking Age people it studies. The seminar introduces the concept of "epistemic humility": the recognition that all disciplinary knowledge is partial, and that the goal of cross-disciplinary work is not omniscience but richer, more complex understanding.

## Lecture 9: Integrating the Modules — Cross-Disciplinary Synthesis II

The second synthesis lecture focuses on the module projects that students are completing. Each student presents their work-in-progress to the group, and the discussion focuses on the methodological challenges of cross-disciplinary work.

**The Method Translation Problem:** A recurring theme in the presentations is the difficulty of translating methods between disciplines. The student who applies Digital Humanities methods to a literary question must decide: what is the appropriate unit of analysis (the word? the sentence? the chapter?)? what counts as a significant pattern? how should quantitative results be presented to a readership trained in qualitative interpretation? The student who applies Gender Studies theory to archaeological data must translate concepts (performativity, heteronormativity, intersectionality) into terms that can be operationalized in the analysis of grave goods, spatial distributions, and osteological data. The "method translation" challenge is at the heart of cross-disciplinary work, and the seminar devotes substantial attention to it.

**The Integration Standard:** Based on the project presentations, the instructor articulates the standard for successful cross-disciplinary integration: a project that uses one discipline's methods to answer the other discipline's questions is additive (useful but basic); a project that uses each discipline to interrogate the other's assumptions is synthetic (the target); a project that generates a question that neither discipline could have asked alone is transformative (rare, but the ideal). Students are encouraged to aim for the synthetic level: not "here's what archaeology tells us, and here's what anthropology tells us" but "anthropology's concept of the gift economy challenges archaeology's assumption that grave goods reflect personal wealth — and the archaeological evidence of grave good distribution challenges anthropology's assumption that gift economies are inherently egalitarian."

## Lecture 10: Workshop — Cross-Disciplinary Project Development

A working session in which students develop their module projects and their capstone reflective essays with faculty and peer feedback. The workshop format: each student brings a specific question, problem, or draft section; the group reads it and provides focused feedback; the student leaves with an action plan.

**Common Workshop Issues:**

- **The "Two Separate Papers" Problem:** A student submits two module projects that are excellent within their respective disciplines but have no connection to each other. The capstone reflective essay must forge the connection — but if the projects are wholly unrelated, the essay will be forced. Solution: identify a linking theme early in the semester, even if the projects address different topics.

- **The "Dabbling" Problem:** A student's project demonstrates familiarity with the module discipline but does not engage deeply enough to produce real insight. The project reads like a summary of the module readings applied superficially to a Viking question. Solution: focus the project on a specific, narrow question that forces genuine engagement with the discipline's methods — not "applying environmental humanities to the Viking Age" but "using dendroclimatological data to test the hypothesis that the 10th-century settlement expansion in Iceland was driven by the Medieval Warm Period."

- **The "Methodology Dominance" Problem:** A student's project uses the host discipline's methods competently but never questions them — the student accepts the discipline's assumptions uncritically. Solution: the cross-disciplinary project should include a critical reflection on the method itself: what does this method assume? what does it miss? how does the Viking material complicate the method's usual operation?

**The Advisor's Role in Cross-Disciplinary Work:** Students are encouraged to consult not only the VS407 instructor but also the module instructors (from the relevant UoY departments) and their thesis advisors (if different). Cross-disciplinary work benefits from multiple perspectives — but it also risks receiving contradictory advice. The student's responsibility is to synthesize the advice, not to follow it all; the final product must represent the student's own intellectual synthesis, not a committee compromise.

## Lecture 11: Capstone Reflective Essay Workshop

The capstone reflective essay is the most unusual assignment in the Viking Studies program: it is about the student's learning process, not about the Viking Age. The essay asks the student to reflect on what the cross-disciplinary modules have taught them about: (1) the Viking Age, (2) the nature of disciplinary knowledge, and (3) their own intellectual trajectory. The workshop session helps students move from "here is what I did" to "here is what I learned."

**The Reflective Essay as Genre:** Reflective writing is a distinct genre with its own conventions. It is personal but not confessional — the student should write about their intellectual experience, not their emotional life. It is analytical but not argumentative — the student is analyzing their own learning, not advancing a thesis about the Viking Age. It is honest about difficulty — the most interesting reflective essays are those that acknowledge confusion, struggle, and unresolved questions.

**Structuring the Reflective Essay:** The recommended structure (not mandatory, but proven effective in previous years):

1. **Introduction (200 words):** Which modules did you choose, and why? What did you expect to learn?

2. **Module Synthesis (1,000 words):** For each module, identify the most important insight you gained — about the discipline, about the Viking Age, about the relationship between them. Be specific: cite a particular reading, a seminar discussion, or a moment in your project work that crystallized the insight.

3. **Cross-Disciplinary Reflection (500 words):** What did you learn about the relationship between your two modules? Did they complement each other? Conflict? Reveal different aspects of the same phenomenon? What questions emerged from their juxtaposition that neither discipline alone could have generated?

4. **Intellectual Trajectory (300 words):** How has this course changed your understanding of Viking Studies as a field? Of your own scholarly interests and strengths? Of what you want to do next (thesis topic, graduate study, career)?

**The Reflection Standard:** A successful reflective essay is specific, honest, and demonstrates genuine intellectual growth. The essay that says "I learned that interdisciplinarity is important" is a failure. The essay that says "The Digital Humanities module taught me that I had been assuming that 'reading the sagas' was an unproblematic activity — topic modeling revealed patterns in the legal vocabulary that I had missed in close reading, and this forced me to confront the limits of the interpretive methods I had been taking for granted" is a success. The essay is graded on the quality of the reflection, not on the student's agreement with any particular disciplinary position.

## Lecture 12: The Disciplined Generalist — Viking Studies and the Future of Knowledge

The final lecture returns to the theme that opened the course: the intellectual map of Viking Studies and the place of cross-disciplinary thinking in the production of knowledge.

**The Generalist in the Age of Specialization:** Knowledge production in 2040 is characterized by ever-narrowing specialization. The typical academic researcher knows more and more about less and less — a necessary condition of scientific and scholarly progress, but also a limitation. The researcher who has spent a career studying, say, the metallurgy of Viking Age iron knives in central Jutland knows that subject with extraordinary depth, but may be unable to connect it to larger questions about trade, social organization, or cultural meaning. The specialist needs the generalist — the scholar who can read across disciplines, identify connections, and synthesize knowledge — to connect the dots.

The Viking Studies program is designed to produce not narrow specialists but "disciplined generalists" — scholars who have deep knowledge of the Viking Age (the four-year program ensures this) but who also have the cross-disciplinary literacy to connect their knowledge to other fields, to communicate with scholars in other disciplines, and to address questions that transcend disciplinary boundaries.

**Viking Studies as a Model for Cross-Disciplinary Education:** The Viking Age is an ideal subject for cross-disciplinary education because it sits at the intersection of so many disciplines. It is historical (it happened in time, documented in texts), archaeological (its remains survive in the ground), literary (its stories survive in the sagas), linguistic (its language survives in runes and place-names), environmental (its people interacted with climate and landscape), and comparative (it exemplifies patterns of social organization that recur across human history). No single discipline can capture it; every discipline illuminates part of it. The Viking Studies student who learns to navigate this disciplinary landscape is acquiring skills that transfer well beyond Viking Studies — to any field that requires synthetic thinking, critical evaluation of evidence from multiple sources, and communication across intellectual boundaries.

**The 2040 Professional Landscape:** The lecture concludes with a realistic discussion of the professional landscape awaiting VS407 students. The academic job market remains competitive, but the skills developed in a cross-disciplinary program — critical thinking, research design, data analysis, clear writing, and the ability to synthesize diverse types of information — are valued across a wide range of professions. UoY Viking Studies alumni work in heritage management, museum curation, digital media, game design, environmental consulting, cultural policy, education, journalism, and the tech sector (where AI-training in humanities contexts is a growing field). The cross-disciplinary training of VS407 prepares students not for a single career path but for the adaptive, lifelong learning that the 2040 professional landscape demands.

**Final Reflection:** The course closes with a prompt, not an answer: What does the Viking Age look like from outside Viking Studies? The student who has spent a semester in another discipline returns to the Viking Studies program with new eyes — seeing questions they had not seen before, questioning assumptions they had not known they held. This defamiliarization — making strange what had become familiar — is the most valuable gift of cross-disciplinary education. The Vikings, seen from a new angle, become strange again — and the student becomes, once more, a beginner, looking at the evidence with fresh curiosity. That posture — the willingness to be a beginner, to ask naive questions, to see with new eyes — is the posture that produces the best scholarship, in any discipline, at any career stage.

## Assignments

### Assignment 1: Module Reading Response Papers (2 × 10% = 20%)

**Objective:** Demonstrate close engagement with the module reading lists and the ability to critically evaluate scholarship from an unfamiliar discipline.

**Task:** For each of your two selected modules, write a reading response paper (1,000 words) addressing the following:

1. **Synthesis (400 words):** What are the central arguments, methods, and debates in this module's key texts? Do not merely summarize each text; synthesize them into a coherent account of the discipline's approach to the Viking Age (or comparable pre-modern societies).

2. **Critical Engagement (400 words):** Identify one assumption, method, or conclusion from the readings that you find particularly illuminating — or particularly problematic. Articulate your response, drawing on your Viking Studies training to evaluate the discipline's claims.

3. **Connection (200 words):** How does this discipline's approach connect to, challenge, or complement the approaches you have learned in the core Viking Studies curriculum?

**Deliverable:** Two response papers (one per module), each 1,000 words. Module A/Paper 1 due Week 4; Module B/Paper 2 due Week 6.

**Grading Rubric:**
- Synthesis accuracy and depth (35%)
- Critical engagement specificity and insight (35%)
- Connection to core curriculum relevance (20%)
- Writing quality and citation accuracy (10%)

### Assignment 2: Module Project A (25%)

**Objective:** Produce a substantial piece of work that applies the first module discipline's methods to a Viking Studies question.

**Task:** Complete the module project as described in your module's lecture (see Lectures 2-7). All module projects share common elements:

- A clearly articulated research question that the module discipline is well-suited to address
- Application of the discipline's methods (computational, critical, comparative, design-based) to Viking Age evidence
- Substantive engagement with the module's key texts
- Critical reflection on the method's limitations

**Deliverable:** A paper or project (2,500-3,000 words depending on format). Due Week 9.

**Grading Rubric (module-independent):**
- Research question clarity and appropriateness to discipline (20%)
- Methodological execution competence (30%)
- Evidence use, analysis, and interpretation (25%)
- Critical reflection on method limitations (15%)
- Writing/presentation quality and professional formatting (10%)

**Objective:** Produce a substantial piece of work that applies the second module discipline's methods to a Viking Studies question.

**Task:** Same as Assignment 2, for your second module. Due Week 12.

**Grading Rubric:** Same as Assignment 2.

### Assignment 4: Capstone Reflective Essay (30%)

**Objective:** Synthesize the cross-disciplinary learning of the entire course into a reflective account of your intellectual development.

**Task:** Write a reflective essay (2,000 words) as described in Lecture 11. The essay should address:

1. **Module Synthesis (1,000 words):** For each module, the most important insight you gained — about the discipline, about the Viking Age, about the relationship between them. Be specific.

2. **Cross-Disciplinary Reflection (500 words):** What did you learn about the relationship between your two modules? How did they complement, challenge, or illuminate each other?

3. **Intellectual Trajectory (500 words):** How has this course changed your understanding of Viking Studies, your scholarly identity, and your plans for the future?

**Deliverable:** A reflective essay of 2,000 words. Due Week 14.

**Grading Rubric:**
- Module synthesis specificity and intellectual depth (35%)
- Cross-disciplinary reflection insight and integration (30%)
- Intellectual trajectory honesty and self-awareness (20%)
- Writing quality and reflective genre appropriateness (15%)
