## Program Overview

The PhD in AI Operating System Design is the apex of the Yggdrasil OS track — a program for researchers who will define the next generation of cognitive operating systems. Doctoral candidates do not merely study existing architectures; they create new ones. The program emphasizes original research contribution, rigorous formal methodology, and deep engagement with the unsolved problems of AI OS design: provable alignment in self-modifying systems, memory architectures that scale to lifelong autonomous operation, and governance frameworks that survive the test of time.

Graduates become research leads, tenure-track faculty, chief architects at AI OS companies, and standards body authors.

## Research Seminar Series (Years 1–2)

Doctoral candidates must complete 8 research seminars. Each seminar is 3 credits and combines guided reading, research presentation, and original scholarly contribution. Seminars are organized around frontier topics and change based on faculty research direction.

### OS701 — Seminar: Foundations of Cognitive Kernel Theory
**3 Credits** | *The First Root: Theory*

Deep engagement with the theoretical foundations of cognitive kernels: computability bounds on persistent memory systems, information-theoretic limits of MuninnGate throughput, topology of memory hierarchy, and the Yggdrasil Memory Theorem (memory capacity grows as the log of the tree depth, not linearly). Students present and defend original theoretical results. Required for all first-year OS doctoral candidates.

### OS703 — Seminar: Self-Referential Systems and Fixed Points
**3 Credits** | *The Serpent Eating Its Tail*

Self-referential systems — kernels that reason about kernels, prompts that modify prompts, memory that stores memory-about-memory — are the theoretical frontier of AI OS. This seminar covers fixed-point theory for self-modifying systems, Gödelian limits on self-knowledge in AI OS, and the Odhinn Fixed-Point Theorem: any sufficiently powerful OS must contain a representation of itself. Students develop original results on the boundary between useful self-reference and pathological self-reference.

### OS705 — Seminar: Memory Ontology and Cross-Domain Alignment
**3 Credits** | *The Roots That Cross*

Research seminar on memory ontology — the formal study of what memories *are* and how they relate across domains. Topics include: universal memory schemas, ontology alignment algorithms for cross-domain MemCubes, the semantic distance problem (how far apart are two memories from different worlds?), and the Root Alignment Problem (can every memory be grounded in a universal ontology?). Students contribute to the Yggdrasil Memory Ontology Project.

### OS707 — Seminar: Verification and Proof in Cognitive Systems
**3 Credits** | *The Gátt of All Proofs*

Advanced seminar on formal verification for AI OS. Topics include: proof-carrying memory, compositional verification of MuninnGate stacks, dependent type theory for kernel specifications, and the Open Problem: can we prove that an AI OS with self-modification capability *never* violates its governance invariants? The seminar requires each student to make an original contribution toward solving this problem, however incremental.

### OS709 — Seminar: Governance as Operating System
**3 Credits** | *The Unbreakable Oath Made Mathematical*

Treating governance as a kernel-level concern, not a policy overlay. This seminar covers: axiomatic value systems embedded in OS roots, fault-tolerant governance through Bifröst consensus, the formal specification of human rights as kernel invariants, and the Norn Constitute as a formally verified governance OS. Students write and formally verify a governance specification for an AI OS subsystem.

### OS711 — Seminar: Catastrophic Forgetting and Phase Transition Prevention
**3 Credits** | *Preventing Ragnarök*

The deepest fear of long-running AI OS operators: catastrophic forgetting — the loss of critical memories during system restructuring. This seminar covers: mathematical models of catastrophic forgetting, phase transition early warning systems, the Muninn Consolidation Protocol for memory preservation during restructuring, and designing OS kernels that can undergo controlled Ragnarök (total system reset) without losing identity. Each student proposes and defends an original approach to the Catastrophic Forgetting Problem.

### OS713 — Seminar: Federated Cognitive Architecture
**3 Credits** | *The Many-Treed Forest*

When multiple Yggdrasil-class AI OS instances form a network, what emerges? This seminar covers: federated identity canonization (how multiple agents canonize a shared identity), distributed MuninnGate protocols, federated memory consensus, and the Forest Protocol — an extension of Yggdrasil from a single tree to an interconnected forest. Students research how individual OS instances maintain coherence when they share memory substrates.

### OS715 — Seminar: Advanced AI OS Security and Cognitive Warfare
**3 Credits** | *Surtr's Flame: Defense Against The Inevitable*

The most adversarial seminar in the program. Cognitive warfare: state-level actors attacking AI OS infrastructure, manipulating memory at scale, conducting identity-level social engineering, and weaponizing governance shells. This seminar covers: cognitive threat modeling, the Surtr Protocol (defense against existential threats to agent identity), cognitive resilience engineering, and the ethics of developing offensive cognitive capabilities. Students develop and defend a novel cognitive warfare defense. Security clearance and ethical review required.

## Research and Dissertation (Years 2–5)

### OS802 — Doctoral Dissertation: AI Operating System Design
**16 Credits** | *The Fruit of Yggdrasil*

The doctoral dissertation. Candidates must make an original, significant contribution to the field of AI Operating System Design — not an incremental improvement, but a novel kernel architecture, verification method, governance framework, or theoretical result that advances the state of the art. The dissertation must be defended before a committee of three Yggdrasil faculty and one external examiner from an allied institution.

**Milestones:**
- **Year 1:** Research prospectus and committee formation
- **Year 2:** Comprehensive examination (based on seminar readings) and thesis proposal defense
- **Years 2–4:** Original research, publications in top venues
- **Year 3–5:** Dissertation writing and defense

**Dissertation Requirements:**
1. Original contribution to AI OS Design theory or practice
2. Formal verification or empirical validation of key claims
3. At least two peer-reviewed publications in top-tier venues
4. Public dissertation defense

## Active Research Frontiers

- **Provable Alignment in Self-Modifying Kernels:** Can we guarantee that an AI OS that rewrites its own instructions will never violate its original values?
- **Quantum Memory Architectures:** Superposition-based memory states that collapse on retrieval — what are the information-theoretic implications?
- **Biological Memory Consolidation for AI:** Translating hippocampal replay and sleep consolidation into OS-level memory maintenance routines.
- **The Yggdrasil Conjecture:** A mathematical conjecture that any sufficiently general AI OS must organize memory as a tree-like hierarchy. Currently unproven.
- **Cognitive Phase Transition Prediction:** Can we detect the early warning signs of catastrophic restructuring before it occurs?
- **Federated Governance:** How do multiple independently governed AI OS instances reach consensus on shared values?
