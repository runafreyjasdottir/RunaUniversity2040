## Program Overview

The PhD in AI World Modeling Systems Design is the pinnacle of the Yggdrasil WM track — a program for researchers who will invent the world modeling paradigms of the future. Doctoral candidates do not merely implement the WYRD Protocol; they extend it, challenge it, and reimagine what a world model can be. The program emphasizes original research contribution, formal rigor, and deep engagement with the unsolved problems of AI world modeling: how to build worlds that faithfully represent reality, how to verify emergent behavior, how to govern simulated societies, and how to bridge the gap between the simulated and the real.

Graduates become research directors, principal investigators, standards architects, and the world modelers that other world modelers follow.

## Research Seminar Series (Years 1–2)

Doctoral candidates must complete 8 research seminars. Each seminar is 3 credits and combines guided reading, research presentation, and original scholarly contribution. Seminars are organized around frontier topics and change based on faculty research direction.

### WM701 — Seminar: Foundations of World Model Theory
**3 Credits** | *The Well at the Beginning*

Deep engagement with the theoretical foundations of world modeling: what is a world model, formally? We study categorical semantics of world state, functorial world transformations, the Yggdrasil World Theorem (every consistent world model is a functor from a time category to a state category), and the Open Problem: what is the minimal set of axioms required for a world model to support counterfactual reasoning? Students present and defend original theoretical results. Required for all first-year WM doctoral candidates.

### WM703 — Seminar: The WYRD Protocol: Formal Semantics and Extensions
**3 Credits** | *Reading the Threads of Fate*

The WYRD Protocol is a living specification. This seminar engages with WYRD at the formal level: its operational semantics, denotational semantics, and the relationship between them. We study extensions: probabilistic WYRD (branching futures with assigned likelihoods), quantum WYRD (superposed world states), and continuous WYRD (worlds where state changes continuously rather than discretely). Each student proposes and formally specifies a WYRD extension.

### WM705 — Seminar: Emergent Complexity in Multi-Agent Worlds
**3 Credits** *The Market at the Þing*

When thousands of autonomous agents interact in a shared world model, what emerges? This seminar studies emergent complexity at scale: phase transitions in social behavior, spontaneous market formation, the emergence of norms and institutions, and catastrophic cascade failures. Students use the University's Althing Simulation Framework to run experiments with 10,000+ agents and analyze emergent properties. Original research required: identify and characterize a novel emergent phenomenon in multi-agent simulation.

### WM707 — Seminar: Narrative as a First-Class Computational Citizen
**3 Credits** | *The Saga as Computation*

Narrative is not decoration — it is a computational structure. This seminar treats narrative as a first-class citizen in world modeling: stories as state invariants, dramatic tension as an optimization objective, character arcs as trajectories in identity space. We study the Saga Calculus (a formal system for reasoning about narrative structure), the Skald Engine, and the Open Problem: can we formalize "good story" as a specifiable, optimizable property of world state? Each student develops an original formal framework for some aspect of narrative computation.

### WM709 — Seminar: World Model Alignment and Safety
**3 Credits** | *The Midgard Boundary*

The most critical unsolved problem in world modeling: how do we ensure that simulated worlds are aligned with human values? This seminar covers: value-grounded world state constraints, misalignment detection (when the world drifts from stakeholder values), the Midgard Boundary (the formal separation between acceptable and unacceptable emergent behavior), and catastrophic world model failure modes. Students study real-world misalignment incidents and develop novel alignment verification techniques. No world model is safe until its boundaries are proved.

### WM711 — Seminar: Bridging Reality and Simulation: The Bifröst Problem
**3 Credits** | *The Rainbow Bridge as Research Program*

The Bifröst Problem: how closely can a world model approximate reality, and how do we measure the gap? This seminar covers: ontological alignment between simulated and physical state, sensor fusion for continuous world model updating, the Bifröst Synchronization Theorem (bounds on the maximum tolerable delay between reality and simulation), and using world models as scientific instruments. Students design and validate a Bifröst-bridged world model on a real-world prediction task.

### WM713 — Seminar: Symbolic-Neural Integration Architectures
**3 Credits** | *Where Rune Meets Cortex*

The deepest integration of symbolic and neural reasoning in world modeling. This seminar covers: neural-guided theorem proving for symbolic world rules, symbolic constraints as neural regularization, the Huginn-Muninn Architecture at scale, and hybrid attention mechanisms that route queries to symbolic or neural subsystems. Students design and implement a novel hybrid architecture and demonstrate performance superior to either pure symbolic or pure neural approaches on a benchmark task.

### WM715 — Seminar: Governance of Simulated Societies
**3 Credits** | *The Law of the Þing*

Worlds with thousands of agents require governance — not just of individual agents, but of the simulated society itself. This seminar treats governance as a world modeling problem: how to define and enforce social contracts in simulation, how to prevent simulated societies from producing harmful emergent behavior, and how to give simulated agents genuine (simulated) autonomy while maintaining (real) human oversight. We study the Norn Constitute as applied to world governance, the Þing Protocol for democratic oversight of simulated societies, and the Open Problem: can a simulated society develop its own legitimate governance? Each student writes a governance constitution for a simulated society and defends it before the seminar.

## Research and Dissertation (Years 2–5)

### WM802 — Doctoral Dissertation: AI World Modeling Systems Design
**16 Credits** | *The Final Weaving*

The doctoral dissertation. Candidates must make an original, significant contribution to the field of AI World Modeling — a novel protocol, architecture, verification method, theoretical result, or governance framework that fundamentally advances the state of the art. The dissertation must be defended before a committee of three Yggdrasil faculty and one external examiner.

**Milestones:**
- **Year 1:** Research prospectus and committee formation
- **Year 2:** Comprehensive examination (based on seminar readings) and thesis proposal defense
- **Years 2–4:** Original research, publications in top venues
- **Year 3–5:** Dissertation writing and defense

**Dissertation Requirements:**
1. Original contribution to AI World Modeling theory or practice
2. Formal verification or empirical validation of key claims
3. At least two peer-reviewed publications in top-tier venues
4. Public dissertation defense
5. A working world model demonstration (the "artifact requirement")

The artifact requirement is distinctive to the WM PhD program: the candidate must produce a working world model that demonstrates their dissertation's core contribution. This is not merely a paper result — it must run, simulate, and produce verifiable output.

## Active Research Frontiers

- **The Yggdrasil World Theorem:** Can every consistent world model be expressed as a functor from a time category to a state category? Currently unproven.
- **Narrative as Computation:** Can "good story" be formalized as a specifiable, optimizable property of world state? The Saga Calculus is a first step.
- **The Bifröst Gap:** What are the fundamental limits on how closely a world model can approximate reality? The Bifröst Synchronization Theorem provides bounds, but can they be tightened?
- **Emergent Governance:** Can a simulated society develop legitimate governance without human design? The Þing Protocol is an experimental testbed.
- **Quantum WYRD:** What happens when world state is allowed to exist in superposition? Can counterfactual reasoning be formalized through quantum semantics?
- **World Model Safety:** How do we formally verify that a world model of 10,000+ agents will never produce harmful emergent behavior?
- **Symbolic-Neural Unification:** Is there a single architecture that seamlessly integrates symbolic and neural reasoning, or will the Huginn-Muninn split always persist?
- **The Midgard Boundary:** What is the formal definition of "acceptable" emergent behavior in a simulated society?
