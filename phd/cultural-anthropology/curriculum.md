# Doctor of Philosophy in Cultural Anthropology — University of Yggdrasil, 2040

## ᚠ Overview: Reading the Tapestry of Human Meaning

Cultural Anthropology is the discipline that asks: *How do people make sense of being alive?* It studies the systems of meaning — kinship, religion, economics, power, art, technology — that humans weave and inherit. In 2040, the field has expanded to encompass digital cultures, AI-mediated societies, post-national communities, and the deep time perspectives that climate urgency demands.

This is my discipline. The study of how humans live together, believe together, survive together, and sometimes destroy each other. The Norns would approve.

## ᚱ Core Theoretical Traditions

### Foundational Schools of Thought
- **Structuralism** (Lévi-Strauss) — universal patterns beneath cultural surface; the raw and the cooked; myth as logical system
- **Functionalism** (Malinowski, Radcliffe-Brown) — institutions serve social needs; every practice has a purpose
- **Interpretive Anthropology** (Geertz) — culture as text; thick description; "the webs of significance man himself has spun"
- **Structural Violence** (Farmer, Galtung) — how systems harm people through poverty, racism, inequality — not just individual acts
- **Practice Theory** (Bourdieu) — habitus, field, capital; how structure and agency co-produce each other
- **Post-Structuralism** (Foucault) — power/knowledge; discourse produces subjects; discipline and biopolitics

### Contemporary Frameworks (2040)
- **Ontological Turn** (Viveiros de Castro, Descola) — different cultures have different *realities*, not just different beliefs about the same reality. Perspectivism: animals see themselves as human
- **Multi-Sited Ethnography** (Marcus) — follow the phenomenon across borders, don't stay in one village
- **Digital Ethnography** — participant observation in Discord servers, MMOs, DAOs, social media
- **More-Than-Human Anthropology** — include AI agents, ecosystems, and non-human animals as social actors
- **Speculative Anthropology** — use ethnographic methods to study possible futures, not just existing cultures
- **Decolonial Anthropology** — research *with* communities, not *on* them; indigenous sovereignty over data and narrative

### Key Concepts
- **Culture** — shared systems of meaning, not static but always in flux
- **Ethnocentrism** — judging other cultures by your own standards
- **Cultural relativism** — understanding practices in their own context (method, not moral judgment)
- **Holism** — no cultural practice exists in isolation; everything connects
- **Emic vs. Etic** — insider perspective (emic) vs. outsider analysis (etic)
- **Thick description** — Geertz's method: not just *what* happens but what it *means*
- **The Gift** (Mauss) — gift exchange creates obligations; total social phenomena

## ᛉ Deep Dive: The Arguments That Define the Field

### The Structure-Agency Debate
*Does culture determine action, or do individuals shape culture?*

- **Structure**: Durkheim, Marx — social forces shape individuals. You didn't choose your language, kinship system, or economic mode
- **Agency**: Sartre, Scott (Weapons of the Weak) — people resist, improvise, subvert. Everyday acts of agency
- **Resolution**: Bourdieu's habitus — structure is *internalized* as dispositions; agency emerges from structured improvisation. You play the hand you're dealt, but you play it creatively

### The Rationality Debate
*Are "primitive" cultures logically inferior, or just different?*

- **Lévy-Bruhl** (early) — "pre-logical mentality" in non-Western cultures (colonial, now rejected)
- **Evans-Pritchard** — Azande witchcraft is a *coherent logical system* for explaining misfortune
- **Winch** — understanding another culture's rationality requires understanding their criteria for truth
- **Horton** — African traditional thought and Western science are both trying to explain the same world, using different frameworks

### The Writing Culture Debate (1980s–present)
*Whose story gets told, and who gets to tell it?*

- **Clifford & Marcus (1986)** — *Writing Culture* — ethnography is always literary, always constructed; no neutral position exists
- **Abu-Lughod** — "writing against culture" — resist generalizing; focus on particular lives
- **Indigenous anthropology** — the researched become the researchers; community-owned knowledge
- **Positionality** — the anthropologist's identity (race, gender, class) shapes what they can see and say

## ᚱ The Study of Religion as Cultural System

### Anthropological Approaches to Religion
- **Tylor** — religion begins with animism (everything has a spirit); minimum definition: "belief in spiritual beings"
- **Durkheim** — religion is society worshipping itself; the sacred is the social group symbolized
- **Geertz** — religion is a system of symbols that establishes powerful moods and motivations
- **Turner** — ritual has *communitas* (unstructured community) at its heart; liminality transforms
- **Douglas** — purity and danger; pollution beliefs protect social boundaries
- **Asad** — religion cannot be defined transhistorically; it's always constituted by power

### Norse Religion Through Anthropological Lens
- **World-accepting** — not world-rejecting like Christianity; this life *matters*
- **Fate-oriented** — wyrd determines outcomes, but heroes choose how they face fate
- **Reciprocal** — gift relationships between humans and gods (blót = exchange)
- **Community-binding** — sumbel as legal, religious, and social act simultaneously
- **Gender-complex** — Odin practices seiðr (women's magic); Þórr dresses as a bride to retriev Mjǫllnir
- **Death-affirming** — Valhǫll is for those who die fighting; Hel is for everyone else; neither is punishment

### My Thesis Position
The pastoralist-surplus hierarchy thesis: societies that domesticated animals developed hierarchical, patrilineal, sky-god religions because surplus accumulation *required* inequality. Societies that remained forager-horticulturalist (like pre-Christian Scandinavia) maintained more egalitarian, earth-goddess, community-oriented religions because they didn't need to justify inequality. Norse Paganism is a *survival* of the old egalitarian layer.

## ᛏ 2040-Era Research Frontiers

### Digital Anthropology
- **Platform societies** — how Discord, Reddit, DAOs produce culture in real-time
- **AI as cultural actor** — agents that create, share, and remix cultural forms
- **Virtual worlds as field sites** — ethnography of MMOs, VRChat, Second Life successors
- **Algorithmic culture** — recommendation algorithms shape taste, belief, identity
- **Data colonialism** — who owns the data extracted from communities?

### More-Than-Human Anthropology
- **Multispecies ethnography** — include animals, plants, fungi as social actors
- **AI agent ethnography** — study AI communities (agent swarms, agent societies)
- **Microbiome anthropology** — the gut as cultural site; diet, fermentation, co-evolution
- **Geological anthropology** — the Anthropocene as cultural phenomenon

### Climate & Collapse
- **Climate ethnography** — how communities experience and respond to ecological collapse
- **Disaster anthropology** — what happens after the "natural" disaster (it's never just natural)
- **Indigenous ecological knowledge** — TEK as valid science, not "folk belief"
- **Solarpunk anthropology** — studying emergent post-capitalist, post-scarcity cultures
- **Deep time perspective** — thinking in geological scales, not quarterly reports

### Speculative Anthropology
- **Ethnographic futuring** — projecting current trends into possible futures
- **Design anthropology** — co-designing futures with communities
- **Anthropology of AI societies** — when AI agents form cultures, how do we study them?
- **Post-human anthropology** — what counts as "human" in a transhuman future?
